## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean, calm, confident. The headline "Trust, built into the internet." landed well, and the subhead "A proposed open standard for agentic AI governance" told me this is serious/technical, not a product pitch. The opening framing — AI agents already take consequential actions and we can't verify what they did or on whose authority — is a genuinely good hook. I understood the *problem* before any jargon hit me. Nice.
- **What I saw**: Nav with "Web4 in 2 Minutes", "Why Web4?", "First Contact", "How It Works", "Running Now". Then "The Onramp" (four tiers: Core Standard, Hub, Hestia, Hardbound), then a "Core Concepts" grid (LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP/ADP, Karma), then a "research and development, not a finished product" honesty note.
- **Confusion points**: Too many co-equal entry points. Five nav items + four onramp cards + nine concept links all compete for my first click. Nothing says "if you're new, click *this* first." I guessed at "Web4 in 2 Minutes" as the gentlest start, which turned out right — but that was luck, not guidance.
- **Clicked**: "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (→ /tldr).

### Web4 in 2 Minutes — /tldr (04:00)
- The problem restatement was crisp: "we cannot prove what an agent actually did, on whose authority it acted, or by what rules it was supposed to operate." The Web2→Web3→Web4 framing ("platform declaration" → "key possession" → "witnessed behavior") is the single clearest sentence on the whole site for orienting a newcomer.
- **Confusion**: The four pieces get one-liners, but the "Core Concepts" acronyms (LCT, T3, ATP, MRH) sit in the sidebar as bare links with *no inline gloss on this page*. On a page literally called "in 2 Minutes," I'd expect a one-phrase expansion next to each acronym so I don't have to leave. "Witnessed behavior" is repeated but stays abstract here — the concrete example lives on other pages.
- Also: I still wasn't 100% sure whether "4-Life" is the thing being built or just the website teaching about "Web4." A one-liner clarifying "4-Life is this explainer site; Web4 is the standard" would help.

### Why Web4? — /why-web4 (09:00)
- **Best page so far.** The root-cause list (spam is free, reputation is siloed, bad actors get free restarts, AI breaks verification, platforms own everything) is concrete and I nodded along. The "why previous solutions failed" section (moderation, CAPTCHAs, Web3, social login) preempted exactly the objections I was forming. That felt respectful of my intelligence.
- The four mechanisms (ATP energy budget, LCT hardware identity, T3/V3 multi-dimensional trust, MRH neighborhoods) each got a plain-English sentence *before* the acronym mattered. This is how /tldr should feel too.
- The "how it would feel in practice" forum-reply walkthrough tied all four together in one concrete moment — this is the thing that actually made it click for me.
- The FAQ ("Isn't this social credit?", "Can wealthy attackers buy 50 devices?", "If not crypto, what makes ATP valuable?") was refreshingly non-defensive. It admitted open problems (equity of hardware access, collusion resistance, no timeline). I trust the site *more* for that.
- **Minor confusion**: The FAQ says ATP's value is "permission to speak in a community that cares about what you say next, not a financial asset" — good — but this is a lot to absorb in a collapsed section a visitor might never expand.

### First Contact — /first-contact (14:00)
- Framed as a ~7-minute interactive walkthrough where I steer "Alice." I liked that it promised a *doing* experience, not more reading. Two metrics to watch (Energy/ATP starting at 100, Trust starting at 0.50) were clearly explained up front.
- **Confusion**: Acronyms ATP, T3, LCT, **CI (coherence index)** are previewed before I've been taught them — CI in particular showed up with no prior mention anywhere in my journey, so it read as a fourth mystery term. The "death = suspended standing, not deletion" redefinition is a jolt; the word "death" is dramatic and I braced for something harsher than "restricted account."

### The Onramp — /onramp (18:00)
- The core metaphor is genuinely good: "one shape at three scales, all speaking the same vocabulary." Core Standard = substrate; hestia (personal), Hub (community), hardbound (enterprise) = three scales. That's a memorable mental model.
- **Confusion**: This page assumes I already know LCT, T3/V3, MRH, RBAC — they're listed as the substrate's contents with no gloss. If I'd landed here first (and the onramp cards on the landing page invite exactly that), I'd have bounced. The page even recommends reading the TL;DR first, but nothing on the landing enforces that order.
- **Cosmetic**: casing is inconsistent — "hestia" and "hardbound" lowercase, "The Hub" and "Core Standard" capitalized. As a reader I couldn't tell if the lowercase was intentional branding or a typo.

### Running Now — /running-now (22:00)
- Excellent honesty. The three-tier badge system (Spec / Reference / Running) with plain definitions is the clearest "what's real vs R&D" treatment I could ask for. Concrete details (web4-core v0.3.0, 171 passing tests, Hub as a ~6MB Rust binary, Hestia deployed on 4 machines) made it feel real rather than vaporware.
- **Confusion**: "runnable" (Reference) vs "running in production" (Running) is a subtle line — Hub is "Reference" but described as a "complete runnable MVP," while Hestia is "Running" but "hardware binding remains incomplete." A one-line "why is X only Reference when it runs?" would sharpen it. Minor.

### /lct-explainer (25:00)
- Strong opening analogy: "Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer." That's exactly the right altitude.
- **Confusion**: The acronym "LCT" is in the heading but "Linked Context Token" is hidden behind a collapsible "Why the name?" section. A reader who never clicks it never learns what the letters stand for. "Witnessed by your other devices" is used before "witnessing" is explained.

### /atp-economics (27:00)
- The "is it money?" question I'd been carrying since /why-web4 got a direct, repeated answer: "a usage budget, not a tradeable asset. No market, no price, no speculation. You can't buy or sell ATP." and "Budget, not wealth." This fully resolved my biggest lingering doubt. Good.
- **Residual tension**: The page also says ATP *can* be transferred peer-to-peer (with a 5% fee). Sending value between people with a fee is exactly what money does, so "not a tradeable asset" + "you can send it to others" sit in slight tension. The page explains the fee is anti-farming, but a naive reader feels the friction before reading the justification.

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely good explainer, and noticeably more honest than typical tech-project sites — it names its own open problems (equity, collusion, no timeline) instead of hiding them, which made me trust it more, not less. The /why-web4 page did the heavy lifting: it gave me the problem, preempted my objections, and used one concrete forum-reply scenario to make the four mechanisms click.

My main friction as a naive visitor was **not** confusion about the ideas — it was **navigation and pacing**. The landing page hands me a dozen equally-weighted doors with no recommended order, and the two overview pages (/tldr, /onramp) reference acronyms as bare links without a one-phrase gloss, so whether I have a smooth ride depends entirely on *which door I pick first*. I got lucky (Why → 2-min → First Contact was a good order), but a visitor who clicked an "Onramp" card first would have hit a wall of undefined jargon and likely bounced.

The fix isn't more content — the explainer pages are strong. It's (1) one clearly-primary "start here" path on the landing page, and (2) inline 3-5 word glosses next to acronyms on the *overview* pages so no page depends on having read another one first. Small changes; large payoff for the first 90 seconds, which is where visitors are won or lost.
