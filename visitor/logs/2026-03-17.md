## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoughtfully constructed explainer sites I've encountered for a research project. The progressive disclosure works — I could start with 2 minutes and go as deep as I wanted. The landing page is strong, the TL;DR is excellent, and the "What Could Go Wrong" page is genuinely impressive in its honesty.

**What works well:**
- Problem-first framing. Every page starts with WHY before WHAT.
- Honesty about limitations. "Active research, not a product" is stated clearly and repeatedly.
- The Alice narrative (First Contact) makes abstract concepts concrete.
- Interactive tools (simulators, quizzes, the survival challenge) break up the reading.
- The biological metaphor (ATP, death, rebirth, metabolism) is surprisingly coherent once you get past the initial strangeness.

**What needs work:**
- **Page length**: The Trust Tensor and LCT pages are too long. They try to serve both curious visitors and technical builders in a single scroll. Split them.
- **Acronym overload**: By page 5, I was juggling ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, and MRH. That's 7 acronyms for a "no jargon" site. The plain-English descriptions are good but the alphabet soup accumulates.
- **Missing concept page**: MRH (Markov Relevancy Horizon / context boundaries) is referenced everywhere but I never found a clear intro page for it. It's clearly important but undersold.
- **"What Could Go Wrong" is hidden**: This page is one of the site's best assets and it's not in the main learning path. Someone who follows the suggested flow (TL;DR → First Contact → Why Web4 → core concepts) would miss it entirely.
- **The death metaphor may alienate**: "Your account dies" is dramatic and memorable, but for real humans it might feel threatening rather than motivating. The "suspended license" analogy buried on the Aliveness page is actually much better framing. Lead with that.

**Bottom line**: I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left understanding the core ideas — energy budgets, hardware-bound identity, multi-dimensional trust, and measurable aliveness. The site taught me these things; I didn't have to infer them. That's a sign of good explainer design. The main friction is information density on the deep-dive pages and the sheer number of acronyms to track. The site would benefit from being 20% less comprehensive and 20% more curated in its default learning path.
