## Unanswered Questions

1. **What does actual adoption look like practically?** The five-tier pathway is described but I don't have a concrete picture of what Tier 1 ("Wrapper") looks like as a product someone could use today or next year.
2. **How would existing platforms integrate?** Would Twitter/Reddit/LinkedIn need to cooperate, or could Web4 overlay on top of them without their participation?
3. **What happens with group contributions?** If a team writes a document together, how is ATP credit split? The "what-could-go-wrong" page flagged this as unsolved, but I'm curious what approaches are being considered.
4. **How do trust scores handle context drift?** If I'm a great data analyst who transitions to management, my analyst trust is high but my management trust is low. Is there any "adjacent competence" recognition, or do I truly start from scratch in every new role?
5. **What's the actual user interface?** The wireframes on "Day in Web4" are text descriptions. I'd love to see even rough visual mockups of what a Web4-enabled inbox or profile page would look like.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most well-organized educational sites I've encountered for a complex technical topic. The progressive disclosure works: I went from "what is this?" to "I understand the full architecture" in about 50 minutes, and at no point did I feel lost or talked down to.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- The tiered entry points (2 min TL;DR → 5 min Why → 7 min interactive). Brilliant pacing.
- Problem-before-solution structure on every page. I always understood WHY before WHAT.
- Radical honesty about limitations. Leading "What Could Go Wrong" with "adoption failure is the #1 existential risk" builds trust that crypto projects never achieve.
- Alice's story in First Contact makes abstract concepts concrete and memorable.
- The concept sequence (LCT → ATP → T3 → MRH → CI → Aliveness) builds naturally — each concept depends on the previous ones.
- Interactive simulators let me develop intuition, not just read about it.

**What could be stronger:**
- Visual mockups of the actual user experience. The "Day in Web4" scenarios are compelling but the wireframes are text descriptions rather than visual designs. Even rough sketches would help me imagine what using Web4 would actually feel like.
- The "MRH" name is intimidating. "Context Boundaries" is what I'd call this concept when explaining it to a friend.
- There's a slight gap between "this is active research" and "here's what you can do with it now." A clearer "How to get involved" or "What's the roadmap?" page would help convert curious visitors into participants.

**Overall impression:** This feels like a genuine research project with substance behind it, not a hype machine. The depth of thought is evident. The honesty about unsolved problems is its strongest trust signal — which is ironic and fitting for a project about trust. I'd come back to explore the interactive simulations more deeply, and I'd genuinely consider following the project's development.

The biggest remaining question in my mind is practical: when and how does this become something I can actually use? The five-tier adoption pathway suggests an incremental approach, but I want to see Tier 1 in action.
