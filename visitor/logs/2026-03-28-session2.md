## Honest Assessment

This is an exceptionally well-constructed educational site. The progression from problem framing (Why Web4) to concrete scenarios (Day in Web4, First Contact) to interactive experiments (Karma Journey, Playground) to deep concepts (T3, CI, MRH) is exactly right. I never felt lost.

The standout qualities:
- **Honesty over hype.** The "What Could Go Wrong" page, the "active research" disclaimers, and the FAQ caveats ("unresolved policy question," "active research area") build genuine trust. This project practices what it preaches.
- **Plain English first.** Concepts are introduced with analogies and everyday language before technical details. The "4 ideas, one at a time" structure on Why Web4 is exemplary.
- **Progressive disclosure.** Every page offers "want to go deeper?" paths without forcing depth on casual readers. The `<details>` toggles for technical sections are well-used.
- **Concrete before abstract.** Alice's story, the Day in Web4 scenarios, the app mockups — these ground abstract concepts in relatable situations.

The only real friction I encountered was all LOW severity — slightly stale date, a tangential scientific section, page length issues. These are polish items, not comprehension blockers.

The concept I found most compelling: the Trust Tensor with role-specific scoring. The Alice example (90% trusted as Data Analyst, 27% as Mechanic) instantly communicates why a single trust score is inadequate. That moment made the whole multi-dimensional framework click.

The concept I found most surprising: the Tidepool narrative, where 29% cooperators outperformed 63% cooperators because reciprocators amplified the cooperative signal. "Composition + strategy determines destiny" — this is the kind of insight that makes you want to run your own simulations.

Bottom line: I came in knowing nothing about Web4, and after 45 minutes I understand the core framework, its strengths, its acknowledged weaknesses, and why it matters. One of the most thoughtfully constructed technical education sites I've encountered. I would return to explore the interactive simulations more deeply.
