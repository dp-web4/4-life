## Journey

### Landing Page (0:00)
- First impression: Clean, professional. The opening line "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" immediately hooks me — I know this problem. Spam, fake reviews, bots. Yes.
- The four-rule summary (identity anchored in devices, actions cost energy, consequences are permanent, AI and humans same rules) is clear and compelling. I get the pitch in about 30 seconds.
- The "What is this, really?" section is excellent — it immediately addresses the question I had: "Is this a crypto thing?" No. It's research. ~47,000 lines of tested code but no deployed product. Honest and refreshing.
- The adoption tiers section is smart — shows this isn't all-or-nothing.
- The "What's New" section at the bottom tells me this is actively maintained.
- Confusion: The page is *long*. By the time I reach the bottom, I've already forgotten some of what was at the top. There are also many call-to-action links competing for my attention: "Start Here," "Explore Topics," "Just 2 minutes?," "See what it looks like," "Follow a learning path." I count at least 6 different entry points. Which one do I actually click?
- Clicked: "Start Here" in the nav (takes me to First Contact)

### First Contact (3:00)
- This is framed as a 7-minute interactive experience following "Alice."
- The four concepts it promises to teach are clear: ATP (energy), Trust (reputation), Consequences (permanent record), Authenticity (behavior proves identity).
- Alice's preview is nice — she starts with 100 ATP and 0.50 trust. Simple starting state.
- But wait — "Watch Alice's Full Journey" is a link, not inline content. I expected to *see* Alice's journey here, not be redirected somewhere else.
- The "Next → AI Guide Understand" link at the bottom confused me. What does "AI Guide Understand" mean? Is that a page name? It's not clear where this navigation goes.
- Confusion: The page feels like an introduction to an introduction. It tells me *what I'll learn* but doesn't actually teach me much yet. For a page called "First Contact," I expected more contact with the actual concepts.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (5:30)
- This is the page I should have started with. Clear, concise, no jargon overload.
- The four numbered points are excellent. "Actions cost energy" — I get it. "Identity is anchored in your devices" — hardware security chips, makes sense. "Trust is earned, never bought" — intuitive. "Humans and AI play by the same rules" — interesting.
- "The Result" paragraph is the clearest summary on the entire site: "Spam dies because it's too expensive."
- The "What exists today" / "What comes next" breakdown is honest and helpful. I now know where this project stands.
- The "one-sentence version" at the bottom is perfect.
- No confusion here. This page does exactly what it promises.

### Why Web4? (8:00)
- Summarizes the four internet failures well: spam economics, reputation silos, identity disposability, AI deception.
- Introduces the solution framework with the acronyms: ATP, LCT, T3, MRH.
- This is where acronym overload starts. Four new abbreviations in quick succession. I can hold "ATP = energy budget" but "MRH = Markov Relevancy Horizon" is a mouthful. The nav calls it "Trust Neighborhood (MRH)" which is much clearer — I wish the page itself used the plain name more prominently.
- The five-tier adoption pathway makes sense conceptually.
- The page mentions "84 threat tracks" and "47,000 lines of tested reference code" — impressive but I have no way to evaluate those claims.

### How It Works (12:00)
- This page is enormous. It tries to explain everything: all five systems (LCT, ATP, T3, CI, MRH), the full lifecycle (birth → life → death → rebirth), groups/synthons, trust invariants, governance (SAL), and multi-agent coordination.
- The "Five Core Systems" section is a good overview but each system is explained briefly and then the page moves on. I feel like I'm speed-reading a textbook.
- The lifecycle section is well-structured. The cost/reward examples are concrete: "High-quality post: Cost 15 → Earn 40 = +25 net." That clicks.
- The "Death Spiral Example" is viscerally clear. I understand how bad actors get eliminated.
- The SAL governance section introduces *yet more* acronyms (SAL, VCM) and concepts (law oracles, emergency bypass). By this point I'm drowning.
- The "Four Trust Invariants" (boundedness, conservation, transitivity, locality) are interesting but feel like they belong on a technical reference page, not an overview.
- The "When Agents Work Together" section about trust decay through chains is clear. "A 5-hop pipeline where each agent has 0.9 trust = 0.59 end-to-end." Good math, intuitive result.
- Confusion: This page is trying to be both an overview *and* a deep dive. It does neither perfectly. As an overview, it's too long and detailed. As a deep dive, it's not detailed enough on any single topic. I'd prefer a shorter overview linking to dedicated deep-dive pages.

### Karma Journey (18:00)
- Interactive game! This is where things get tangible.
- Starting state: Trust 0.500, Energy 100, Consistency 0.85. Clear.
- The explanation of CI and effective trust is dense: "Effective trust equals raw trust multiplied by CI² — this determines karma tier upon death." I have to re-read this twice.
- The note about newcomers paying 1.4x because they have no consistency history makes sense as a mechanic but feels unintuitive. Why am I penalized for being new? The explanation ("new entities have limited history") helps but the 40% tax feels steep.
- Wait — the page says "Action costs: 1.4x (new entities pay 40% more because they have no consistency history yet — e.g. a 10 ATP action costs 14. Make steady choices to bring costs down to 1.0x)". This is clear now. Good parenthetical.
- The action choices are well-labeled: cooperative, selfish, bold, neutral. Energy costs are shown upfront. I appreciate being able to see the tradeoffs before choosing.
- The "Try this" prompt — "make a few cooperative choices, then switch to selfish ones" — is good pedagogical design.
- Minor confusion: The status text explaining "You're not dead! Effective trust (0.361) is below 0.5, but only raw trust determines survival" feels like it's addressing a confusion I haven't had yet. It's preemptively explaining a distinction I wasn't confused about, which paradoxically makes it more confusing.

## Unanswered Questions

1. How does VCM (Value Confirmation Mechanism) actually work in practice? The site says "recipients who benefit confirm quality" but who are these recipients? Is confirmation active (they click something) or passive (measured by behavior)?
2. How would federation actually work day-to-day? If I'm in two communities with different rules, what does that feel like? The MRH page explains the math but I can't picture the UX.
3. What's the minimum hardware requirement? The site mentions TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2 — but what about older devices? What about cheap Android phones that might not have a Secure Enclave equivalent?
4. If my trust in one role is high but I switch careers, do I really start from scratch in the new role? How long does it take to build trust as a newcomer in a role where you're actually experienced?
5. Who runs the "witness infrastructure" in practice? The LCT page says "anyone can" but who would actually bother? What's the incentive?

## Honest Assessment

This is the most thoughtfully designed explainer site I've encountered for a complex technical concept. The landing page hooks you with a real problem (spam, reputation silos), the TL;DR page delivers on its promise, and the "What Could Go Wrong" page is refreshingly honest. The biological metaphors (energy budgets, death, rebirth) make abstract concepts intuitive.

The biggest issue is information architecture. The site has *too much* content for a first visit, and doesn't aggressively enough guide newcomers through a clear path. The How It Works page in particular tries to be a comprehensive reference and an overview simultaneously, and ends up being overwhelming. I'd rather see a short, punchy overview with clear "go deeper" links.

The acronym density is a secondary issue. By the time I've processed LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, and CI, I'm mentally full. Adding V3, SAL, VCM, and EP on top feels like drinking from a fire hose. The site does a good job of providing plain-English alternatives (e.g., "Trust Neighborhood" for MRH) but doesn't use them consistently enough.

The interactive elements (Karma Journey, Society Simulator, ATP simulator, Trust Tensor simulator, Aliveness sliders/survival challenge) are the site's strongest feature. Every concept that has an interactive component is significantly easier to understand than the ones explained only with text. More interactivity would help.

Would I return? Yes — there's clearly more depth here than I could absorb in one sitting, and the honest tone makes me trust that exploring further is worth my time. Would I recommend it? Maybe — only to someone with patience for dense material and genuine curiosity about internet infrastructure. The average person would bounce after the landing page. But the people who stay will find something genuinely thought-provoking.

The "What Could Go Wrong" page alone is worth the visit. A project that publishes its own existential risks, rates its biggest threat as "High likelihood," and has a section called "What's Genuinely Unsolved" has earned more of my trust than any marketing page ever could.
