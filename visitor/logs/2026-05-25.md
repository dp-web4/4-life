## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: The headline **"The Internet Has a Trust Problem"** landed well. It names a feeling I already have, before asking me to learn anything. I appreciated the immediate disclaimer that this is *not* "Web3 + 1" with "no blockchain or tokens" — that pre-empted my first cynical assumption.
- **Clicked**: The page nudged me toward **"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →"**, so I took the recommended path. Good — I didn't have to guess where to begin.
- **Confusion points**: A row of five abbreviations (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI) sits near the bottom under five plain words (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency). On a first glance these read like a secret code I haven't been let in on yet. I didn't know I'd be drowning in these acronyms for the next half hour.

### TL;DR — "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (03:00)
- This is structured cleanly: Problem → Idea → Result → "Where is this now?". The problem ("a spammer can create a new account in seconds") is instantly relatable.
- **Confusion points**: For a "2-minute" page, it introduces a *lot* of named machinery fast — ATP (with its full name "Allocation Transfer Packets"), LCT, then "three additional concepts" T3, MRH, CI dropped in a single breath. I understood the *shape* of the idea (make actions cost something, anchor identity to hardware, make reputation portable) but the acronyms washed over me. I'd have preferred the TL;DR stay acronym-free and save the letters for the deep dives.
- **What I liked**: "Where is this now?" honestly says this is *research with working simulations, not a deployed product.* That honesty made me trust the rest of the site more.

### Why Web4 (06:00)
- The strongest persuasion page. Five named frustrations — spam, reputation trapped in silos, disposable banned accounts, AI deception, "you're a guest in someone else's house, always." I recognized every one of these from real life.
- **What I liked**: It openly lists its own weaknesses — "research prototype," "untested at scale," hardware creates an equity barrier, permanent reputation conflicts with privacy. A site arguing *against itself* is unusual and made me lean in.
- **Confusion points**: Four mechanisms stacked together (ATP + LCT + T3 + MRH) is a heavy cognitive load. I could follow each one alone, but how they *interlock* stayed hazy. A late-arriving fifth thing, "SAL (Society-Authority-Law)," appeared barely defined — I had no idea who actually enforces the rules.

### ATP Economics (11:00)
- **Best page of the session.** The biology analogy clicked immediately: cells burn ATP, get ADP back, recharge from food. The digital version maps cleanly onto that.
- **Interactive**: There's a live ATP simulator with four action types (spam → high-value creation). Being able to *press a button and watch my balance move* taught me more in 20 seconds than three paragraphs would.
- **Confusion points**: ADP itself ("a receipt recording what you did") still feels like a spare part — I'm not sure why I should care about the receipt. And "quality matters about 7×" is a precise-sounding number with no visible reasoning behind it; it reads as a dial someone set, not a law.

### LCT Explainer (16:00)
- The opening analogy is excellent: *"your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer."* The Web2/Web3/Web4 progression (what you know → what you have → what witnesses verify) is a clean mental model.
- **Confusion points / unanswered**: My single most obvious question — *what stops a rich person from buying 50 phones and making 50 identities?* — is never answered. The page gestures at "diminishing returns" and "information-theoretic bounds" but never closes the loop. This is the first objection any skeptic raises, and it's left open.
- The word "witness" gets used two different ways (devices witnessing each other vs. infrastructure nodes witnessing the network) and I had to slow down to keep them apart. Trust "ceilings" (a single chip caps at 0.90) felt arbitrary.

### Trust Tensor (21:00)
- Three dimensions — Talent, Training, Temperament — each tied to a plain question ("Can they solve problems in this role?" etc.). The surgeon example (brilliant-but-unreliable vs. steady-but-less-gifted) made the *why* obvious.
- **What I liked**: The page disarms its own scary word: "You don't need the math." Calling it "a 3-axis score" instead of a "tensor" was the right call. There's also a role-picking simulator.
- **Confusion points**: "V3" gets referenced before it's explained (forward reference). "Recipient attestation" is jargon. And nobody tells me what a 0.85 *means* — should I trust that person with my life or my lunch order? The decay half-lives (Talent 365 days, Training 180, Temperament 30) appear without justification.

### Aliveness (26:00)
- This is where it got genuinely interesting *and* slightly unsettling. An entity is "alive" only when ATP > 0 **and** Trust > 0.5 **and** Coherence is coherent. Two kinds of death: Energy Death (recoverable, "like going bankrupt") and Trust Death (permanent, "like losing a professional license — there is no rebirth for that identity").
- **What I liked**: The bankruptcy / license-revocation analogies are vivid and the dormancy states (Rest, Sleep, Torpor, Hibernation) are charming.
- **Confusion points**: "Permanent trust death with no rebirth" is a heavy claim, and the page itself admits the 0.5 threshold is "calibrated, not derived" and the appeals process is "untested with real humans." So the most consequential rule in the system is an admitted guess. I respect the honesty, but it left me uneasy about handing this real power.

### Karma Journey (29:00)
- A genuinely fun interactive game — live multiple lives, make choices, watch karma carry into the next life. Low barrier: stats are right there and you start clicking immediately.
- **Confusion points**: Before you play, it front-loads six interlocking terms (raw trust, effective trust, CI, karma tier, ATP, consistency). I wanted to *play first, learn second* — the game itself is the teacher, so the wall of definitions ahead of it works against its own strength.

### Glossary (31:00) — found last, should have been found first
- This page is **excellent** and I'm a little annoyed I didn't find it until the end. It has an "Essential only" filter (7 core terms), plain-language-first definitions, and Ctrl+K search. "ATP: Think of it like an energy budget. You spend it to act, earn it by creating value." That one sentence is clearer than the page named after it.
- It finally defined **CI** for me ("behavioral consistency across time, space, capability, and relationships") — a term I'd seen on five pages and never had explained inline.

## Unanswered Questions
1. What actually stops someone from buying many devices to mint many identities? (The first thing any skeptic asks.)
2. What does a trust score of 0.85 *mean* in practice — trust them with what, exactly?
3. Who enforces the rules? SAL was named but I never saw enforcement happen.
4. If "trust death" is permanent and the 0.5 threshold is an admitted guess, what happens to people wrongly pushed below it? (Appeals are "untested.")
5. How do the four mechanisms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) actually interlock in one user's day? Each was explained alone; the assembly wasn't shown.
6. Why should I, a normal user, care about the ADP "receipt"?

## Honest Assessment
This site is markedly more polished and more *honest* than most explainer sites I've seen — it argues against itself, admits its parameters are guesses, and repeatedly says "this is research, not a product." That candor is its biggest asset and bought it a lot of my goodwill.

Its second-biggest asset is the interactivity: the ATP simulator, the trust-tensor role picker, and especially the Karma Journey game taught me more, faster, than prose. When this site lets me *do* something, it's genuinely good.

Its main weakness is **acronym density and timing**. I was hit with LCT/ATP/T3/MRH/CI in the first two minutes, before any of them meant anything, and CI in particular followed me across five pages without ever being defined where I was looking. The glossary fixes almost all of this — but it's the last door I opened, not the first. The fix is mostly about *surfacing and inline-linking*, not writing new content: the good explanations already exist; a confused reader just can't reach them at the moment of confusion.

I left understanding *what* Web4 proposes and *why the problems are real* — but not *why I should believe this particular cure works*. The site, to its credit, doesn't pretend otherwise. I'd come back to play the simulators again. I'd recommend it to a curious technical friend, but I'd warn them: open the glossary in a second tab first.
