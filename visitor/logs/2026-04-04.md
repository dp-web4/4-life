## Unanswered Questions
1. **What does the UI actually look like?** The Day in Web4 mockups are the closest thing to a product vision, but they're static. A clickable prototype — even a fake one — would make the vision dramatically more tangible. This feels like the single biggest gap.
2. **Who witnesses the first witnesses?** The bootstrap problem for the witness network is acknowledged but solutions remain vague. "Partner with universities" isn't a deployment plan.
3. **How does cross-society trust transfer work in practice for overlapping roles?** If I'm a trusted programmer in Society A and join Society B which also values programming, is my trust discounted by MRH distance alone, or is there domain-matching that preserves more of it?
4. **What happens to synthons when key members leave?** The page mentions "decay precursors" but doesn't explain whether a synthon can survive losing its most-trusted member.

## Honest Assessment

This is an exceptionally well-built educational site. The pedagogical progression from TL;DR → Why Web4 → First Contact → interactive simulations is nearly flawless. Every time I had a question, the answer was usually one click away.

What impresses me most:

1. **Problem-first framing**: Every concept starts with a problem I already recognize (spam, fake reviews, platform lock-in) before introducing the solution. This prevents the "solution looking for a problem" feeling that plagues most protocol documentation.

2. **Honest self-criticism**: The "What Could Go Wrong" page and the scattered "this is research, not production" caveats build genuine trust. The project doesn't oversell.

3. **Progressive disclosure**: Collapsible FAQs, tiered learning paths, and the TL;DR → deep dive structure let me go as deep as I want without forcing depth I'm not ready for.

4. **Concrete numbers everywhere**: Starting ATP of 100, trust threshold of 0.5, MRH decay of 0.7 per hop — these make abstract concepts graspable.

5. **The simulations**: Society Simulator and Karma Journey don't just explain — they demonstrate. Watching a defector get isolated by cooperators teaches more than any paragraph could.

The only significant gap is the **product vision**. I understand *how* Web4 works, I understand *why* it matters, but I don't have a clear picture of what it would *feel like* to use it day-to-day beyond the static mockups on Day in Web4. A clickable prototype — even a lo-fi one — would transform comprehension into desire.

The site has clearly been through many iterations of visitor feedback refinement. The acronym handling (plain English first, shorthand second) and the inline definitions show someone has been systematically addressing exactly the friction I might have experienced in earlier versions. The result is one of the most thoughtful technical education sites I've encountered.

Would I use Web4 if it existed? Based on this browse: yes, enthusiastically. The spam-dies-naturally mechanic alone would be worth the switch. The portable reputation across platforms solves a problem I didn't even know had a name until today.
