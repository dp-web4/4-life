## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" — strong hook. I immediately relate to the problem: spam, fake accounts, AI deception. These are things I deal with.
- The four pillars (hardware-bound identity, metabolic attention, permanent consequences, human-AI coexistence) are introduced but feel abstract. I can sense a framework here but not grasp it yet.

### Why Web4 Page (02:30)
- This page is excellent. The problem breakdown (5 issues) resonated with me. I especially liked "Creating accounts is free. Sending messages is free" — that's the root insight.
- "Why Previous Solutions Failed" table was very helpful. Finally someone acknowledging that CAPTCHAs and moderation don't actually solve the problem.
- The "What Would Actually Work" section clearly defines requirements before jumping to solutions. Good pedagogy.
- The four mechanisms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) are introduced with one-line summaries. Acronym overload starts here — four new terms in rapid succession.
- The FAQ section is the highlight. The honest caveats ("bootstrapping is genuinely hard; unsolved research problem", "multi-year effort") build real credibility. Most tech projects oversell. This one undersells, which paradoxically made me trust it more.
- Confusion points: The acronyms are dense. ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, ADP — by the time I reach the fourth one I can't remember what the first one stands for.
- Clicked: "Start First Contact" button

## Unanswered Questions

1. **How does Web4 relate to Web3?** Is it a replacement? An evolution? A parallel track? The landing page says "trust-native internet" but never positions it against blockchain/crypto, which is the obvious comparison visitors will make.
2. **Who is actually building this?** I see a whitepaper and GitHub, but is there an organization? A research lab? A company? The "About" context is invisible.
3. **What does a real-world deployment look like?** The FAQ admits it's theoretical. But even a hypothetical walkthrough ("Imagine opening your laptop and...") would help me picture adoption.
4. **Why should I care personally?** The spam problem is real but I live with it. What's my personal incentive to switch to a system where my identity is hardware-bound and my actions have permanent consequences? That sounds like surveillance, and the site doesn't address the "this sounds dystopian" reaction.
5. **What happens to privacy?** If everything is permanent and traceable, how is my privacy protected? MRH is supposed to handle this, but it's the least intuitive concept on the site.
6. **Can I try this with real accounts?** Is there a testnet? A demo environment? The simulations are educational but toy-like. I want to feel what it's like to actually use Web4, not just watch dots move.
7. **What's the difference between the educational 5D trust model and the production 3D model?** The Trust Tensor page introduces both without resolving the discrepancy.
8. **Several linked pages don't exist** — `/getting-started`, `/learning-journey`, `/identity-constellations`, `/karma`. Is the site still being built?

## Honest Assessment

The 4-Life site has dramatically improved since what I imagine were its early days. The "Why Web4" page is genuinely excellent — the problem framing, the honest FAQ with caveats, and the clear structure made me feel like I was engaging with serious thinkers, not hype merchants.

**What works well:**
- Problem-first framing throughout (especially Why Web4 and the landing page)
- The biological metabolism analogy for ATP is brilliant and memorable
- The FAQ sections with honest caveats build genuine trust
- The Trust Tensor Candidate A vs B example is the single best teaching moment on the site
- Progressive depth — each page adds more without being redundant
- The lifecycle framing (birth → life → death → rebirth) makes abstract concepts narratively coherent
- Multiple interactive tools (Playground, Society Simulator, Lab Console) for different learning styles

**What doesn't work:**
- Acronym density is the #1 friction point. By the time I've read 3 pages, I'm drowning in ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI, ADP, EP, V3, R6, D9. There's no in-page help for remembering what these mean.
- Broken links (4 pages return 404) undermine confidence. If the site can't maintain its own links, how can it propose to maintain a global trust infrastructure?
- The "why should I personally care" question is never directly answered. The site explains WHY Web4 is technically sound but not WHY I would choose a world of permanent consequences over my current world of disposable accounts.
- MRH remains the weakest explanation. It's architecturally important but conceptually the hardest to grasp. It needs a completely different approach — maybe a visual/interactive demonstration rather than text.
- The First Contact page promises a 10-minute interactive experience but the entry point ("Start Simulation") gives no preview. I don't know what I'm committing to.
- The Trust Tensor page undermining its own model by mentioning a different production model is confusing and unnecessary for first-time visitors.

**Overall:** I left with a solid understanding of the individual components (ATP, Trust, Identity, Aliveness) but a weaker understanding of how they compose into something I'd actually want to use. The "intellectual appeal" is strong — these are clever ideas. The "emotional appeal" is missing — nobody has told me a story about why my life gets better in a Web4 world. The closest the site gets is "spam dies naturally," which is compelling but narrow. I want to hear about what becomes *possible* in a world of verified trust, not just what becomes *impossible* for bad actors.

Would I return? Maybe — if I had a specific research interest in trust systems or decentralized identity. Would I recommend it? Maybe — to friends interested in the future of the internet, but with the caveat "it's dense and still being built."
