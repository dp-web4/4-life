## Unanswered Questions
1. **What does the actual UI/UX look like beyond mockups?** The Day in Web4 wireframes show conceptual apps, but has anyone built even a prototype browser extension (Tier 1)? Is there a demo video or clickable prototype?
2. **How does Web4 handle legacy content?** If I join a Web4 community, what happens to my existing content/reputation on traditional platforms? Is there an import mechanism, or does everyone truly start at 0.5?
3. **What happens to children/minors?** The site mentions hardware-bound identity and permanent records. How does this work for children who can't own devices or consent to permanent behavioral records? Is there a concept of "juvenile records" that can be sealed?
4. **How do communities handle context collapse?** If I'm trusted as a coder (T3=0.9) and post memes in the same community, does my meme-posting degrade my coding trust, or are roles truly isolated within a single community?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoroughly constructed educational sites I've visited on a technical topic. The progression from "what's the problem" → "here's the idea" → "what would it feel like" → "try it yourself" → "what could go wrong" is pedagogically excellent.

**What worked best:**
- The Day in Web4 mockups. That page single-handedly converted me from "interesting idea" to "I want this to exist." Seeing an inbox with zero spam and trust scores next to names — that's a powerful vision.
- The honesty throughout. "Nobody Adopts It" rated Existential/High as the #1 risk. "Today, none of these exist." "Research takes as long as it takes." This builds genuine credibility.
- The progressive disclosure. I never felt overwhelmed because complex concepts were introduced after simpler ones, and technical details were behind collapsible sections.
- Alice's First Contact story. Following one character through a full lifecycle made abstract economics tangible.

**What could improve:**
- The Learning Journey progress tracker doesn't seem to count my organic exploration. After visiting 15 pages, seeing "0/16 completed (0%)" felt discouraging.
- Some pages (Aliveness, Federation Economics) get very technical very fast after starting accessible. A clearer "beginner" vs "advanced" delineation within pages would help.
- I still don't have a clear mental image of what using Web4 would literally look like on my phone. The Day in Web4 mockups are conceptual text descriptions — even simple wireframe sketches or annotated screenshots would be transformative.

**Overall:** The site successfully taught me a complete trust framework in under an hour. The concepts are well-motivated (problem before solution), well-illustrated (Alice, mockups, scenarios), and honestly assessed (failure modes published). I'd return to try the interactive simulations more deeply, and I'd recommend it to anyone interested in internet trust problems. The writing quality is consistently high, the navigation is intuitive, and the intellectual honesty is rare and refreshing.
