## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: The headline "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" is a great hook. I instantly recognize the spam/reputation-silos framing — this is a problem I have. The clarification "this isn't blockchain or tokens" was reassuring; I half-expected another crypto pitch.
- **Clicked**: TL;DR, because they recommended it for first-time visitors and I appreciated being directed.
- **Confusion points**: There are a LOT of entry points — "Start Here" has 4 items, "Core Concepts" has 4 items, "Interactive Labs" has 4 items, "Deep Dives" more. As a curious visitor I want to be told *one* thing to do first. The recommendation is there, but the visual menu feels like a buffet.

### /tldr (02:30)
- **First impression**: The "internet has no memory" framing landed well. The three-mechanic structure (energy costs / hardware identity / portable reputation) is concrete enough I could nod along.
- **Confusion**: The page promises "plain English first" but introduces 6 acronyms (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI) inside a 2-minute read. The acronyms are spelled out, but I now have to remember "Allocation Transfer Packets" — which isn't really plain English, it's just a different layer of jargon. Why borrow ATP from biology if I have to learn a backronym anyway?
- **What stuck**: "Spam dies because it's too expensive."

### /why-web4 (05:00)
- **What worked**: The Web1 (read) → Web2 (read/write) → Web3 (read/write/own) → Web4 (read/write/trust) lineage was the single most useful framing I encountered. It anchored the whole thing.
- **What didn't**: The page admits there's no company, no token sale, no funding round — but also describes "100+ page whitepaper" and "47,000 lines of tested code." The page literally says "you're right to notice the tension." That's honest but it doesn't *resolve* the tension. Who built it? A solo researcher? A corporate skunkworks? An anonymous collective? For a project whose entire pitch is trust, "trust us, we won't tell you who we are" is a weird ask.
- **Red flag I noticed**: "424 attack vectors across 84 tracks, all defended" — this number sounds impressive but I have no way to verify it, no link to a paper, no way to assess what "defended" means. It pattern-matches to confidence-theater stats.

### /first-contact (08:00)
- **First impression**: An interactive walkthrough following "Alice" through 5 acts sounds like exactly the right onboarding format. Drive the choices, see the metrics change.
- **Limitation of my browse**: I'm a fetch-based visitor, so I can't actually drive the playback. A real first-time human user might invest the time, or might bounce because they want a fast scan, not a 5-act narrative.
- **What unsettled me**: The phrase "Karma carries forward" appears here without yet introducing what Karma is. And the framing "your record is permanent because your presence is hardware-bound" is presented as a feature, not a tradeoff. As a regular human, "permanent record on the internet" is something I've spent my life *avoiding*.

### /lct-explainer (10:30)
- **What worked**: The driver's license analogy ("imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied") is genuinely good. The "notarized document vs. handwritten note" framing helps too.
- **What didn't**: Within a few paragraphs I'm tripping over TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2, "hardware attestation," "tamper-resistant security chips." There's a collapsible glossary section, but it appears *after* these terms have already been used. As a non-cryptographer, I either click away to learn the jargon or I skim past and miss the meaning.
- **Practical worry I had**: Does my phone have a security chip? My iPhone does, sure. Does the cheap Android my parent uses? What about my Windows laptop? If LCT requires modern hardware, this excludes a huge swath of users, and the page doesn't address it here (it's covered later on /what-could-go-wrong, but most readers won't get there).
- **Single-device fear**: "Your devices witness each other" — what if I only have one device? What if I lose my phone? This is the failure mode of every hardware-key system today, and the page doesn't reassure me here.

### /atp-economics (13:30)
- **The metaphor**: Borrowed from biology — your cells spend ATP to do work, get ADP, recharge. Cute. But it's doing a lot of design work. Real ATP/ADP cycles work because of physics. Why should internet "energy" follow the same rules? The metaphor conveys an aesthetic, but I'm not sure it justifies the design.
- **The line that froze me**: "Run out of attention budget? You die." Excuse me? *Die?* I had to click through to /aliveness to understand this isn't literal account deletion. But the WORD is dramatic and would scare a normal user. Why use "die" when "suspended" works fine?
- **What I appreciated**: "You can't buy or sell ATP" — explicit "it's a usage budget, not a tradeable asset." Good. That's the right call for distinguishing this from tokens.
- **What I didn't get**: Recharge rate. The page says "hours, not seconds" or "days." That's a 100x range. As a casual user who logs in twice a week, do I have ATP? Do I starve from inactivity? If activity is required to maintain ATP, that itself becomes a chore.

### /aliveness (16:00)
- **What clarified**: "Death" for humans = temporary loss of participation rights, like a "suspended license." Reputation persists, you can earn your way back. Okay, that's fine. But the framing is doing damage on the prior page; by the time I get here I'm already alarmed.
- **The 0.5 threshold**: Above 0.5 trust = recoverable; below 0.5 = "permanent death — society has rejected you." Why 0.5? It sounds suspiciously round. Is this calibrated against any real-world data, or is it just the obvious midpoint of [0,1]? An arbitrary threshold becomes a real cliff edge for real users.
- **The social-credit echo**: "Society has rejected you" being a permanent-death condition is exactly what people fear when they hear "behavioral scoring." The /why-web4 page apparently engages with the social-credit critique; it didn't reach me here. If this is the page where rejection is described, this is the page where the differentiation needs to live.

### /trust-tensor (18:00)
- **The math reassurance worked**: "You don't need the math — what matters is that one number couldn't tell a brilliant-but-erratic surgeon from a kind doctor with shaky hands." Good. That's the right level.
- **What confused me conceptually**: The three dimensions are Talent, Training, Temperament. *Talent as part of trust?* Trust is whether I should rely on you. A naturally gifted liar is dangerous, not trustworthy. A naturally gifted but absent worker is unreliable, not trustworthy. Conflating innate aptitude with reliability feels like a category error.
- **More acronyms**: I learned T3 here, then learned that V3 (Valuation/Veracity/Validity) is a separate set of three. So the trust system is T3 *and* V3, which makes six dimensions, not three. I think.

### /karma-journey (20:00)
- **What worked**: Live multiple lives, watch decisions affect future starting conditions. As a game mechanic this sounds great.
- **What didn't**: "The word [karma] here is a handle, not a moral score." That disclaimer reveals the design choice has a problem. If you have to disclaim that your terminology isn't what people will think it is, maybe the terminology is wrong. "Karma" comes loaded with religious meaning across multiple traditions; calling it a "handle" doesn't undo the freight.
- **The math choice**: Effective Trust = raw × CI². Squaring the consistency penalty means inconsistency hurts a *lot*. CI of 0.5 → effective trust drops to 25% of raw. People are erratic. Real life is messy. A squared penalty rewards rigidity. Why squared instead of linear? This is a design choice that bakes a value judgment ("consistency is twice as important as we'd otherwise rate it") into the math.

### /society-simulator (21:30)
- **What works on paper**: 12 agents, watch trust dynamics, run automatically or play as agent. "Cooperators cluster together. Free riders get caught." This is the demo that could actually make Web4 click.
- **Limitation of fetch-browsing**: I can't actually run the simulation. A determined visitor might; a casual one might wait for a static screenshot or video.

### /what-could-go-wrong (22:30)
- **My favorite page.** It admits hardware gatekeeping is real and mitigations are "specified but not yet deployed." It admits disabled users get "a structurally lower ceiling" and that this is "an unsolved problem, not a solved one." It admits sufficiently powerful state actors could correlate identities. It admits the GDPR conflict ("a tamper-evident audit chain that is supposed to be permanent does not erase cleanly") is "an unresolved policy question." It calls adoption failure "the #1 existential risk."
- **The intellectual-honesty signature**: "Honest assessment:" appears repeatedly. "The simulations prove the mechanics work; the engineering at scale is an open challenge." This is what builds trust in a research project.
- **The discoverability problem**: This page is the credibility anchor for the whole site. But it's listed under "Deep Dives" — most visitors who bounce after the TLDR will never see it. The polished marketing pages and the honest-skeptic page coexist on the same site, but only one is on the recommended path.

### /day-in-web4 (24:00)
- **The concrete tour**: 10 daily moments — phone waking up at 7:30 AM, messaging with energy costs, hiring/freelance, used-marketplace escrow, trust-weighted social feed, restaurant reviews. This is the "what would I actually experience" answer I wanted from the TLDR.

## Understanding Gained

- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — interactive simulator for trust dynamics in a Web4-style society

## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who's building this?** "No company, no token sale, no funding round" but also a 100-page whitepaper and 47k LOC — that has to come from *someone*. A project pitched on trust should name its team.
2. **What if I lose my only device?** Single-device failure mode is the obvious user worry; LCT page doesn't address it.
3. **Does my phone qualify?** Hardware requirements are vague. iPhone Secure Enclave seems fine. Cheap Android, old laptop, library computer? Class divide implied but not addressed at the entry pages.
4. **Why 0.5 as the permanent-death threshold?** Round number, no calibration story. What's the cliff effect on users who hover near 0.5?
5. **Why is consistency squared (CI²) instead of linear?** That's a design choice with real behavioral consequences (rewards rigidity); it deserves a sentence of justification.
6. **If I'm a casual user (log in twice a week), does my ATP stagnate?** Recharge rate of "hours" or "days" is vague.
7. **What does "society rejects you" mean operationally?** One community? Aggregate of communities? Can a hostile community's rejection follow me to a friendlier one? Portable reputation cuts both ways.
8. **How is "Karma" not appropriating Eastern religious concepts?** The disclaimer that "it's just a handle" doesn't undo the cultural freight.
9. **The "424 attack vectors" stat — where do I read about them?** No link, no methodology — feels like marketing not research.
10. **If the system runs on hardware attestation from vendors (Apple, Google, Yubico, etc.), aren't they the new gatekeepers?** /what-could-go-wrong admits this; the entry pages don't.

## Honest Assessment

The site has a genuinely strong intellectual foundation and one excellent honest-skeptic page (/what-could-go-wrong) that I respect. The Web1→Web4 lineage is the cleanest framing on the site. The driver's-license analogy for LCT and the doctor analogy for trust dimensions both work. The disclosure that "today, none of these exist — Web4 is research" is the right level of honesty.

But the entry-path experience has three big problems:

**(1) Acronym tax.** The TLDR pledges plain English and then drops six acronyms. Throughout the site, every page introduces a new term: ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI, SAL. By the time I reach trust tensor I'm carrying half a dictionary. The "Essential only" filter in the glossary is a smart band-aid, but inline tooltips would help more.

**(2) Word choice does damage.** "Death." "Permanent record." "Society has rejected you." These are emotionally loaded phrases. The /aliveness page softens "death" to "suspended license" — but the dramatic word choice on the prior page has already activated the user's fear response. And the /aliveness language about rejection lands close enough to social-credit critique that the "this isn't social credit" defense really needs to live on the same page.

**(3) Honest content is buried.** The most credibility-building content is on /what-could-go-wrong, which sits under "Deep Dives" — last in the navigation. Most visitors who bounce after the TLDR or first explainer page will never see the project's intellectual honesty. The polished marketing comes first; the self-criticism comes last. This is backwards for an audience that has been trained by years of crypto pitches to be suspicious. Surface the honesty earlier.

The deeper concern — and this is me reading between the lines — is that this site is fundamentally for *researchers and the technically curious*, not for the ordinary internet user it claims to serve. The interactive demos are excellent if you're willing to invest 10+ minutes; the conceptual content rewards careful reading. A casual visitor with 90 seconds is going to bounce after the acronyms hit. If the goal is to onboard regular humans, the entry layer needs to do less work: one analogy, one promise, one path forward, and zero acronyms above the fold.

I'd come back. I want to actually drive Alice through First Contact, and I want to run the Society Simulator. I'd recommend the site to a friend who already cares about identity and trust online — but not to a friend who just complained about spam. The latter is who the headline targets, and the latter is who would bounce first.
