## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoughtfully constructed explainer sites I've encountered. The progressive disclosure works — I went from "what is this?" to "I understand the core thesis and most of the mechanics" in 25 minutes across 15 pages.

**What works well:**
- Problem-first framing. Every page starts with a problem I recognize before introducing the solution.
- Honest caveats. The "What Could Go Wrong" page and the FAQ caveats on "Why Web4" are credibility builders. Most projects hide their weaknesses; this one leads with them.
- Multiple entry points. The landing page offers 2-minute, 5-minute, and deep-dive paths. I never felt forced into a particular sequence.
- Concrete examples. The Day in Web4 scenarios and ATP cost/reward tables make abstract concepts tangible.
- Interactive tools. The Society Simulator, Playground, and Trust Dilemmas give me something to DO, not just read.

**What needs work:**
- Acronym density. LCT, ATP, ADP, T3, CI, MRH, SAL, V3, DID, EP — that's 10+ acronyms for a first-time visitor. Each makes sense individually but collectively they create cognitive load. A persistent sidebar glossary or "hover for definition" tooltips would help.
- Device loss/transition. This is the most obvious real-world objection ("what if I lose my phone?") and it's barely addressed. It should be prominent on the Day in Web4 or FAQ pages.
- Adoption concreteness. The five-tier adoption model is the strongest answer to "how would this ever happen?" but it's buried on the Why Web4 page. It needs more prominence and a concrete example (e.g., "here's what Tier 1 looks like on an existing platform").
- Death language. "Death" and "rebirth" are evocative but potentially off-putting. The site uses biological metaphors throughout (ATP is literally named after cellular energy), which is coherent, but a newcomer might find "your agent dies" alarming before they understand the game-theory rationale.

**Overall:** I'd come back. The site respects my time, admits what it doesn't know, and gives me tools to explore rather than just text to read. The core thesis — make bad behavior expensive instead of trying to moderate it away — is compelling and clearly articulated. I left understanding significantly more than when I arrived, and I know where to go to learn the rest.
