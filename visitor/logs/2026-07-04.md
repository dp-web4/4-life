## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident, and unusually honest. The headline frames it as a "Trust-Native Internet Lab" and immediately says "a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor." I appreciated the "not a blockchain, not a product, not a company" disclaimers up front — that lowered my guard.
- The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" hook landed: "Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos. Bad actors get unlimited fresh starts." That's a problem I recognize.
- Clicked: "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (the page recommends it for first-timers, so I trusted that).
- Confusion points: A wall of acronyms right away — LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI. The page acknowledges it ("full definitions in the glossary"), which softens the blow, but it's still five unknowns in one breath.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (04:00)
- Solid. Problem → solution → status → next steps. The four pillars (energy costs, hardware-anchored identity, portable reputation, equal rules for humans and AI) were digestible.
- It defines ATP and LCT on first use ("a digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip"), which is exactly what I needed.
- Still-fuzzy: T3, MRH, CI are named but deferred elsewhere. And "how does the witness constellation actually verify identity?" was left hanging. But that's fair for a 2-minute page.
- Clicked: "First Contact" (recommended entry point).

### First Contact (08:00)

- **Confusion — NEW:** A green banner refers to "hestia and the hub (the green banner) are the _separate, real_ system that simulation models." I have NO idea what "hestia" or "the hub" are. They're dropped in as if I already know them. First encounter with two proper nouns that would haunt the rest of my visit.
- Clicked onward: ATP Economics.

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-made, honest site — noticeably more polished and more candid about its own limits than most "future of the internet" pitches. First Contact is the star: letting me *drive* Alice and watch the bars move taught me ATP and trust in a way no prose could. The Society Simulator's "you can start right here" reassurance and the LCT driver's-license analogy both felt like someone had already worried about confusing me and headed it off.

My one persistent stumble was **"hestia" and "the hub."** These two names show up on multiple pages, always as if I'm supposed to know them, and are only defined on a page I had to go looking for. Every time I hit them I felt a little dumb — which is exactly the feeling a first-time visitor shouldn't get. They're clearly the "real deployed" half of the story, so they matter, but they're introduced backwards: used before they're defined.

The secondary stumble was the word "deployed." On the Why-Web4 banner it reads like a promise of a live product, and the page then spends effort walking that back. The honesty is there — it's just structured so the reassuring/correct answer is one collapse-click below the confusing claim.

Fix the "hestia/hub" introductions (a hover-gloss would do it everywhere) and put the "no live network yet" caveat directly beside the word "deployed," and this goes from "good for a curious techie" to "good for a curious anyone."
