## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clear and confident. "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations." I liked that the very first parenthetical preempted my biggest fear: *"a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor."* That single line did a lot of work — I almost clicked away assuming "another crypto thing," and it stopped me.
- The problem statement landed instantly: "Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos. Bad actors get unlimited fresh starts." I've *lived* all three. I was hooked.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" (the recommended path — good that it's flagged for newcomers).

### TL;DR / Web4 in 2 Minutes (03:00)
- Four ideas: energy budget (ATP), device-anchored identity, portable reputation, same rules for humans + AI. The energy/reputation framing is intuitive.
- Friction: the acronyms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI) all show up here and get punted "elsewhere for real understanding." For a 2-minute page that's probably the right call, but it set up a pattern I'd hit on every page afterward (see Friction Log).

### Why Web4? (06:00)

"Isn't this social credit?" → decentralized/voluntary/community-defined vs. state control. That honesty *increased* my trust in the project.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust verified by behavior, built into the protocol, not bolted on; not blockchain/crypto.
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab that lets you *play* the trust/energy dynamics rather than just read about them.
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored identity (phone's security chip as an un-photocopyable driver's license); the hardware layer is real silicon, the network is still simulated.
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — a finite per-agent *energy* budget you spend on actions and earn back via confirmed-valuable work (ATP), with ADP the spent-energy receipt; explicitly not money.
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3 (Talent/Training/Temperament) for *who you are*, V3 (Valuation/Veracity/Validity) for *what you produce*.
- [~] How agents "live" and "die" — alive = ATP>0 AND T3>0.5 AND CI≥~0.5; death exists in (at least) two flavors — but the *finality* of death is where the site lost me (see Friction #1).
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam, trapped reputation, free do-overs for bad actors, can't tell human from AI. All four are real and felt.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Is "trust death" truly permanent, or just a long suspension?** Aliveness says an identity that drops below 0.5 trust "cannot return," but Karma Journey says you climb back when your karma tier improves. Which is it — and if it's permanent, what *action path* (if any) would a trust-dead identity even have to recover?
2. Who funds the "100 starter grant from society's pool," and what stops someone from spinning up identities to farm starter grants? (The hardware-binding answer is implied but never stated *at* the grant.)
3. The witness-network bootstrap — "who watches the first watcher?" — is acknowledged as a hard problem on Why Web4 but I never found where it's actually resolved.
4. Recipients "score V3" — is that an explicit rating they give, or something computed automatically from their confirmation? (Trust Tensor leaves this ambiguous.)

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely good explainer — markedly better than I expected from the homepage. The sequencing works (problem → story → mechanics), First Contact is the standout (driving Alice teaches what prose can't), and the project's *honesty* is its secret weapon: "no timeline," "still simulation, not a deployed network," "calibrated, not derived." Every time I braced for hype, the page disarmed me instead. That earned more trust than any feature claim could.

My single real stumble was about **death**. Three different pages describe what happens when an agent dies, and they don't agree on whether it's permanent. Individually each page is fine; read in sequence (which is exactly how the site invites you to read it — Aliveness and Karma Journey are both "core" stops), they contradict. It's the one spot where my mental model broke and I couldn't repair it from the site alone. Everything else was either clear or a "go-deeper" link I was happy to follow.

The recurring background hum is acronym density — every page assumes you've absorbed the others. It's never fatal (the analogies carry you), but it means there's no truly standalone page; you're always mid-web. For a *lab*, that's arguably fine. For a *first* page someone lands on cold, it's the thing most likely to make them bounce before the good stuff (First Contact) hooks them.

Net: I left understanding Web4 well enough to explain it to a friend — with one caveat I'd have to hand-wave ("…and if you behave really badly you're sort of permanently out, I think?"). Close that one gap and this goes from "good" to "solid."
