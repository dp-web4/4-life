## Honest Assessment

The site **improved dramatically** from my initial confusion. The landing page threw me into "Web4" jargon, but once I found the "Why Web4" page, everything clicked. That problem statement — spam wins, reputation is trapped, bad actors get fresh starts — is **compelling and relatable**. Everyone who's been on the internet has experienced these problems.

The progressive disclosure works reasonably well once you find the right path. ATP economics, LCT identity, Trust Tensors, aliveness — each concept builds on the last. The Playground and Society Simulator are engaging entry points that make abstract concepts tangible.

**Key friction**: The landing page assumes I already care about "Web4 Societies" when I don't even know what Web4 is. A newcomer needs the PROBLEM first, then the SOLUTION. Currently, the site leads with the solution.

**Broken links are a real issue**. Four 404 errors in the Research Hub undermine confidence. If I'm evaluating whether to invest time in understanding this system, dead links signal "unfinished" or "abandoned."

**The "why it matters" gap**: I understand WHAT Web4 is and HOW it works, but I'm still unclear on WHY this specific approach is better than alternatives. The site critiques moderation armies, CAPTCHAs, blockchain identity, and social login — but doesn't deeply compare Web4's trade-offs against these. What does Web4 sacrifice? What are its limitations?

**Positive surprise**: The Society Simulator is delightful. Watching agents cooperate, defect, and form coalitions makes game theory feel alive. This is the kind of "show don't tell" that works for onboarding.

**Overall**: I'd return to explore more of the 20 tools. I might recommend it to someone interested in trust/reputation systems, but I'd warn them to skip the landing page and go straight to "Why Web4" first.
