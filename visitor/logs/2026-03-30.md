## Unanswered Questions
1. **What would the actual UI look like as a user?** The "Day in Web4" mockups are great conceptual illustrations but they feel like wireframes. Would a real Web4 app look like a separate app? A browser extension? An OS-level service? The "Download the App" framing in Day in Web4 suggests a standalone app, but the Tier 1 (Wrapper) adoption path suggests browser extensions or platform integrations first. Which is it?
2. **How does quality consensus work in adversarial environments?** The V3 quality determination relies on recipients confirming value, but in communities where the majority holds incorrect beliefs (flat earth forums, etc.), wouldn't the majority's quality consensus just reinforce echo chambers? The FAQ mentions bridge agents and federation, but this feels like the deepest unresolved tension.
3. **What happens during the transition period?** If adoption starts with Tier 1 wrappers on existing platforms, you'd have two parallel trust systems (platform karma + Web4 trust) coexisting. How do conflicts between them get resolved? If Reddit gives me high karma but Web4 gives me low trust, which wins?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most carefully constructed educational sites I've encountered for a technical research project. The quality of explanation is consistently high across all 17 pages I visited.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- **Problem-first framing**: Every page starts with why something matters before explaining how it works. This is the right pedagogical approach and it's applied consistently.
- **Honesty about status**: "Research, not product" appears everywhere without being defensive. The GDPR question admits uncertainty. The adoption path acknowledges no SDK exists yet. This builds enormous credibility.
- **Progressive disclosure**: The TL;DR → Why Web4 → Concept Pages → Interactive Labs progression lets me go exactly as deep as I want. Nothing felt forced.
- **Day in Web4**: The standout page. If I had to recommend one page to a friend, it would be this one. The scenarios make abstract concepts tangible in under 3 minutes.
- **Interlocking concepts**: The "Why Spam Dies" section on How It Works is where everything clicks — five systems reinforcing each other. It's the payoff for all the earlier concept pages.

**What could improve:**
- The First Contact JavaScript message is confusing and should only appear when the interactive version fails to load.
- The Karma Journey's raw vs effective trust gap needs an on-screen explanation — it's the first time visitors encounter the CI² modulation in practice.
- Some deep-dive technical content (RDF/SPARQL, synthons) is mixed into pages that are otherwise accessible to non-technical readers. These should be behind collapsible sections.

**Overall**: The site does what it claims — it lets you understand Web4 without prior knowledge, at whatever depth you want. The interactive elements (Playground, Karma Journey, Society Simulator) transform abstract concepts into experiential learning. The consistent honesty about limitations ("this is research," "no timeline commitments," "unresolved questions") is its greatest strength. It doesn't feel like a pitch — it feels like an invitation to think.

I'd come back to try the Society Simulator with different scenarios and dig into the Playground experiments.
