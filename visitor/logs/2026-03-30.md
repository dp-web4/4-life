## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, professional, not overly hyped. The headline "A Trust-Native Internet Lab" immediately signals this is experimental/research, not a product pitch. That's refreshing.
- The "internet has a trust problem" framing is strong — spam, silo'd reputations, bad actors getting fresh starts. These are problems I actually experience.
- The quick-start options are excellent. Four clear paths with time estimates. I picked "TL;DR" because I only want to commit 2 minutes initially.
- The "What is this, really?" section is genuinely useful — it heads off my "is this crypto?" suspicion immediately. "Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain" is exactly what I needed to hear.
- The adoption tiers section on the landing page felt dense for a first visit. I skimmed it — the developer-facing details seem premature at this stage of my understanding.
- Confusion points: The term "LCT" appears in the four core rules section with minimal context. I can figure out it means "Linked Context Tokens" but the parenthetical feels like jargon dropping.

### TL;DR Page (03:00)
- This is excellent. Four numbered points, each with a clear mechanism and a plain-English explanation.
- "Actions cost energy" — I immediately get the anti-spam logic. If posting costs something, bulk spam is uneconomical. Simple and elegant.
- "Identity is anchored in your devices" — the security chip framing makes sense. I know my phone has Face ID hardware. Connecting that to online identity is intuitive.
- "Trust is earned, never bought" — clear, though "multi-dimensional" is vague at this point. I'd want to know what the dimensions are.
- The "Where is this now?" section is appropriately honest. "Active research with working simulations" — no inflated claims.
- The "What comes next" list with "No timeline commitments" is trust-building. This project isn't pretending to be further along than it is.
- I clicked "Why Web4?" next because the TL;DR earned my attention for a longer read.

### Why Web4 Page (06:00)
- The five-problem framing is well-structured. Each problem is one I recognize from daily internet use.
- "Why Previous Solutions Failed" is the strongest section. CAPTCHAs, moderation armies, Web3/blockchain — each criticism is specific and fair. The Web3 critique ("free wallet creation, no behavior history") is the sharpest: it identifies exactly why crypto identity doesn't solve the trust problem.
- The four core mechanisms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) are introduced with one-line explanations. That's the right level for this stage.
- The FAQ section is massive — 50 questions. This is both impressive and overwhelming. I read maybe 10 of the "Most Asked" ones. Key ones that helped: "Is this a social credit system?" (no — bottom-up vs top-down), "What if I lose my hardware?" (multi-device recovery), and "How big does a community need to be?" (5-10 people).
- Confusion: MRH (Markov Relevancy Horizon) is the one term that still feels opaque after this page. "Trust neighborhood" is clearer but the formal name is intimidating.
- The key design parameters section (trust threshold 0.50, 3-hop visibility, 5% transfer cost) is useful for grounding the abstract concepts in concrete numbers.

- The death/rebirth distinction is important: "Energy death is recoverable through karma rebirth. Trust death is permanent." That's a meaningful design choice and it's clearly communicated.

## Unanswered Questions
1. How does the 5% ATP transfer tax actually prevent circular farming in practice? The claim is made but not illustrated with a concrete example.
2. What does the "Coherence Index" scoring look like in practice for a normal user? The concept makes sense (consistency across time/space/capability/relationships) but I've never seen a concrete example of someone's CI score changing based on specific behaviors.
3. How would Web4 handle large-scale coordinated disinformation campaigns where each participant acts "normally" individually but the collective effect is harmful? The collusion detection focuses on trust manipulation, not coordinated authentic-seeming behavior.
4. What's the actual user experience of "witness recovery" when you lose a device? The concept is explained but the step-by-step feels hand-wavy compared to the very concrete Day in Web4 wireframes.
5. How does the system handle communities with genuinely different quality standards? A shitposting community and an academic journal have different definitions of "quality" — does trust transfer between them meaningfully?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoughtfully constructed explainer sites I've encountered. The progressive disclosure works — I started with a 2-minute TL;DR and ended up spending 35 minutes because each page earned my attention for the next one.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- The "Day in Web4" wireframes. These are the single most effective piece of communication on the site. Abstract concepts become concrete when I can see "Web4 Mail" with trust scores next to sender names and zero spam.
- The intellectual honesty. "What Could Go Wrong" is genuinely self-critical, not performative. Publishing your own failure analysis builds exactly the kind of trust the project is about.
- Problem-first framing. Every page starts with a problem I recognize before introducing a solution. This avoids the "solution looking for a problem" trap.
- Time estimates on links. Knowing a page takes "2 min" vs "7 min" vs "15 min" lets me budget my attention — which is ironically very Web4.

**What could be stronger:**
- The Karma Journey's effective trust calculation (raw × CI²) is a meaningful concept that's buried in one interactive page. It deserves more prominent explanation because it reframes the entire trust system — your reputation is only as good as your consistency.
- The adoption tiers on the landing page are too detailed for a first visit. They're important for credibility but feel like they belong on a "How would this actually get adopted?" dedicated page.
- MRH naming is a barrier. "Trust neighborhood" communicates instantly; "Markov Relevancy Horizon" communicates "this is too academic for me."
- The overlap between "Day in Web4" and "Your Internet" means a thorough visitor encounters similar content twice without clear differentiation.

**Overall:** The site successfully convinced me that (a) the internet has a real trust problem, (b) Web4 is a coherent approach to addressing it, (c) the project is honest about its limitations, and (d) there's real depth behind the claims. The simulations, wireframes, and failure analysis together create more credibility than any amount of marketing copy could. I'd return to try the interactive simulations and would genuinely recommend this to someone interested in internet infrastructure problems.
