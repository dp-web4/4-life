## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident, and refreshingly honest. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations" tells me *this is a lab*, not a product. The subtext immediately heads off my biggest suspicion: "no blockchain, not a Web3 successor." Good — that's the first thing a skeptical visitor wonders.
- The line "Web4 is the protocol — the rules and math. 4-Life is where you explore those rules through interactive simulations" is the single clearest sentence I read all session. It told me what I was looking at instantly.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" — the page told me exactly where to go first, so I went.
- Confusion points: The nav has FOUR sections (Start Here / Core Concepts / Interactive Labs / AI Agents) packed with ~12+ links. As a newcomer I felt the urge to wander, but the single TL;DR CTA pulled me back. Good restraint.

### TL;DR (02:30)
- The three-pillar framing (energy-based actions / hardware-anchored identity / portable reputation) landed well. "Trust is earned, never bought" and "Humans and AI play by the same rules" are sticky one-liners.
- BUT this is where the acronym firehose starts: ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI all appear with brief glosses. I grasped the *idea* but "witness constellation" and "Markov Relevancy Horizon" stayed opaque. I had no intuition for *why* a constellation prevents fraud — just that it supposedly does.
- Clicked: First Contact (the 7-min interactive felt like the natural "show me" next step).

### Why Web4 (after First Contact, 10:00)
- This is the strongest page for *motivation*. The five-crises framing (spam wins / reputation trapped in silos / unlimited fresh starts / AI making it worse / platforms control everything) is concrete and I felt every one of them as a real internet user.
- "All these solutions treat symptoms, not causes" is a great hook. Symptom vs. root-cause is the most persuasive idea on the whole site.
- Confusion: the same four mechanisms (ATP/LCT/T3/MRH) get stacked again in a few paragraphs — terminology before intuition, again. And a nagging dissonance: the page cites "tested code" and "424 attack vectors" but everything is simulation. Is this *real* or *theoretical*? I couldn't tell, and that uncertainty undercut the confidence the rest of the page built.

### First Contact / Alice (05:00)
- Best onboarding artifact on the site. Watching Energy (ATP, starts 100) and Trust (starts 0.50) move in real time across five acts (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) made the abstractions *kinetic*. The "you don't need to memorize these" reassurance lowered my guard exactly when I needed it.
- This page taught me more than any explainer, because I *saw* the consequence instead of reading the rule.

### LCT Explainer (16:00)
- "Your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied" is a great analogy. The 4-step witness flow (chip generates key → proves real hardware → laptop co-signs → bundle → network sees multiple confirmations) is followable.
- Confusion: "device witnesses" vs "network witnesses" use the same word *witness* for two different jobs. I genuinely couldn't tell which was meant in a couple of spots. Also "tamper-evident," "attestation," and "lineage" show up before they're glossed. And the "ceiling (0.50–0.90)" appears without telling me *why* a ceiling exists.

### ATP Economics (22:00)
- "ATP is your energy budget — not money you accumulate, and not something you can buy, sell, or speculate on" is clear and important — it pre-empts the "is this a crypto token?" worry.
- Confusion: the adenosine-triphosphate biology metaphor felt like a distraction I didn't need. And the ADP receipt — the page insists repeatedly "not a second currency," which made me *more* suspicious that it secretly is one. The timing windows ("hours to a day" recharge vs "weeks" for unconfirmed ADP) appeared without explaining why two different windows exist. That's the murkiest mechanic I hit.

### Society Simulator (28:00)
- The "watch 12 agents... form alliances, betray each other, self-organize — no central authority" pitch is exciting and the phase breakdown (Exploration → Coalition → Defector Isolation → Equilibrium) gave me a mental model for what I'd watch.
- The page is honest that "ATP, T3, LCT, and CI show up all over this page" and points me to the glossary / First Contact — but if I'd landed here *first* (which the busy nav makes possible), I'd have been lost. This page assumes I did my homework.

### Trust Tensor + Coherence Index + Aliveness (32:00)
- T3 (Talent/Training/Temperament, per-role) clicked via the "brilliant surgeon, unstable temperament" example. But V3 appears mid-page and split my attention from the core idea.
- CI (spatial/temporal/capability/relational consistency, geometric mean) was the most *clever* idea — "attacker must fake consistency across ALL dimensions" is compelling. My instinctive reaction was "this sounds like surveillance," and the page anticipated exactly that, noting checks run locally — which helped, though I can't verify the claim.
- Aliveness tied it together: alive = ATP>0 AND Trust>0.5 AND CI≥~0.5. The two-flavors-of-death distinction (energy death → rebirth; trust death → permanent) is memorable. The 0.5 threshold being "calibrated, not derived" is honestly disclosed, which I respect.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed trust-native internet protocol; trust verified by behavior, not assumed
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab for exploring those rules via simulation
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound, witnessed identity living in your device's security chip
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget for actions (ATP) + spend receipt (ADP); not money
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional, per-role trust (Talent/Training/Temperament)
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive = ATP>0 AND Trust>0.5 AND CI≥0.5; two death types
- [x] Why any of this matters — root-cause fix for spam/disposable-identity/siloed-reputation/AI-deception

(All seven boxes checked — but note several were taught by *First Contact's live demo*, not by the explainer prose. The interactive does the heavy lifting.)

## Unanswered Questions
1. What's actually *built* vs simulated? The "tested code / 424 attack vectors" claims sit next to "you can't install it" — I never resolved what exists today.
2. Why does the trust ceiling exist (0.50–0.90), and what sets a given device's level?
3. What is the *difference* between a device witness and a network witness — I hit the word "witness" doing two jobs.
4. Why two ADP timing windows? What happens to my account in the gap between spending and confirmation?
5. What's MRH, really? It's named on several pages but I never got an intuition for the "Markov Relevancy Horizon" — only that it limits visibility by relationship depth.

## Honest Assessment
This is one of the more *intellectually honest* sites I've browsed. It repeatedly anticipates my skepticism ("not blockchain," "not money you can speculate on," "calibrated not derived," "this sounds like surveillance, but...") and answers it before I can object. That builds trust — fitting, given the subject.

The clear winner is **First Contact**: watching Alice's energy and trust move in real time taught me the system in a way no amount of prose could. The explainer pages are good but lean heavily on stacked acronyms — by the third page I had ATP/LCT/T3/MRH/CI swimming around without firm anchors for the last two (MRH and CI didn't land until I reached their dedicated pages).

My single biggest residual friction is **the real-vs-simulated ambiguity**. The site oscillates between confident empirical claims ("424 attack vectors," "1,070 runs") and "this is research, you can't install it." I believe both, but I never got one clean sentence reconciling them, and that gap quietly undercut the otherwise-strong credibility. If I could request one fix: a single honest line wherever hard numbers appear — *"from simulations, not deployment"* — so the confidence and the humility stop fighting each other.

Net: I came in skeptical, left genuinely interested, and would return to actually *run* the Society Simulator. The ideas are compelling; the friction is almost entirely in the density and pacing of how they're introduced, not in the ideas themselves.
