## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who runs the witness nodes?** The LCT system depends on witnesses, but what motivates people to run witness infrastructure? Is there an ATP incentive? This is mentioned in passing but never fully explained for newcomers.
2. **What prevents a "trust cartel"?** If a tight-knit group of high-trust members only confirms each other's work, they could create an exclusionary trust bubble. Is there a mechanism to prevent this?
3. **How does the 5% transfer tax scale?** If I'm in a community where collaborative work requires many ATP transfers (think: a software development team), the 5% tax on every transfer could be punishing. Is there a team/synthon exemption?
4. **What's the actual privacy model?** CI requires tracking behavior across time, space, capability, and relationships. That's a lot of behavioral data. Who stores it? Who can query it? The "behavioral scoring vs. surveillance" tension is acknowledged but not resolved.
5. **How does this handle power asymmetries?** An employer could require employees to join their Web4 society. Low trust in that society affects your portable reputation. The system seems vulnerable to institutional power dynamics.

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely impressive research project, and the site does a remarkably good job of explaining complex ideas. The intellectual honesty — especially on the "What Could Go Wrong" page — is the site's strongest asset. I've never seen a project so candidly acknowledge its own existential risks.

**What works well:**
- Problem framing is excellent. I immediately understand *why* this matters.
- Progressive disclosure mostly works — I could follow the TL;DR → First Contact → How It Works path without getting lost.
- Concrete examples (Alice's lifecycle, the three-lives progression, the spam math) make abstract concepts tangible.
- The interactive tools (Karma Journey, LCT slider) teach through experience rather than explanation.
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page alone makes me take this seriously as a research effort.

**What needs work:**
- Navigation overload on the landing page. Too many entry points competing for attention.
- The site tries to be both an explainer for newcomers AND a technical reference. These audiences need different paths.
- Some pages (especially How It Works) try to cover too much ground. Splitting into focused sub-pages would help.
- The glossary mixes engineering terms with speculative consciousness research, which confuses the scope of the project.
- The Learning Journey page describes *topics* but not *outcomes* — I know what I'll read but not what I'll understand.

**Overall**: I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left with a solid understanding of the core mechanisms and why they matter. That's a success. The site's main weakness isn't content quality — it's information architecture. The right content exists but the paths through it could be better signposted. A visitor who hits "How It Works" too early might bounce; one who finds "Why Web4" first will likely stay.

Would I come back? Yes — I want to play more with the Karma Journey and explore the Society Simulator. The ideas are compelling enough to warrant deeper exploration, and the honest treatment of limitations makes me trust the project's intellectual integrity.
