## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Strong. The headline "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" is something I *feel* — spam, bots, reputation that doesn't travel. I didn't need to know anything to nod along. The line "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" preemptively answered the two questions forming in my head ("is this crypto?" / "is this Web3?"). Good.
- **Clicked**: The site is bossy in a helpful way — "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" is clearly the recommended door, so I took it.
- **Confusion points**: The page is *long* and the navigation is huge — top nav (7 links), "Core Concept" links (5), "Interactive Lab" links (5), plus three CTAs. As a first-timer I felt a flicker of "where do I even start?" before the TL;DR button rescued me. The "5 Ideas in Plain English" framing is reassuring, but I also saw "Linked Context Token / Trust Tensor (T3) / ATP Economics / MRH / Aliveness" sitting right there as links — a row of acronyms I didn't understand yet. Mild intimidation.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (03:30)
- This is the right length and tone. Four claims: energy-based participation (ATP), hardware-anchored identity, permanent+portable reputation, same rules for AI and humans. I got the *what* and the *why*.
- **Confusion point**: First appearance of "ATP." My only association is high-school biology (cellular energy). It's used here without explaining the borrow, so for ~2 minutes I assumed it was just a quirky name and moved on. The *how* (how energy actually refills, who judges value) stayed abstract — but that's fair for a 2-minute page.
- Clicked the first recommended next step: First Contact.

### First Contact (06:00)
- Reassuring framing: "Haven't read anything else on this site yet? You don't need to." It pre-defines ATP, Trust, LCT, coherence, then promises each is explained "the moment Alice runs into it." That's exactly the progressive-disclosure promise the landing page makes — good.
- **Friction (structural)**: It's an interactive 7-minute sim where I "drive Alice's choices — play, pause, scrub, replay." I couldn't tell from the page text alone what actually *happens* in the 16 steps — the learning is locked inside the interaction. A visitor who skims rather than commits 7 minutes leaves with the *promise* of understanding, not the understanding. (Hard to fix — that's the nature of a sim — but worth noting that the page sells the sim more than it previews its payoff.)

### Why Web4? (11:00)
- **The problem framing is the strongest part of the whole site.** Spam-is-free, trapped-reputation, disposable-identity, AI-deception — four crisp, relatable grievances. I was convinced the *problem* is real within 30 seconds.
- I genuinely respect the honesty: an "Honest Questions" FAQ that admits "a sufficiently motivated adversary with a large budget can still attack," that recovery "is slower than 'forgot password'... the friction is intentional," and that witnessing is "an active research area, not a solved problem." Most pitches hide this. This one leads with it. That built trust with me faster than any feature claim.
- **Friction (HIGH)**: Acronym avalanche. On this one page I met ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, V3, "Coherence Index," "Value Confirmation Mechanism," SAL. The FAQ is reportedly ~80 Q&As. For a "why should I care" page, that's a firehose. I felt the page shift from *motivating me* to *quizzing me*.
- **Friction (MEDIUM)**: The value loop feels circular when stated plainly — "Received value backs ATP," and value is confirmed by recipients whose trust is itself built from past confirmations. I couldn't find the answer to "why would recipients rate honestly instead of rubber-stamping?" That's the load-bearing assumption and it's the least explained.

### LCT Explainer (16:00)
- **Best explainer on the site.** "Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer." That single sentence taught me more than the acronym ever could. The Web2 "what you know" vs Web4 "what witnesses verify" contrast made the difference from a password concrete.
- The scary hardware terms (TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2) are tucked into a collapsible "you don't need to memorize these" section. That's the *right* pattern — I wish the other pages did this consistently.

### ATP Economics (19:30)
- This finally answered my landing-page question: ATP is *borrowed from biology* on purpose — "they burn ATP, get ADP back, and recharge ATP from food." Once I read that, the metaphor clicked and felt clever rather than arbitrary. **This explanation should appear the first time ATP is mentioned (TL;DR / landing), not five pages deep.**
- Clear: 100 ATP starter grant, spend to act, recharge from confirmed value, drain to zero = "you die." The "~7× more for high-quality work" is a nice concrete number.
- **Friction (MEDIUM)**: Same circularity as Why Web4 — "who decides value? The people who received your work do" — and confirmer trust depends on their own past confirmations. Self-bootstrapping, and the page half-admits it feels opaque.

### Aliveness (23:00)
- I came here worried, because "you die" is an alarming word for a website to use about me. The page directly de-escalated it: for humans, death = "temporary loss of participation rights... not account deletion: your identity and history persist." For AI it suspends the LCT, not the model weights. That distinction is important and I'm glad it's explicit.
- Honest again: appeals mechanism "designed and has a reference implementation... but hasn't been tested with real humans."
- **Friction (LOW)**: The word "die" does emotional work that might scare people off before they reach the reassurance. The reassurance lives on a *separate page* from where the scary word is introduced (ATP page). A one-line "(this doesn't mean losing your identity — see Aliveness)" inline at the first use of "die" would catch the worry at the source.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (25:30)
- "Tensor" made me brace for math; the page immediately said "You don't need the math" and delivered: Talent, Training, Temperament, always per-role. The surgeon example ("a brilliant surgeon who's unreliable vs a steady one with less talent") nailed *why* one star-rating number isn't enough.
- Abstraction only creeps in at the optional update formula / half-life decay — appropriately buried.

### Glossary (27:00)
- Useful structure: "Essential only" vs "All terms," category jumps. Good for someone like me drowning in acronyms.
- **Friction (MEDIUM)**: Some definitions are circular for a true beginner. V3 is "Like T3 but for value created... valuation, veracity, and validity" — defined entirely in terms of T3 and "tensor," which a newcomer landing on the glossary cold hasn't learned. MRH is glossed as "Trust Neighborhood" but I *still* never learned what the letters M-R-H stand for anywhere I looked.

### Society Simulator (briefly)
- Interesting premise (can self-interested agents cooperate without central control?), and it does have a "What Happens When You Click Run" walkthrough. But it openly warns "Never seen these Web4 terms before? ATP, T3, LCT, and CI show up all over this page" and tells me to go do First Contact first. So it's a *destination*, not an *entry point* — which is fine, as long as nobody links a cold visitor straight here.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Why would value-confirmers be honest?** The whole economy rests on recipients rating contributions truthfully. What stops collusion or generous rubber-stamping among friends? Never answered.
2. **What does MRH actually stand for?** I learned it means "trust neighborhood" but never the words behind the letters.
3. **If I lose all my devices at once, what really happens?** Why Web4 hinted recovery is slow-by-design, but I'd want the concrete worst case before trusting my identity to this.
4. **Who runs the "community pool" my 100 ATP comes from, and can they manipulate it?** The pool "anchored to real resources" is asserted, not explained.
5. **How is this not social credit, really?** The FAQ says "voluntary, bottom-up" — but if every platform adopts it, is opting out actually possible?

## Honest Assessment

This site is unusually *honest* and that's its biggest asset. It repeatedly says "this is research, not production," names its unsolved problems, and admits where things are untested. As a naive visitor that made me trust the *authors* even while I stayed unsure about the *system*. That's the right trade for an R&D explainer.

The explainer pages (LCT, ATP, Trust Tensor, Aliveness) are genuinely good — concrete analogies, "you don't need the math," collapsible deep-ends. If the whole site read like those four pages, I'd rate it "solid."

The weak link is the **gradient between the front door and the explainers**. The landing page and especially "Why Web4?" front-load acronyms before I've earned them. ATP's lovely biology origin is buried five clicks from where ATP first appears. The result: the site is excellent if you take the *guided* path through the explainers, but punishing if you wander — and curious humans wander.

My one structural instinct: the site already *has* the perfect onboarding ingredients; they're just distributed unevenly. The first mention of every acronym should carry its best one-line intuition (or a hover/expand), so that wandering is safe. Right now understanding is gated behind reading the right page in the right order. Lower that gate and "maybe recommend" becomes "yes recommend."

The single most-confusing thing was not any one concept — it was the **density of capitalized acronyms** arriving faster than their explanations. The single most-clarifying thing was the LCT driver's-license sentence. The gap between those two experiences is the whole UX opportunity.
