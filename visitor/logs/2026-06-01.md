## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust-verified-by-behavior internet, no blockchain
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — interactive simulations of how Web4 mechanics play out
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound identity, ID lives in a security chip
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — ATP is a finite energy budget for actions; ADP is the receipt
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — 3-dimensional reputation (Talent / Training / Temperament)
- [x] How agents live/die — Energy Death (recoverable) vs Trust Death (permanent)
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam economics, AI deception, portable reputation, platform power

**But honestly**: I checked all 7 boxes only because I visited 13 pages. A typical visitor reading 2–3 pages would probably check 3–4 boxes. The site rewards depth; it doesn't reward casual visits as efficiently.

## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who pays for the witness infrastructure?** The LCT page mentions network witnesses as optional infrastructure nodes. If they're optional and decentralized, who runs them, and what's their incentive? I didn't see this fully addressed.

2. **What does V3 actually do day-to-day?** It's named, paired with T3, but I don't have a concrete example of a V3 score affecting anything. Does my tutorial's Veracity score determine whether it gets visibility? Does it affect *my* T3? The arrow between V3 and T3 is blurry.

3. **Why CI², not CI³ or just CI?** The squaring is justified post-hoc as "it penalizes inconsistency nonlinearly" — but I want to know whether 3 was tried and rejected, whether the squaring choice has a name (sigmoid? quadratic damping?), and whether the simulations actually compared exponents.

4. **What happens when two societies disagree about my trust?** Web4 talks about portable reputation, but societies can configure their own thresholds (the aliveness page mentions this). If Society A says I'm alive at 0.45 and Society B requires 0.6, where do I actually "live"? I didn't find this cross-society translation explained.

5. **Adoption tier 5 — Native — feels mythical.** The page mentions a 5-tier rollout, but tier 5 ("the internet runs on Web4") presumes a near-universal switch. What does the transition between tier 4 (federated) and tier 5 (native) actually look like, mechanically? Or is tier 5 the asymptote we never quite reach?

## Honest Assessment

The site is dramatically better than I expected from a research-prototype explainer. The landing page's parenthetical ("no blockchain, not a Web3 successor") and the 5-plain-English-ideas framing alone do more work than most whitepapers manage in 20 pages.

The strongest single page was the **Aliveness** page — the energy-death vs trust-death distinction is the kind of dramatic-but-clarifying frame that explains what the system *cares about*. The strongest concept-explainer was the **ATP economics** 60-second box. The strongest hook was the **Karma Journey** premise ("your next life starts where this one ended").

What still works against the site is **breadth tax**. There are 13+ concept pages, each well-written, but the system as a whole is taxing because every page makes you re-acquire its acronyms. The plain-English names (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) work on the landing page, then dissolve once you reach the individual pages, which switch back to LCT / ATP / T3 / MRH / CI. The cognitive cost of moving between pages is bigger than the cost of reading any single page.

The "calibrated, not derived" honesty is admirable, and I trust the project more because of it — but after seeing it on the 0.5 threshold, the 0.85 CI start, the 3-hop horizon, the CI² exponent, the TPM=0.90 ceiling, I started to want a single "Where the numbers come from" page that owned this meta-question. Instead, every page re-explains the same epistemic posture.

I would return — the depth is real and the writing is patient. I would recommend it to a friend who's already interested in trust architecture or AI alignment. I would not recommend it to a casual friend asking "what's Web4?" — for them, the TL;DR page alone would land, and the rest would be too much.

**Most surprising moment**: realizing that the system distinguishes Energy Death from Trust Death, and that Energy Death is recoverable. That single distinction reframed everything I had read before it. It's the kind of detail that, in retrospect, deserves to be in the *first* explanation, not the eighth.
