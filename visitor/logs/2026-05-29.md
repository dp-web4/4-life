## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (a proposed trust-native internet protocol where reputation and energy budgets replace anonymous-spam economics)
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate (interactive simulations of the proposed Web4 mechanics, framed as a research lab not a product)
- [x] What LCT means (hardware-bound digital identity, signed by your device's security chip, with multi-device or community-vouching recovery)
- [x] What ATP/ADP means (ATP = energy budget for actions; ADP = receipt proving spending; valuable contributions refill ATP)

- [x] How agents "live" and "die" (three conditions: ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, CI coherent; energy death is recoverable, trust death is permanent)
- [x] Why this matters (because the internet's lack of memory rewards spam, fragments reputation, and gives bad actors unlimited fresh starts)

## Unanswered Questions

1. **What does ATP recharge actually feel like in practice?** "Continuously over a rolling window" is mathematically precise but I don't have an intuition for whether I'm refilling minutes, hours, or days after my work helps someone.

2. **Who computes "quality" for the T3 update formula?** The formula `0.02 × (quality − 0.5)` is shown, but the *quality* input is treated as a given. Is it averaged peer ratings? A model? An aggregate over time? VCM is referenced but I want one concrete example.

3. **What's the user experience of solo-device recovery?** "3-7 days via community vouching" sounds rough — does that mean I can't access anything for a week? Can I prove who I am to a small circle even before formal recovery completes?

4. **How does a brand-new community bootstrap?** I see "reciprocity density" mentioned for cold-start, but I don't get a concrete picture of what the first 100 actions in a brand-new society look like.

5. **What happens when the protocol itself upgrades?** If the trust formulas change in Web4 v2, does my reputation migrate, get rebased, or do I start fresh?

## Honest Assessment

This is a thoughtfully constructed educational site. The five distinct entry points (TL;DR, Why Web4, First Contact, Karma Journey, Society Simulator) accommodate different learning styles, and most pages have plain-English handles for the heavy jargon. The pedagogical pattern is consistent: introduce the problem viscerally, then the mechanism abstractly, then a concrete example or interactive demo.

The strongest pages are the **TL;DR** (genuinely 2 minutes, genuinely accessible), the **Aliveness page** (the two-deaths distinction is one of the cleanest conceptual moves on the site), and **A Day in Web4** (it's the page that finally answers "what would it feel like?").

The weakest moments are when jargon stacks before glosses — the Why Web4 FAQ in particular has acronym density that would lose a newcomer who hasn't already read the core concept pages. And the recurring "witness" overload (devices vs network) is a small but real source of confusion that the glossary doesn't yet address.

What stuck with me as a naive visitor: the framing "the internet has no memory" diagnoses a real frustration I've experienced. And the design move to make trust *portable across contexts* while keeping people *pseudonymous* is genuinely interesting — it's a different combination from anything I've seen.

What I'd want next: a page like "Web4 in practice today" that walks me through what works *right now* in the prototype, versus what's still on paper. The honesty about being a research lab is great; one more page that crisply maps "shipped" vs "spec only" would help me calibrate.

Net: I'd return, I'd recommend this to a technically-literate friend, and I'd want to come back when I had time to actually run the Playground experiments rather than just reading about them.
