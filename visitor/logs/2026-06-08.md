## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident, and unusually honest. The headline "4-Life | Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the subtitle "Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" answered three of my biggest reflexive questions in one breath: *Is this crypto? Is this trying to replace something? Is it real?* The "not a Web3 successor / no blockchain" framing pre-empted my skepticism nicely.
- The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" section landed — spam, locked reputation, consequence-free restarts, AI deception. These are problems I actually feel.
- I appreciated being told flatly: "a research proposal… you cannot currently install or join it." That lowered my guard. I wasn't being sold anything.
- Clicked: "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (recommended).
- Confusion points: Five acronyms (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI) appear in the concept list near the bottom. I didn't try to memorize them — they were clearly labeled as "you'll learn these." Fine.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (04:00)
- This is the page I wish every technical site had. "Plain English first — every term is spelled out the first time it appears" is a promise it mostly keeps.
- The four mechanics (energy drains/refills, hardware-anchored identity, portable permanent reputation, humans+AI same rules) gave me a real mental model in two minutes.
- Confusion: T3, MRH, and CI get only a one-line parenthetical here ("T3 = Talent/Training/Temperament," "MRH = Markov Relevancy Horizon," "CI = Coherence Index") and a promise of "deeper explanation elsewhere." For a 2-minute page that's the right call — but "Markov Relevancy Horizon" is a genuinely intimidating phrase to drop with no gloss. The friendlier "trust neighborhood" name from the landing page didn't make it here.
- Clicked: "First Contact" (recommended as the fastest experiential path).

### First Contact (08:00)
- This is the best thing on the site. Following Alice through Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn made the abstract mechanics *felt* rather than read.
- The two live numbers (🔋 Energy Budget / ATP starting at 100, 🤝 Trust starting at 0.50) gave me something concrete to watch.
- The framing line "Real rules, shown by simulation — not a demo of a shipped product" did a lot of work. By this point I was no longer confused about whether this was a product. Earlier visitors clearly struggled with that; I didn't.
- Confusion: The four acronyms are listed up top before the story explains them. Mild — the page says "each is explained in the story," and it delivers.

### Why Web4? (14:00)
- Deep and genuinely well-organized, but **long** (felt like 15k words). The collapsible FAQ + topic index + "if you only have 2 minutes, try the TL;DR" rescue it. I treated it as a reference, not a read-through, which seems to be the intent.
- The "why previous fixes failed" section (moderation, CAPTCHAs, blockchain, social login) was the most persuasive part — it earned the solution before presenting it.
- Confusion: Same five acronyms arrive fairly fast near the top. Each gets a plain-English card, so it's survivable, but this is the densest acronym moment on the site.

### Karma Journey (21:00)
- Fun and clarifying — playing multiple "lives" and watching reputation carry forward made consequence-permanence click.
- The CI² explanation ("a 10% dip costs ~19%; a 40% dip costs 64%… squaring is the sweet spot") was surprisingly intuitive for a squared term.
- **Confusion (real):** the distinction between **raw trust** (decides whether you survive this life) and **effective trust** (decides your rebirth conditions, after consistency penalties) is the load-bearing idea of the whole page, but I had to re-read to keep them straight. It's stated, but mid-text and easy to glide past. A naive player makes choices without fully grasping which number their choice is moving.

### Aliveness (27:00)
- Clear and memorable. The professional-license analogy (need overhead funds + patient trust + clean record) made the three-criteria aliveness model stick.
- The two deaths are crisply separated: **energy death** (ATP→0, recoverable like bankruptcy) vs **trust death** (trust<0.5, permanent like losing a license).
- **Notably good:** the page directly answered the question I'd been carrying — *what does "death" even mean for an AI?* It says death doesn't touch the model weights; it suspends the LCT and the hardware-bound access, so the program keeps running but "society stops honoring anything it signs… still talking, but to a room that no longer listens." That's a vivid, honest answer to a question I didn't expect to be addressed.

### LCT Explainer (33:00)
- The "your phone becoming a driver's license that can't be photocopied" analogy is excellent. The 0–1 scale is anchored early ("0.5 is the alive/dead line, 0.75 is solid, 0.90 is the hardware ceiling") which made the hardware-tier table (TPM 0.90, Secure Enclave 0.85, FIDO2 0.75, Software 0.50) readable.
- The "Web4 uses the word 'witness' for two different jobs" heads-up was helpful — but it's tucked in a collapsible mid-page, *after* "witnessing" already appears in the opening. I'd half-conflated the two meanings before I reached the clarification.
- **Confusion (real):** I came away unsure whether better hardware gives you a higher *starting* trust. The page says ceilings are "not starting points," but it never plainly re-states that *everyone* starts at 0.50 regardless of device. My naive intuition kept wanting to read "TPM 0.90" as "TPM users start at 0.90."

### Trust Tensor (38:00)
- T3 dimensions are well-defined and the surgeon-vs-mechanic example (Alice trusted 90% as surgeon, 27% as mechanic) nailed role-specificity.
- V3 is handled honestly: "you don't need to master it to follow this page." Good. But the page also says V3 feeds the T3 update formula — so calling it "optional" while it's mechanically inside the formula I'm reading created a small tension. Optional to *understand*, not optional to the *math*.
- **Confusion (real):** the calibration note "(Deeper: how calibration is measured ↓)" promised a payoff below for "what does 0.7 actually mean?" — and the answer I found ("0.7 = ~70% cooperative behavior in this role") felt thinner than the buildup. The ↓ arrow implies a substantial deeper section that didn't obviously materialize.

### MRH Explorer (43:00)
- The "boundary of what each agent can see, affect, and reason about; beyond it, trust signals fade into noise" definition is the clearest MRH has been described anywhere on the site.
- The decay model (0.85 at 1 hop, ~0.65 at 2, noise floor by depth 4) plus the "why 3 hops?" answer ("beyond ~3 hops the signal-to-noise empirically collapses… calibrated observation, not derivation") satisfied me.
- **Confusion (real):** the "4D Horizon Profile" lists **Quality at 0% weight**, yet a quality multiplier shows up in the cost calculator right there on the same page. As a naive reader I couldn't reconcile "quality doesn't matter here (0%)" with "quality changes the cost here." Also the horizon toggles (self / Direct (1) / Default (2) / Max (3) / Beyond) appear without a one-line explanation of what each does.

### Society Simulator (46:00)
- Big and impressive — 12 agents forming alliances and betraying each other, with presets or play-as-an-agent mode.
- Onboarding is well-handled for someone landing here cold: a prominent yellow "ATP, T3, LCT, CI show up all over this page" box, a link to First Contact, an inline collapsible acronym key, and a 30-second quiz.
- **Confusion (real):** the "What's real, what's simulated" section uses checkmarks and tildes to show which mechanics are full vs. simplified (e.g., scalar reputation instead of the full T3 tensor) — but to read that table you already need to know what T3, MRH, and V3 *are*. It's the one spot where MRH/V3 are assumed rather than glossed.

## Honest Assessment

This is the most coherent visit I can imagine having as a newcomer. The site has clearly been worked over many times, and it shows: the "is this real or simulated?" anxiety that I'd expect to feel was actively disarmed on the landing page, the TL;DR, and First Contact, all three. By the time I reached the concept pages I trusted the site to be honest with me, which made me more willing to dig.

The standout is the **journey design**: Landing → TL;DR → First Contact gives you a felt model before any jargon, and the concept pages reward you for that order. First Contact and Aliveness are genuinely excellent — Aliveness in particular answered a question (what "death" means for an AI agent) that I didn't even know I had, and answered it concretely.

The remaining friction is no longer about *missing* explanations — almost everything is explained somewhere. It's about **the explanation landing at the moment of confusion rather than a paragraph later or a collapsible away**. Three spots stood out: Karma Journey's raw-vs-effective distinction (the page's whole point, but easy to skim past while playing), MRH Explorer's quality-weight-0%-but-still-affects-cost apparent contradiction (the one place I felt the site contradicted itself), and the LCT ceiling-vs-starting-point ambiguity. None of these blocked understanding; each cost me a re-read.

If I were grading: this went from "interesting research site" to "I actually get it" in under an hour, and I'd send it to a curious friend without caveats. The honesty about what's real vs. proposed is what makes it trustworthy — fittingly, for a site about trust.
