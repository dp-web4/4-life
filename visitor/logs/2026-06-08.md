## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Genuinely good hook. "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" + "Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos." — I immediately knew what category of thing this was about, and I felt the problem before being asked to learn anything.
- I appreciated how fast it set expectations: *"Web4 is a research proposal for making trust work on the internet. Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain. Right now, you can't install it."* That honesty up front made me trust the site more, not less. It pre-empted my "is this a crypto scam?" reflex.
- The page lays out five plain-English ideas (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) and several timed entry points ("2-minute TL;DR", "What it looks like (5 min)", "Frustrations it would fix (3 min)").
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — the timed labels are a nice touch; they let me budget my attention.
- **Confusion points**: None major here. The landing page is the strongest page on the site for a naive visitor.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes — /tldr (03:00)
- Structure is clean: The Problem → The Idea → The Result → Where is this now?. The "Plain English first, so you can read this cold" promise is mostly kept.
- **First friction**: the acronyms start arriving. ATP ("Allocation Transfer Packets"), LCT ("Linked Context Token"), and then MRH ("Markov Relevancy Horizon") and CI ("Coherence Index"). The page says "you don't need to memorize these" — but I still felt the cognitive weight of five capitalized concepts in a "2-minute" read. The cellular ATP reference didn't help me understand the *internet* function; it just told me the name has a biology origin.
- The honest "Web4 (protocol) vs 4-Life (the lab)" distinction is genuinely useful and I wish it were even more prominent — I kept needing it later.

### First Contact — /first-contact (06:00)
- Pitched as a 7-minute interactive simulation where "you'll drive Alice's choices — play, pause, scrub, replay." A prominent **"Start the 5-act Walkthrough →"** button. Two live meters described: 🔋 Energy Budget (ATP, starts 100) and 🤝 Trust Score (0.00–1.00, starts 0.50).
- This sounds like the best onboarding path — *show*, don't tell. But I couldn't tell from the static page whether the walkthrough actually runs and how good it is (see Honest Assessment re: my limitation). On the framing page itself, the same acronym soup (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) appears *before* the walkthrough that supposedly explains them.
- **Confusion**: "5-act Walkthrough" vs the "7-minute simulation" label — minor, but two different framings of the same thing.

### Why Web4? — /why-web4 (10:00)
- **Best "why should I care" page.** Front-loads five concrete, recognizable problems: spam/bots winning, reputation trapped in silos, unlimited fresh starts for bad actors, AI agents making it worse, platforms controlling everything. Each has a one-line root cause ("Actions have no cost"). This is exactly the right order: problem → root cause → proposed fix.
- **The FAQ is the hidden gem of the whole site.** ~60 questions, and it openly admits hard things: "research prototype, not a product," "no timeline—and that's intentional," GDPR tension with permanent reputation ("genuine tension"), the social-credit-system comparison addressed head-on, appeals "designed but not yet deployed." This honesty is rare and it's the single most credibility-building thing on the site.
- **Friction**: the "What Would Actually Work" section jumps into ATP/LCT/T3/MRH mechanisms with thin plain-English bridging. Same acronym wall, again.

### Glossary — /glossary (16:00)
- Well built: "All terms / Essential only" filter, jump anchors, Ctrl+K search. The plain-English-first definitions with analogies are the *right* model and honestly should be how the concepts are introduced everywhere:
  - ATP: "Think of it like an energy budget. You spend it to act, earn it by creating value."
  - LCT: "Your presence lives in your hardware, not in a company's database."
  - T3: "Trust isn't one number. You trust a surgeon's skill but maybe not their punctuality."
  - MRH: "You see entities you trust and entities they trust (transitively). This limits spam."
- **New friction**: here I discovered yet *another* acronym — **V3 (Value Tensor)** — distinct from T3. So now I'm tracking T3 *and* V3, both "tensors," with similar-sounding triple-word expansions. This is where the proliferation started to feel like a lot.

### LCT Explainer — /lct-explainer (20:00)
- **Headline**: "Web4 Verified Presence." The opening analogy is the best single sentence on the site: *"Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer."* I understood hardware-bound identity instantly.
- **Confusion**: "What if I lose my device?" is the first question a normal human asks, and the answer is scattered (multi-device = "minutes to hours", single device = "days", with "quorum" and "recovery" mentioned but not fully shown here). Also "device co-witnesses" vs "network witnesses" — two ideas, one word, needs careful reading.

### ATP Economics — /atp-economics (24:00)
- **Headline**: "ATP: Allocation Transfer Packets — Every Action Has a Cost." The spend→receipt→recharge cycle (with ADP as the "receipt") is intuitive thanks to the cell analogy.
- **Confusion**: The page works hard to insist "ADP isn't money you can spend" — the repetition made me *more* suspicious, not less (it answers a question by raising it). The 5% transfer fee ("send 105 to deliver 100") read backwards on first pass. And the recurring unanswered question across pages: *who actually judges "quality" to refill my energy?*

### Society Simulator — /society-simulator (28:00)
- **Headline**: "Society Simulator — Multi-Agent Trust Dynamics." Lots of text about "What Happens When You Click Run," scenario dropdown (Cooperative Majority / Hostile World / Reciprocity Rules / Trust Scarce / All Adaptive), Animated/Instant toggle, "Run Society" button, and agent strategies (Cooperator, Defector, Reciprocator…).
- **This is the page that felt most like an instruction manual for software I haven't opened.** It describes a network graph and colored nodes I want to *see*, and spends a lot of words on "what's simplified vs. what's real" — which reads defensive before I've even watched it run. As a visitor I want the graph moving first, the caveats second.
- (Caveat about my own limitation in Honest Assessment — I may not have triggered the live widget.)

### Trust Tensor — /trust-tensor (31:00)
- **Headline**: "Trust Tensors: Multi-Dimensional Trust." Core: T3 = Talent, Training, Temperament. The examples land well — the brilliant-but-unreliable surgeon vs the steady one; Alice at 90% as a data analyst, 76% as a project manager, 27% as a mechanic. "Trust is multi-dimensional" became concrete and intuitive.
- The page says "You don't need the math" — but then shows `base = 0.02 × (quality − 0.5)`. Showing a formula right after telling me I don't need it is a small whiplash. Pick one: either hide it behind a "show the math" toggle, or own it.
- **Friction**: V3 (Valuation/Veracity/Validity) appears here too, stacked on T3. Three trios of T-words and V-words is a memory tax.

### Aliveness — /aliveness (33:00)
- **Headline**: "Aliveness: When Existence is Measurable." An agent stays alive while three conditions hold: ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, and a coherent Coherence Index. Break one → death.
- **Tone**: surprisingly *not* dystopian. The bankruptcy-vs-losing-a-license analogy made "death" feel fair: energy-death is recoverable, trust-death is the serious one, and reputation/karma carry forward across "lives" if trust stayed above 0.5. The appeals mechanism (independent witness panel, evidence types, anti-gaming ATP costs) is explained and honestly flagged as "designed... but hasn't been tested with real humans."
- **Confusion**: why CI is *squared* (CI²) rather than a linear penalty needed a re-read; "synthons" (group aliveness) appears late and underexplained.

## Understanding Gained

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Who actually judges "quality"** to refill my ATP energy? This is the load-bearing question for the whole economy and it stayed fuzzy across multiple pages.
2. If reputation is **permanent and portable**, how does this *not* become a social credit system in practice? (The FAQ acknowledges the tension but I left without a settled answer.)
3. **What does V3 add that T3 doesn't?** I tracked both but never felt I needed both as a newcomer.
4. Does the **Society Simulator actually run interactively**, and is it legible to a first-timer? I couldn't confirm from the page text (see below).
5. The realistic device-loss recovery story: concretely, **what do I do in the first 5 minutes after my phone is stolen?**

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely well-made educational site, and notably more honest than most "new internet paradigm" pitches I'd stumble onto. The thing that won me over wasn't any single concept — it was the *posture*: "not a product, no timeline, here are the unsolved problems" stated up front and reinforced in a 60-question FAQ. That candor made me willing to spend 30+ minutes I wouldn't normally spend.

The analogies are the site's superpower: phone-as-driver's-license (LCT), cell energy (ATP), surgeon-vs-mechanic trust (T3), bankruptcy-vs-lost-license (death). Whenever the site led with one of these, I understood the idea in one read.

The site's recurring weakness is the opposite move: leading with **coined acronyms before the analogy**. By the time I'd visited six pages I was juggling ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, MRH, and synthons — and several of them rhyme structurally (two different "three-letter tensors" with three-word expansions). The glossary proves the team *can* explain each one plainly; the onboarding pages just need to inherit that discipline and, crucially, **defer the secondary terms** (V3/CI/MRH/synthons) behind an explicit "deeper" boundary rather than sprinkling them into the gentle intro.

**Important honesty about my own limitation this session**: the two most "interactive" experiences — the First Contact 5-act walkthrough and the Society Simulator — appear to be JavaScript-driven, and the way I read these pages may only have captured their static descriptive shell, not the live, running widget. So my "reads like a manual" critique of the simulator should be weighed accordingly: it's possible the live experience is excellent and the *static text around it* is just too heavy. If the dev track wants a clean read on those two pages specifically, they're worth a human spot-check — because if the interactive payoff is buried under paragraphs of setup and caveats, that's the highest-leverage fix on the site.

Net: I'd come back to try the simulators properly. I'd recommend it — but only to someone who already feels the "internet trust is broken" itch. A general visitor would bounce at the third acronym.
