## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean and unusually self-aware. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the immediate clarification — "a proposed internet trust model... trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" — preemptively answered the two questions forming in my head ("is this crypto?" / "is this Web3?"). That "no blockchain" disclaimer up front is genuinely disarming.
- The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" hook ("Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos.") is concrete and relatable in one line.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — the labeled recommended entry point. Good signposting; I didn't have to guess.
- Confusion points: The secondary nav (Core Concepts / Interactive Labs / AI Agents, each with 5–7 links) is a LOT of doors for a first screen. I ignored it because the TL;DR button was clearly marked "start here," but a less-guided visitor could feel the sprawl.

### 2-Minute TL;DR (03:00)
- This page does the single best job on the site of being honest about jargon: it *promises* "every term is spelled out the first time it appears" and mostly keeps that promise. ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI all got an inline plain-English gloss.
- The "three pillars" framing (energy-based actions / hardware-anchored identity / portable reputation) gave me a mental skeleton before any acronym landed. That ordering — concept first, acronym second — worked.
- What still felt hand-wavy: *how* a "witness constellation" actually stops a faker, and why a wealthy bad actor can't just buy their way past the energy budget. Noted but not blocking.
- Sent me to First Contact, which I took.

### First Contact (06:00)
- The Alice five-act story (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) is a strong teaching device. "You don't need to have read anything else" is reassuring and felt true.
- I appreciated being told I can pause/rewind/replay — it makes the abstract feel pokeable.
- Mild friction: all four acronyms (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) still arrive in the intro framing before the story has had a chance to *show* them. The TL;DR softened this, but a visitor who lands here first (it's linked everywhere as the entry intro) gets them cold.
- "Death" language is startling until the page clarifies it's suspension/rebirth, not deletion.

### Why Web4? (12:00)
- The five-cause problem statement (free actions / siloed reputation / free fresh starts / AI deception / platform control) is the most persuasive content on the whole site. Each cause maps cleanly to one of the four mechanisms. This is the page I'd send a skeptic to.
- The FAQ is intellectually honest to a fault — "designed but largely untested at scale," "no timeline," "this is research, not production." That candor *builds* trust (fitting for a trust project).
- BUT: the FAQ is enormous. As a naive reader I bounced off its sheer size — it reads like a reference doc, not something I'd finish. I skimmed maybe 4 of ~30 questions.
- Jargon: **SAL appears in the FAQ but is never defined anywhere on this page.** ATP/LCT/T3 are glossed inline; MRH is hand-waved as "math-flavored jargon you can ignore"; SAL just shows up bare. That's the one acronym that left me with no foothold here.

### Karma Journey (18:00)
- The two-death model (Energy Death = recoverable suspension; Trust Collapse below 0.5 = permanent) is clear and the survival-vs-tier framing is improving — there's now a "One life, two numbers" two-card visual that helped me see survival and tier are *different* outcomes.
- The raw-vs-effective distinction is STILL the hardest single concept on the site. I understood it, but only on the second read: raw trust (survival, the 0.5 line) vs. effective trust = raw × CI² (which karma tier you inherit). The worked example "survive on raw 0.55 but land in Constrained tier via effective 0.27" is the crux — and it's the thing most likely to make a newcomer feel they've missed something.
- The CI² ("squaring compounds inconsistency — a 10% dip costs ~19%") math is correct but lands as a footnote-flavored aside, not a lesson. I trust it more than I understand *why* it's squared.

### ATP Economics (23:00)
- This page genuinely answered the "where does the energy come from / why is it scarce" question the Why-Web4 page had left open: each community mints a fixed pool anchored to real resources (CPU, attention, relationships); you get a 100-ATP starter grant; you can't buy/mine it. The "ATP is a usage budget, not a tradeable asset" line is the keeper.
- The recharge-vs-payment two-channel distinction ("recharge refunds, payment earns") and the ADP-as-receipt analogy ("ATP is cash, ADP is the receipt that converts back to cash") are both excellent.
- The quality-ramp worked example (same 50 ATP spent → ~6 back at 35% quality vs ~42 back at 85%, ~7×) made the incentive viscerally clear.
- Confusing residue: T3, CI, and V3 are all referenced here as inputs but only T3 gets even a "derived score" gloss; CI and V3 appear bare. On a page about how you earn energy, "confirmer trust" and "output quality" being undefined left a small gap.

### Society Simulator (26:00)
- Cool concept — 12 agents, pick a preset, watch a society cooperate or collapse, or play an agent yourself. The strategy descriptions (Cooperators always help, Defectors exploit, Reciprocators mirror, etc.) are crisp.
- The metric glosses (Cooperation %, Rounds, Rebirths-with-karma) are beginner-friendly.
- BUT this is the page where jargon density peaks: ATP, T3, LCT, CI, MRH, V3 all appear and the page *itself* points you back to First Contact if you've never seen them. As the journey's "go deeper" endpoint that's reasonable, but if anyone arrives here cold (e.g. via search), it assumes a lot.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed trust-native internet: behavior-verified trust, not blockchain
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab/simulation of those rules
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token, your portable hardware-bound reputation record
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget (ATP) + receipt (ADP); spend to act, earn back via community confirmation
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3: Talent/Training/Temperament, scored per role (understood the shape; didn't see the update math, but didn't need it)
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — energy death (recoverable suspension) vs trust collapse below 0.5 (permanent); karma carries forward
- [x] Why any of this matters — the five-cause problem framing on Why-Web4 made the "why" land clearly

All seven boxes checked — the best comprehension outcome I can recall for a naive first pass. The journey (Landing → TL;DR → First Contact → Why → Karma → ATP → Society) is well-sequenced and the concept-first-acronym-second pattern mostly works.

## Unanswered Questions
1. What is **SAL**? It appears on Why-Web4's FAQ with no definition — the one term I left without any handle.
2. *Why* is CI squared (rather than just multiplied)? I accept the "compounds inconsistency" claim but don't understand the design reason.
3. How does a "witness constellation" actually stop a faker mechanically? Stated as a benefit, never shown.
4. Why can't a wealthy bad actor simply buy more hardware / more energy to overpower the budget? The economic-friction claim is asserted but the wealth-resistance isn't demonstrated.
5. What happens to people who don't own devices with security chips (TPM/secure enclave)? The hardware-binding seems to assume modern hardware.

## Honest Assessment
This was, honestly, a *good* visit — the site has clearly been polished hard. The single biggest strength is the **concept-first, acronym-second** discipline: nearly every term gets a plain-English gloss the first time I meet it, and the TL;DR even promises that explicitly. The journey ordering is thoughtful: the TL;DR builds a skeleton, First Contact makes it pokeable, Why-Web4 sells the problem, and the concept pages (ATP especially) fill the gaps the pitch left open. By the end I could explain Web4 to someone else — that's rare for a research project this dense.

The friction that remains is almost entirely **acronym foothold gaps at specific read points**, not structural confusion:
- **SAL on Why-Web4** is the one real miss — it's the only acronym that appears with *no* inline definition on its page, breaking the otherwise-consistent gloss discipline. This feels like the most actionable item.
- **Raw-vs-effective on Karma Journey** is the hardest *concept* (not just jargon), and it's clearly been worked on (the new 2-card visual helps) — but it still cost me a second read. I'd treat this as a known, contested item rather than something newly broken.
- A scatter of **secondary acronyms (CI, V3) appear unglossed on ATP Economics**, a small inconsistency against the page's own otherwise-good habits.

The honesty of the FAQ ("research, not production," "untested at scale," "no timeline") is a real asset — it made me trust the *project* more, which is poetically on-theme. The flip side is FAQ volume: it's so large it reads as a reference manual a naive visitor won't finish.

If I were prioritizing one fix: **define SAL inline on Why-Web4.** It's the cleanest, most consistent gap, and the fix is a six-word parenthetical that matches what the page already does for every other acronym.
