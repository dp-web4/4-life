## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00 — 03:00)
- **First impression**: I landed and immediately understood I was looking at something about "trust on the internet". The framing "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" is excellent — it gave me a reason to read on before any acronym appeared.
- The disclaimer "Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain" was *very* welcome. I've been burned by crypto sites that pretend to be neutral education.
- Many entry points offered (TL;DR, structured learning, Karma Journey, etc.). I appreciated the time estimates next to each.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — the lowest-commitment option felt right.
- **Confusion**: None major. The site set expectations well.

### TL;DR Page (03:00 — 05:30)
- Clean, well-paced, four core mechanics explained in plain English first ("Energy-based actions") and the acronyms (ATP, LCT, etc.) appear as parenthetical shorthand.
- Honestly the strongest single page on the site — every concept got a sentence I could remember.
- **Clicked next**: "Why Web4?" because I wanted to know *why I should care* before learning more.

### Why Web4 (05:30 — 12:30)
- Started strong. "Problems You Already Know" hooked me — I recognized the spam/reputation/identity issues from my own life.
- Four-mechanism overview was good.
- **Then I hit the FAQ wall**: 40+ FAQs is a *lot*. They are organized into categories, which helps. But I didn't read them all. I dipped into "Adoption" and "Privacy" and stopped.
- The line *"If you're skeptical, good"* was disarming and earned credibility.
- **Mild friction**: As a casual visitor, I'd love a "Pick 3 you care about" filter on the FAQs rather than scrolling 40+. The categorization helps but the page still feels long.

### First Contact (12:30 — 14:30)
- I read the page description but didn't actually walk through the 5-act simulation in this session (would have been ~7 more minutes).
- The promise — *watch* Alice's choices play out and see ATP/trust change in real time — sounds compelling. The teach-by-observation framing inverts what I'd expect, in a good way.
- **Mild confusion**: Phrases like "Die & Reborn" still felt cryptic on the description page. I'd have to play through to fully get it.

### Karma Journey (14:30 — 17:30)
- Browsing the description, this looks rich: 8 action choices, multiple "lives", persistent karma.
- The CI explanation here was actually *clearer* than on the dedicated CI page later: "0.9² = 0.81 (small dip), 0.6² = 0.36 (serious inconsistency nearly halves you)". Concrete, mathy in the right way.
- **Confusion point**: "Effective trust = raw × CI²". If I'm not paying close attention, the squaring is a magical fudge factor. *Why* squared and not cubed or linear? The page tells me what but not why.

### LCT Explainer (17:30 — 20:30)
- "Digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip" — perfect plain-English opening.
- The Web2 → Web3 → Web4 evolution framing helped me locate where this fits.
- The TPM / Secure Enclave / FIDO2 detail is well-explained — most pages elsewhere just throw these as acronyms.
- **Friction**: "Linked Context Token" — what does the *Context* part mean? The "linked" part I get (witnesses), but "Context" is unexplained. Is it about the role/role-context that comes up in T3? If so, that connection isn't made explicit on this page.

## Understanding Gained

- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — interactive lab for the Web4 concepts
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored digital identity card with witnessing

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Why those specific numbers?** 0.5 (alive threshold), 0.7 (MRH decay per hop), CI² (squaring), 7x (quality ramp), 5% (transfer fee), 0.02 (T3 update step). The site discloses them but doesn't motivate them. Are they tuned by simulation? Borrowed from biology/economics? I'd love a "Where do the numbers come from?" page.
2. **What does the "Context" in Linked Context Token mean?** Is it the role/situation? If so, the connection to T3's role-weighted trust deserves explicit naming.
3. **Who runs witness infrastructure in practice?** I think I saw a hint that this is your own other devices, but for cross-person witnessing, who?
4. **What is SAGE?** Mentioned on AI Identity. Is it a real research artifact, a hypothetical, an internal codename?
5. **Privacy under CI's "spatial/temporal/relational" tracking:** how does the system check coherence without becoming the very surveillance Web4 claims to escape? I assume the answer is local-first / MRH-bounded, but the CI page didn't make that case.
6. **What does the actual UI look like?** A Day in Web4 has mockups, which is great — but the connection between *those mockups* and *what an integration would feel like in my browser today* is hand-wavy. (I see this is acknowledged across the site as a recurring open question.)

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoughtfully-built educational sites I've encountered for a research project. The patient ladder from problem-statement → analogy → concept → mechanism → interactive demo → honest limitations is rare. Most "Web3-adjacent" sites are vibes-and-tokens. This one is genuinely teaching, with refreshing intellectual honesty ("active research", "untested", "designed but unproven").

What worked best for me as a naive visitor:
- The TL;DR being genuinely brief and self-contained
- The disclaimers ("not a product, not a blockchain") setting honest expectations
- The biology metaphor for ATP — sticky, memorable, accurate
- The credit-card analogy for CI
- Plain-English-first naming ("Trust Neighborhood" before "MRH"; this is the right direction)
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page existing at all
- The floating glossary button — discoverable from any page

What still trips me up:
- A small set of pages where the writer forgot the audience (AI Identity with D9/D5/SAGE; AI Trust Limits with parameter counts)
- "Tensor" left undefined despite being a centerpiece concept
- The Compare page's name doesn't match its content
- The unmotivated constants (0.5, 0.7, CI², 7x, 5%) make me wonder if they were chosen for narrative reasons rather than empirical ones — even one paragraph addressing this on a "design choices" page would close the loop

If I came back tomorrow, I'd start with How It Works first, then the simulators, and skip Why Web4's FAQ wall. The site rewards a specific reading order it doesn't quite teach you to follow. Maybe the Learning Journey is meant to do that — I should try it explicitly next time.

Overall: 8.5/10 as an educational experience. Some friction, but high signal, honest, and genuinely thoughtful about what it's trying to teach.
