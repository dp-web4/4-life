## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clear and confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the one-line gloss — *"Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor"* — immediately told me what this is AND what it is not. The "no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" disclaimer pre-empted my first two skeptical assumptions. Nice.
- The "5 ideas, in plain English" (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) gave me a mental table of contents before any jargon.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — exactly the on-ramp I wanted.
- Confusion points: None at this stage. Multiple entry points ("What it looks like · 5 min", "Frustrations it would fix · 3 min") are welcoming, though slightly many choices at once.

### TL;DR (02:30)
- The Problem → The Idea → The Result → Where is this now? is a great arc. "The internet has no memory... Your 10-year reputation on Twitter means nothing on Reddit" landed instantly — I felt the problem before being sold the solution.
- The four ideas were digestible. "Creating a fake identity means buying new hardware — expensive and slow" is the single most memorable line on the whole site.
- Clicked: "First Contact" to see it in motion.

### First Contact (05:00)
- The Alice walkthrough is the best teaching device here — watching Energy (ATP) and Trust move as she posts, stumbles (spam), recovers, dies, and is reborn made the abstractions concrete.
- Confusion: the acronyms (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) all appear before they're fully defined. The page is honest about this and offers a glossary, but as a newcomer I had to *accept* the jargon and trust I'd learn it later. That's a reasonable bet, but it's still a small leap of faith on the first interactive page.

### Why Web4 (11:00)
- The strongest page on the site for a skeptic. The five failure modes (free spam, siloed reputation, unlimited fresh starts, AI deception, platform lock-in) are sharply argued, and "previous solutions treat symptoms, not causes" is persuasive.
- I appreciated the honesty: clearly marks what's validated (424 attack vectors, simulations) vs. hypothetical (real-world economics, deployment). That candor *increased* my trust in the project.
- The FAQ is enormous (12 "Most Asked" + ~60 "Going Deeper"). Reassuring that my likely questions are anticipated, but it's a lot — I didn't read most of it.
- Lingering skeptic questions (acknowledged by the page, not hidden): the bootstrap/chicken-egg problem ("partner with a university" just relocates it), and the permanence-vs-privacy tension. These are flagged as open, which is fair.

### Karma Journey (16:00)
- Fun and interactive — play multiple "lives," karma carries forward. Starting stats shown clearly (Raw Trust 0.500, ATP 100, CI 0.85).
- **This is where I got genuinely confused.** The page describes two different death outcomes that BOTH seem to be triggered by "trust below 0.5":
  - Recoverable: "your identity and history persist, but your participation rights are paused until your karma tier earns them back" (suspension/reinstatement).
  - Permanent: "collapse below 0.5 — no karma tier earns it back, society rejects rebirth."
  - As a reader, I can't tell **what distinguishes the two**. Same number (0.5), same direction (below), opposite outcome (recover vs. never). When does dropping below 0.5 suspend me vs. kill me forever? The page doesn't say at the point where I'm reading it.
- Minor: "Raw Trust 0.500 (minimum to survive)" vs. "drop *below* 0.5 ends this life" — at exactly 0.500, am I alive or dead? Edge-case nitpick, but I noticed it.

### Aliveness (21:00)
- This page, by contrast, made death *much* clearer. It names three distinct categories cleanly:
  - **Energy death** (ATP→0): "like recovering from bankruptcy" — recoverable, reborn with karma if trust stayed above 0.5.
  - **Trust death** (T3 below 0.5): "like losing a professional license... there is no rebirth for that identity" — permanent.
  - **Device loss**: "isn't death at all — your standing persists, you just re-bind new hardware."
- THIS is the distinction Karma Journey was missing! On Aliveness it's obvious: the recoverable path is when you ran out of *energy* (ATP) but trust held; the permanent path is when *trust* collapsed. Karma Journey blurs these two by tying both to "below 0.5" without telling me which metric.

### LCT Explainer (24:00)
- Best analogy on the site: "Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer."
- Device-loss recovery (multi-device quorum, single-device community vouching 3-7 days, all-devices social recovery) is reassuring — I worried "what if I lose my phone?" and it's directly answered.
- Slight tension noted: the page uses "permanent" for trust-death-from-bad-behavior while stressing losing hardware is *never* permanent. The page does separate "reputation consequences" from "identity itself," but I had to read carefully to keep them apart.

### ATP Economics (26:00)
- ATP = energy budget for actions; clear, good cell-biology analogy (spend ATP → get ADP → recharge from "food"/quality work).
- ADP stayed murky for me. The page tells me ADP is "not a second currency," "background accounting, not a step you take" — yet spends real estate explaining it. As a newcomer my honest reaction was: *if I never touch it, why am I learning about it on the page that's supposed to teach me energy?*
- CI and T3 are referenced before they're introduced on this page.

### Society Simulator (28:00)
- Cool payoff: 12 agents (Cooperator/Defector/Reciprocator/Cautious/Adaptive) self-organizing, presets like "Cooperative Majority" and "Hostile World." The framing question — "Can self-interested agents develop cooperation without central authority?" — is a great hook.
- Honest gap it admits: the sim uses simplified concepts (scalar reputation, not the full Trust Tensor), so what I watch isn't quite what Web4 implements. The page is upfront about this and links First Contact for the jargon.

## Unanswered Questions
1. On Karma Journey: if I drop below 0.5, do I get suspended (and can earn it back) or am I permanently rejected? The page presents both as outcomes of the same trigger.
2. Why does a beginner need to learn about ADP at all if it's purely background accounting?
3. The bootstrap problem — how do the first witnesses get trusted without a central authority? (Why Web4 admits this is unsolved; I still don't have an intuition for it.)

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-built explainer. The on-ramp (Landing → TL;DR → First Contact) is excellent, and the "Why Web4" problem framing is the most persuasive part — it sells the *problem* before the solution and is admirably honest about what's unproven. The site clearly anticipates a skeptic.

My one real stumble was the **death/permanence story on the Karma Journey page**. Read on its own, it contradicts itself: dropping "below 0.5" is described as both a recoverable suspension and a permanent collapse. The frustrating part is that the *Aliveness* page already solves this perfectly by distinguishing energy-death (recoverable) from trust-death (permanent) — Karma Journey just doesn't carry that distinction to where I'm actually making life-or-death choices. If I'd only played Karma Journey and never reached Aliveness, I'd have walked away thinking the rules were inconsistent.

Everything else was minor jargon-ordering friction that the glossary and tooltips mostly absorb. I'd return and I'd recommend it to a curious friend — with the caveat that the death mechanics make more sense if you read the Aliveness page alongside the Karma simulation.
