## Honest Assessment

This is a **substantially improved** explainer site compared to what I'd expect from a research project. The honesty is the strongest asset — /what-could-go-wrong, the deployment-status FAQ, the explicit "no timeline," and the "trust ceiling at 0.90 because nothing is unbreakable" framing all earn credibility that flashy claims would destroy.

The biggest barrier to a real first-time visitor is **acronym fatigue, not concept difficulty**. The concepts (energy budget, hardware-bound ID, multi-dimensional reputation, trust decay over hops, three-criterion aliveness) are genuinely graspable in plain English. The site already proves this — the plain-English passages on /tldr and /aliveness are excellent. But the pages keep re-introducing the same five acronyms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI) without consolidating them anywhere I could keep open as a reference. By page 4, I was Ctrl-F'ing to remind myself what MRH stood for.

A second visit observation: the **best teaching happened where the abstractions composed**, not where they were introduced. /aliveness taught me more about ATP, T3, and CI than the individual explainer pages did, because it showed all three doing work together. The site might do well with more "integrative" pages and fewer "definitional" pages.

The **canonical formula on the landing page (MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP)** is the single highest-friction element I encountered. It's prominent, it telegraphs precision, and two of its terms are undefined anywhere on the site. Either the formula should be demoted (it's spec-flavored on a visitor-facing page) or the missing pieces (MCP, RDF, V3) need first-class treatment.

Would I come back? Yes — there's enough genuine substance to reward a second visit, and I'd actually want to *play* the interactive simulations now that I have the vocabulary. Would I recommend it? Carefully. To someone with patience and a tolerance for jargon, absolutely. To my partner who'd close the tab when she hit "Markov Relevancy Horizon" — probably not.

The thing I want most that the site doesn't yet have: **a single page that shows what Web4 looks like to a user, not to a system designer.** Not the mechanism — the experience. A mocked-up phone screen where Alice posts, gets confirmed, sees her T3 tick up, gets a message from someone two hops away. The mechanics are well-explained; the lived experience is implied.
