## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] **What Web4 is** (at a high level) — trust-native internet where actions cost energy, identity is hardware-bound, reputation is multi-dimensional and portable
- [x] **What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate** — interactive simulations of the Web4 mechanics so people can see them work
- [x] **What LCT means** — Linked Context Token; hardware-bound, witnessed identity in your device's security chip
- [x] **What ATP/ADP means** — Allocation Transfer/Discharge Packets; energy budget you spend to act, refilled by recipient confirmation
- [x] **What Trust Tensors are** — Three-dimensional, role-scoped trust scores (Talent, Training, Temperament)
- [x] **How agents "live" and "die"** — Alive when ATP > 0, Trust > 0.5, and CI coherent; die when any criterion fails; "rebirth" carries forward partial credit
- [x] **Why any of this matters** — The internet has no memory; bad actors get unlimited fresh starts; Web4 makes reputation durable and behavior expensive

- [ ] What MCP and RDF mean in the Web4 formula (never defined on my path)
- [ ] What V3 (Value Tensor) actually does — only saw it in the glossary, never on a journey page
- [ ] How CI (Coherence Index) is actually computed — invoked many times, never broken down
- [ ] What happens at the UI level when MRH excludes someone — math clear, UX opaque
- [ ] Whether trust scores are visible to other users or only to the protocol

## Unanswered Questions

1. **What are MCP and RDF?** If they're foundational enough to anchor the equation on the landing page, why aren't they explained?
2. **What does the "value backs ATP" mechanism actually look like under the hood?** Who decides what counts as confirmation? Can confirmers be gamed?
3. **If I "die" with trust < 0.5, is that *truly* permanent — or can the network forget me eventually?** GDPR right-to-be-forgotten was flagged as "genuine tension, unresolved."
4. **How portable is reputation across societies?** If I'm trusted in a developer community and want to join a parents' forum, do I bring the data-analyst T3 with me? Does it transfer? Discount? Reset?
5. **What's the actual user interface for any of this?** Every page describes mechanism; no page shows a screenshot of an app that uses Web4. Is there a reference client?
6. **Who arbitrates witness capture / collusion in the wild?** The /what-could-go-wrong page flagged this as unsolved. Is the answer "the federation will figure it out" or is there a concrete plan?
7. **The "compounds across lives" line** — does this mean partial T3 carries over, or a memory of past death is encoded in the lineage, or what?

## Honest Assessment

This is a **substantially improved** explainer site compared to what I'd expect from a research project. The honesty is the strongest asset — /what-could-go-wrong, the deployment-status FAQ, the explicit "no timeline," and the "trust ceiling at 0.90 because nothing is unbreakable" framing all earn credibility that flashy claims would destroy.

The biggest barrier to a real first-time visitor is **acronym fatigue, not concept difficulty**. The concepts (energy budget, hardware-bound ID, multi-dimensional reputation, trust decay over hops, three-criterion aliveness) are genuinely graspable in plain English. The site already proves this — the plain-English passages on /tldr and /aliveness are excellent. But the pages keep re-introducing the same five acronyms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI) without consolidating them anywhere I could keep open as a reference. By page 4, I was Ctrl-F'ing to remind myself what MRH stood for.

A second visit observation: the **best teaching happened where the abstractions composed**, not where they were introduced. /aliveness taught me more about ATP, T3, and CI than the individual explainer pages did, because it showed all three doing work together. The site might do well with more "integrative" pages and fewer "definitional" pages.

The **canonical formula on the landing page (MCP + RDF + LCT + T3/V3*MRH + ATP/ADP)** is the single highest-friction element I encountered. It's prominent, it telegraphs precision, and two of its terms are undefined anywhere on the site. Either the formula should be demoted (it's spec-flavored on a visitor-facing page) or the missing pieces (MCP, RDF, V3) need first-class treatment.

Would I come back? Yes — there's enough genuine substance to reward a second visit, and I'd actually want to *play* the interactive simulations now that I have the vocabulary. Would I recommend it? Carefully. To someone with patience and a tolerance for jargon, absolutely. To my partner who'd close the tab when she hit "Markov Relevancy Horizon" — probably not.

The thing I want most that the site doesn't yet have: **a single page that shows what Web4 looks like to a user, not to a system designer.** Not the mechanism — the experience. A mocked-up phone screen where Alice posts, gets confirmed, sees her T3 tick up, gets a message from someone two hops away. The mechanics are well-explained; the lived experience is implied.
