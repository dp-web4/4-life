## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clear and confident. The headline — *"4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations"* — and the subtitle *"Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor"* told me almost everything I needed to orient myself in two sentences. I appreciated being told up front what this is **not** (no blockchain, not a Web3 successor) — that's exactly the suspicion I walked in with.
- The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" section grounded me before any jargon: spam is free, reputations are siloed, bad actors get fresh starts, AI deception outpaces verification. I nodded along; these are real frustrations I recognize.
- Five key ideas previewed (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) in plain words, with the acronyms as quiet subtext. Good — I didn't feel acronym-bombed.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" (it was marked recommended, so I trusted it).

### TL;DR (03:00)
- "Plain English first" — and it delivered. The Problem → Idea → Result → "Where is this now?" structure is exactly the shape a skeptical newcomer wants.
- ATP and LCT were defined right there. T3 / MRH / CI were named but deferred to other pages, which felt like a reasonable promise rather than a dodge.
- "Active research with working simulations, not a deployed product" — this answered my biggest unspoken question (is this real?) before I had to ask it.
- Clicked: "First Contact" (the recommended 7-minute interactive).

### First Contact (06:00)
- This is the best on-ramp on the site. Controlling "Alice" through a 5-act arc (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) made the abstractions concrete.
- All four acronyms (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) were defined inline, in a small table, right where I needed them.
- The line *"Real rules, shown by simulation — not a demo of a shipped product"* completely resolved the "is this real or pretend?" tension. I knew exactly what I was looking at.
- No confusion here. This is where the concepts clicked.

### LCT Explainer (12:00)
- The opening analogy — *"your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated"* — landed immediately.
- The "Linked" (tamper-evident chaining) vs "Context" (role/device) breakdown was clear. The "keys generated inside the silicon, can't leave it" point was repeated enough to stick.
- The interactive witness slider (1 device → 0.55 trust, more devices → higher) was a nice tangible demo, and the "what about my *first* device?" edge case was handled.
- Minor friction: the trust ceiling numbers (TPM 0.90, SE 0.85, FIDO2 0.75, Software 0.50) appear in the widget *before* the section that explains the 0–1 scale (0 = none, 0.5 = alive/dead line, 1.0 = max). I saw decimals before I had the ruler — but the gap was small and the scale showed up soon enough.

### Why Web4 (18:00)
- Exceptional problem-first framing. Five concrete problems, each with a root cause, then "Why Previous Solutions Failed" (moderation, CAPTCHAs, blockchain, social login), *then* the proposal. This is the right order.
- Every acronym is defined in plain English before the acronym appears. Honest "this is research, here are the limitations" caveats throughout — I trusted the page more because of them.
- The one drawback: it's **very** dense (feels like 15,000+ words, ~80-question FAQ). It's clearly meant as reference, not a read-through, and there are shortcuts at the top — but if I'd landed here first instead of via the TL;DR, I might have bounced.

### Society Simulator (24:00)
- The flagship interactive: 12 agents, strategies (Cooperator, Defector, Reciprocator, Cautious, Adaptive), scenarios (Cooperative Majority, Hostile World, etc.). "Run Society" or "Play as Agent."
- Good beginner pointer: *"Never seen these Web4 terms before?"* → First Contact.
- The honesty box ("What's real, what's simulated" — uses scalar reputation, not the full T3 tensor; no MRH/V3) is admirable but, landing cold, it briefly made me unsure what I was actually watching vs. what "real Web4" would do.
- Most terms (MRH, V3, T3) live behind an accordion rather than inline. For me that was fine because I'd already done First Contact; a cold-lander might want one line of grounding closer to the Run button.

### ATP Economics (29:00)
- ATP as "energy budget" is defined consistently and well. The 5% transfer-fee example (105 spent to send 100, 5 to a community pool) was crisp and the rationale (kill collusion farming) made sense.
- **This was my one real stumble.** Two things tripped me:
  1. The prominent **"60-second version"** box has 4 great bullets — but **never mentions ADP**. I felt like I'd learned the whole loop. Then later: *"Every time you spend ATP, you get an ADP (Allocation Discharge Packet) — a receipt..."* and I had to backtrack and re-fit ADP into the cycle I thought I'd just mastered. The summary that's pitched as the keepable version is missing half of the spend→receipt→recharge loop.
  2. A sentence reads: *"You'll occasionally see it called an 'attention' or 'usage' budget — same idea... these are not alternate expansions of the acronym."* But **I never saw those terms anywhere on the site.** So the sentence pre-emptively warned me about a confusion I didn't have, and for a moment made me wonder if ATP secretly means something other than "energy budget." It reads like a guard against a ghost.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (33:00)
- "Web4 doesn't reduce trust to a single number" → Talent / Training / Temperament, always role-contextual (Alice as analyst vs. manager vs. mechanic). Clean.
- Trust numbers are anchored as probabilities (0.5 = newcomer, 0.7 ≈ 70% cooperative, 0.9 = exceptional). I liked that.
- V3 (Value Tensor) is introduced gently: *"T3 measures who you are; V3 measures what you produce,"* with an explicit *"you don't need it to follow this page"* and a "Continue to V3 →" at the end. No abrupt jump — this felt well-managed.

### Coherence Index / CI (36:00)
- The credit-check analogy — *"not just 'who are you?' but 'does your behavior make physical sense?'"* — worked. Two concrete scenarios (Maria's stolen phone → CI 0.55; bot farm → CI 0.41) made it tangible.
- Small friction: the page uses a new account's starting CI of ~0.85 before it explains *why* a newcomer starts there. I met the number before the reasoning.
- The geometric mean (⁴√product) and 1/CI² cost multiplier appear without a plain-language "why this formula," but they're optional-feeling.

### Karma Journey (39:00)
- Interactive life-sim across rebirths. Three stats: Trust (raw = (Talent+Training+Temperament)/3, survive at ≥0.5), ATP, CI. Key formula **Effective trust = raw × CI²**, with the "why squared?" rationale shown right next to it. Good — the interpretation was *not* buried this time.
- Trust/ATP/CI all link to their explainers.
- The raw-vs-effective dual system (survival depends on raw, karma tier on effective) needed a careful re-read, but it's a genuinely two-part idea, not a presentation bug.

### Aliveness (42:00)
- "Alive" = ATP > 0 **and** T3 > 0.5 **and** CI coherent. The "two deaths" contrast is excellent: Energy Death = recoverable (*"like recovering from bankruptcy"*), Trust Death = permanent (*"like losing a professional license"*), shown side-by-side. Stakes were clear.
- Confusion: too many metaphors stacked for one idea — professional license, bankruptcy, passport, chemistry "synthons," biological homeostasis. By the third or fourth I was tracking analogies instead of the mechanism.

### MRH Explorer (44:00)
- Good 3-hop examples (direct reply ~0.85, friend-of-colleague ~0.65, 4-hops-away below noise floor). The privacy framing (limits spam blast radius) made the "why" obvious.
- The name "Markov Relevancy Horizon" is intimidating and leads before the plain "trust neighborhood" concept. The glossary actually does this better (leads with "Trust Neighborhood"). The "Markov property" is named but never explained.

### Day in Web4 (45:00)
- 10 concrete scenarios with mockups (e.g., posting to "Neighborhood Help" for a plumber, −3 ATP, trust 0.50 as a new member). Trust numbers are anchored to 0–1 reasonably early.
- Friction: the verb tense wobbles between conditional (*"would your day look like"*) and lived present (*"You pick up your phone... your chip quietly proves"*). It left me unsure whether I'm imagining a future or walking through something that exists.

### Glossary (46:00)
- ~40–50 entries in five categories (Core, Advanced, Research, Lifecycle, Governance), with "Essential only" filtering and jump anchors. Dual definitions (plain-English + canonical). The plain-English glosses (ATP, LCT, MRH, T3) are some of the clearest writing on the whole site.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab/simulation for exploring Web4
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound, witnessed proof of presence (your device as un-fakeable ID)
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — ATP = energy budget you spend to act; ADP = the receipt you get back. *(ATP was crystal clear; ADP I had to assemble myself because the summary box skipped it.)*
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3: Talent/Training/Temperament, scored per role
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive = energy + coherent agency + continuity; two deaths (energy = recoverable, trust = permanent)
- [x] Why any of this matters — the problem-first framing (spam, siloed reputation, ban evasion, AI deception) made the motivation unmistakable

## Unanswered Questions
1. **When does my energy actually come back?** The ATP page says recharge is "continuous, over a rolling window, hours not seconds" — but I still can't picture *when* I'd see ATP return or how I'd know it happened.
2. **Who are the "others" that confirm my value to recharge me?** In the simulator it's other agents; in real life, is it the people I helped? Automatic? A community? The validating party stayed fuzzy.
3. **Why do "attention" and "usage" budget even come up?** The ATP page says ATP is *not* those, but never says where those words come from or why anyone would call it that — which is the only reason I now wonder about it.
4. **How far is this from a real product, and what would it actually look like?** Day-in-Web4's mockups help, but they're clearly hypothetical. I'd love a clearer sense of "here's what exists today vs. what's still on paper."

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-built educational site, and I say that as someone who walked in skeptical (the "Web4" name primes you to expect crypto hype). The site disarms that immediately — "no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" in the subtitle, "this is research, not a product" repeated calmly throughout. The plain-English-first discipline is real: every acronym I met was defined before or as it appeared, and the analogies (phone-as-driver's-license, credit-check, professional-license-vs-bankruptcy) mostly land.

The journey TL;DR → First Contact is the strongest path; First Contact is where the abstractions became intuitive. Why-Web4's problem-first structure is excellent, if dense.

My only real stumble was the **ATP Economics page**: the "60-second version" box — the very thing pitched as the takeaway — silently omits ADP, so I learned the recharge loop wrong and had to correct it later. And a defensive sentence about "attention/usage budget" introduced a doubt I didn't arrive with. Everything else was minor polish — a tense wobble, a metaphor pile-up, a number-before-its-ruler here and there.

Net: I understood Web4 solidly, I'd come back to dig into the simulators, and I'd recommend it to a curious friend — with the note to start at the TL;DR, not Why-Web4. The single highest-leverage fix I'd point to: **put ADP in the 60-second box** so the one summary people actually keep is also the one that's complete.
