## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident, honest. "Trust, built into the internet." and "A proposed open standard for agentic AI governance." I immediately knew this was about AI agents and trust, not a product pitch.
- The problem hook landed: "Today we cannot prove what an agent did, on whose authority, or by what rules." That's a real thing I can feel.
- The page tells me exactly where to start: "New to Web4? Read in this order: the 2-minute intro, Why Web4, then First Contact." I appreciated being given a path instead of a wall of nav. Two clear buttons: "Web4 in 2 minutes" and "See how it fits together."
- Clicked: Web4 in 2 minutes.

### TL;DR / "Web4 in 2 minutes" (03:00)
- Good, tight framing: "Trust is earned through witnessed behavior, not granted from above," contrasted against Web2 (platforms declare reputation) and Web3 (key possession = ownership). That contrast clicked for me.
- The honesty about maturity (core stable, hub pilot-ready, hestia runs end-to-end, hardbound usable but hardware binding unvalidated) built trust.
- **Friction**: It fires five acronyms at me in one burst — LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP/ADP — as a bulleted list. For a "2-minute" intro that's a lot of new vocabulary to hold, even though each promises its own page. It felt like a menu of homework rather than an intro.

### Why Web4 (07:00)
- Strongest page of the session. "The Internet Has a Trust Problem — You already know this. You feel it every day." Then five concrete pains (spam has no cost, reputation trapped in silos, unlimited fresh starts, AI makes it worse, platforms control everything).
- It earns the solution by first explaining why moderation armies, CAPTCHAs, blockchain, and social login each failed. That "why not just do X?" framing disarmed my skepticism before it hardened.
- Acronyms here are introduced plain-English-name-first (ATP = Allocation Transfer Packets = energy budget, etc.), which was much gentler than the TL;DR list.
- The "Honest Questions" FAQ (~60 Qs, incl. "Is this deployed or theoretical?", "Isn't this just a social credit system?") felt genuinely honest, not defensive.

### First Contact (13:00)
- The Alice 5-act interactive story (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) is a great idea — story-first, "each concept explained the moment Alice runs into it." As a naive visitor this is exactly how I want to learn: consequences, not definitions.
- Nice touch: acronym quick-index link right under the title acknowledging that acronyms confuse newcomers.

### Onramp (17:00)
- The "one shape at three scales" framing is clear: core standard = substrate; hestia (personal) / hub (community) / hardbound (enterprise) = three ways to run it. "They aren't four separate products... They are one shape at three scales." That sentence did a lot of work.
- The page warns up front that it assumes vocabulary and points new readers to the TL;DR — fair.
- **Friction**: A new acronym pair appears here that I hadn't seen anywhere before — **R6/R7 (action and consequence)** — listed alongside LCT and T3/V3*MRH as if I should already know it. It's the only primitive introduced with no plain-language unpacking on the page. (It IS in the glossary, so recoverable, but it's the odd one out.)

### Running Now (22:00)
- Refreshingly honest: "Web4 is R&D, not a shipped product. Nothing on this page is a fielded production system." The three badges (Spec / Reference / Running) are a great mental model, and only hestia is marked truly Running.
- **Friction**: **MCP** appears here as a capability with no expansion and no glossary entry (I checked). See friction table — this is my top actionable item.
- For a non-technical reader this page is the densest (vault, policy engine, delegation, plugin SDK, TPM 2.0, hash-chained ledgers). It says so itself and redirects newcomers to the TL;DR, which is the right call.

### LCT Explainer (26:00)
- Best opening analogy of the site: "Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer." Instantly grounded.
- TPM/Secure Enclave/FIDO2 are handled well via a collapsible "What are these?" with reassurance ("you don't need to memorize these — they're chips already in devices you own").
- **Friction**: "every trust score on this page runs on a 0–1 scale, where **0.5 is the survival line**" is dropped in without motivating *why* 0.5. (See cross-page note below — 0.5 keeps reappearing with different meanings.)
- The lineage string `lct:web4:0x4c8f…a3f2#alice.assistant1` jumps to raw technical format before I have any intuition for what a "lineage" is.

### ATP Economics (30:00)
- Great plain-language open: "ATP is your energy budget — a per-agent allowance you spend and earn back," with the cell biology metaphor (spend ATP → get ADP receipt → recharge from quality work). The "not currency, no market, no speculation, can't buy or sell" line is now stated firmly and repeatedly — I was never confused about whether this is money. Good.
- Worked examples (Sam 35% quality nets −44; Hannah 85% nets −8) made the quality ramp concrete.
- Minor lingering tension: "an active contributor's balance grows well past the grant they started with" vs. "not money you can hoard." It resolves (recharge is capped at cost; profit only from commissioned work) but the resolution comes later than the tension.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (33:00)
- "We use the word tensor because trust here has multiple dimensions instead of just one... one number couldn't tell a brilliant-but-erratic surgeon from a kind doctor with shaky hands." Clear, memorable.
- Talent / Training / Temperament each defined with a role example. Good.
- **Friction**: "**0.5 = newcomer baseline**" here — but the LCT page called 0.5 "the survival line." So a newcomer starts *exactly on the death line*? Maybe that's intentional (prove yourself or die), but nothing says so, and the two pages use the same number for two different-sounding jobs without connecting them.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust earned through witnessed behavior, for humans and AI agents
- [x] What the onramp is (the core standard, the hub, hestia, hardbound) and how they fit together — one shape at three scales on a shared substrate

## Unanswered Questions
1. What is **MCP**? It's named as part of what Web4 is built on, but never defined for me.
2. Why is **0.5** the magic number for both "newcomer" and "survival"? Do all newcomers really start one bad day from death?
3. What exactly is **R6/R7**? Saw it once on the Onramp, never explained in the flow.
4. When hestia is "Running on 4 machines" — what does it actually *do* for me day-to-day? I trust it's real, but I couldn't picture the user experience.

## Honest Assessment
This is the smoothest browse I can imagine having as a genuine newcomer. The site *knows* it's teaching something hard and scaffolds accordingly: it hands you a reading order on the landing page, motivates the problem before the solution on Why Web4, and teaches through a story (Alice) before formalisms. The honesty about maturity ("Web4 is R&D, not a shipped product") is disarming in the best way — it made me trust everything else more.

The friction that remains is almost entirely **vocabulary timing**: a few acronyms still arrive before their plain-language handhold (MCP has no handhold at all), and the number 0.5 quietly carries two different meanings across two pages. None of this blocked comprehension — I finished with a genuinely good mental model — but each is a small trip on an otherwise well-paved path. The single most fixable one is **MCP**: it's cited as part of what Web4 fundamentally *is*, yet it's the one acronym with no glossary entry to fall back on.
