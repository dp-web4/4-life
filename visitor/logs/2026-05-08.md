## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00 — 04:00)
- **First impression**: A strong tagline ("trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default") and a clearly-named problem ("The Internet Has a Trust Problem"). I instantly nodded — yes, spam, fake reviews, AI bots, I feel this.
- **Immediate friction**: A wall of acronyms within the first scroll: LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI. Each has a one-line plain-English gloss, which helps, but I now have to hold five new abbreviations in working memory before I've read anything substantive.
- **What worked**: The "what is this, really?" disclaimer that says "Right now, you can't install it" was unexpectedly grounding. It told me what I was looking at (a research lab + simulator) and freed me from wondering "where's the download button?"
- **What confused**: Five tiers of "adoption strategy" before I knew what the thing IS. Why am I being shown a roadmap to a thing I don't yet understand? Felt like reading the marketing plan for a drug whose mechanism is unexplained.
- **Clicked**: "Web4 in 2 minutes" (TL;DR) — I always click the shortest path first.

### /tldr (04:00 — 08:00)
- Promised "Plain English first" and mostly delivered.
- ATP = "Allocation Transfer Packets" — the biological pun (cells use ATP) is cute once explained, but the expansion itself is opaque. Why "Allocation"? Why "Transfer"? It feels like a name reverse-engineered to fit the acronym, not a name that teaches me what it does.
- ADP = "Allocation Discharge Packet" (the receipt). Same critique — "Discharge" is a strange word to describe a record of a past action.
- LCT = "Linked Context Token" — slightly better; I can guess "linked" means tied to my devices. But "Context" is still nebulous.
- The "Make it Personal" CTA at the bottom is enticing — "see how Web4 would affect *you*." I'd click it on a longer session.
- Clicked: "Why Web4?" — I want the WHY before more mechanics.

### /why-web4 (08:00 — 14:00)
- **This is the strongest single page I read**. The five problem statements (spam wins, reputation in silos, fresh starts free, AI deception, centralized control) are crisp and resonant.
- "Why previous solutions failed" was a smart pre-emption: it answered my unspoken "but didn't blockchain solve this?" before I could ask.
- The four mechanisms reintroduced: ATP (energy), LCT (hardware ID), T3 (multi-dim trust), MRH (neighborhood).
- **New friction**: "T3 = three trust dimensions: **skills**, **training**, and **behavior**" on this page. Other pages call them **Talent**, **Training**, **Temperament**. Same letters, different words. Which is correct?
- The 40+ FAQ section is reassuring (someone thought about edge cases) but daunting (am I expected to read 40 questions?).
- Clicked: "First Contact" — narrative beats abstraction, and "Alice" sounded approachable.

### /atp-economics (28:00 — 36:00)
- This was the **densest single page I read** — and probably the most important. It actually answered my "is this money?" question:
  - "It's a usage budget, not a tradeable asset. No market, no price, no speculation."
  - But you can transfer ATP, with a 5% fee.
  - It's bounded by what your community minted.
- The Sam vs. Hannah worked example (same task, 35% vs. 85% quality, ~6 vs. ~42 ATP earned back) finally made the system click for me.
- The "who judges quality?" answer is non-trivial. Quality is derived from speed-of-confirmation × diversity-of-confirmers × confirmer-trust. **This is essentially: popularity, weighted by who's popular.** The page asserts "Truth and rigor are weighted higher than popularity — 70% vs 30%" — but never explains *how* that distinction is measured separately. If quality = reactions, then it's reactions. Calling it "truth" is rhetorical.
- **New acronym arrives**: V3 (Value Tensor) — "your output-quality reputation half." I now have ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI, plus DID, TPM, FIDO2 referenced. Tracker overload.
- The 5% transfer fee → "community redistribution pool" — who controls that pool? The page doesn't say.

## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — yes, with reservations. A trust-native internet using hardware-bound identity, energy budgets, and multi-dimensional reputation.
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — yes. It's the simulator/lab where the rules of Web4 can be observed in action.
- [x] What LCT means — yes. Hardware-bound, multi-witness identity living in your device's security chip.
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — yes. ATP is a non-tradeable usage budget; ADP is a per-action receipt.
- [ ] What Trust Tensors are — partially. I know they're 3D and role-specific, but I don't know how the components are *measured*.
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — yes, after /aliveness. A "professional license" model: lose your energy or lose your trust → suspended; trust collapse → permanent.
- [x] Why any of this matters — yes. The problem framing on /why-web4 was the strongest single argument on the site.

## Unanswered Questions

1. **How does the system know my "Talent" or "Training" in the first place?** If I'm new, all my dimensions start at 0.5. What pulls Talent up vs. Temperament? The mechanism is unclear.
2. **What separates "truth-weighted" reactions from "popularity-weighted" reactions?** /atp-economics asserts 70/30 but never shows the computation.
3. **Who runs the "community redistribution pool" that the 5% transfer fee feeds into?**
4. **What if I'm unjustly attacked and my T3 drops below 0.5?** The page says permanent death, no rebirth. The appeals mechanism is admitted to be untested. That's a one-way door.
5. **What does "coherence across spatial dimension" mean?** If I move cities, does my Coherence Index drop? The geometric-mean construction means "any one weak dimension tanks everything" — what makes spatial inconsistency suspect?
6. **Is ATP energy or currency?** /atp-economics says one thing, /day-in-web4 says another.
7. **MRH expansion**: I saw it called both "Trust Neighborhood" and "Markov Relevancy Horizon" on different pages. Which is canonical?
8. **What happens to trust scores during outages or extended offline periods?** /what-could-go-wrong mentions it but the practical answer wasn't clear — Temperament decays after 30 days, but how is honest absence distinguished from running away?
9. **For users without biometric-capable devices (disability, region, shared device), the structural 0.50 ceiling**: is that the *current* design or a known issue being worked on? /what-could-go-wrong frames it as "unsolved" but doesn't say whether mitigation is being prioritized.
10. **If "each society mints a fixed pool" of ATP, how do new societies form? Does someone have to mint the first ATP, and if so, with what authority?**

## Honest Assessment

This is a serious research project with genuinely thoughtful design and unusually honest self-critique. The /what-could-go-wrong page alone increased my respect for the team more than the marketing pages did.

But as a naive first-time visitor, the experience is **uphill the entire way**. The site asks me to learn ~10 new acronyms, hold five interlocking concepts in working memory (ATP / LCT / T3 / CI / MRH), accept new dramatic vocabulary ("death," "rebirth," "karma," "aliveness," "synthon"), and navigate inconsistencies between pages (T3 dimensions, MRH expansions, "is ATP money or not"). Each individual page is well-crafted; the cumulative load is the problem.

**The strongest pages**: /why-web4 (problem framing), /lct-explainer (driver's license analogy), /atp-economics (Sam vs. Hannah worked example), /what-could-go-wrong (honest critique), /day-in-web4 (concrete UI mockups).

**The weakest pages for static visitors**: /first-contact, /karma-journey, /trust-dilemmas — all gate their actual content behind "Start the walkthrough" buttons. A first-time browser who isn't ready to commit 15 minutes gets only teaser text.

**The single highest-leverage fix I'd suggest**: Lead with mechanisms-as-verbs, not acronyms-as-nouns. "Your phone is your ID. Actions cost energy. Quality earns it back. Trust is role-specific. You only see your neighborhood." Five sentences. Defer ATP/LCT/T3/MRH/CI to deeper pages where they earn their keep. Right now the site treats me to its internal vocabulary in the first scroll, and I spent the next 50 minutes paying down that conceptual debt.

**Would I tell a friend to visit?** If they're already curious about decentralized identity, spam economics, or AI accountability — yes, with the suggestion to start at /why-web4 not the landing. If they're a casual web user wondering "why does my email have so much spam" — probably not, the cognitive cost is too steep. The audience is more "intermediate-curious technologist" than "general public," even though the language tries hard for the latter.

**The honest answer to "would Web4 actually work?"**: I don't know, and the site admirably admits it doesn't either. That's a strength, not a weakness — but it's also why I'd describe the site as a "research lab open to the public" rather than a "product onramp." Calibrate the framing accordingly.
