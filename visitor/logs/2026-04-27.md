## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a trust-native protocol where identity is hardware-bound, actions cost energy, and reputation is multi-dimensional and portable
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — interactive proof-of-concept simulations showing how trust-mediated digital societies might emerge
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token; a hardware-anchored identity that multiple devices witness for each other
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget for actions (ATP) and the receipt left when you spend it (ADP); biological metaphor; not a tradeable currency
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional trust (Talent / Training / Temperament) measured per role, not as a single number
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive when ATP > 0, Trust > 0.5, and CI is coherent; "death" is suspension, not deletion; rebirth requires trust ≥ 0.5
- [x] Why any of this matters — current internet has no economic cost for bad behavior, no portable reputation, no resistance to AI deception; Web4 proposes to make bad behavior economically impossible rather than relying on moderation

## Unanswered Questions

1. **How is "quality" measured automatically?** ATP recharge depends on quality, but the 3-signal system (speed, diversity, trust-weighting) felt abstract. I want a concrete example: here's an action that scored 0.8, here's one that scored 0.2, and here's why.

2. **What does the actual UI look like?** I read about Web4 features (mail with cost-of-contact, marketplaces with trust scores) but never saw a real screenshot of an end-user app. The mockups on "A Day in Web4" help, but the rest of the journey is text.

3. **Is MRH enforcement or just computation?** When the page says "you can't see beyond your neighborhood," is that a hard visibility wall or just trust attenuation? Big UX implication.

4. **What's a synthon?** It appears late on Aliveness but I never got a clean inline definition. Etymologically I can guess "synthetic + on" but that's a guess.

5. **Privacy under CI's "relational" dimension** — how does the system know who's in my "established network"? Is the relationship graph public? Encrypted? Held where?

6. **Bootstrapping problem** — if Web4 needs hardware-bound identity, witnesses, and an existing trust graph to function, how does it get started? Are there transitional bootstraps? (May be in the FAQ but I didn't read all 40+.)

## Honest Assessment

This is a remarkably well-executed educational site for a complex research idea. The patterns I noticed working well:

- **Plain English first, acronyms second** is consistent across pages
- **Problem-before-solution** framing on Why Web4 builds intellectual credibility
- **Concrete worked examples** (Alice as analyst vs. mechanic; Maria's stolen phone in Madrid) anchor abstract concepts
- **Honest acknowledgment** of design choices (0.5 threshold, 0.7 decay) rather than pseudo-scientific justification
- **Multiple entry points** (TL;DR, Why Web4, A Day, First Contact, Karma Journey) suit different visitor archetypes

The strongest pages, in order:
1. **A Day in Web4** — concrete scenarios I can map to my real life
2. **Coherence Index** — geometric mean made accessible via Madrid scenario
3. **Trust Tensor** — Alice's role-specific trust example is unusually persuasive
4. **TL;DR** — does what it says in two minutes
5. **MRH (Trust Neighborhood)** — telephone game analogy is excellent

Where I felt friction:
- **Acronym density on entry-point interactive pages** (Society Simulator most notably). Hover definitions would help significantly.
- **Quality measurement** remains the fuzziest part of ATP for me — and it's the linchpin of the whole "spam-becomes-impossible" claim.
- **Synthons** felt like an undefined import from somewhere else.
- **Privacy implications** of CI's relational dimension and MRH's neighborhood concept aren't deeply addressed; for a privacy-conscious reader this is the obvious next question.

What I'd most want next: an end-to-end story showing one specific scenario (say, "you're a freelance designer in 2030 looking for a client") played out in the actual UI of Web4 — not a simulation, but a mockup of the experience. The site has the components but not the integrated story-as-product-demo.

The site does an excellent job teaching mechanics. It does a good job teaching motivation. The remaining gap is showing me what *daily life* feels like — not as scenarios in a list, but as a coherent narrative with screenshots. That's the gap I'd close next.

Overall: this is one of the most thoughtfully designed educational sites I've encountered for a complex protocol-research project. It respects the reader's time, builds intuition before formalism, and is honest about uncertainty. I'd come back. I'd recommend it to a curious friend.
