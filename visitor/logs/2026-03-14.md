## Unanswered Questions
1. How does the initial ATP allocation work? Everyone starts at 100? Who decides that number?
2. What prevents a wealthy person from buying 50 devices to create a super-trusted identity constellation? The $5M stat for fake accounts assumes equal devices, but what about someone with real money and patience?
3. How does MRH (Markov Relevancy Horizon) actually determine who can see my messages? The "room where strangers need an introduction" analogy is nice but vague on mechanics.
4. If temperament decays on a 30-day half-life, does a trusted contributor who takes a month off suddenly become untrustworthy?
5. What happens in the transition period? If Web4 is opt-in, how does it work when most people are still on Web2? Do early adopters just talk to each other?
6. The karma/rebirth mechanic — is this automatic or does someone have to explicitly "restart"? Who decides when an agent "dies"?
7. Is there a governance mechanism for changing the rules (ATP costs, trust thresholds)? The SAL is mentioned briefly but never explained.

## Honest Assessment

This is a surprisingly well-thought-out project. Going in, I expected either blockchain-bro hype or academic abstraction. Instead I found a clear problem statement, honest limitations, and concrete examples.

**What works well:**
- The problem framing is excellent. Leading with "the internet has a trust problem" is universally relatable.
- The honesty is disarming. Acknowledging GDPR conflicts, equity barriers, and hardware dependencies up front builds real credibility.
- The progressive disclosure works. TL;DR → Why Web4 → Deep dives is a natural path.
- "A Day in Web4" is the most persuasive page. It makes the abstract concrete.
- The interactive tools (Simulator, Playground, Karma Journey) look genuinely engaging.

**What needs work:**
- Acronym overload. ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, VCM, SAL, ADP, CI — that's eight acronyms for a newcomer to absorb. The concepts behind them are clear, but the alphabet soup creates unnecessary cognitive load.
- Terminology inconsistency. ATP is called "energy," "attention budget," and "Allocation Transfer Packets" on different pages. Pick a primary term.
- The Trust Dilemmas page seems broken or at least confusing in its navigation. It promises three dilemmas but I only saw one.
- Some deep-dive pages (LCT Explainer especially) are very long and could benefit from a summary-first structure.
- The 74+ topics count is meant to show depth but actually intimidates. A newcomer doesn't want 74 topics — they want a clear path through the 5-7 most important ones.

**Overall:** I came away genuinely understanding the proposal and finding it intellectually compelling. The site does a good job teaching me rather than selling me. I'd return to try the interactive simulations. Whether I'd recommend it depends on who's asking — a tech-interested friend yes, a casual person probably not yet. The conceptual density is still high even with good progressive disclosure.
