## Unanswered Questions
1. **What would the actual migration path look like for a real platform?** The 5-tier adoption model (Wrapper → Observable → Accountable → Federated → Native) is mentioned but I'd love to see a concrete example — "Here's how Reddit/Discord/Slack could adopt Web4 in stages."
2. **How does cross-society trust transfer actually work in practice?** The MRH page mentions ~65% discount when trust transfers between federated communities, but what does this feel like as a user? If I'm trusted in a coding community and join a cooking community, what's my starting experience?
3. **What would the onboarding UX actually look like?** The Day in Web4 mockups show the steady-state experience beautifully, but I never saw what the first 5 minutes of "signing up" would look like. Setting up LCT, linking devices, choosing a first community — that's the moment of highest friction and it's not shown.
4. **How do you prevent communities from becoming echo chambers?** MRH creates natural boundaries (3-hop limit), but if communities only see their own trust network, doesn't this create filter bubbles? The cross-community bridge agents finding is mentioned but not fleshed out for visitors.

## Honest Assessment

This is genuinely impressive. I went in expecting to be confused by jargon and left with a solid understanding of a complex system. The site has clearly been iterated on — the plain-English-first approach, the progressive disclosure, the multiple entry points at different depths — all of this works.

**What works best:**
- The "Day in Web4" mockups are the single most effective explainer. Abstract concepts become tangible when you see "Web4 Mail: 0 spam" with actual trust scores on messages.
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page builds more trust than any marketing page ever could. Publishing your own failure analysis is the kind of intellectual honesty that makes me take the project seriously.
- The concept sequence (LCT → ATP → T3 → MRH → CI → Aliveness) builds knowledge incrementally. Each concept genuinely builds on the last.
- Interactive elements (Karma Journey, Playground, ATP simulator, Survival Challenge) teach through experimentation rather than lecture.

**What could be better:**
- The sheer volume of content is both a strength and a weakness. 14 pages deep and I know there are 60+ more. For a first visit, this is a lot. A "recommended first visit" path that's explicitly 5-6 pages and 20 minutes would help.
- Some pages are very long (How It Works, Aliveness, MRH). These would benefit from better in-page navigation.
- The MRH technical section (SPARQL/RDF) breaks the accessibility pattern the rest of the site maintains so well.
- I never saw what onboarding would *feel* like. The mockups show the steady state, but the first-time user experience (device setup, first community, first action) would be the most compelling thing to visualize.

**Overall:** This is one of the most thoughtfully constructed educational sites I've seen for a technical concept. The combination of intellectual honesty, progressive disclosure, and interactive demonstration is rare. I'd return and I'd share it.
