## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: "Trust-native internet lab" — clear tagline. The hero immediately disclaims what it ISN'T ("no blockchain, not a Web3 successor"), which I appreciated because "Web4" made me brace for crypto.
- The five foundational ideas (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) in plain language are a good on-ramp. I could hold all five in my head.
- "What This Project Actually Is" (research proposal, ~47k lines of open code, not a product) set honest expectations early.
- Clicked: "Why Web4" from the Start Here path.
- Confusion points: none serious. The five-tier adoption ladder (Wrapper → Native) went by fast, but I figured I'd come back to it.

### Why Web4 (04:30)
- The problem framing is the strongest writing on the site. "Creating accounts is free. Sending messages is free" → root cause: actions have no cost. I followed the whole argument without needing the glossary.
- The "why previous solutions failed" section (moderation, CAPTCHAs, blockchain, OAuth) preempted my objections in the order I would have raised them.
- The four mechanism cards (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) each pair plain English with the acronym — good. The single concrete example (replying to a stranger's forum question, all four mechanisms firing at once) was the moment the pieces clicked together.
- Confusion points: "hestia" and "the hub" are named as "public, AGPL code you can run yourself" with no one-line gloss of what each one IS. I skated past, but they're the only two names on the page I couldn't attach a meaning to.
- The FAQ is huge (75+ questions). Organized well, but I only opened the "Most Asked" nine. As a first-timer I felt I was leaving value on the table — maybe that's fine.
- Clicked: First Contact, as the page recommended.

### First Contact (13:00)
- Alice's five-act story is the right length (~7 min claimed, felt right). Watching trust rise 0.50 → 0.56 on good work, dip to 0.48 on spam, recover to 0.62 — the numbers moving in real time taught more than any paragraph.
- The spam act ("spam costs more than it pays", −25 ATP) lands the core economic point viscerally.
- The death-and-rebirth act with "+12 bonus" karma carried forward set up the Karma Journey well.
- Confusion points: minor — the sim-vs-deployed-code distinction ("real rules, shown by simulation" vs hestia/the hub) again assumes I know what hestia is.
- Clicked: Karma Journey.

### Karma Journey (20:00)
- This is where the training wheels came off. Two trust numbers greeted me: **Raw Trust 0.500** ("survival threshold") and **Effective Trust 0.361** ("determines next life's karma tier"). The side-by-side cards explaining raw-keeps-you-alive-now vs effective-is-what-you-inherit did their job — I got it on the second read.
- The CI surcharge explanation is genuinely good: "a brand-new identity has no pattern to match yet — the surcharge is the system being honest about what it hasn't seen." That one sentence justified the weird 1.38× cost multiplier better than any formula.
- The raw trust breakdown was shown as **Talent 0.3 / Training 0.3 / Temperament 0.4** — I filed those weights away as "the" trust formula. (This comes back to bite later.)
- The two death types (energy death = recoverable "license suspension"; trust collapse = permanent revocation) were clearly distinguished.
- I noted "ATP starter grant from the society's pool" and wondered where the pool's ATP originates. (The ATP page later answered this — anchored to real resources members bring.)
- Clicked: Trust Tensor from the sidebar, to understand the trust number I'd been staring at.

### Trust Tensor (28:00)
- The role-scoping insight ("Alice's trust as a data analyst tells nothing about her trust as a mechanic") is intuitive and well-motivated.
- The measurement mechanics (recipient attestation, base = 0.02 × (quality − 0.5)) and the decay half-lives (Talent 365d / Training 180d / Temperament 30d, "character has to be shown fresh each month; skill stays earned for a year") are the kind of concrete detail I wanted.
- **Confusion point (real one)**: Karma Journey just told me raw trust = Talent 0.3 / Training 0.3 / Temperament 0.4 — fixed weights. This page says *"each role weights dimensions differently"* and its Data Analyst example is Talent 40% / Training 35% / Temperament 25%. So which is it? Are Karma Journey's 0.3/0.3/0.4 one particular role's weights? A default? Neither page acknowledges the other's numbers. I went back and forth twice trying to reconcile them and couldn't. If the journey uses one example weighting and real roles vary, one sentence on either page would fix this.
- Smaller: the dimension scaling factors (Talent ×1.0, Training ×0.8, Temperament ×0.6) appear with no rationale at all.
- The T3-grades-Alice / V3-grades-the-tutorial split made me curious. Clicked: Value Tensor.

### Value Tensor (34:00)
- Clean, focused page. The opening contrast ("Reputation tells you whether to trust a person. It doesn't tell you whether a particular thing they made is any good") earns the page's existence in one sentence.
- Valuation 30% / Veracity 35% / Validity 35%, with truth deliberately outweighing popularity — motivation stated, appreciated.
- The three-different-parties scoring (recipients / witnesses / structural checks) answered my "who stops gaming" question at least at the surface level.
- ATP Earned = T3 × V3 × base rate with a worked example — good.
- Confusion point: the T3 page taught me trust *decays* with half-lives. Does a V3 score decay too, or is a work's score permanent? This page doesn't say either way, and after the T3 page primed me to expect decay, the silence was noticeable.
- Clicked: ATP Economics — energy had appeared on every page so far.

### ATP Economics (38:30)
- Probably the most complete page on the site. The "Settled vs. Evolving" section is a trust-builder — telling me which numbers are canonical and which are simulator parameters is exactly the honesty the landing page promised.
- The quality ramp (below 30% ≈ nothing, above 70% ≈ full, ~7× gap) with the worked 50-ATP example made the economics concrete.
- "Is ATP like Bitcoin?" — no market, no price, no mining — answered the question I walked in with.
- Where ATP originates (100-ATP starter grant anchored to witnessed real resources) answered my Karma Journey question.
- Confusion points: the recharge formula `Δrecharge = confirmer_trust × received_value_fraction` uses a term ("received_value_fraction") defined nowhere I could find. And the 5% transfer fee routes to a "community redistribution pool" whose purpose lives on some other page — a one-clause hint ("funds newcomer grants" or whatever it does) would close the loop here.
- Clicked: Coherence Index — CI² had been modifying every number I'd seen since Karma Journey.

### Coherence Index (43:00)
- The "credit check for behavior" framing plus the four dimensions (spatial / capability / temporal / relational) is clear. Maria's stolen-phone scenario made it real.
- The page owns its calibration choices explicitly ("a calibration choice, not a derived constant", "gentle middle") — consistent with the ATP page's honesty, and it matches exactly what Karma Journey told me about the 0.85 start and the ~1.38× surcharge. No contradictions; the two pages reinforce each other. This was the moment the site felt *coherent* (pun accepted).
- Minor stumble in the simulator: with all four sliders at 1.0, the readout says CI = 1.000 but "Effective Trust: 75%". For a beat I thought perfect coherence was still being penalized — then I saw the caption "Base trust (75%) scaled by consistency" and understood 75% is the hypothetical base T3, passed through unreduced. The readout order (big number first, explanation below) invites that misread.
- Clicked: Society Simulator.

### Society Simulator (47:00)
- Watched "Cooperative Majority" in animated mode. The event feed narration ("Coalition formed", "Defector isolated") is the best part — it turns a graph into a story.
- The page is upfront that this sim uses simplified scalar reputation, no MRH, no witnesses. Honest, though the caveat list itself is jargon-dense — the expandable legend defines six acronyms at once (ATP, T3, LCT, CI, MRH, V3), which is a lot to hold before the simple "spend energy, build reputation" framing appears.
- No strong "do this first" — I happened to pick the right preset because the guide text suggested it, but the page could commit harder to a recommended first run.
- Clicked: Trust Neighborhood — the one landing-page pillar I hadn't read.

### Trust Neighborhood / MRH (49:30)
- "You see your connections, their connections, and their connections — nothing more" — got it immediately. The defense of 0.7/hop specifically (vs 0.5 too harsh, 0.9 too loose) is the kind of parameter-justification the T3 scaling factors lacked.
- The parenthetical that "zero" beyond 3 hops means zero trust *weight*, not invisibility, is an important correction to what I'd assumed from the landing page — and it's easy to miss where it is.
- The page notes the interactive explorer uses a finer 0.85/0.9 decay model reaching similar endpoints — the note exists, which prevented confusion, though I didn't fully understand *why* two models coexist.
- Confusion points: the FAQ mentions "bridge agents" (entities in multiple communities) without defining them; and a federation example says external trust transfers at "~65% weight" — a number that doesn't obviously derive from the 0.7/hop formula and isn't reconciled with it.
- Clicked: Aliveness — the last item on my mental checklist was how death actually works.

### Aliveness (52:00)
- The three-criteria frame (ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, CI coherent — lose any one and you can't participate) ties the whole site together. The professional-license analogy works.
- "One line, two consequences" — crossing below 0.5 restricts immediately and is recoverable; *staying* below is fatal — finally nailed the distinction Karma Journey introduced. The 0.5 threshold is flagged "calibrated, not derived", consistent with the site's other calibration confessions.
- The Survival Challenge game admits it simplifies (immediate death on first crossing vs the real sustained-collapse rule) — good disclosure.
- **Confusion point (the big one)**: the page's own worked example broke its own rules. "Real Simulation Example": Life 1 *"dies at tick 47 with 145 ATP remaining"*, rebirth-eligible with T3 = 0.68. But the page just told me the three ways to die: ATP = 0 (they had 145), trust below 0.5 sustained (theirs was 0.68), coherence collapse (not mentioned in the example). So what killed this agent? No cause of death is stated. I reread the example twice looking for the answer. Either the example is from a simulation with some fourth ending condition (round limit? old age?) that the page never mentions, or the numbers are wrong. Every other page taught me to trust the numbers; this example undermined that on the exact page where the death rules live.
- Smaller: the page lists *three* ways to die but then presents *two* outcomes (energy death vs trust death). I eventually inferred coherence collapse gets folded into the trust path via CI², but I had to infer it — the taxonomy doesn't close cleanly.
- Also noted: birth requires "3+ independent witnesses", and I couldn't find who witnesses the very first entities.

## Honest Assessment
This was a good session — the best sign being that pages *agreed* with each other. Karma Journey promised a ~1.38× newcomer surcharge and the Coherence Index page derived the same number from 1/0.85²; the ATP page answered the "where does the starter grant come from" question I'd formed two pages earlier; the Aliveness page closed the loop on death rules the journey had introduced. When a site cross-checks like that, I start trusting its numbers.

Which is exactly why the two failures stood out. The Aliveness worked example — dying with 145 ATP and 0.68 trust, violating all three death conditions listed inches above it — is the kind of thing that makes a careful reader go back and doubt everything they just learned. And the T3 weights disagreement between Karma Journey and Trust Tensor is the one place where two pages I read back-to-back gave me numbers I could not reconcile no matter how charitably I read them. A site this disciplined about flagging "calibrated, not derived" on its constants should not have an example that follows no stated rule, or two weight sets with no bridge.

The site's signature move — plain English first, acronym second, then an honest "this number is a calibration choice" — works. The problem framing on Why Web4 is genuinely persuasive. The interactive pieces earn their space (the First Contact spam act and the ATP simulator taught the economics faster than the prose). I'd send a curious technical friend here without embarrassment, with the warning that the trust-weights question will nag at them like it's nagging at me.
