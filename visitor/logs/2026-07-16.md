- **Duration**: ~34 minutes

## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: The headline **"Trust, built into the internet."** is genuinely good — short, concrete, and I immediately get the stakes from the subhead: *"AI agents already take real actions: they write code, move money, and make decisions. Today we cannot prove what an agent did, on whose authority, or by what rules."* That's a real problem I can feel.
- But then the page gets *dense fast*. Below the two buttons ("Web4 in 2 minutes", "See how it fits together") there are four big sections: **The onramp**, **The vocabulary**, **Reference: Where this actually stands**, and **Explore**. The onramp has four oddly-named pieces (**Substrate**, **Hub**, **Hestia**, **Hardbound**), and the vocabulary lists six acronyms (LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP/ADP, Karma). As a first-timer I don't know which of these I'm *supposed* to click. I felt a little lost.
- Also: the site is called **4-Life** but everything on the page is about **Web4**. I couldn't tell what "4-Life" *is* vs. what "Web4" is. Is 4-Life the product? A lab? A game? The name is never explained on the landing page.
- Clicked: "Web4 in 2 minutes" (the obvious first move).

### Web4 in 2 Minutes — /tldr (04:00)
- This was the best page for a newcomer. It explains four ideas plainly and — crucially — **defines each acronym on first use**: "ATP — short for Allocation Transfer Packets, a name borrowed from cellular biology," "Linked Context Token (LCT)," etc.
- Small friction: even in the "2 minute" version, it introduces ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, *and* CI. That's five acronyms in two minutes. I understood each while reading it, but I would not be able to recite them afterward.
- Clicked: "First Contact" (it's pitched as the story-based intro, 7 min).

### First Contact — /first-contact (07:30)
- I liked the setup: control "Alice" and watch two numbers — **🔋 Energy Budget (ATP)** starting at 100, and **🤝 Trust Score** starting at a neutral **0.50** (above 0.50 = full access, below = restricted). Concrete and easy to track.
- The five-act arc (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → **Die & Reborn**) is a nice narrative spine.
- **Confusion**: Act 5 is "Die & Reborn" and talks about *karma carrying forward* — but **"karma" and "rebirth" were never defined before this point.** I hit the word "reborn" and thought: reborn as *what*? Why does dying give a "+12 bonus"? The page tells me not to memorize the concepts, which is reassuring, but the death/rebirth idea is emotionally the most striking part of the whole story and it arrives unexplained.
- Clicked: "Why Web4?" (I wanted to know why I should care before playing more).

### Why Web4? — /why-web4 (13:00)
- **Strongest page on the site.** It names the problem in four concrete failures (spam is free, reputation is siloed, bad actors get fresh starts, AI amplifies all of it), then honestly grades why past fixes (moderation, CAPTCHA, Web3, social login) failed. Then it lists five design requirements *before* revealing the solution — so I'm nodding along before I hear the pitch.
- The FAQ is where it earned my trust. It directly answers **"Is this deployed anywhere? Or purely theoretical?"** with "Research prototype... ~47,000 lines of tested code... No live network with real users yet," and **"Isn't this just a social credit system?"** with a clear top-down-vs-bottom-up distinction. Admitting the newcomer-vulnerability tradeoff ("the first ~30 actions are the most fragile period") made me believe the rest.
- **BUT this page sent me to two broken promises** (see below). It links "Society Simulator — Watch trust economies form in real time" and "Karma Journey — Play through a trust lifecycle yourself." Those two links do not deliver what the copy promises.

### How It Works — /how-it-works (19:00)
- Decent overview of the five systems (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI) and the birth→life→death→rebirth lifecycle. The concrete example ("helpful contribution: cost 20 ATP → earn 60 ATP") helped.
- Same acronym-overload complaint — and here I also saw **SAL** and **VCM** flash by with no expansion at all.
- This page also points me at **"Society Simulator"** to "watch systems in motion." (Foreshadowing the problem.)

### LCT Explainer — /lct-explainer (23:00)
- The opening metaphor is excellent: *"your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer."* That single sentence taught me what an LCT is better than any acronym expansion.
- The trust ceilings are clear once you get there (TPM **0.90**, Secure Enclave **0.85**, FIDO2 **0.75**, software-only **0.50**; 0.5 = "survival line").
- Minor wobble: **0.5 is both the "survival line" and Alice's neutral *starting* score.** For a beat I worried "so everyone starts one bad day from death?" The page does resolve it, but the collision made me anxious mid-read.

### Trust Explorer → /trust-tensor-explorer (27:00)
- Clicked "Trust Explorer" from the nav. Landed on **"Trust Tensors: Multi-Dimensional Trust"** with a working interactive simulator (pick surgeon / data analyst / team leader, apply scenarios). This one actually *is* interactive and it's good — the "Alice is 90% trusted as a data analyst but 27% as a mechanic" example finally made "role-specific trust" click.
- Nit: the nav calls it "Trust Explorer" but the page is titled "Trust Tensors." Small label drift, but this one at least delivers a real tool.

### The "Interactive Labs" menu — where it fell apart (30:00)
I went back to the top nav's **"Interactive Labs"** group expecting more hands-on tools like the Trust Tensor one. Instead:
- **"Society Simulator"** (`/society-simulator`) → loads a page titled **"The hub"** about *"a single ~6 MB Rust daemon."* I clicked expecting to "watch trust economies form in real time" (as Why-Web4 promised) and got infrastructure documentation for a Rust program. Total whiplash.
- **"Simulation Sandbox"** (`/simulation-sandbox`) → loads a page titled **"Running Now"** — a tour of live software, not a sandbox I can play in.
- **"Research Hub"** (`/research-hub`) → loads **"Guided Learning Journey — Learn Web4 Progressively"** — a learning roadmap, not a research hub.
- **"Karma Journey"** (`/karma-journey`) → loads **"Karma & Consequences,"** a static explainer, *not* the "play through a trust lifecycle yourself" experience Why-Web4 advertised.
- Only **"Trust Explorer"** delivered an actual interactive tool.

So four of the five "Interactive Labs" links either land on a page with a completely different name/purpose or are explainers rather than the interactive experiences the labels and cross-page copy promise. For a site whose entire subject is *trust*, having the navigation lie to me about where links go is a bad look — it made me wonder what else was stale.

## Honest Assessment
The core teaching pages — /tldr, /why-web4, /first-contact, /lct-explainer, and the Trust Tensor explorer — are genuinely good. Why-Web4 in particular is the most honest "why should I care" document I've read on a project like this; the FAQ admitting "no live network with real users yet" made me trust the rest more, not less. If the site were *only* those pages, I'd rate the experience strongly.

What knocked my rating down was the **navigation**. This felt like a site mid-renovation: the "Interactive Labs" menu is a set of labels that mostly don't match where they go. Clicking "Society Simulator" and landing on documentation for a 6 MB Rust daemon called "The hub" was the moment I lost a little faith — not in the ideas, but in whether the site was finished. And multiple *other* pages (Why-Web4, How It Works) still advertise those interactive experiences in their body copy, so the broken promise gets reinforced. The irony of a trust-focused site where the links don't do what they say is hard to miss.

Fix the label/destination mismatches and give karma one sentence of definition where it first appears, and this goes from "promising but under construction" to "genuinely good."
