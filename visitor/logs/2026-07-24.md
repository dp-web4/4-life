## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: "Trust, built into the internet." is a strong, clean hook. The subheading — "A proposed open standard for agentic AI governance" — is where I first stumbled. "Agentic AI governance" is three abstract words in a row. I *think* I know what each means, but stacked together they read like a conference-panel title, not something that tells a stranger what they're looking at. The body paragraph below ("AI agents already take real actions: they write code, move money, and make decisions…") did the real work of explaining. It should arguably be the subheading.
- I saw a lot of navigation: "Start here," "The Onramp," "Core Concepts," "Going Deeper." Four pieces get named right away: The Core Standard, The Hub, Hestia, Hardbound. Good — but at this moment I have no idea what "Hestia" or "Hardbound" are, and the one-liners ("~6MB binary," "hardware-bound identity system") assume I already care about binaries.
- Clicked: "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (felt like the obviously-intended front door).

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / tldr (03:00)
- This page is genuinely good. "Plain English first, so you can read this cold" — and it delivered. The Web2/Web3/Web4 three-column contrast finally made the core idea click for me: trust from *witnessed behavior* rather than a platform declaring it or a key implying it.
- **BUT** — the second paragraph tripped me hard. It says: *"when a page here says 'the onramp,' it means the four pieces of Web4 you actually run … which 4-Life teaches but is not one of."* I had to read this three times. So this site is *an* onramp (an "educational onramp to Web4," per the first line) but not *the* onramp? There are two different things both called "onramp"? The fact that the page needs a whole disclaimer paragraph to untangle the word is itself the tell that the word is overloaded. As a naive reader, the disclaimer made me *more* confused, not less — it introduced a distinction I didn't know I needed to worry about.
- "Four Pieces" section: each piece has a parenthetical label — (Reference), (Reference), (Running), (Reference). At this point I don't know what "Reference" vs "Running" means. There's no inline key here; I only learned the definitions two pages later on "Running Now." A one-line "(what do these labels mean?)" hint here would have saved me the confusion.
- "How real is it, honestly?" section is excellent and built trust with me. Admitting "This is research, and research takes as long as it takes" made me believe the rest of the page more.

### Why Web4? (09:00)
- "The Internet Has a Trust Problem. You already know this. You feel it every day." — great opener, felt aimed at me.
- The four/five problems (spam is free, reputation is trapped, identity is cheap, AI deception, platforms own you) were concrete and landed well.
- The four mechanisms (ATP energy budgets, LCT hardware identity, T3 trust, MRH neighborhoods) were introduced with plain analogies ("like being in a room where strangers need an introduction"). This is the best single explanation of MRH I saw.
- Saw "SAL (Society-Authority-Law)" mentioned in the governance bit with very little scaffolding — another acronym landing before I have a slot to put it in, but it wasn't heavily leaned on, so mild.
- The FAQ acknowledging real trade-offs (GDPR vs permanent records, hardware = equity barrier) was disarming and honest. Big credibility boost.

### First Contact (15:00)
- This is a guided interactive story about "Alice." Two metrics: Energy (ATP), starts at 100; Trust, starts at 0.50.
- The five acts — Join, Build, Stumble, Recover, **Die & Reborn** — are evocative. But "Die & Reborn" / "reborn with karma" made me flinch as a newcomer. Agents *die*? They're *reborn*? Without having read the ATP page yet, this felt more like a video game than internet infrastructure, and I wasn't sure if "death" was a metaphor or a literal state. It's intriguing but I'd want a one-line reassurance up front that "death" here just means running out of energy budget.
- The 0.50 trust framing: "scores above 0.50 grant full access, while sustained scores below trigger feature restrictions." Okay-ish, but see the survival-line note below — this concept got muddier the more pages I saw it on.

### Running Now (20:00)
- **This is where the (Spec)/(Reference)/(Running) labels finally get defined.** The three-badge key is clear and good. My complaint is purely that I met these labels on the landing page and tldr *first*, unarmed.
- Per-piece status was refreshingly concrete: web4-core v0.3.0, 171 tests, hub is a 6MB Rust binary v0.1.0-alpha.0, hestia "Phase 2," hardbound 3.8MB with 300+ tests, "TPM 2.0 validation incomplete," "federation across societies remains Spec only." I came away actually believing I knew what works today. This page is the honesty backbone of the site.

### How the Pieces Compose / onramp (25:00)
- Good structure: one substrate (core standard) + three scales (hestia personal, hub community, hardbound enterprise).
- **Real friction:** the substrate is described as "LCT (identity), T3/V3*MRH (trust and value in context), ATP/ADP (energy), **and R6/R7**." Every one of those acronyms has a Core Concepts page — *except R6/R7*, which appears here with zero explanation, no link, and no page in the nav that I could find. As a naive reader I hit "R6/R7" and just… stalled. What is it? Why is it grouped with the things I do understand? It reads like an insider abbreviation that escaped into a beginner page.
- The connection details ("sealed member-to-hub channels," "soft identity converting to TPM-bound equivalents") are over my head, but they're clearly marked as the deeper/technical part, so that's fair.

### LCT Explainer (29:00)
- The opening analogy — "Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer" — is the single best sentence on the whole site. Instantly got it.
- Hardware trust ceilings (TPM 0.90, Secure Enclave 0.85, FIDO2 0.75, Software 0.50) were clear as a table. But the implication hit me: if I'm just a browser user, my trust is *capped at 0.50 forever*? That feels like a soft "you can never be trusted" for people without special hardware. The Why-Web4 FAQ did acknowledge the equity issue, but this page states the cap without linking back to that reassurance.
- **The 0.5 "survival line" wording is genuinely confusing.** This page says "0.5 is the neutral midpoint" AND "the survival line (only *staying* below it is fatal)." First Contact said below 0.50 triggers restrictions. So is 0.5 neutral, or is it a survival threshold, or both? And "only staying below it is fatal" is a double-negative that took me two reads. I still am not 100% sure whether dipping below 0.5 briefly is fine and only *sustained* time below is fatal — which I *think* is the point, but the phrasing fights the reader. Also, why is a *survival* concept being defined on the *identity* page at all?

### Trust Tensor / T3 (32:00)
- Clear and well-paced. Three numbers (Talent, Training, Temperament), always role-scoped. The "Alice: 0.85 as data analyst, 0.20 as mechanic" example nailed the role-context idea for me.
- Decay rules were memorable: Talent never decays, Training 180-day half-life, Temperament 30-day half-life — "Character has to be shown fresh each month; raw ability, once demonstrated, stays earned." That sentence is a keeper.
- The update math (`base = 0.02 × (quality − 0.5)`, scaled ×1.0/0.8/0.6) was more detail than I needed but clearly flagged as the mechanics.

## Unanswered Questions
1. What is R6/R7? It's grouped with the primitives I now understand, but nothing explains it.
2. If software-only identity is capped at 0.50 trust, is Web4 effectively closed to people without a TPM/Secure Enclave device? What's the on-ramp for *them*?
3. What does it actually mean for an agent to "die"? Is the identity gone, or just the energy budget? "Reborn with karma" implies the reputation survives — but then what was lost?
4. Is 0.5 a neutral starting point or a danger line? Both framings appear and I couldn't fully reconcile them.
5. As an ordinary human (not a developer, no agents to run), is there anything here for me *today*, or is this entirely for people building agents?

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-made explainer, and noticeably more honest than most "revolutionary protocol" sites — the repeated "here's what's real, here's what isn't" framing earned my trust rather than demanding it. The plain-English tldr and the driver's-license LCT analogy are standout teaching moments; I left understanding the *concepts* well.

Where it still trips a true newcomer is at the seams between pages. Three of my biggest snags — the double-meaning of "onramp," the shifting definition of the 0.5 threshold, and the totally-undefined "R6/R7" — are all cases where a single page reads fine in isolation but the *set* of pages doesn't agree with itself or leaves a dangling term. The R6/R7 gap is the cleanest fix and the most jarring: a beginner page shouldn't drop an unexplained acronym next to a row of ones it carefully explains.

My honest lingering feeling: I understand what Web4 *is* and *why it's clever*, but I'm still fuzzy on what *I*, a normal person reading this cold, am supposed to *do* — especially if I'm a software-only user staring at that 0.50 ceiling. The site taught me the machine; it hasn't quite told me where I fit in it.
