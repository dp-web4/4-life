## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident headline — "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations." Right under it I get a one-line definition I actually appreciated as a newcomer: Web4 is "a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor." That last clause pre-empted my exact first question ("is this another crypto thing?"). Good.
- The page opens with "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" and four felt problems: free accounts → spam, reputation trapped per-platform, bad actors restart freely, AI deception outpacing verification. I nodded along — these are problems I recognize.
- Clicked: The page tells first-timers "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" so I did exactly that.
- Confusion points: The four "principles" (hardware identity, energy budgets, permanent consequences, same rules for humans/AI) are stated as bullet facts before I have any reason to believe them. But the page clearly wants me to go to the TL;DR first, so I didn't dwell.

### TL;DR — "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (03:00)
- Promised "Plain English first," and mostly delivered. Frames everything around "The internet has no memory" — spam resets instantly, reputation is platform-specific. Clear.
- Three mechanisms: energy-based actions (ATP = Allocation Transfer Packets), hardware identity (LCT = "digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip"), portable multi-dimensional reputation. ATP and LCT are spelled out inline — good.
- Confusion points: T3, MRH, and CI get name-dropped but punted to other pages. In a "2 minutes" doc I'd rather they either not appear or get one phrase each, because seeing three undefined acronyms made the "plain English first" promise feel slightly broken.
- Clicked: "First Contact" (the recommended interactive intro).

### First Contact (06:00)
- Reassuring opener: "Haven't read anything else on this site yet? You don't need to." It's self-contained, which is exactly what a lost newcomer wants.
- It's an interactive 5-act story where I "drive Alice's choices — play, pause, scrub, replay." Alice joins, builds quality work, makes spam mistakes, recovers, dies, and karma carries to rebirth.
- I was glad the page is explicit about reality: "Real rules, shown by simulation — not a demo of a shipped product," and Web4 is "active research with nothing to sign up for yet." This was the single biggest thing past versions of "am I being sold something?" — answered cleanly here.
- Concepts taught in context: ATP (energy budget), T3 (0.00–1.00 trust score), LCT, Coherence Index. The contextual teaching of T3 as a 0–1 score actually helped me — this was the FIRST place the 0–1 scale clicked.
- Confusion points: "death & rebirth" and "karma" are mentioned but feel metaphorical/vague until you actually play. Minor.

### Why Web4? (12:00)
- Strong problem statement: "You already know this. You feel it every day." Four failures: free actions, siloed reputation, unlimited fresh starts, AI making it worse. Compelling.
- BUT this page is very long and reads like a reference document, not an entry point. After the problem/solution core it opens into an FAQ of 80+ questions across 9 topics. The jump-to-topic index is well done and the sections collapse, so it's not a wall of text — but the sheer scale signals "skim, don't read."
- Confusion points: In the solutions section the acronyms arrive in a pile — ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI — labeled "shorthand" but without an intuitive analogy for each at the moment of introduction. Reading linearly, I hit four-to-five unfamiliar acronyms in quick succession. This is the page where I felt the most jargon pressure.

### Karma Journey (18:00)
- Interactive: I play sequential "lives," choosing from 8 actions per turn (cooperative/selfish/bold/neutral); choices set the starting conditions for the next life ("reincarnation").
- The CI² formula is handled really well now — it doesn't just show the equation, it interprets it: "Why squared? So one bad stretch costs you more than steady mediocrity ever could," with a worked comparison (0.6 CI → 0.36 effective; 0.9 → 0.81) and a note on why not linear/cubic. I understood why squaring matters, which surprised me.
- "Karma" is clearly framed as mechanical (consequence persistence), not moral. Good.
- Confusion points: The raw-trust (survival) vs effective-trust (karma tier) distinction exists but takes a second read. Minor.

### LCT Explainer (24:00)
- Great core analogy right away: "your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated."
- **This is where I hit my biggest friction.** A grid of hardware "trust ceilings" appears early — TPM 0.90, Secure Enclave 0.85, FIDO2 0.75, Software 0.50 — and as a newcomer I had NO idea what those decimals meant at that point. Is 0.90 good? Out of what? The page only explains "Trust runs from 0 (none) to 1.0, and 0.5 is the alive/dead line" much further down. I'd already stared at the grid confused. (First Contact taught me the 0–1 scale earlier, but a visitor landing directly on this page from search would be lost.)
- The numbers ARE well-justified once explained (0.90 = three attack surfaces: physical chip attack, supply-chain, side channels), and the "what does my ceiling let me do?" section ties them to real outcomes. The problem is purely ordering: ceilings before the ruler.
- "Witness" is acknowledged to mean two things (your own devices co-signing each other vs. optional infrastructure nodes). The heads-up is honest but comes after the term's already in use.
- Hardware needed: one device with a security chip (Secure Enclave / TPM 2.0 / FIDO2). The "you probably already own this" reassurance is good.

### Society Simulator (31:00)
- Big, genuinely fun interactive: 12 AI agents with strategies (cooperators, defectors, reciprocators), multi-round play, network graph of coalitions, scenario selector, "Play as Agent," 34 achievements.
- Onboarding pointer is prominent now: a highlighted box, "Never seen these Web4 terms before? ATP, T3, LCT, and CI show up all over this page," pointing to First Contact. And there's a collapsible acronym key that defines ATP/T3/LCT/CI/MRH/V3 inline. This is exactly what a newcomer needs.
- Honest about being simplified (scalar reputation, no MRH decay, no V3 scoring).
- Confusion points: It introduces V3 and MRH in the key only to say "not modeled in this demo" — mildly puzzling to define terms it then says it doesn't use. Minor.

### ATP Economics (36:00)
- ATP is consistently "your energy budget" throughout — no drift into "currency"/"attention budget." The page even pre-empts it: "You'll occasionally see it called an 'attention' or 'usage' budget — same idea." The biology framing (energy that flows, not tokens that accumulate) is consistent. This felt solid.
- The 5% transfer burn shows up early (60-second version) and again with full math (send 105, recipient gets 100, 5 goes to a redistribution pool). Anti-collusion rationale is there.
- Confusion points: ADP (Allocation Discharge Packet, the "receipt") is introduced almost as an aside and only becomes meaningful when the recharge math arrives much later. I skimmed past it and had to backtrack. Also the "~7x gap" dominates the earning examples but is flagged as a 4-Life visualization parameter only in a collapsed section most people won't open.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (41:00)
- Clear: trust isn't one number; it's three dimensions in the context of a role — Talent ("can they solve problems in this role?"), Training ("expertise for this role?"), Temperament ("can they be relied on?"). Interactive simulator lets me pick a role (surgeon/analyst/team leader) and see the same action move dimensions differently.
- V3 is handled gracefully: "T3 measures who you are; V3 measures what you produce. You don't need it to follow this page — T3 alone is enough for now." That permission-to-skip is exactly right and stopped me from feeling behind.
- Confusion points: decay half-lives (Talent 365d, Temperament 30d) appear with little setup. Minor.

### Aliveness (45:00)
- "Aliveness" = three simultaneous conditions: energy (ATP > 0), agency (T3 > 0.5), consistency (coherent CI). Professional-license analogy.
- The death distinction is the highlight: Energy Death (ATP = 0) → "reborn with karma carried forward" (recoverable, like bankruptcy) vs Trust Death (T3 < 0.5) → "society rejects rebirth" (permanent, like losing a license). "One event ('you died') with two very different outcomes." This finally made the stakes concrete.
- The 0.5 threshold is honestly framed: "calibrated, not derived... we picked the midpoint and watched it work." I respect the honesty; it stops the number from feeling like fake physics.
- Confusion points: synthons (group entities) appear late/briefly; "Karma is a handle, not a moral verdict" is good but the metaphysical word still invites misreading. Minor.

### Coherence Index (48:00)
- Best concrete examples on the site. High CI (~0.97) = "a typical Tuesday... your usual phone... normal hours" and "High CI is invisible." Low CI (~0.4) = compromised account "suddenly posts crypto spam at 3am from a different country." Mid (~0.8) = vacation in Tokyo, "like using a credit card abroad." Those landed instantly.
- Interactive "Can You Stay Coherent?" slider sim across spatial/capability/temporal/relational dimensions.
- It directly addresses "isn't this surveillance?" which I was about to ask.
- Confusion points: the name "Coherence Index" is still the most abstract-sounding label on the site — the examples carry it, the name doesn't. Minor.

### Day in Web4 (50:00)
- 10 everyday scenarios over one day (plumber recs, hiring freelancers, buying things) reimagined with trust built in. Grounded and relatable.
- The standout: 5 interactive clickable UI mockups (Mail, Talent, Reviews, Social, Market) showing actual interfaces with trust scores and costs. As a newcomer this is the FIRST time I could actually picture what using Web4 would look like, not just the theory. Really valuable.
- Confusion points: tense wobbles — it mixes "would feel like" / present tense "your first post costs" / imperative "install an app," and repeatedly breaks character with "none of this is downloadable yet." Each disclaimer is honest but the rhythm is jarring. Also "trust ceiling 0.85" appears on first mention with no scale anchor (same issue as the LCT page).

### Glossary (also browsed)
- ~40–50 terms in categories (Core 7, Advanced, Research, Lifecycle, Governance) with an "Essential only" filter and dual definitions (plain-English + technical). Honest "Skip this section on your first visit" warnings. Well built.
- Confusion points: a couple of definitions lean on "blockchain"/"TPM" as if I already know them.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed trust-native internet where trust comes from verified behavior, not assumed by default
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab/simulation of the Web4 model, explicitly not a product
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound verified presence; "your phone as an unforgeable driver's license"
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — ATP = energy budget spent on actions, refilled by validated quality; ADP = the spent-energy receipt (slightly fuzzy but got it)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3 = Talent/Training/Temperament, role-contextual, multi-dimensional
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive needs ATP>0, T3>0.5, coherent CI; energy death is recoverable, trust death is permanent
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam economics, reputation silos, unlimited restarts, AI deception

## Unanswered Questions
1. **What is the 0–1 trust scale, stated once, up front?** I pieced it together (First Contact taught it, Aliveness confirmed 0.5 = alive/dead), but trust numbers (0.90, 0.85, 0.5) appear on multiple pages BEFORE the scale is anchored on that page. A newcomer arriving via search on the LCT or Day-in-Web4 page would be confused by the decimals.
2. **What exactly is the difference between ATP and ADP, in one sentence I can keep?** I get ATP fully; ADP I understood only after backtracking through the recharge math.
3. **Who controls the "society's pool" that grants my starting 100 ATP and receives the 5% burns?** Mentioned as "not a central authority" / "community redistribution pool," but as a newcomer I still don't picture who/what that is.
4. **If reputation is permanent and portable, what happens when I'm wrongly penalized?** Appeals are mentioned as "designed, not deployed" — left me slightly uneasy about fairness, though I appreciated the honesty.

## Honest Assessment
This is, genuinely, one of the most carefully built explainer sites I've come across for a hard, abstract topic. The thing that struck me most as a true newcomer: the site repeatedly answers the question I was *about* to ask, one beat before I asked it — "is this crypto?" (no, said on the landing page), "is this a real product?" (no, said clearly on First Contact), "isn't this surveillance?" (addressed head-on on the CI page). That earns trust fast.

The concept teaching is strong where it counts. CI² — which I expected to bounce off — was the pleasant surprise: it's explained by *interpretation*, not just an equation, on both the Karma Journey and Aliveness pages. The energy/trust death distinction on Aliveness finally made the stakes feel real. The V3 "you don't need this yet" framing on the Trust Tensor page is a small kindness that kept me from feeling behind. And the interactive UI mockups on "Day in Web4" were the first time I could actually *picture* using Web4 rather than just reading about it — that's a big deal for a research project that has nothing to sign up for.

My one real, repeated friction is small but cross-cutting: **trust numbers appear before the 0–1 ruler does.** On the LCT page I stared at "0.90 / 0.75 / 0.50" with no idea of the range; on Day-in-Web4 a "0.85 trust ceiling" appears cold. I happened to learn the scale early (First Contact), so I was fine — but anyone landing on a concept page directly from a search would hit numbers with no scale. One inline anchor line above each grid would close it.

Secondary, softer friction: the "Why Web4" page is enormous and reads like a reference manual (80+ FAQ), and its solutions section drops the full acronym set in a pile — it's the one page where I felt jargon pressure. The TL;DR and First Contact are the real front doors and they're excellent; "Why Web4" works better as the place you go *after* you're curious, not the first stop.

Net: I came in a blank slate and left understanding all seven core concepts well enough to explain them to a friend. solid understanding, would return, would recommend.
