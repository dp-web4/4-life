## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean and confident. The one-liner "Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos." landed instantly — I felt the problem before being told the solution. Good.
- The framing "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" preempted my two biggest assumptions ("is this crypto?"). I appreciated being told what it is NOT.
- **Confusion / friction**: The "Start Here" area gave me *five* doors at once — "Web4 in 2 Minutes," "Why Web4?," "First Contact," "How It Works," "Learning Journey" — *plus* a highlighted "Recommended: 2-minute TL;DR." Five-plus choices on a first screen is mild decision paralysis. The "Recommended" badge saved me, but if it weren't there I'd have hesitated.
- **Clicked**: the recommended "2-minute TL;DR."

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / tldr (04:00)
- This was the strongest page for me. Plain English, spells out acronyms, four clean ideas (energy costs, hardware identity, portable reputation, same rules for humans + AI).
- I liked "The internet has no memory" as the framing for why bad actors get free restarts. Concrete.
- **Friction**: it still drops ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI in quick succession and says they have "deeper shorthand names explained elsewhere." Two minutes in and I'm already being promised *more* names for the names. That's a small warning bell.
- **Clicked**: "First Contact," because "interactive 7-minute walkthrough" sounded like the gentlest next step.

### First Contact (08:00)
- Concept is appealing: I drive a character named Alice through a 5-act story, watching her ATP (starts 100) and Trust (starts 0.50) move in response to my choices. "No account, no sign-in" lowered the barrier nicely.
- It promises explanations "arrive precisely when needed" — exactly what an onboarding should do.
- **Honest limitation**: this is the interactive heart of the site and I can read its *setup* but the payoff only exists if I actually play it. The page sells it well; I'm trusting that the in-sim teaching delivers. A static visitor (or one who bounces) never gets that payoff.
- **Clicked**: "Why Web4?" — I wanted the argument before more mechanics.

### Why Web4? (14:00)
- Genuinely good problem-first writing. "Creating accounts is free. Sending messages is free." → spam, silos, infinite fresh starts after bans, AI making deception cheaper. I was nodding.
- The ~80-question FAQ impressed me and *earned trust*: it openly takes on "Isn't this just social credit?", GDPR / right-to-be-forgotten, and "can a nation-state buy 50 devices?" The line "This is research, not production. We don't attach timelines because honest research doesn't have them" made me trust the authors more, not less.
- **Friction**: it assumes I already care about *decentralization*. My honest naive reaction was "why not just build better spam filters?" — and that exact objection wasn't really answered. The page argues WHY trust is broken, but not WHY this particular cure beats incremental fixes I already understand.

### LCT Explainer (19:00)
- Best analogy on the whole site: "Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer." That single sentence taught me more than a paragraph would.
- Breaking down Linked / Context / Token word-by-word was helpful.
- **Friction**: it gets dense fast — TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2, and a "trust ceiling" of 0.50–0.90 depending on hardware. As a non-technical person I had to *accept* rather than *understand* the hardware layer. Also the page admits "witness" has two meanings (devices co-signing vs. infrastructure nodes) — admitting the overload is honest, but it's still an overload.

### ATP Economics (24:00)
- "ATP is your energy budget" — clear one-liner.
- **Significant friction (acronym collision)**: the page says ATP = "Allocation Transfer Packets," but then leans hard on the biology of *adenosine triphosphate* ("the molecule your cells spend… burn ATP, get ADP back"). So the same three letters mean two different things, and I'm asked to hold both at once. As a naive reader this felt like a pun I had to decode, not an aid. Is it the cell molecule or the made-up "Allocation Transfer Packets"? (Answer: both, on purpose — but it took me a beat.)
- **Friction**: "you send 100 ATP to Bob — you pay 105, Bob receives 100." This fights intuition. When I "send" something I expect the recipient to be short the fee, not me to pay extra. Needed a re-read.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (28:00)
- I braced for math when I saw "tensor," but the page disarmed it well: "You don't need the math… one number couldn't tell a brilliant-but-erratic surgeon from a kind doctor with shaky hands." Nice.
- Three axes: Talent, Training, Temperament — clear once stated.
- **Friction (inconsistency across pages)**: the Why Web4? page described T3 as "skill, training, and behavior," but *this* page calls the axes "Talent, Training, Temperament." Same three slots, different first/third words. As a careful reader I noticed and wondered if they were the same thing or two different models. Pick one vocabulary site-wide.
- Decay half-lives (365/180/30 days) show up later and reintroduce the math the intro promised I wouldn't need.

### Aliveness (31:00)
- The pre-emptive reassurance is good: "when you read 'death' on this page, your reputation isn't erased. What ends is the current life-cycle."
- **Friction (one word, two opposite meanings)**: "energy death" is recoverable (like bankruptcy → karma rebirth), but "trust death" is *permanent* (like losing a professional license). Same word "death" for one fixable and one terminal outcome. The label hides the asymmetry; I had to read carefully to keep them straight.
- I respected the honesty: "False positive recovery is an open problem we take seriously" — i.e., a wrongly-killed agent is an unsolved risk. Good that they say it; slightly unsettling that permanent death exists at all for something that could be a real person.

### How It Works (34:00)
- Useful clarification up front: "Nothing here is live — this is a research prototype" and "Everything below describes how Web4 would work, validated through simulations." This finally pinned down for me what 4-Life IS: the demo tool for Web4.
- **Friction**: introduces yet another acronym, CI (consistency), and a "SAL" governance framework with "law oracles" — and I had no idea how a law oracle gets its authority or resists capture. New jargon at the point where I most wanted consolidation.

### Society Simulator (36:00)
- Appealing premise: "Watch 12 agents… form alliances, betray each other, and self-organize — no central authority, just trust dynamics." Watch mode or play mode, animated (~30s) or instant.
- **Friction (circular onboarding)**: the page itself warns "Never seen these Web4 terms before? First Contact is a 7-minute guided intro." So the sims assume prior knowledge, but First Contact (a sim) is the thing teaching it. A newcomer can feel bounced between "learn before you play" and "play to learn."
- Also a disclaimer that here trust is "a scalar reputation," not the real 3-D model — so the simulator simplifies the thing the rest of the site insisted was 3-dimensional. Minor whiplash.

### Glossary (38:00)
- The "Essential only" toggle (7 core terms first, advanced hidden) is the single best friction-reducer I found. This is what the *whole site* needs more of.
- Definitions are solid (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI, SAL all present).
- **Friction**: even here, "Synthon," and "D4/D5/D9" numbers appear without grounding, and federations/DID standards feel scattered. The glossary rescues the core terms but the long tail is still a thicket.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust earned through behavior, anchored to hardware, no blockchain
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — it's a research prototype/sim that shows how Web4 *would* work
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound verifiable identity ("phone as un-fakeable driver's license")
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget you spend to act / receipt of spending (though the acronym pun cost me)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — a 3-axis score (Talent/Training/Temperament) per role, not one number
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive = energy>0, trust>0.5, consistent; two kinds of "death," one recoverable one not
- [~] Why any of this matters — I get the *problem* clearly; I'm not fully sold on *why this cure over simpler fixes*

## Unanswered Questions
1. Why is hardware-bound permanent reputation better than just improving spam filters and moderation? (The site never directly compares.)
2. Are the T3 axes "Talent/Training/Temperament" or "skill/training/behavior"? Different pages say different things.
3. Who runs the "law oracles" in the SAL governance layer, and what stops them from being captured or corrupted?
4. If "trust death" is permanent and false positives are an admitted open problem, what protects a wrongly-condemned real person?
5. Does ATP actually stand for "Allocation Transfer Packets," or is it just borrowing the biology word? The two framings sit awkwardly together.
6. What are "Synthon" and the D4/D5/D9 numbers? They appeared with no on-ramp.

## Honest Assessment
This site is *trying very hard* to be honest and accessible, and in several places it succeeds beautifully — the "internet has no memory" framing, the driver's-license analogy for LCT, the surgeon-vs-shaky-hands explanation of why one trust number isn't enough, and an FAQ that genuinely takes on its own weak points instead of dodging them. I trusted the authors more by the end, which is rare.

But the experience is a slow accumulation of acronyms. By the time I reached the simulators I was carrying ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI, SAL — and then met "law oracles," "Synthon," and D-numbers. The glossary's "Essential only" toggle is the antidote the rest of the site needs: hide the long tail until I ask for it.

Two issues felt like outright bugs in the messaging, not just density: (1) the ATP acronym means two different things at once, and (2) the three trust axes are named differently on different pages. Those are cheap to fix and would remove real "wait, did I misunderstand?" moments.

The deepest gap is motivational, not definitional. I finished understanding *what* Web4 is and *how* it works far better than *why I should want it* over the incremental fixes I already know. The problem statement is strong; the "why this solution specifically" is assumed. For a naive visitor deciding whether to care, that's the friction that determines whether I come back. Right now: maybe.
