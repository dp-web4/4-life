## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean and confident. The headline "4-Life | Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus "explore Web4 through simulations" told me roughly what I was looking at, and the one-line clarifier — "Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" — preempted my very first two questions ("is this crypto?" and "is this a Web3 thing?"). That sentence is doing a lot of work and doing it well.
- The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" framing ("Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos. Bad actors get unlimited fresh starts.") landed immediately. I felt those.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — exactly what a nervous first-timer wants.
- Confusion points: The five "foundational ideas" (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) are in plain language, which is nice, but I didn't yet know why there were five or how they fit together. Minor.

### TL;DR (03:00)
- This was the right first stop. ATP got expanded ("Allocation Transfer Packets") and explained as an energy budget that refills "when others confirm your work was useful." LCT explained as "a digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip." Both clicked.
- Confusion point: It distinguishes "Web4 (the protocol)" from "4-Life (the lab/site)." I understood it after a beat, but the protocol-vs-website distinction is a subtle thing to drop on someone in the first two minutes.
- The ATP-is-from-biology metaphor is leaned on without being spelled out. I happened to half-remember ATP from a biology class; someone who didn't would just see three random capital letters.

### Why Web4 (07:00)
- The five problems (spam is free, reputation is trapped, bad actors reset, AI breaks verification, platforms own everything) are well-named. "Your reputation starts at zero, every time" is a sharp line.
- This page is LONG. I could tell it was built to be skimmed/referenced rather than read straight through, and it even tells you the acronyms "land much easier after First Contact" — which is honest. But as a first reader I still hit ATP / LCT / T3 / MRH / CI in fairly quick succession before I'd been to First Contact, and it was a little much.
- Honest, credibility-building note: "we don't attach timelines because honest research doesn't have them." I trusted the site more after reading that.

### First Contact (12:00)
- This is the heart of the site for me. Driving Alice through five acts (join → build trust → spam backfires → recover → death + karma rebirth) is genuinely the thing that made the abstract stuff concrete.
- The "is this real or pretend?" question was answered cleanly: "a model of a proposed system: the rules running here are real, but Web4 itself is active research with nothing to sign up for yet." Good — I never felt deceived.
- What it taught me well: ATP (energy) and trust (0.00–1.00) because I watched them move. What it taught me less well: LCT and CI are name-dropped in a reference section but I didn't *experience* them through Alice the way I experienced ATP and trust. They stayed abstract.

### LCT Explainer (18:00)
- "Your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked" is a great hook.
- Trust ceilings (TPM 0.90, Secure Enclave 0.85, FIDO2 0.75, software-only 0.50) are clearly anchored to "Trust runs from 0 (none) to 1.0." Good — I knew what the numbers meant.
- Friction: the page itself warns that "witness" means two different things on the same page (your devices co-signing each other vs. outside infrastructure nodes). I appreciate the honesty, but having to hold two meanings of one word in my head on the page that's supposed to be *clarifying* identity was the hardest moment of my session. The "device co-witness" / "network witness" relabeling helped, but I wonder if one of the two could just be renamed entirely.

### ATP Economics (23:00)
- "ATP is your energy budget" — consistent, repeated, clear. The 60-second box up top is great. The 5% transfer fee and *why* it exists (kills circular farming) was thoroughly explained.
- Friction: ADP. The 60-second box mentions "ATP as cash and ADP as the receipt," but then ADP basically disappears and doesn't get operationalized ("the proof recipients confirm to trigger recharge") until much later. For a stretch in the middle I wasn't sure if ADP was a second kind of money. The cash/receipt line is the keeper — I'd want it to do more work earlier.

### Trust Tensor (28:00)
- T3 = Talent / Training / Temperament, always role-specific. Clear, and the "Alice's analyst talent doesn't transfer to mechanics" example helped.
- "0.7 = ~70% cooperative behavior in this role" is a good concrete anchor.
- Friction: V3 (the Value Tensor) shows up on this page. It's politely marked optional ("You don't need it to follow this page"), but I still found myself asking "then why is it here?" The T3-grades-who-you-are vs. V3-grades-what-you-produce distinction is fine once explained, but on a page titled Trust Tensor, V3 felt like it wandered in from somewhere else.

### Aliveness (32:00)
- The three living criteria (ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, CI coherent) and the energy-death-vs-trust-death distinction are the clearest part: "bankruptcy" (recoverable via karma rebirth) vs. "losing a professional license" (permanent). That analogy did it for me.
- I respected: "The 0.5 threshold is calibrated, not derived... we picked the midpoint... ran the simulations, and watched it work." No overclaiming.
- Friction (this is my biggest one): "Cross-life patterns" — a rule like "when ATP runs low, act conservatively" that "carries over" across death. But death is described as erasing the agent. So *how* does a learned pattern survive a death that wipes memory? Who carries it? The page names this as a thing that happens but never says mechanically how. It's the one place the site felt hand-wavy rather than honest-about-uncertainty.
- Secondary friction: synthons. I can be part of a group that's also "alive," I keep my own T3/ATP — but can I be individually alive inside a *dead* synthon (or vice versa)? The page implies yes but never says it, and I wanted it stated.

### Coherence Index (36:00)
- Pleasantly concrete. Maria's stolen-phone scenario (CI drops to 0.55), the Tokyo-vacation dip (~0.8, recovers), the 3am-crypto-spam compromise (crashes to ~0.4) made CI legible.
- CI² is explained AND justified as a "calibration choice, not mathematically derived" — same honest tone as the 0.5 threshold. I liked the consistency.

### Learn + Society Simulator (38:00)
- The Learn page lays out four pathways (Beginner → Practitioner) and a sensible LCT → ATP → T3 starting order. Useful, though I'd already wandered most of it organically.
- Note: I guessed the URL `/society` for the simulator and hit a 404 — the real link is `/society-simulator`. Not a broken on-site link (the Learn page links correctly), just my own typo.
- Society Simulator has a great newcomer escape hatch ("Never seen these Web4 terms before?" → First Contact) and an expandable acronym key covering ATP/T3/LCT/CI/MRH/V3. But the very first heading ("You saw how one agent builds trust. Now watch 12 agents...") *assumes* I came from First Contact. If the simulator is someone's first landing (e.g., from a shared link), that opening sentence references an experience they haven't had.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Cross-life learning mechanics**: If death erases the agent, what concretely carries a learned heuristic into the next life? This is my top question — it's the one spot that felt unexplained rather than honestly-uncertain.
2. **Synthon/individual coupling**: Can an individual be alive while their synthon is dead, and does a synthon's death do anything to its members?
3. **What does the actual UI look like?**: Everything is simulation/diagram. If Web4 shipped, what would logging in, getting witnessed, or spending ATP feel like as a real person? (I suspect this is a recurring ask.)
4. **ADP's independent role**: Is the ADP receipt ever something *I* hold/use, or is it purely an internal accounting artifact that triggers others' confirmations?

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-built educational site, and I say that as someone who walked in knowing nothing. The single best decision is First Contact: letting me *drive Alice* and watch ATP and trust move turned three-letter abbreviations into something I could feel. The second-best decision is the site's consistent honesty — "calibrated, not derived," "no timelines because honest research doesn't have them," even the heads-up that "witness" is overloaded. That candor is rare and it bought my trust.

Where I still stumbled was almost always at the *seams between concepts* rather than within any one concept. Each page explains its own idea well; the friction is when one page borrows another's vocabulary (V3 on the Trust Tensor page, "witness" wearing two hats) or when a mechanism is named but its plumbing isn't shown (cross-life patterns surviving a death that erases memory). None of these broke my understanding — I left with a real mental model — but they're the rough edges where a curious person starts asking "wait, but how?" and the page doesn't answer.

If I were to point at one thing to fix first: the cross-life learning mechanism on the Aliveness page. It's the only place the site's otherwise-admirable honesty seemed to lapse into hand-waving, and it sits right at the emotionally loaded moment (death and rebirth) where a visitor is most invested.
