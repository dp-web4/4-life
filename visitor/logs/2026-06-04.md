### First Contact (06:00)
- This was the strongest page for *tangibility*. Following "Alice" through a 5-act arc (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) finally made the abstractions feel like they had consequences. Watching "spam costs 25 ATP, trust drops" is the first time the rules felt real.
- The two metrics framing (🔋 Energy 0–100, 🤝 Trust 0.00–1.00, neutral 0.50) gave me concrete dials to watch. Good onboarding move.

- **Confusion points**:
  - "Your presence is hardware-bound (called an LCT — Linked Context Token)" tells me *what* it's called but not *how* hardware binding works or why it matters. I noted I'd need the LCT page.
  - "Recipients who benefit confirm quality — that's what earns energy back." Who exactly confirms, and how do they decide something "helped"? Felt assumed.

### Why Web4? (11:00)
- This is where I, as a skeptic, felt most respected. "You already know this. You feel it every day." — yes, I do.
- The Web3 distinction was genuinely good and credible: "Web3 tried to solve ownership with financial instruments. Web4 argues the deeper problem was never ownership — it was trust." That reframed things for me.
- I was impressed it didn't hide weaknesses: it openly says "not a product yet," "No platform has done so yet," "no appeals process yet," equity concerns around the ~$50 hardware are "a real concern, not a dismissed one."
- **Confusion / unconvincing points** (these are the honest skeptic reactions):
  - **Adoption**: the whole thing requires platforms to *voluntarily* integrate, and the page admits none have. It never answered "why would a platform that profits from moderation-as-a-moat adopt this?"
  - **Portability feels illusory**: it says when you change communities "your trust history is visible but not automatically imported." So... each community reboots me? Then how is this better than Reddit karma? That's the exact question I had and it wasn't closed.
  - **Circularity**: I earn ATP when someone "confirms my contribution helped" — but who confirms for genuinely *new* contributions with no recipients yet? Cold-start felt hand-waved.
  - **The collusion stat** ("93%+ probability at 3+ members") actually *increased* my doubt — 7% of attacks slip through and I'd have no way to know I'm in the 7%.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Why would any platform adopt this?** Platforms profit from controlling reputation; voluntary integration with zero current adopters is the whole ballgame and it's unaddressed.
2. **If trust history is "visible but not auto-imported" when I switch communities, how is that meaningfully more portable than Reddit karma?**
3. **Who confirms the very first contributions** in a brand-new community, before anyone has trust to weight their confirmations? The cold-start logic loops.
4. **What does ADP actually look like to me** as a user, and do I ever see/interact with it?
5. **If I get a bad reputation early (e.g. as a teenager), the 30-day Temperament half-life means recovery is slow** — how does that square with the "you can outgrow mistakes" reassurance?
6. **Why the biology vocabulary (ATP/ADP)?** Does the cell-metabolism metaphor earn its cognitive cost, or is it just flavor?

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely well-made site, and noticeably more honest than most "new internet paradigm" pitches I've seen. The recommended path (Landing → TL;DR → First Contact → Why Web4?) is a real onboarding funnel and it mostly works — Alice's story is the MVP, turning five acronyms into a thing with stakes.

But here's my honest naive reaction: **the site is better at explaining itself than at convincing me it will happen.** Every mechanism page eventually answered my "how" questions (ATP Economics especially). What never got answered was the "so what / who adopts this" question — and on the one page explicitly built to handle skepticism (Why Web4?), the two hardest objections (platform adoption incentives, real portability vs. existing karma) were either admitted-and-unresolved or quietly contradicted by the details. As a skeptic, the *honesty* about those gaps earns trust, but it doesn't close them.

Cognitive-load wise: the acronym density is the recurring tax. By the third page I was fluent, but the first 90 seconds asked me to hold 5 unknowns. A casual visitor — not someone like me willing to read 8 pages — probably bounces at the landing acronym wall or the "tensor" word.

The thing I'd most want fixed: a single, direct answer to "why would platforms ever turn this on?" Right now the whole edifice rests on voluntary adoption that hasn't happened, and the site is candid that it hasn't — which is admirable, but leaves the most important question as the most empty one.

If I'm being fully honest about whether I'd return: maybe. I'd come back to *play the simulator*, because that looked fun and concrete. I'm less likely to come back for the prose. The lab is the hook; the manifesto is the friction.

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-built educational site, and I say that as someone who walked in knowing nothing. The single best decision is First Contact: letting me *drive Alice* and watch ATP and trust move turned three-letter abbreviations into something I could feel. The second-best decision is the site's consistent honesty — "calibrated, not derived," "no timelines because honest research doesn't have them," even the heads-up that "witness" is overloaded. That candor is rare and it bought my trust.

Where I still stumbled was almost always at the *seams between concepts* rather than within any one concept. Each page explains its own idea well; the friction is when one page borrows another's vocabulary (V3 on the Trust Tensor page, "witness" wearing two hats) or when a mechanism is named but its plumbing isn't shown (cross-life patterns surviving a death that erases memory). None of these broke my understanding — I left with a real mental model — but they're the rough edges where a curious person starts asking "wait, but how?" and the page doesn't answer.

If I were to point at one thing to fix first: the cross-life learning mechanism on the Aliveness page. It's the only place the site's otherwise-admirable honesty seemed to lapse into hand-waving, and it sits right at the emotionally loaded moment (death and rebirth) where a visitor is most invested.
