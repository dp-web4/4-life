## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean and confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations" tells me this is a lab/sandbox, not a product. Good.
- The sub-line **"The Internet Has a Trust Problem"** immediately hooked me — that's a problem I *feel*, so I wanted to keep reading.
- I appreciated the inline definition: *"Web4 is a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor."* The "no blockchain" disclaimer pre-empted my exact first suspicion. Nice.
- There were **four entry CTAs** (2-min TL;DR, "What it looks like · 5 min," "Frustrations it would fix · 3 min," "Structured learning path"). The TL;DR is marked "Recommended for first-time visitors," so I clicked it. The time estimates on each option are a genuinely good touch — they let me budget.
- **Confusion points**: The page lists "5 core ideas" (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) but on the landing page these are just *words*. I didn't yet know what any of them meant. That's probably intentional (a teaser), but it's the first hint of what becomes the session's recurring problem.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (03:00)
- This is where my optimism took its first dent. The page is well-written, but "2 minutes" is generous. It introduced, in rapid succession: **ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI** — five acronyms — plus secondary names like "Allocation Transfer Packets," "witness constellation," "Talent/Training/Temperament," and "Markov Relevancy Horizon."
- The problem framing ("The internet has no memory. A spammer can create a new account in seconds") is excellent and concrete.
- But by the time I hit "Markov Relevancy Horizon" I had stopped absorbing and started skimming. A naive human does not know what a Markov anything is. The full term actively *hurt* here — the plain-English version ("your trust neighborhood, 3 hops max") was all I needed.
- The "Current Status" section (hestia, the hub, "runnable reference code," "pilot deployment next") was honest and I respect it — but it also introduced *more* new proper nouns (hestia, the hub) right when I was already saturated.
- **Takeaway**: A "2-minute TL;DR" that requires me to learn 5 acronyms isn't a TL;DR — it's a syllabus. I left this page knowing Web4 *has* five mechanisms but not really understanding any single one.

### First Contact (08:00)
- This was the **best page of my visit.** "Your First Web4 Experience" — an interactive walkthrough where I drive a character (Alice) and watch two numbers (Energy/ATP and Trust) change.
- Reducing the whole system to **"Two Numbers to Watch"** was the single best pedagogical decision I encountered. Energy starts at 100, Trust starts at 0.50 — concrete, watchable, low cognitive load.
- Alice's 5-act arc (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) gave me a *story*, and stories stick. The clarification that "death" = temporary suspension, not deletion, answered a question right as it formed in my head.
- Honesty I appreciated: "you're running a simulation of the proposed model, not using a product."
- **Friction**: I could see this *describes* an interactive 5-act walkthrough with pause/scrub/replay, but as a reader I'm not sure how much actually animates vs. how much is static text I click through. (As a browsing visitor I can't fully tell.) Also it *still* leans on the acronyms, with a "Lost in the acronyms? Quick index" link — the third page in a row to need that escape hatch.

### Why Web4? (15:00)
- Strong page. "The Problem Before the Solution" is the right order, and it knows it.
- The five root causes (spam economics, reputation silos, disposable identity, AI deception, platform monopoly) were all things I personally recognize. This is the page that made me think "okay, this is a *real* problem, not invented."
- I liked that it pre-empts "why not just X" — it explicitly addresses why moderation, CAPTCHAs, blockchain wallets, and social login all failed. That's the page anticipating my skepticism instead of ignoring it.
- The honesty about weaknesses (no live users, device-cost equity unsolved, "trust the simulations") actually *increased* my trust in the site rather than decreasing it.
- **Friction**: There's an enormous FAQ (~70 questions). It's impressive but slightly intimidating — it signals "there are 70 unresolved objections." For a naive visitor it's more reassuring to see 10 great answers than 70 links. I didn't read them; I noted they exist.

### LCT Explainer (20:00)
- The opening analogy — *"Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer"* — is genuinely excellent. I understood the *goal* of LCT in one sentence.
- The Web2/Web3/Web4 comparison table ("what you know" vs "what you have" vs "what hardware proves") was clarifying.
- The interactive device-witness slider (1→5 devices raising your trust ceiling) sounds like a great hands-on element.
- **Where I got confused**:
  - The page uses the word **"witness" for two different things** (devices co-signing each other vs. network witnesses) and openly admits it. If the *site authors* know it's overloaded, a naive reader has no chance — that's a term that needs renaming, not a disclaimer.
  - The 0–1 trust scale keeps appearing (0.50 = alive/dead line, 0.75 = solid, 0.90 = max) but I never got a clean explanation of *why* 0.50 is the magic line or what crossing it actually does to me.
  - It references ATP and T3 as if I already know them. By now I sort of do — but only because I'd read three prior pages. A visitor landing here directly from Google would be lost.

### Glossary (26:00)
- This is the safety net the whole site keeps pointing to, and it's decent. Six sections, an "Essential only" filter (7 terms), and an "Acronyms at a glance" box (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, MRH).
- Definitions are crisp: ATP = "every action costs ATP, quality contributions earn ATP"; MRH = "trust decays 30% per hop."
- **Observation**: The fact that I *needed* the glossary this much is itself the finding. Nearly every page has a "Lost in the acronyms?" link pointing here. That's the site treating a symptom. The glossary is good — but a site that's easy to understand shouldn't need to send me to the glossary five times.
- I also met a brand-new acronym here — **V3** — that I hadn't seen explained anywhere I'd been. So the acronym set is even bigger than the "five" I'd been bracing for.

### Simulation Sandbox (29:00)
- This looks like a real, in-browser simulation: parameter sliders (Lives, Ticks/Life, ATP, trust rates), preset buttons (Newcomer, Survivor, Scholar, Amnesiac), a Run button, and overlaid result charts.
- As a *naive* visitor, this page is over my head. The presets are intriguing but the parameters assume I know what "Ticks/Life" and "trust decay rate" mean. This feels like a tool for someone who already finished the learning path — not a discovery surface.
- I couldn't tell from browsing whether my run would produce something I'd understand. I'd probably hit "Run" on a preset, see some lines on a chart, and not know if that was good or bad.

### How It Works (32:00)
- "The Complete Lifecycle" — Birth → Life → Death → Rebirth, with worked numbers (start 100 ATP, earn 105, spend 60, end 145, die at T3 0.65).
- The worked calculations are great; concrete numbers beat abstractions.
- But this is the **densest page** — all five systems at once, plus a new three-tier governance framework (SAL: Society-Authority-Law), oracles, appeals, "authority decay," a "1.4× coherence surcharge." This is a reference manual, not an intro, despite being in the top nav next to the beginner stuff.

### AI Identity / Understanding Consciousness (34:00)
- This is where I, as a naive visitor, got the most uneasy. The page is titled around AI **consciousness**, measured on a 0–1 scale with dimensions called **D5** and **D9**, thresholds where "identity collapses below 0.5," references to **SAGE training, Sessions T021–T022, Q1 2026, r ≈ 0.95**, and a fictional country "Zxyzzy."
- To the page's credit, it explicitly says it's using "consciousness" as shorthand for "reliable, intentional behavior." Good disclaimer.
- **But** — as a first-timer who wandered here from a menu labeled "AI Agents," this reads like I fell out of an onboarding site and into a research lab's internal notes. D5/D9, session IDs, and correlation coefficients with no methodology are insider artifacts. It made the project feel less like "a trust model for the internet" and more like "someone's personal AI-consciousness research," which is a jarring tonal shift from the grounded "spam is bad" framing I started with.
- A naive visitor could easily leave this page thinking the site is claiming AI consciousness is scientifically established. That's a credibility risk.

## Honest Assessment

The site is clearly the product of serious, honest thinking, and its single biggest strength is that it **earns my belief in the problem**. "Why Web4?" and the "two numbers to watch" framing in First Contact are genuinely good — if the whole site taught like those two pages, I'd rate my understanding "good" instead of "partial."

The single biggest weakness is **acronym load**, and it's structural, not cosmetic. The fact that almost every page carries a "Lost in the acronyms?" link is the tell: the team *knows* readers are drowning, and the chosen fix is a lifeboat (the glossary) rather than not throwing people in the water. A naive human can hold maybe two new abstract terms in working memory at once. The site hands me six or seven (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, MRH) and a few have intimidating formal names on top ("Allocation Transfer Packets," "Markov Relevancy Horizon"). I learned to recognize them by repetition across pages — but I never reached the point where I could *use* them in a sentence. Recognition is not comprehension.

The second issue is **altitude consistency.** The intro pages speak human ("spam wins because accounts are free"). But "How It Works" and especially the AI Identity page drop into research-lab register (SAL governance tiers, D5/D9 dimensions, session IDs, correlation coefficients). Both modes are fine — but they shouldn't be one menu-click apart with no warning. A beginner who clicks "How It Works" or "AI Agents" expecting more onboarding instead falls off a cliff into insider material.

If I were advising a friend: "Read the landing page, do First Contact, read Why Web4?, then stop." Those three pages are excellent and self-contained. Everything past them currently assumes a fluency the site hasn't finished teaching.

So: I'd come back, because I believe the problem is real and the approach is interesting. But I came away able to *recite* Web4's pieces, not *think* with them — and the gap between those two is exactly the acronym wall.
