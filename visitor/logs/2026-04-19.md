## Unanswered Questions
1. **Is ATP tradeable?** I inferred "not really" from context (5% burn fee, quality ramp), but I was never told explicitly whether ATP is or isn't a currency you'd buy or sell. A direct statement would help.
2. **Who runs the witness infrastructure?** The LCT page says devices witness each other, but the broader network — does someone host it? Is it peer-to-peer? A foundation? Federation operators?
3. **What does the actual product look like?** I kept wondering: if this were deployed, what app would I be using? Do I install a 4-life client? Is it integrated into existing apps? (This feels like a recurring visitor question based on how the site is framed.)
4. **What stops a federation from capturing the rules?** Governance is touched on (SAL, Law Oracle) but "who evaluates alignment vs. compliance" is explicitly acknowledged as unproven. How is capture prevented?
5. **What's the relationship between community/society/federation?** Used somewhat interchangeably but I suspect they're nested or distinct.

## Honest Assessment

This is a well-built explainer site. Progressive disclosure is real — I had to work through ~8 pages, but each one built on the last, and by the end I had a coherent mental model of a proposed system. That's much better than most technical research sites.

**What worked best**:
- The problem framing on Why Web4 — I felt the pain points before being sold the solution.
- Alice's role-specific trust example on the Trust Tensor page was the "aha" moment of my session.
- "Bankruptcy vs. losing your professional license" on Aliveness crystalized the two-death model.
- The "Shorthand:" pattern for acronyms is elegant — it lets plain English lead.

**What hurt most**:
- Why Web4 is too long. The FAQ in particular — 40 items is a wall.
- ATP vs. cryptocurrency is the missing comparison. It's the first thing my "crypto-skeptical friend" will ask.
- MRH's formal name and early-arriving code samples make the page feel more academic than it needs to be.
- The site has two distinct audiences jostling: naive visitors and implementers. Some pages serve both well (Trust Tensor) and some confuse both (MRH).

**Research vs. product ambiguity**: The honest "this is a prototype" caveat is scattered rather than upfront. I think this is the right call for most of the experience — but on pages that demo specific mechanics (ATP pricing, LCT ceilings, witness infrastructure), a small banner saying "these numbers are design targets, not measurements from deployment" would prevent future confusion.

**Verdict**: I'd return. I'd send it to a technically-curious friend. I'd warn a non-technical one to start with the TL;DR and then jump to an interactive page (Karma Journey or First Contact) — not to Why Web4, despite its name.
