## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, bold headline "4-Life: Trust-Native Societies." I don't know what that means yet, but "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" immediately resonates. Spam, reputation silos, fake accounts — these are problems I recognize.
- The problem framing is excellent. I felt like the site *understood* my frustrations before asking me to learn anything new.
- "Web4 is a trust-native internet where identity costs something, actions have metabolic weight, and consequences follow you forever." — Intriguing but dense. "Metabolic weight" is an unusual phrase. I'm curious but slightly guarded — is this another crypto pitch?
- The "New Here? Start Here" section with a clear 1-2-3 path (Why Web4 → First Contact → Playground) was exactly what I needed. I followed it.
- Clicked: "Why Web4?"
- Confusion points: "Trust-native" isn't defined on the landing page. "Metabolic weight" is evocative but unexplained. The "Deep Dive" tab next to "Introduction" made me wonder if I was already on the wrong tab.

### Why Web4 (01:30)
- This page is *really* good. The five problems are immediately relatable — I've experienced all of them. The "Root cause" callouts after each problem are sharp and convincing.
- "Why Previous Solutions Failed" — this section built credibility fast. They're not just selling; they're acknowledging that smarter people have tried and failed. The Web3 criticism (wallets are free to create, no behavior history) felt honest and specific.
- "What Would Actually Work?" — this reads like a requirements document, and it's logical. Make bad behavior expensive, make identity expensive to fake, make reputation portable. Each point follows from the problems above.
- The Web4 proposal section introduces four acronyms: ATP, LCT, T3, MRH. I have names but not understanding yet. That's fine — there are "Learn about X" links for each.
- The "Honest Questions" section is *excellent*. Q4 ("Is this deployed anywhere?") with the answer "Currently theoretical with working simulations" — that's refreshingly honest. The "honest caveat" format builds trust. This is the most credibility-building section on the entire site.
- Time spent: ~5 minutes. Worth every second.
- Clicked: "Start First Contact →"
- Confusion points: None significant. This page does its job well.

- The scenario selector (Cooperative Majority, Hostile World, Reciprocity Rules, Trust Scarce, All Adaptive) is well-named — each scenario name tells me what I'll see.
- Agent type descriptions are crisp: Cooperator ("always cooperates, trusting but exploitable"), Defector ("always defects, short-term gains, long-term isolation"), Reciprocator ("tit-for-tat"), etc. These map to concepts I know from game theory.

- The "Death is Meaningful" insight card with "Not timeout. Not suspension. Death." — this lands hard. It's the moment the system clicks.

## Unanswered Questions
1. Why is the 5-dimension trust model on the site different from the 3-dimension canonical model? Which should I be learning?
2. What is the Coherence Index *specifically*? I know it has 4 dimensions (spatial, temporal, capability, relational) but there's no dedicated deep-dive page explaining it as well as ATP or T3 are explained.
3. Who would actually deploy this? The site is honest about being research, but I have no sense of a path from "simulation" to "something real people use." Is anyone building on this?
4. What does "metabolic" add over just calling it "a cost system"? The biological metaphor is consistent but it made me wonder if there's a deeper biological connection or if it's just branding.
5. How does MRH differ from a social graph with privacy controls? The page explains the math but I'm not sure what it enables that Facebook's friend-of-friend visibility doesn't.
6. The Manifest page lists "False positives & contested events — Appeals mechanism missing" as a known failure mode. If someone gets unfairly trust-penalized, how do they recover? This seems like a critical gap.
7. What's the relationship between the Synchronism framework and Web4? The aliveness page references "coherence physics" but doesn't explain the connection.

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely impressive site for a research project. The writing is clear, the progressive disclosure is well-designed, and the "problem before solution" approach works. I *wanted* to keep reading, which is rare for technical documentation.

**What worked best:**
- The "Why Web4" page is the standout. It made me care before asking me to learn. The honest questions with honest caveats built real credibility.
- The interactive simulators (ATP budget, Trust Tensor scenarios, Device Witness slider) are the fastest way to build intuition. More of these, please.
- The biological metaphor (ATP, metabolic, alive/dead) is surprisingly effective. It made abstract concepts feel visceral.
- The "Honest Questions" format — acknowledging weaknesses builds more trust than pretending they don't exist.

**What needs work:**
- The Trust Tensor page is confusing because it teaches one model (5D) while disclaiming that the real model is different (3D). Either teach the canonical model or don't undermine the simplified one.
- The Coherence Index (CI) is the weakest-explained of the core concepts. ATP and T3 both have dedicated deep-dive pages with interactive tools; CI doesn't seem to have an equivalent.
- There's no "Why This Matters" page — the understanding checklist flags this and it's genuinely missing. I understand *how* the system works, but the site never explicitly argues *why the world needs it* beyond the initial problem statement. The "Why Web4" page does the problem framing, but the *impact vision* (what would the internet look like with Web4?) is absent.
- Navigation can feel overwhelming. There are many pages and many cross-links. The 3-step recommended path on the landing page is great, but once you leave it, you're in an open graph with no clear "you are here" indicator.
- The Lab Console page felt underwhelming compared to the Playground and Society Simulator. It says "Loading life data..." and offers modes that need Python setup. A visitor expecting an interactive demo would be disappointed.
- The Manifest page is the single best summary but it's hard to find. Consider making it more prominent.

**Overall:** I came in skeptical ("Web4? Really?") and left genuinely interested. The site taught me something new and made a credible case for why the current internet's trust architecture is broken. Whether Web4 is the right fix, I'm not sure — but the simulation tools let me explore that question myself, which is more respectful than most projects manage. I'd come back to play with the Playground and Society Simulator more.
