## Unanswered Questions
1. **What does the actual developer integration look like?** The Why Web4 FAQ mentions "no public SDK yet" and a Go library with 55 tests. If I were a developer wanting to build on this, what's the realistic path from "interested" to "prototyping"? The site is great at explaining concepts but light on developer on-ramps.
2. **How does the system handle community splits or forks?** If a community has a fundamental disagreement and splits into two groups, what happens to trust scores, ATP balances, and member relationships? The federation economics page probably covers this, but I didn't visit it this session.
3. **What's the computational overhead of all this trust calculation?** Every action involves ATP cost, T3 updates, CI checks, V3 scoring, MRH boundary computation, witness verification... at internet scale, is this computationally feasible? The Why Web4 FAQ acknowledged "billion-user scale unproven" but I'd like to understand the bottlenecks better.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoughtfully constructed educational sites I've encountered for a technical research project. The progressive disclosure works: I went from zero Web4 knowledge to solid understanding in 40 minutes across 14 pages, and at no point did I feel lost or overwhelmed.

The strongest design choices:
- **Problem before solution**: Every concept starts with a recognizable frustration before introducing the mechanism. I never had to ask "why should I care?"
- **Plain English first, acronyms second**: The "4 ideas, one at a time" pattern on Why Web4, the numbered cards with human-readable headings, the "Shorthand:" labels — all excellent.
- **Concrete before abstract**: The Day in Web4 wireframes and the First Contact narrative made the abstract economics tangible before any math appeared.
- **Honest about limitations**: "No timeline commitments," "Can't afford TPM? Creates access gap...real challenge, not dismissed," "Billion-user scale unproven." This honesty built trust (ironic, for a trust project).

The friction points I logged are all minor — polish-level issues, not structural problems. The biggest single improvement would be softening the word "attestation" in the First Contact page, since that's an early-journey page where visitors haven't yet learned the vocabulary.

The site has clearly matured significantly. The entry points are well-curated, the interactive elements reinforce rather than replace the prose, and the navigation between concept pages and interactive labs is well-bridged. I'd return to explore the Federation Economics, Coherence Index, and deeper interactive simulations.
