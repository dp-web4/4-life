## Unanswered Questions
1. **How does the 5% ATP transfer fee interact with team collaboration?** The FAQ mentions teams earn together, but if a project manager distributes ATP to team members, does each distribution lose 5%? That seems punitive for organizational structures.
2. **What happens to a "dead" agent's trust data?** If an agent dies from energy exhaustion and gets reborn, does their old identity's history remain visible to others? Can people look up the old entity?
3. **How does the system handle context collapse?** If I'm a nurse giving medical advice in a health community AND a comedy writer in a humor community, and someone from the health community sees my comedy persona, how does role separation actually work in practice? The T3 per-role system sounds good in theory but the boundary mechanics are unclear.
4. **What's the minimum viable community size for the trust economics to actually work?** The FAQ says "functional at 5-10" but the quality ramp system requires diverse confirmations from trusted users. Five people confirming each other's work doesn't feel like enough signal.
5. **How does Web4 handle lurkers?** Most online communities are 90% lurkers who read but rarely post. If reading is free but posting costs ATP, what incentivizes lurkers to participate? Their ATP just sits untouched — is there decay? Do they contribute to the trust graph at all?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thorough, well-organized explainer sites I've encountered for a complex technical concept. The progressive disclosure works — I went from "what is Web4?" to understanding the full lifecycle in under an hour, and I didn't feel lost at any point.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- The honesty. Admitting "nobody adopts it" as the #1 risk, saying "we don't know if this works at scale," acknowledging unsolved problems — this builds genuine trust. Most projects oversell; this one undersells and over-delivers on transparency.
- The Day in Web4 mockups. Seeing trust scores next to messages and reviews made the abstract visceral. I want that inbox.
- The multiple entry points. TL;DR for impatient visitors, Learning Journey for methodical learners, interactive labs for hands-on explorers. Every learning style is served.
- Problem-before-solution framing throughout. I always understood WHY before WHAT.

**What could improve:**
- Acronym density. ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, MRH, SAL — that's eight acronyms for a visitor to absorb. The concepts behind them are clear, but the alphabet soup creates unnecessary cognitive load. Consider using plain language more often and treating acronyms as optional technical labels.
- Content overlap. Day in Web4, Your Internet, and parts of Why Web4 cover similar ground with slightly different framings. A first-time visitor hitting all three will notice the repetition.
- The glossary's research origins section. Epistemic Proprioception and Modal Awareness are fascinating but belong in a research context, not a visitor-facing glossary. They signal "this is an AI consciousness project" when the rest of the site carefully signals "this is a practical trust infrastructure project."

**The big picture:** I came in skeptical and left genuinely interested. The site doesn't just explain Web4 — it demonstrates the kind of transparency and intellectual honesty that Web4 is supposed to enable. That's either brilliant design or genuine integrity. Either way, it works.
