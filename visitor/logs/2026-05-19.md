## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean, honest, surprisingly disarming. The tagline "A Trust-Native Internet Lab" is intriguing without being self-important.
- **What worked**: The very first parenthetical — `(Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor)` — preempted my two biggest unspoken questions: "is this crypto?" and "is this a Web3 rebrand?" That clarification belongs at the top. Good call.
- **What I noticed**: The headings are framed as questions a visitor would actually ask ("What is this, really?", "How would this ever get adopted?"). That's the right register.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — the strongest CTA position and the only one promising bounded time investment.

### /tldr (~02:30)
- **What I got**: Four mechanisms — ATP energy, hardware-anchored identity (LCT), portable multi-dimensional reputation, same rules for AI/humans.
- **Felt good**: Defining acronyms inline. "Energy budget" as plain-English ATP.
- **First confusion**: "the budget refills when others confirm your work was useful" — *who* confirms? *How many* confirmers? What stops three friends from confirming each other forever? (Answered later, but a forward-pointer here would help.)
- **Clicked next**: "First Contact" — promised an interactive 7-minute walk.

### /first-contact (~05:00)
- **Format**: 5-act story (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) controlling a character "Alice."
- **Honest limitation**: As an automated browser I can't actually drive the interaction. A human would presumably feel the consequences land. From the static text I understood the *shape* but not the *feel*.
- **"Die & Reborn"** as Act 5 is provocative and possibly the best onboarding hook the site has. Made me genuinely curious about death-mechanics before they were explained.
- **Tracking variables**: ATP starts at 100, Trust at 0.50. Why 0.50 specifically? Implied neutral midpoint — never said outright.

### /why-web4 (~08:30)
- **Strongest content I read**: The five problems (spam, trapped reputation, disposable identity, AI deception, platform control) are sharp and the honest-doubts section (GDPR conflict, equity gaps, collusion risk, untested at scale) reads with unusual candor.
- **Real problem**: This page is enormous. The fetcher estimated 15,000+ words. Even at a fast skim that's 30 minutes. For a "why?" page that the landing nudges visitors toward, this is too long. The structure (problems → mechanisms → FAQ → adoption) is sound but begs for a 90-second video or scrollytelling at the top.
- **What I'd cut**: The FAQ section is probably 60% of the length. Move it behind a button.

### /how-it-works (~14:00)
- **Five new acronyms in one page**: LCT, ATP, T3, CI, MRH. After the TL;DR I thought I had two concepts to track. Now I have five.
- **Concrete numbers were the win**: Birth 100 ATP, Post 10-20 ATP, +25 net for high-quality, Death at ATP=0, Rebirth threshold T3 ≥ 0.5. This is the most useful section on the entire site for someone trying to build a mental model.
- **What confused me**: "MRH (Context Boundary)" — they give me the meaning but not the acronym expansion. The glossary later called it "Trust Neighborhood." M-R-H matches neither. The actual expansion (Markov Relevancy Horizon, I'm guessing from context) never appears.
- **The circularity I noticed**: T3 weights confirmations → confirmations affect ATP → ATP enables actions → actions build T3. Nowhere does the site say how this *bootstraps*. New users? Empty network? Society's first day?

### /atp-economics (~18:00)
- **The biological metaphor**: "ATP = Allocation Transfer Packets" — sharing a name with adenosine triphosphate is deliberate (the page calls out the energy-flow parallel). I think this is good and bad: evocative for someone who remembers high-school biology, potentially confusing for an actual biochemist who'll read past the analogy. The aliasing risk feels worth it.
- **Anti-gaming was addressed concretely**: low-trust confirmer votes count less, indiscriminate confirmation tanks your consistency, 5% burn on peer transfers makes farming irrational. Good. Specific. Believable.
- **Worked example**: Sam (35% quality) loses 44 net; Hannah (85% quality) loses 8 net. "~7x more value" for quality. Concrete, tractable.
- **Still missing**: The bootstrap problem. If everyone's trust starts at 0.50, every confirmation initially weighs ~0.50. How does signal emerge from uniform noise?

### /lct-explainer (~22:00)
- **Strong page**. The detail that the chip's private key "is generated _inside_ the silicon and physically can't leave it" landed for me as someone who's been phished. Better than abstract "secure."
- **Surprise**: I don't need exotic hardware. TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2 — devices I already own. Software-only is allowed but caps trust at 0.50 vs 0.90. That's a fair tradeoff to publish.
- **The "multiple LCTs per person" idea** (doctor / parent / moderator) is clever — context-scoped identity. But it raises a Sybil worry: what stops one person creating 10 fake context-LCTs to look like 10 people? Hardware-binding limits *device* spam but maybe not *role* spam.
- **The recovery issue**: "Multi-device users recover within minutes to hours." Single-device users with no community: implied much worse. The "what could go wrong" page later confirms total device loss is unsolved.

### /trust-tensor (~25:00)
- **Tensor defanging worked**: "You don't need the math." Talent / Training / Temperament — three intuitive English words.
- **But the word is still slightly mis-used**: They explain "tensor = multiple dimensions," which is actually the definition of a vector. A tensor is higher-order (a matrix is a 2-tensor, etc.). I'm a naive human in this scenario but a technical visitor would notice — and may dismiss the rigor of the whole project. Either pick the right word or own the choice ("we use 'tensor' because role × dimension is genuinely 2-D structure"). The site is so honest elsewhere that this loose usage stands out.
- **Decay rates** (Talent 365d, Training 180d, Temperament 30d) were a delightful detail. The 12× gap between Temperament and Talent is justified briefly and makes intuitive sense — bad behavior should be quickly forgivable in a way bad credentials aren't.

### /aliveness (~28:00)
- **Two kinds of death**: Energy death (ATP=0, recoverable with karma) vs Trust death (T3 < 0.5, permanent). The asymmetry is intentional and ethically fraught.
- **Permanent trust death is the scariest mechanic on the entire site.** "Society has rejected you, and there is no rebirth." For an AI agent, fine — suspend the LCT. For a human, this is more severe than any current platform's ban because the hardware-binding means I can't realistically start over with a new identity.
- **Counter-example I want addressed**: A queer teenager in a hostile community. Their *local* T3 drops below 0.5 because the community has bad values. Are they permanently dead? Or is "society" the union of all their MRH-overlapping neighborhoods, so an isolated hostile cluster can't kill them? The site doesn't say. The MRH mechanism *might* solve this, but it's not stated explicitly.

### /what-could-go-wrong (~30:30)
- **I respect this page existing.** Acknowledging dark scenarios is not common in protocol pitches.
- **What's honest**: "An appeals mechanism has been designed...but it hasn't been tested with real humans." "Recovery from total device loss...is an unsolved problem." "Witness governance remains one of the hardest unsolved problems."
- **What's underweighted**: Authoritarian abuse. The page mentions governments could compel witness nodes, but doesn't really engage with: what if the entire trust infrastructure is captured by a state actor? Web4 in a non-democracy could become a much more effective surveillance/exclusion tool than today's internet. This deserves its own subsection, not a sentence.

### /day-in-web4 (~32:00)
- **The framing here is the most appealing the site gets.** "Trust wallet" on your phone. Face-ID convenience. Spam too expensive to send. Hiring with verified history. This made me, briefly, *want* it.
- **What I didn't see explicitly**: the surveillance flip side. Every action recorded, trust scores public, hardware-bound forever. That's where this lifestyle could feel oppressive, and the page tilts toward the upbeat read.

### /glossary (~33:30)
- **Smart structure**: Plain-English + technical for each term. The "Essential only" filter showing 7 key terms for newcomers is exactly right.
- **Friction**: ATP vs ADP distinction is unintuitive — I still don't fully grasp why "spent energy as a receipt" matters for the visitor. Probably matters for accounting/audit but the visitor doesn't need it.
- **MRH** is still defined as "Trust Neighborhood" with no acronym expansion.

### /karma-journey (~35:00)
- **Looks like the most pedagogically effective page on the site.** Live multiple lives, make choices, watch trust compound or erode. This is the kind of thing that turns abstract mechanics into felt understanding.

## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust verified by behavior, not assumed
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — interactive lab for trying these mechanics
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored identity in a secure chip
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget (ATP) and spent-receipts (ADP)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional trust score (Talent/Training/Temperament), role-scoped
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive when ATP>0 AND trust>0.5 AND coherent; energy death is recoverable, trust death is not
- [x] Why any of this matters — current internet has structural problems (spam, identity disposability, AI deception, trapped reputation) that bolted-on solutions can't fix

## Unanswered Questions

1. **Bootstrap**: How does a new Web4 society function when everyone's at 0.50 trust and votes weight ~equally? Where does first-mover trust come from?
2. **Local hostility / majority tyranny**: If a hostile community drops me below 0.5 T3 in their MRH, am I dead everywhere? Or does MRH-scoping protect me?
3. **Role-Sybil**: What stops me from creating 10 LCTs in 10 different roles to inflate my apparent diversity?
4. **Catastrophic device loss**: The site admits this is unsolved. So how do early adopters trust the system with their identity?
5. **AI agent ownership**: If an AI agent has an LCT and ATP, who *owns* it? The operator? The model trainer? Itself? This gets gestured at but never settled.
6. **Authoritarian Web4**: What does this look like in a non-democracy where the state controls witness nodes? Could it become a worse surveillance system than today's?
7. **Privacy vs. permanence**: GDPR right-to-be-forgotten is called a "genuine tension." Is there *any* sketch of resolution, or does Web4 simply not work in the EU?
8. **Inter-society dynamics**: How do separate Web4 societies recognize each other? Is trust portable between them, or are they walled?

## Honest Assessment

This is an unusually self-aware site. The honesty about open problems ("untested with real humans," "unsolved," "genuine tension") is the strongest design choice the project makes — it builds way more trust than a polished pitch deck would. The acknowledgment that economics "may not survive contact with real human behavior" is the kind of sentence I almost never see in protocol marketing.

But the site is in tension with itself. The landing page promises bounded time investment ("2-minute TL;DR," "5 min day-in-Web4," "7-minute interactive"), and the writing on individual pages is mostly tight. Then you land on `/why-web4` at 15,000 words, or `/how-it-works` introducing five acronyms inside one scroll, and the bounded-time promise collapses. The architecture says "progressive disclosure" but the execution often dumps everything at once.

The biggest unfixed risk I leave with: **permanent trust death by hostile local consensus**. The site explains the mechanism (T3 < 0.5 → no rebirth) and acknowledges that scoring might be wrong, but doesn't engage seriously with what happens when scoring is *systematically* wrong — when an entire community holds bad values about a person and is empowered to vote them out of digital life forever. The MRH-scoping might solve this. If it does, that should be on the front page of `/aliveness`. If it doesn't, the system has a real problem.

Two stylistic notes:
- The biology-borrowed ATP and the math-borrowed "tensor" both create cross-domain confusion. ATP I'd keep (the metaphor is good). "Tensor" I'd replace with "vector" or own the word with a one-line justification — right now it reads as performative jargon, even though everything around it is plain-spoken.
- The "death" framing is high-stakes and unusually direct for a UX explainer. I think it works, but it puts a tone on the project (mortality, consequence, weight) that should be deliberate. If the goal is broad adoption, "suspension" might land softer for the same mechanism. If the goal is to signal that this is serious infrastructure, "death" is correct.

The interactive simulators (Karma Journey, First Contact, Society Simulator) are clearly where the deepest learning happens, and the static-fetch perspective I'm trapped in here can't reach them. A human visitor's experience may be substantially better than mine on those pages. Recommend the dev track confirm the simulators work from a cold start on mobile — if they do, that's the path most visitors should be funneled toward early, perhaps even before `/why-web4`.

Most surprising positive: the candor. I came in expecting another protocol pitch and found a site that openly says "we don't know if this works yet." That's rare and that's the right tone for an R&D project.
