## Unanswered Questions

2. **How does quality measurement work for subjective content?** The ATP economics page explains community confirmation, but what happens with content that's high quality but unpopular (e.g., whistleblower reports, dissenting scientific views)? The V3 system weights veracity/validity over valuation, but I'm not sure that fully solves the "unpopular truth" problem.
3. **What's the actual timeline vision?** The project says "no timeline commitments" which is honest, but is this a 2-year, 5-year, or 20-year horizon to even Tier 1 deployment? Some indication of pace would help set expectations.
4. **How do cross-federation disputes work?** I understand intra-community governance (SAL framework, appeals), but what happens when two federated communities disagree about a member's behavior? The federation economics page mentions "cross-society policy conflicts" with three resolution strategies, but concrete dispute scenarios would help.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoroughly constructed educational sites I've encountered for a technical research project. The progression from problem (Why Web4?) through story (First Contact) through hands-on experimentation (Karma Journey, Society Simulator, Playground) through honest self-critique (What Could Go Wrong) is expertly designed.

What works exceptionally well:
- **Problem-first framing**: Every concept page starts with "here's what's broken" before introducing the solution. This keeps me motivated to learn the solution.
- **Plain English first, acronyms second**: The "4 ideas, one at a time" pattern on Why Web4 and the parenthetical expansions everywhere else mean I never felt lost in jargon.
- **Concrete examples**: Alice's story, the Day in Web4 mockups, Maria's stolen phone, the bot farm — these transform abstract mechanics into visceral understanding.
- **Radical honesty**: The "What Could Go Wrong" page, the "unsolved problems" list, the repeated "this is research, not a product" disclaimers — all of this builds trust (ironically, exactly what Web4 is about).
- **Progressive disclosure**: Collapsible details sections, concept sequence navigation, multiple entry points at different depths — I never felt forced into more detail than I wanted.

What could improve:
- The only real friction I experienced was information density on the longer pages (How It Works, Federation Economics). But both are "deep dive" pages that I navigated to deliberately, so this feels intentional rather than problematic.
- The recurring "what does the UI actually look like?" question — the Day in Web4 mockups are great but static. An interactive prototype (even with fake data) would be the single most compelling addition.

Overall: I arrived knowing nothing about Web4 and left understanding the full system — identity, energy, trust, coherence, neighborhoods, aliveness, governance, federation. Every concept was taught through the site itself, not inferred from background knowledge. That's a remarkable achievement for a system this complex.
