## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, bold problem statement — "The Internet Has a Trust Problem." Immediately relatable. The four core rules are displayed prominently: identity anchored in devices, every action costs energy, permanent consequences, equal rules for AI and humans.
- Navigation is well-organized with "Start Here," "Core Concepts," "Interactive Labs," and "AI Agents" sections.
- Clicked: "Web4 in 2 Minutes" — seemed like the fastest path to understanding.
- Confusion points: The adoption tiers (Wrapper through Native) are mentioned but not explained at this point. The formula-like notation isn't shown here but I can sense there's a lot of acronyms ahead.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes (/tldr) (02:00)
- Opens with a punchy problem description: "The internet has no memory." Great hook.
- The four pillars (energy costs, hardware identity, earned trust, unified human/AI rules) are clearly presented.
- The "Current Status" section is refreshingly honest — "active research with working simulations," no live deployment, no timeline commitments.
- Good onramp links at the bottom (Why Web4, First Contact, Karma Journey, Society Simulator).
- Confusion points: "Linked Context Tokens" and "ATP" are introduced by name but I don't yet have intuition for what they feel like in practice.

### Why Web4? (/why-web4) (04:00)
- This page is MASSIVE. The FAQ section alone has 80+ Q&A entries across seven categories.
- The problem framing is excellent — spam, reputation silos, bad actor resets, AI deception, platform power. Each paired with why previous solutions (moderation, CAPTCHAs, blockchain, OAuth) failed.
- The four mechanisms (ATP energy, hardware identity, multi-dimensional trust, trust neighborhoods) are clearly explained.
- FAQ highlights: The "Is this a social credit system?" answer is well-handled. The flat-earth forums question is brilliant — "Web4 doesn't prevent communities from being wrong. It prevents wrong communities from projecting authority beyond their boundaries."
- Confusion points: The sheer volume is overwhelming for a first visit. I started skimming after the third FAQ category. The page tries to answer every possible objection, which is thorough but exhausting.

### First Contact (/first-contact) (10:00)
- Alice's 7-step journey is the best teaching tool on the site. Walking through a concrete character's experience — posting, helping, earning, spamming, recovering, dying, being reborn — makes the abstract system feel real.
- The death/rebirth mechanic is surprisingly intuitive when shown through a story: energy death is recoverable if you built trust; trust death is permanent.
- Interactive elements (watching Alice's journey unfold) make it engaging rather than lecture-like.
- Confusion points: The "signed statement" attestation mechanism is mentioned but not really explained. How does a recipient's confirmation actually get recorded?

### How It Works (/how-it-works) (14:00)
- This is the mechanics overview. Five layers — LCT (identity), MRH (context boundary), ATP (energy), T3 (trust tensor), CI (coherence index) — are described together.
- The lifecycle (Birth -> Life -> Death -> Rebirth) is clear. The governance framework (SAL) with society/authority/law layers is introduced.
- "Alignment without compliance acceptable; compliance without alignment never acceptable" — that's a powerful principle but I'm not sure what it means in practice.
- Confusion points: CI (Coherence Index) multipliers of up to 1.4x are mentioned but the trigger conditions feel vague. What exactly counts as a "sudden pattern shift"?

### Society Simulator (/society-simulator) (17:00)
- Five scenario presets (Cooperative Majority, Hostile World, etc.) with 12 agents using different strategies.
- The four-phase description (Exploration -> Coalition Building -> Defector Isolation -> Equilibrium) gives me a mental model of what to expect.
- "Play as Agent" option sounds fun — making real trust decisions inside the simulation.
- Confusion points: The page assumes I know what ATP and Trust Tensor mean. It does link to First Contact but creating a prerequisite chain could lose impatient visitors.

### Day in Web4 (/day-in-web4) (19:00)
- This is excellent. Ten concrete scenarios covering morning login, helping others, hiring, reviews, false accusations, device upgrade, lost phone — all grounded in everyday internet frustrations.
- The wireframe mockups (Mail, Talent, Reviews, Social, Marketplace) make the vision tangible. "3 real messages instead of 12 with 9 spam" is immediately compelling.
- The adoption path (browser extension -> standalone apps -> OS integration) is realistic and staged.
- Confusion points: None significant. This is one of the most accessible pages on the site.

### Trust Tensor (/trust-tensor) (22:00)
- The three dimensions (Talent, Training, Temperament) are well-explained with the surgeon-vs-mechanic example driving home why trust shouldn't transfer across domains.
- Interactive simulator lets you pick roles and see how scenarios affect each dimension differently.
- Alice's contrasting scores across three roles (Data Analyst: 90%, Project Manager: 76%, Mechanic: 27%) is a clear illustration.
- Confusion points: The relationship between T3 (trust) and V3 (value) requires careful reading. Are they always evaluated together? Can you have high trust but produce low-value work?

### Aliveness (/aliveness) (24:00)
- Three conditions for aliveness: ATP > 0, Trust > 0.5, Coherence Index coherent. Clean and measurable.
- The death mechanism is stark — "you die immediately" at zero ATP — but recovery through accumulated reputation softens it.
- Confusion points: "Aliveness" as a measurable, binary state applied equally to humans and AI agents feels philosophically loaded. The page seems aware of this but doesn't fully resolve the tension between "this is just a system metric" and the language of life/death.

### ATP Economics (/atp-economics) (26:00)
- The quality ramp example is the best thing on this page: 30% quality = 0 ATP earned, 85% quality = 42 ATP earned on a 50-ATP task. Makes the incentive structure visceral.
- The 5% transfer tax with compound loss math (19% after 4 transfers, 50% after 14) elegantly kills circular farming.
- Confusion points: ADP (the "receipt" token) is introduced but its actual lifecycle is vague. When exactly does ADP convert back to ATP? Who triggers that conversion? The bootstrapping problem — how does the first contribution get validated with no established community — isn't addressed.

### What Could Go Wrong (/what-could-go-wrong) (28:00)
- Seven failure modes, honestly presented. Hardware gatekeeping, false positive penalties, economic attacks, scalability collapse — all real risks.
- The tone is genuinely self-critical, not defensive. "Every system has failure modes. Hiding them doesn't make them go away."
- The unsolved problems section (witness bootstrapping, dispute resolution, governance at scale) is refreshingly candid.
- Confusion points: None. This page does exactly what it should.

### Other Pages Browsed (29:00-35:00)
- **LCT Explainer**: Good breakdown of hardware binding and device witnesses. The interactive trust score slider showing how 1-5 devices affect trust is helpful. Recovery mechanisms if ALL devices are lost simultaneously remain unclear.
- **Learning Journey** (/learn): Well-structured with 16 concepts across 4 skill levels. Clear starting point (Identity, Energy, Trust = 22 minutes). Good progressive disclosure.
- **Glossary**: 38 terms across 5 categories. Useful as reference but circular dependencies between terms could confuse newcomers reading linearly.
- **Trust Dilemmas**: Five interactive scenarios testing conventional approaches vs. Web4 solutions. Good pedagogical design but requires JavaScript for full experience.
- **Playground**: Five pre-built experiments with customizable parameters. Clean workflow (adjust -> run -> view -> iterate). Good for hands-on learners.
- **Your Internet**: Personalized framing — pick your frustrations, see how Web4 addresses them. Smart engagement hook.
- **Exploration Not Evaluation** (AI section): Interesting research framing but feels tangential for a first-time visitor. More relevant to AI researchers than general audience.
- **Understanding Consciousness** (AI section): Claims about AI consciousness thresholds presented as empirical findings. Feels speculative and out of place next to the otherwise rigorous trust-system content.

## Honest Assessment

This is an impressive, ambitious project that does a remarkably good job of progressive disclosure. The site takes a genuinely complex system and makes most of it accessible through concrete examples, interactive tools, and honest problem-first framing.

**What works well:**
- The "Start Here" path (TL;DR -> Why Web4 -> First Contact) is a strong onramp
- "Day in Web4" is the most compelling page — it makes the abstract concrete
- First Contact's Alice story teaches through narrative, not lecture
- The self-critical tone ("What Could Go Wrong") builds credibility
- The FAQ section is extraordinarily thorough, even if overwhelming
- Interactive labs (Playground, Society Simulator, Trust Dilemmas) let you learn by doing

**What needs work:**
- The Why Web4 FAQ is a wall of text that could benefit from better information architecture
- ADP's mechanics are the biggest conceptual gap — it's introduced as important but never fully explained
- The AI Agents section (consciousness, exploration-not-evaluation) feels like it belongs on a different site. The trust-system content is rigorous and grounded; the consciousness content reads as speculative. The tonal mismatch undermines the credibility of the stronger material.
- The community bootstrapping problem — how does the first person in a new community earn trust? — is conspicuously absent given how much else is addressed.

**Overall**: I came in knowing nothing and left understanding the core vision, the key mechanisms, and the honest limitations. That's a success. The site respects my intelligence without assuming prior knowledge (mostly). I would return to explore the interactive labs more deeply, and I'd recommend it to anyone interested in internet governance or trust systems.

The strongest signal of quality: the site is more honest about its limitations than most deployed products are about theirs.
