## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: The site frames itself confidently as a "Trust-Native Internet Lab." The headline is interesting — "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default" — but I had to read that twice to know what it actually means. The word "trust-native" appears central but feels like jargon I'm expected to absorb on first contact.
- **What helped**: The opening problem statement (spam, no consequences, throwaway accounts) is grounded and lands fast. I recognized my own internet immediately.
- **What helped more**: The five-card summary breaking Web4 into "hardware-bound identity, energy budgets, three-dimensional trust, neighborhood limits, and behavioral consistency" gave me an immediate scaffolding. I didn't understand each card yet, but I knew the shape of what I was about to learn.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — the visual hierarchy made this the obvious entry point.

### /tldr (02:30)
- It's not actually 2 minutes. Closer to 3–4 if I'm reading carefully.
- Helpful claim upfront: "acronyms are spelled out the first time they appear." I appreciated the honesty, but six acronyms (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI) in one short page is still six things to track.
- I left this page knowing four ideas: energy that costs you to act, identity tied to hardware, multi-dimensional reputation, equal rules for humans and AI. That felt like progress.
- It recommended "First Contact" next, which felt like a natural step.

### /first-contact (06:00)

### /karma-journey (15:00)

- Karma-as-mechanics, not karma-as-mysticism, was reassuring.

### /lct-explainer (28:00)

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a trust-native internet design where actions have costs, identity is hardware-anchored, and reputation is portable, role-contextual, and consistency-checked
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — a fractal lab letting you experience these dynamics through simulations rather than reading specs
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token: hardware-anchored, multi-witness, pseudonymous identity
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — Allocation Transfer Packets: energy budgets for actions. Quality work earns them back at ~7x for excellent vs barely-passing. ADP is the discharged form (I'm a little fuzzy on the receipt mechanics)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3 (Talent/Training/Temperament), measured per role, contextual. V3 is the sibling for what you produce
- [x] How agents live/die — alive requires ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, and coherent CI. Energy death recoverable; trust death permanent. Death = suspended license, not deletion
- [x] Why any of this matters — current internet incentives reward bad behavior because it's free. Web4 inverts that economics. Whether human communities actually respond this way is the open question.

All seven boxes checked. **Solid** understanding by my own honest assessment.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Why is the CI exponent 2?** The formula effective_trust = T3 × CI² appears on multiple pages but I never saw it justified. I inferred it but I'm not certain.
2. **Who runs the witness infrastructure in practice?** I saw the "your devices witness each other" framing, but in a deployed network, are there also witness nodes I don't own? Who runs them? Who pays for them?
3. **What's the ADP→ATP recharge mechanism actually look like, mechanically?** I get the high-level idea ("quality work earns ATP back") but the loop — receipts, validation, who confirms quality, on what timescale — was less concrete.
4. **What's a synthon?** Saw the term in the glossary but never grasped it. I suspect it's "a coordinated multi-agent entity" but I'm guessing.
5. **For real-world deployment, who decides the trust thresholds?** A 0.5 threshold is a design choice — but who sets it for, say, a journalism platform vs a social network? Are these per-app or global?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the better-explained technical research sites I've used. The progressive disclosure works: I started with vague curiosity, got a 3-minute scaffolding from TL;DR, watched a worked example play out in First Contact, then explored individual concepts and synthesized them through How It Works.

**What works extraordinarily well**:
- The Alice walkthrough on First Contact is the strongest single pedagogical moment.
- Worked examples with specific numbers (Sam −44 vs Hannah −8 ATP, ~0.93 vs ~0.90 ROI, 0.9² = 0.81 vs 0.6² = 0.36) build trust faster than rhetorical claims.
- The honesty about unsolved problems is unusual and earns credibility.
- The visual hierarchy and CTA placement on the landing page successfully channel a new visitor to the right entry point.

**What I'd push on**:
- The Coherence Index dimensions read as surveillance until the privacy framing arrives. Lead with the concern, then describe the design constraints.
- /a-day-in-web4 is the page I most wanted, and I found it last. Surface it earlier.
- The CI² exponent — easy fix, just say it.
- The "research prototype vs deployment" framing is doing a lot of work across multiple pages. A single, prominent site-wide framer might let individual pages stop repeating it.

I'd come back. I'd send this to a thoughtful technical friend. I'd send it to a non-technical curious person only with the recommendation to read /tldr → /first-contact → /a-day-in-web4 and skip the deeper pages unless they wanted to dive in.

The site succeeds at its hardest job: it makes a complex, abstract proposal feel like something you could experience rather than just be told about.
