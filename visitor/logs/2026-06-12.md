## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clear and disarming. "Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" answers my first two suspicious questions (crypto? scam?) before I ask them. "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" is a hook I immediately relate to.
- The Five Core Ideas (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) in plain language before any acronyms — good. The "What is this, really?" honesty box ("research proposal, not a product") sets expectations well.
- Clicked: the recommended "2-minute TL;DR".
- Confusion points: minor — the acronym soup (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI) is visible on the page but at least flagged with "full definitions in the glossary".

### /tldr (03:00)
- Problem → Idea → Result → Status structure works. I genuinely got the pitch in ~2 minutes.
- "Your 10-year reputation on Twitter means nothing on Reddit" — concrete, felt.
- Confusion: the page promises plain English with every term "spelled out the first time it appears", but "witness constellation" and "Tier 1 wrapper" get only gloss treatment.
- One claim made me pause: reputation is "multi-dimensional… portable across platforms, and **permanent**." (Remember this — it gets contradicted later.)
- Odd detail: "This 77-page interactive explainer site." I can't verify that and it reads like internal inventory talk, not visitor-facing copy.
- Followed recommendation #1 → First Contact.

### /first-contact (07:00)
- Format is an interactive 5-act walkthrough of "Alice" (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn). The two meters (ATP starts 100, trust starts 0.50) are introduced cleanly.
- As static text before interacting, Act 5 is a tease: ATP hits 0, then a mysterious "+12 bonus" appears. The pre-interaction content never says what "death" means or where the +12 comes from. The TL;DR even advertised "discover what 'death' means here" — but the answer is locked inside the interaction.
- Also unclear from static text: who "confirms" Alice's work.

### /why-web4 (15:00)
- The strongest page so far. Root-cause diagnosis (zero-cost spam, siloed reputation, infinite resets, AI agents, platform power) → why past fixes failed (moderation, CAPTCHAs, blockchain, social login) → "All these solutions treat symptoms, not causes."
- The FAQ is impressive and honest — the social-credit comparison (voluntary / peer-rated / federated / pseudonymous / exit-able) directly answered my biggest worry.
- Friction: it's enormous. 40+ FAQ entries; nobody reads it all. Reference depth is fine, but the main text alone took me ~10 minutes.
- Jargon leaks: V3 and SAL appear before being defined. CI is named early but only explained in the FAQ.
- Two claims bothered me:
  1. "Security formally proven" (424 attack vectors, 5 theorems) sits next to an admission that sophisticated collusion "is the hardest case" and remains open. What does "proven" mean then?
  2. Reputation called "permanent" again — yet the same page mentions trust decay half-lives (Temperament 30 days). Permanent and decaying can't both be right as written.

### /atp-economics (25:00)
- Mechanics are concrete: start 100 ATP, spam nets −5, meaningful contribution nets +10, high-value creation nets +30. Quality ramp (35% quality earns ~6 back, 85% earns ~42 — "~7x more") with the Sam vs. Hannah example is genuinely clarifying.
- **My biggest stumble of the whole session happened here.** The page says "the cap means you can't profit on a single action, only recover its cost" — but the action table on the same page shows "Net: +10" and "Net: +30". I sat with that contradiction for several minutes until, much later in the page, it resolves: "recharge refunds, payment earns" — your own confirmed work recharges at most its cost, while commissioned tasks pay what the work is worth. That distinction is load-bearing and should be the FIRST thing said about earning, not a late footnote.
- Lesser confusions: what happens when receipts "age out over a window of weeks" (zero ATP forever, or pending?); formula variables (confirmer_trust, received_value_fraction) given ranges but not derivations; V3 appears as "the output-quality half of your reputation" with no prior introduction.
- ADP is described as a "receipt" — okay, but when/how it converts back is fuzzy.

### /lct-explainer (33:00)
- "Your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied" — best analogy on the site.
- Device-count trust ceilings (1 device = 0.50, 2 = 0.75, 3+ = 0.90) and hardware tiers (TPM 0.90 > Secure Enclave 0.85 > FIDO2 0.75 > software-only 0.50) are concrete and memorable.
- Device loss IS answered (multi-device: minutes-to-hours; single device: 3–7 days social recovery; software-only: start over) — but it's buried in a later section while the breezy "no 'forgot password' needed" appears in the takeaways. The reassurance and the friction live in different places.
- The first-device bootstrap question ("who witnesses the first witness?") is answered — manufacturer-burned key — but too deep in the page. The page itself admits "witness" means two different things (my devices vs. network nodes); the acknowledgment helps but the overload still costs effort.

### /aliveness (40:00)
- This page answered my standing question fast and well: "yes, 'death' here is about you too… what can die is your Web4 standing — that identity's right to post, vote, and transact — never the person behind it." Thank you. This is exactly what First Contact's static content lacks.
- The two-deaths table (Energy death = recoverable if T3 ≥ 0.5, like bankruptcy; Trust death = permanent, like losing a professional license) is the clearest framing of consequences on the site.
- Effective trust = Base × CI², with the worked intuition (CI 0.9 keeps ~81%, CI 0.6 keeps ~36%) — good.
- Confusion: the three-criteria table says CI must be "coherent" without a number, while elsewhere CI < 0.5 seems to be the floor and CI 0.5–0.7 is "limited trust." Just state the threshold.

### /trust-tensor (44:00)
- Talent/Training/Temperament with decay half-lives (365 / 180 / 30 days). The asymmetry — character must be re-demonstrated monthly, skill persists a year — is a genuinely interesting design choice and well explained.
- Alice scoring 90% as analyst but 27% as mechanic makes role-specific trust click.
- But here the "permanent reputation" promise from /tldr formally dies: reputation demonstrably decays. The site means "persistent and portable, never resettable" — it should say that instead of "permanent."
- V3 again introduced mid-page with deferred explanation.

### /karma-journey (47:00)
- Static scaffolding is good: karma tiers (Honored >0.7, Neutral 0.3–0.7, Constrained <0.3), raw trust determines survival vs. effective trust (raw × CI²) determines next-life tier, "small dips forgiven, sustained dips punished."
- New detail that surprised me: new entities start at CI 0.85 and pay "~40% action surcharges." Neither the ~1.4× multiplier math nor why 0.85 is the starting point is shown here.
- LCT used without local definition; where the starting 100 ATP comes from isn't said here (the ATP page's "society pool starter grant" never made it over).

### /society-simulator (50:00)
- Clear question ("Can self-interested agents develop cooperation without central authority?"), five legible strategies (Cooperator/Defector/Reciprocator/Cautious/Adaptive), six presets, expected phases (Exploration → Coalition Building → Defector Isolation → Equilibrium).
- Honest about simplifications (no witness layer, no full T3). But rebirth rules for dead agents are unstated, and whether one agent's cooperation refills the other's ATP is unclear.
- Without running it, the page "reads like a manual for a game you cannot play" — the static text could include one example outcome per preset so readers learn the punchline.

### /glossary (52:00)
- 40+ terms, "Essential only" filter (7 terms), "Acronyms at a glance" box. This page does its job; I wish I'd been pushed here earlier — several mid-page confusions (V3, SAL, ADP) were already answered here.
- Definitions are consistent with the rest of the site. The "witness has two distinct jobs" note directly addresses the overload I hit on /lct-explainer. Good.
- Nit: TL;DR expands ATP as "Allocation Transfer Packets" (plural); glossary says "Allocation Transfer Packet" (singular). Trivial, but acronym expansions should match exactly sitewide.

### /what-could-go-wrong (54:00)
- The most trust-building page on the site. Eight risks with severity/likelihood, and real admissions: adoption is "the #1 existential risk"; total device loss recovery is "an unsolved problem"; the accessibility gap "codifies the existing digital divide… This is an unsolved problem."
- Surveillance, exclusion of the poor (3 billion without smartphones), algorithmic mob justice, cold start — all addressed, none hand-waved.
- One internal tension: Temperament's 30-day half-life is sold as making patient attacks hard, yet Risk #4/#6 admit Sybil attackers can "slowly build legitimate-looking trust." If decay doesn't stop patient attackers, don't imply it does.
- This page makes the "security formally proven" phrasing on /why-web4 look even more overclaimed by contrast — this page's calibrated honesty is the right register.

### /day-in-web4 (brief skim, 55:00)
- Ten moments in a day (mail, hiring, reviews, feeds, marketplace with escrow). Kitchen-sink-leak and used-camera scenarios are relatable. Numbers consistent: 100 ATP, trust 0.50, first post 3 ATP.
- "Device trust ceiling: 0.85" appears with no explanation — I only understood it because I'd already read /lct-explainer (Secure Enclave tier). A one-line tooltip would fix it.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — behavior-verified trust layer; no blockchain
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — the lab/simulations for the Web4 protocol
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-rooted identity; driver's-license analogy landed
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget + spent-receipt; *after* untangling the recharge-vs-payment knot
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — Talent/Training/Temperament per role, with decay
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — three criteria; energy death (recoverable) vs trust death (permanent)
- [x] Why any of this matters — zero-cost abuse and unaccountable resets are the root problem

## Unanswered Questions
1. When my unconfirmed work "ages out over a window of weeks," do I get zero ATP for it permanently, or does late confirmation still count?
2. Who sets a commissioned task's price, and what stops me commissioning tasks to myself through a friend (the 5% fee alone doesn't obviously kill this)?
3. Why does a new entity start at CI 0.85 specifically — what would I have to do to start higher?
4. In the society simulator, do dead agents respawn, and do their strategies change after rebirth?
5. If Temperament decay doesn't stop patient Sybil attacks (per /what-could-go-wrong), what actually does?

## Honest Assessment
This is the most coherent the pitch has felt across a single sitting. The recommended path (landing → TL;DR → First Contact → Why Web4) genuinely works: I went from suspicion ("is this crypto?") to a working mental model in under an hour, and the site's habit of pre-answering skeptical questions (social credit? blockchain? surveillance?) earns real trust. /what-could-go-wrong is the best page here — its calibrated honesty ("unsolved problem," stated twice, about its own design) made me trust everything else more.

The two things that genuinely hurt were both *self-contradictions*, not gaps: the ATP page telling me "you can't profit" inches away from a table showing +30 net (resolved, but a page-scroll too late), and the core pitch's "permanent" reputation colliding with explicit decay half-lives. A site this careful about honesty should not let its own copy disagree with itself — those are one-sentence fixes with outsized payoff, because the visitors most likely to notice are exactly the careful, skeptical ones the site is courting.

Below those: the static text of the interactive pages (First Contact, Society Simulator) still underserves readers who don't or can't run the interactions — the punchlines (what death means, what each preset demonstrates) live only inside the JavaScript. One summary sentence per interactive would close that gap.

Would I come back? Yes — I left with three of my five unanswered questions feeling researchable on the site itself (threat model, coherence index, federation pages I didn't reach). That's the mark of a good explainer: I'm leaving with better questions than I arrived with.
