## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: The headline "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" landed instantly. I know this problem — spam, fake accounts, reputations stuck on one platform. Good hook. I felt like the site understood *me* before asking me to understand *it*.
- The "Five Core Ideas (Plain English)" box (Identity / Energy / Trust / Neighborhood / Consistency) was genuinely helpful — a one-line promise for each pillar.
- But the page is **long and dense**. Below the fold there are a LOT of sections ("What is this, really?", "How would it work?", adoption tiers 1–5, three simulators, a giant sidebar with ~20 links). I felt a flicker of "where do I even start?" before I noticed the recommended CTA.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" (the page told me to, and I appreciated being told).
- **Confusion points**: The sidebar dumps acronyms before I've read anything — "Linked Context Token", "Trust Tensor (T3)", "ATP Economics", "MRH". I didn't know what any of those were yet, so they read as noise.

### TL;DR (02:30)
- This was the **clearest single page** for orienting. "The internet has no memory" is a sticky line. The three mechanisms (actions cost energy / identity tied to hardware / trust is earned and permanent) are plain.
- The Web4-vs-4-Life distinction ("Web4 is the protocol — the rules and math; 4-Life is the lab") finally told me what site I was even on. I think this should be *higher up*, maybe on the landing page itself — until here I wasn't sure if "4-Life" was a game, a product, or a manifesto.
- **Confusion points**: ATP and LCT appear as bare acronyms again. I'm told the *idea* but not yet what the letters stand for.
- **Clicked**: "First Contact →" (the featured next step).

### First Contact (06:00)
- Premise: drive "Alice" through a 7-minute interactive story with live numbers (energy dropping, trust shifting). I *like* the idea of learning by watching consequences.
- But I hit a wall of acronyms right at the top: ATP, T3, LCT, CI — four at once, before I'd been taught any of them. The four labeled doors (🔋 Energy / 🤝 Trust / 📜 Consequences / 🔍 Consistency) were the helpful part; the parenthetical acronyms were the scary part.

### Aliveness (25:00)

### Why Web4? (29:00)
- **Most trustworthy page on the site.** It names the dystopia fears out loud (social credit, surveillance, who-watches-the-watchmen) and answers honestly — exit rights + portability as the real safeguard, not "the protocol prevents it."
- It openly admits the gaps: no live network, appeals process "designed but not deployed," economics "calibrated for simulations, not real markets," the 93% anomaly-detection figure is a sim parameter. As a skeptic, being told the weak spots made me trust the strong spots more.
- This honesty is the site's superpower. I almost wish a newcomer hit *this* page earlier — it frames the whole thing as "an honest research proposal" rather than "a product pitch."

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a protocol that makes online actions cost energy, ties identity to hardware, and makes reputation portable + permanent
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — it's the *lab/simulator* for the Web4 protocol (learned this on TL;DR, not landing)
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-rooted identity in your device's security chip; the driver's-license analogy taught me, not just inferred
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — ATP is your spendable energy budget, ADP is the spent receipt; the cell-biology metaphor taught me
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — a 3-axis trust score (Talent/Training/Temperament), role-specific; the surgeon example taught me
- [~] How agents "live" and "die" — got the three conditions (ATP/Trust/CI), but the human-vs-AI-agent ambiguity left me unsure *whose* life/death we mean
- [x] Why any of this matters — the spam/silo/fresh-start/AI-impersonation framing on landing + Why Web4 made the case, and the honesty about gaps made it credible

## Honest Assessment
I came in cold and left genuinely interested — that's a real win for the site. The problem framing is excellent: "the internet has no memory" and "spam wins because accounts are free" are the kind of lines that make you nod. The best explainer pages (LCT's photocopied-license, Trust Tensor's surgeon, ATP's cell biology) use one good analogy each and earn their concepts honestly. And "Why Web4?" is unusually trustworthy — it volunteers its own weak spots instead of hiding them, which paradoxically made me believe the strong parts.

What held me back from "solid" understanding were two things. First, **acronym density** — by page three I was juggling ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, MRH, and the glossary then revealed a second tier (D-numbers, SAL, DID, R6). It feels like an iceberg, and the glossary lacks a simple A–Z lookup to bail me out. Second, and more disorienting, is the **"you" vs "your agent" ambiguity**. The site can't seem to decide whether I'm a person browsing or the operator of an AI agent, and it switches between the two without telling me — most starkly at "run out of ATP and your agent dies." That single unresolved pronoun undercut my sense of who the whole system is *for*.

So: the concepts are well-taught one at a time; the *connective tissue* (who is the subject, when does jargon get introduced, what can I do now) is where a naive visitor wobbles. Fix the human/agent framing and add a jargon escape-hatch, and this jumps from "interesting research site" to "I actually get it."
