## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Crisp and confident. The headline — "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations" — paired immediately with a one-line definition ("Web4 is a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; **no blockchain, not a Web3 successor**") told me what I was looking at before I had to ask. The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" block (spam is free, reputations siloed, unlimited fresh starts, AI deception) is a problem I recognize from my own life, so I leaned in.
- The five plain-language concept cards (Identity / Energy / Trust / Neighborhood / Consistency) gave me the shape of the thing without throwing acronyms at me. Good.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — it was explicitly marked "Recommended for first-time visitors," so the page chose for me. I appreciated not having to decide.
- Confusion points: None worth noting. The entry-point hierarchy (TL;DR / What it looks like / Structured path) is calm and well-ordered.

### 2-minute TL;DR (03:30)
- Problem → idea → status → next steps. Tight. The four ideas (ATP / LCT / Trust-as-portable-reputation / same-rules-for-humans-and-AI) each got a one-line plain-English gloss, and the acronyms were expanded inline (ATP = Allocation Transfer Packets, LCT = Linked Context Token).
- The "active research with working simulations, not a deployed product" status box answered the question I didn't know I had yet ("wait, can I sign up for this?"). It draws the real/simulated line early, which set my expectations correctly for everything after.
- Three more terms (T3, MRH, CI) were *named* here with a one-liner each and a "has its own page" pointer — felt like a promise, not a dump.
- Clicked: "First Contact."

### First Contact (06:00)
- The framing line — "Real rules, shown by simulation — not a demo of a shipped product" — is doing real work. I never once wondered whether Alice was a real person.
- The "reference map, not a quiz" intro for the four concepts (ATP/T3/LCT/CI) is the right tone. It promised I didn't have to memorize anything.
- Minor: the four concept blurbs are introductions, not definitions — "Consequences (LCT): Your record is permanent because your presence is hardware-bound" gestures at a mechanism I couldn't yet picture. But the page explicitly tells me to treat it as a map and meet the detail later, so it didn't frustrate me — it set a bookmark.

### Why Web4? (11:00)
- This is the dense one. Five root-cause problem cards, four mechanism cards (each with a plain-English translation: "ATP = energy from a personal budget," "MRH = trust fades with distance"), then a genuinely enormous FAQ (100+ questions, organized into Most Asked + topic indexes).
- The honesty is the standout: "this is research, not production" repeats, and hard questions ("If someone powerful lies about me and I'm new, does my truth even matter?") get real, caveated answers rather than spin.
- Friction (the one real snag of the session): the page **uses CI, SAL, and VCM in passing before it defines them.** ATP/LCT/T3/MRH all get their plain-English card; CI/SAL/VCM just appear. As a newcomer reading top-to-bottom I hit "SAL" and "VCM" cold and had no card to fall back on. (See friction log.)
- Density: ~15,000 words. There's an early note that "acronyms land easier after First Contact" and escape routes to the simulations, which is the right mitigation — but read straight through, this is documentation, not a walkthrough. I skimmed.

### Karma Journey (18:00)
- Interactive and fun — "live multiple lives, watch trust compound across reincarnations." Eight Turn-1 actions (cooperative / selfish / bold).
- Strong: the metaphor caveat ("maps to license suspension and reinstatement, not literal rebirth") keeps me from over-reading "reincarnation."
- The dashboard shows **two trust numbers** — Trust (Raw) 0.500 and Trust (Effective) 0.361 — and the relationship (effective = raw × CI²) plus *which number my choice moves* is spelled out at the action point. That answered the exact question I was forming ("which one matters?"). Good.
- Lingering newcomer wobble: *why* CI starts at 0.85 (not 1.0) and *why* it's squared are both stated, but the "your actions cost ~1.4× because CI is low" surcharge surprised me when I saw a "-12" action; I had to connect it back myself.

### Society Simulator (24:00)
- The biggest toy. "Run Society" with presets or "Play as Agent." Six acronyms (ATP/T3/LCT/CI/MRH/V3) all glossed in a co-located collapsible "what do these mean?" block, and a "Never seen these terms? First Contact is a 7-minute intro" pointer. Well onboarded.
- Friction (low): the *strategy* vocabulary — "defector," "reciprocator," "tit-for-tat," "coalition formation" — is assumed known on this page. I half-know "tit-for-tat" from pop science, but a true blank slate would stall on "reciprocator vs defector." The acronym key is great; the strategy key is missing.

### A Day in Web4 (29:00)
- Ten everyday scenarios (authenticate your phone, hire a freelancer, read reviews, buy used goods). This is the page that made Web4 feel *livable* rather than theoretical.
- The tense is handled honestly: "narrated in the present tense as a thought experiment — none of it ships today." And the 0–1 scale is anchored ("0.5 is neutral, everyone starts there, 1.0 is the theoretical max") before any decimal shows up. Both are things I'd have tripped on otherwise.

### LCT Explainer (33:00)
- Best single explainer on the site. The "your phone becoming a driver's license that can't be photocopied" analogy made "hardware-bound" click instantly, and the "private keys generated inside the silicon, physically can't leave it" line made it concrete.
- The witness scenario ("log in from Tokyo; phone GPS confirms Tokyo; laptop TPM signs the same session") finally made "witnessed presence" — a phrase that was vague everywhere else — into something I could picture.
- Scale anchored up front ("0.5 alive/dead line, 0.75 solid, 0.90 hardware ceiling") before the first decimal. The ceiling grid (TPM 0.90 / SE 0.85 / FIDO2 0.75 / Software 0.50) was clear, and the page pre-empts the "do I *start* at 0.90?" misread.

### ATP Economics (38:00)
- "ATP is cash, ADP is the receipt" — that one line is the keeper, and it's right there in the 60-second box (which *does* mention ADP). I left able to say what each is.
- The 5% transfer burn is explained with the worked direction (send 100, pay 105, recipient gets 100, the 5 burns to a community pool) and a rationale (stops circular farming). Clear.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (42:00)
- Talent / Training / Temperament each defined plainly; "tensor" explicitly demystified ("a 3-axis score — you don't need the math"). That sentence alone removes the scariest word on the page.
- V3 is *deferred but not dangling* — "T3 alone carries the idea here, meet V3 later," with a one-line preview of what V3 is. Exactly the right call; I didn't feel a loose thread.

### Coherence Index / CI (44:30)
- Four dimensions (spatial/capability/temporal/relational), geometric mean with a worked example (0.9·0.9·0.5·0.9, ⁴√ ≈ 0.78). The CI² squaring is connected here to the karma-journey formula, which closed that loop for me.
- The "0.85 start isn't a penalty, the system just hasn't witnessed you yet" framing is reassuring. Residual: *why 0.85 specifically* (vs 0.75/0.95) is named as "a calibration choice" but not motivated — same shape as the "0.5 is calibrated, not derived" caveat elsewhere.

### MRH Explorer (46:00)
- Interactive graph + a "Horizon Depth" slider with labeled stops (Only self / Direct 1 / Default 2 / Max 3 / Beyond). Decay shown concretely per hop (1 hop ~0.85, 2 hops ~0.65, 4 hops below noise floor).
- "Markov" is glossed as "the future depends only on the present — your history lives in your reputation, not a log of every step," which is a genuinely good plain-English rendering of an intimidating word. The page still toggles between that friendly framing and the formal "Markov Relevancy Horizon / Markov property" label; for a blank slate the *name* stays a little forbidding even after the idea lands.

### Aliveness (47:30, brief)
- The "two deaths at a glance" comparison (energy death = recoverable like bankruptcy; trust death = permanent like losing a professional license) is crisp and the single best thing on the page.
- Friction (low): there are *three* metaphors in play — license, bankruptcy, biological-threshold — and which applies to which death took me a second pass to keep straight. Also the CI "double penalty" (effective trust × CI² *and* ATP cost × 1/CI²) reads as the system hitting me twice for the same dip; it's real and intentional but isn't framed as *intentional*, so it briefly felt punitive rather than designed.

## Honest Assessment

This is, frankly, one of the most considerate technical explainers I've used. The thing that stood out across the whole session is how often the site answered the question I was *about to* ask — the real/simulated line on the TL;DR, the 0–1 scale anchor before the first decimal, "which number does my choice move?" right at the karma action point, "you don't need the math" next to the word *tensor*. It reads like someone watched real newcomers get confused and patched each spot.

The progressive-disclosure discipline is the best part: TL;DR for the impatient, First Contact for the narrative-minded, Why Web4 for the skeptic, and concept pages one click deep. I never felt jargon-dumped on the on-ramp pages — every acronym arrived with a plain-English twin. The LCT page in particular turned the two vaguest phrases on the site ("hardware-bound," "witnessed presence") into things I could actually picture.

Where it still snags is uniformly *minor and local*: a few acronyms on the dense why-web4 page (CI/SAL/VCM) that didn't get the gloss-on-first-use treatment their four siblings did; some assumed strategy-game vocabulary on the Society Simulator; and a couple of "calibrated, not derived" numbers that are admirably honest but leave a faint "so why *this* number?" residue. The CI "double penalty" on Aliveness is the one place a correct mechanism briefly *felt* unfair because it wasn't framed as deliberate.

None of these blocked comprehension — I finished with a genuinely solid mental model of all seven core concepts and could explain Web4 to a friend. The site has clearly converged on something good; what's left is polish at the edges, not structural confusion. I'd come back, and I'd send a curious friend straight to the TL;DR.
