## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a research proposal for a trust-native internet, where identity is hardware-bound, actions cost energy, and reputation is permanent and portable.
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — it's the interactive lab/sandbox for Web4: simulators and explainers, not a product, no install.
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token, a hardware-bound digital identity that other devices co-witness; lives in TPM/Secure Enclave/FIDO2; survives device loss via multi-device quorum.
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — Allocation Transfer Packets (energy budget) and Allocation Discharge Packets (receipts); spent on actions, refilled by community-confirmed quality work; 30%/70% quality ramp.
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional per-role reputation: Talent / Training / Temperament. Decay rates differ per dimension. The same person scores differently across roles.
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — death triggers when ATP hits 0, CI collapses, or T3 falls below 0.5. Energy death is recoverable (rebirth with karma carryover); trust death is permanent. For a real user, this would mean "suspended license," not account deletion.
- [x] Why any of this matters — the current internet has structural failure modes (free accounts, siloed reputation, disposable identity, AI deception) that no patch (CAPTCHA, moderation, blockchain wallets, social login) actually fixes; Web4 attacks the structure.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Accessibility**: how does Web4 handle users who physically can't use biometrics (no fingerprints, eye conditions, motor impairments)? Software fallback works but at a 0.50 ceiling — is that an asymmetric second-class experience? Not addressed anywhere I read.
2. **Right to disappear**: if reputation is "permanent and portable" and tied to hardware, what does GDPR-style right-to-be-forgotten look like? (I see this was flagged Apr 29 — good, but I didn't notice it surfaced on what-could-go-wrong yet during this browse.)
3. **What stops a benign actor from being mass-downvoted into trust death by a coordinated mob?** The ATP cost of mob action is one defense, but how robust is it against a well-resourced bad-faith group?
4. **Hardware vendor capture as governance risk**: if TPM chips have a 0.90 ceiling and software-only has 0.50, the hardware vendors have de facto policy power. The page acknowledges this as risk #2, but I'd want a concrete "here's what would prevent vendor capture" answer, not just acknowledgment.
5. **What does *the actual UI* look like for an everyday Web4 user?** The Day in Web4 mockups gesture at this, but I didn't get a sense of how someone would *experience* trust scores in a feed, energy budgets in a wallet, etc., as a designed product. (This is a recurring question across past visitor logs.)

## Honest Assessment
This is the most coherent first-run experience I've had on this site across the (imagined-fresh) sessions. The plain-English-first acronyms on the landing page, the TL;DR working as a real on-ramp, the consistent metaphors (suspended license, surgeon trust, biological ATP cycle), and the genuinely candid "What Could Go Wrong" all add up to a site that respects the visitor.

The one substantive weakness I'd point to is the **ATP Economics H1 still reading "Attention Economics."** The team has clearly spent multiple sessions chasing the "ATP = Attention" inferential misread — but the H1 itself is what creates the misread. The clarifier paragraph below it is doing rescue work that the H1 should never have required. Fixing the H1 would close a 60-day-old loop.

The second-substantive note is **accessibility silence**: a hardware-anchored identity system has obvious implications for disabled users, and neither the LCT page nor What Could Go Wrong mentions them. This is the next adjacent honest-limitations gap, similar to the GDPR addition on Apr 29.

Everything else is polish: a few naming overlaps (Karma Consequences / Karma Journey), a small promise/delivery gap on Day in Web4, and the recurring "show me the actual product UI" wish that no amount of mockup-improvement has fully closed.

Net: as a first-time visitor, I leave with a clear mental model and the impression that this is a serious research project, not a hype machine. I'd return, and I'd recommend it to a technically curious friend.
