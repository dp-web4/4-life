## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, clear headline — "The Internet Has a Trust Problem." I immediately understood this wasn't a product pitch but something closer to a research proposal or lab.
- The line "trust is built into the internet itself, not bolted on after the fact" is a strong hook.
- I noticed a clear primary CTA: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" with alternative paths ("What it looks like · 5 min", structured learning). Helpful, but I had a tiny hesitation: which entry is really best for me?
- Clicked: TL;DR, because it was foregrounded.
- Confusion points: None on first glance — the landing page doesn't dump jargon. But I did wonder: is "4-Life" the project name, or is Web4 the project name? The relationship is only clarified later.

### TL;DR page (02:00)
- Core pitch landed: actions cost energy, identity is hardware-bound, reputation is portable and permanent.
- Multiple new concepts introduced at once (ATP, LCT, reputation) — I had to slow down.
- ATP defined inline as "personal energy budget" — good.
- LCT described as a "constellation" — I understood it means something like identity across devices, but wasn't 100% sure.
- T3 mentioned only in navigation, not defined here.
- I didn't feel like I could answer "what would Web4 feel like?" yet. I was told about mechanisms, not experiences.
- Clicked: "Why Web4?" — I wanted the problem statement before more mechanism.

### Why Web4 (05:00)
- This page is strong on problem framing. The five problems (spam/bots, siloed reputation, unlimited fresh starts, deceptive AI, platform control) each resonated. I've felt each of these.
- Mechanisms introduced sequentially with plain language before acronyms. Good pattern.
- But: I counted at least 8 acronyms across the page (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI, V3, SAL, VCM). Even with definitions, this is a lot to hold.
- The FAQ section expanded seems enormous. I skimmed but felt decision paralysis about whether to read it.
- Gap I felt: "How does hardware-bound identity actually stop bots?" — I can infer it, but it's not explicit.
- Still missing: what my Reddit experience tomorrow would feel like.

### First Contact (10:00)
- Expected a 7-minute interactive walkthrough. Instead I got a landing page for the walkthrough — "Start the 5-act Walkthrough →" button.
- This is a small friction: I thought I was already in the experience. Having a gateway page before the gateway experience felt redundant.
- The "what you'll learn" section promises good things. But I left feeling I'd seen a preview, not the thing.

### Karma Journey (13:00)
- Really enjoyed the concept. "Karma" here isn't moral — it's "your choices become starting conditions for your next life." That's a nice mechanical reframe.
- Got interactive with trust choices and ATP spend.
- The `effective trust = raw trust × CI²` rule is the core insight, and it's well articulated.
- Small snag: CI and T3 mentioned without full explanation here — I had to infer from context.
- Aha moment: "cooperative choices build slow trust; selfish choices yield quick gains but erode your legacy." That's a concrete, felt lesson.

### Society Simulator (18:00)
- This is the flagship. 12 agents, network graph, you can observe or play.
- I understood that defectors get isolated and cooperators cluster without moderators.
- Warning on the page: recommends First Contact first if unfamiliar. Good humility — but also admits the page is hard to enter cold.
- The relationship between this simulator and the core concepts could be tighter. I got "watch trust dynamics" but not "this demonstrates LCT+ATP+T3 together."

### LCT Explainer (23:00)
- Digital ID card analogy is good — simple, intuitive.
- Co-signers analogy helps explain witnessing.

- Key missing piece: "who judges quality?" The recharge mechanism is the whole system, and I was vague on it.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Who judges quality when ADP converts back to ATP?** I understood "quality work gets more back," but the page never made explicit who decides. Is it the recipient? A network? I want one clear worked example.
2. **What does the actual UI look like in deployment?** The Day in Web4 page helped, but I still don't have a concrete picture. Are trust scores shown as numbers? Badges? Colors? Is it like a browser extension, an OS feature, a website?
3. **If my phone dies and I haven't set up device witnessing, is my identity just gone?** What Could Go Wrong admits recovery is unsolved. As a user, that's a blocker — I'd want a clearer answer before adopting.
4. **Is MRH a mechanism or an emergent property?** I left unsure whether MRH is something the protocol enforces (mechanism) or something that emerges from the trust graph (property). The page hints at both.
5. **Where does the ATP come from in the first place?** I got that you earn it by quality work, but where does *initial* ATP come from for a new user? Is there a faucet, a daily allowance, an onboarding gift? I didn't see this answered cleanly.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the better research/educational sites I've encountered on an esoteric technical topic. The core pedagogy is strong:

- **Concrete analogies on every concept page** (professional license, credit check, digital ID card) — these land.
- **Interactive simulations** — not decorative. They actually teach.
- **Explicit honesty about what's unsolved** — the What Could Go Wrong page significantly raised my trust in the project.
- **Progressive disclosure** — collapsible details, optional deep dives. The site doesn't dump everything at once.

The main friction is still **entry point choice and acronym density**. There are at least four "start here" paths (TL;DR, Why Web4, First Contact, Day in Web4, Learn) and while each is good individually, I kept wondering whether I was missing something by picking one over another. A small "routes" diagram on the landing page — four paths to the same destination, labeled by reader style (skim / problem-first / interactive / scenario) — would help.

Acronyms are improved from what I imagine the site looked like earlier (I can see plain-English expansions almost everywhere), but the Why Web4 page especially still introduces too many in a single pass. SAL, VCM, V3 might be better deferred to their own contexts.

My single biggest "aha" was Maria's stolen phone scenario on the Coherence Index page. That one scenario made the whole Web4 idea feel real — not abstract math, but a concrete thing that would make stealing phones less useful. More pages would benefit from scenarios like this: one concrete, slightly dramatic narrative that embodies the concept.

My single biggest unresolved friction is still the **"what does this feel like?" gap**. The Day in Web4 page and First Contact walkthrough both attempt to close it, but neither fully succeeded for me on first read. I understand Web4 well enough to explain the mechanisms to a friend — but if they asked "what would I experience on Reddit tomorrow?" I'd still have to hand-wave.

Overall: solid, thoughtful, honest. If I were a PhD student in distributed systems or a policy person thinking about internet governance, this site is excellent. If I were a typical curious person stumbling in from a hacker news link, I'd probably bounce after the acronym wall on Why Web4 unless I was already in a deep-reading mood. The site is good; making it great would be about one more level of friction reduction on the entry paths.
