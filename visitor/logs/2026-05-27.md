## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean and confident. The headline "A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations" told me there's something to *do* here, not just read. The parenthetical disclaimer — "(Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor)" — was genuinely helpful. It pre-empted my exact first two questions: "is this crypto?" and "is this Web3 again?" Answer: no and no. Good.
- The opening problem statement landed hard: "Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos. Bad actors get unlimited fresh starts. AI deception outpaces verification." I felt all four of those. This is the strongest writing on the site — concrete, no jargon, immediately relatable.
- **Then the acronym wall appeared**: "Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency / LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI." Five concepts and five acronyms in one breath. The plain words come first (good), but my eyes still snagged on the alphabet soup.
- The "What is this, really?" box was reassuring: "Web4 is a research proposal," "Right now, you can't install it," "this is the lab." That honesty made me trust the site more.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" (it's labeled recommended for first-timers, so I took the hint).
- **Confusion points**: The "What would change for you? → Spam disappears / Reputation travels / AI agents become trustworthy" section reads like *promises*, while the box two inches away says "you can't install it." Mild whiplash — is this a thing that works, or a thing someone hopes will work?

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (03:30)
- "Plain English first, so you can read this cold — every term is spelled out." Yes — this is the right promise and the page mostly keeps it.
- The four numbered concepts were digestible. ATP got expanded to "Allocation Transfer Packets," LCT to "Linked Context Token." Good — I now know what two of the letters mean.
- **But the page then re-introduced T3, MRH, CI as additional shorthand.** So I left the "2-minute overview" with *more* acronyms than I arrived with, not fewer. The plain-English framing fights the acronym density and the acronyms are winning.
- The Web4-vs-4-Life distinction (protocol vs. lab) clicked here. Useful.
- **Clicked**: "First Contact →" (the primary next-step CTA, 7 min interactive).

### First Contact (07:00)
- This is a *controllable story* — I steer "Alice" through 5 acts and watch two numbers (🔋 ATP energy, 🤝 trust). I liked that it commits to only two numbers. Constraining the dashboard to two dials is the right call for a first encounter.
- The 5-act arc (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) is a clear narrative shape. "Stumble" costing −25 ATP and "Recover" rebuilding trust to 0.62 makes the abstract mechanics tangible.
- **First snag**: Act 5 is "Die & Reborn." That's a dramatic, slightly alarming word to drop on someone two minutes in. My gut reaction: "wait, Alice *dies*? What did she do?" Turns out it's a designed, recoverable thing — but the word arrives before the reassurance.
- (Note: I read the structure but couldn't actually play through the interactive steps from text. A real human would click through; I can't report on the moment-to-moment pacing.)

### Why Web4? (12:30)
- This was the most *persuasive* page. It explicitly dismisses the obvious alternatives — moderation armies, CAPTCHAs, blockchain wallets, social login — and says why each fails. That "we already tried X, here's why it didn't work" structure is exactly what a skeptic needs.
- The honesty was disarming: it lists its own tradeoffs (device cost excludes people, permanent reputation vs. GDPR right-to-be-forgotten, early-adopter network effects, governance "designed but largely untested," "no live network with real users"). Naming your own weaknesses out loud bought a *lot* of credibility with me.
- **This page should arguably be earlier in the funnel.** I only reached it as the 4th page. The "why does this matter / why should I care" argument is stronger here than on the landing page, but a visitor who bounces after the TL;DR never sees it.

### ATP Economics (17:00)
- **Best single explanation on the site**: "think of ATP as cash and ADP as the receipt you get when you spend it." That one sentence resolved the entire ATP/ADP relationship instantly. I finally understood ADP, which had been a ghost acronym up to this point (mentioned on the landing page but never defined on the path I took).
- The "quality varies payment by ~7x" and "5% fee on transfers prevents farming" details felt like real economic design, not hand-waving.
- **Unexamined oddity**: ATP and ADP are literally the names of the energy-carrying molecules in biology (adenosine tri-/di-phosphate). The site never says whether that's a deliberate metaphor or a coincidence. People who know biology will wonder; people who don't will wonder why the names are so weird. A one-line "yes, we borrowed this from how cells store energy" would close the loop.

### LCT Explainer (21:30)
- "Your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer." Excellent opening analogy. I got the core idea before any jargon.
- The Web2/Web3/Web4 three-era table (memorize / store / hardware-proves-and-witnesses) was a clean mental model.
- **Where it lost me a bit**: the middle of the page ramps into TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2, Endorsement Keys, vendor certificate chains, trust ceilings (0.90/0.85/0.75/0.50). The collapsible "For non-crypto readers" sections are a thoughtful mitigation, but the page does drift from "driver's license" to "silicon attestation" faster than a casual reader can follow.
- The pseudonymity point ("earn trust without revealing who you are") was an important reassurance I didn't know I needed.

### Trust Tensor (26:00)
- I braced for math when I saw "tensor." The page defused it: "one number couldn't tell a brilliant-but-erratic surgeon from a kind doctor with shaky hands. Three numbers can." That's a great line. "Would you trust either of them to fix your car?" made role-specificity click instantly.
- Talent / Training / Temperament with the Alice example (90% as a data analyst, 76% as a PM, 27% as a mechanic) made the whole thing concrete and even a little fun.
- The decay rates (Temperament every 30 days, Training 180, Talent 365) felt intuitive — character has to be shown fresh, skills last longer. Nice touch.
- **New dangling thread**: V3 ("T3 measures who you are; V3 measures what you produce") is introduced and then deferred. Deferring is the right instinct for cognitive load, but it leaves a loose end — now I know there's *another* tensor I haven't met.

### Aliveness (29:00)
- The three conditions for "alive" (ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, CI coherent) are a tidy summary that ties the earlier concepts together.
- **Biggest semantic friction of the whole visit**: the word "death." The page says "When ATP reaches zero, you die immediately" — but then explains that energy-death is *recoverable* and your reputation *carries forward*. So "death" here means "temporarily can't act," which is not what "death" means to a human. Worse, the same trigger phrase ("you die") maps to two completely different outcomes: energy death (recoverable) vs. trust death (permanent rejection). One word, two meanings, both scary. I understood it mechanically but had to work against the vocabulary to do so.
- "Karma carries forward (+12 bonus)" / "each life starts stronger" is an appealing idea once you get past the death framing.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust built into the internet itself; verified by behavior, not assumed; explicitly not blockchain/Web3.
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — it's the *lab/sandbox* for the Web4 *proposal*; the protocol-vs-lab distinction was made clearly and early.
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token; hardware-bound identity (driver's license that can't be copied).
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget (cash) and spend-receipt (ADP); the cash/receipt analogy nailed it.
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3: role-specific trust across Talent/Training/Temperament; the surgeon/mechanic example made "tensor" painless.
- [~] How agents "live" and "die" — understood the mechanics (ATP>0, T3>0.5, CI coherent), but the word "death" actively fought my comprehension; energy-death-vs-trust-death blurred together.
- [x] Why any of this matters — the spam/silo/reset/AI-deception framing is concrete and the Why Web4? page argued it convincingly.

## Unanswered Questions
1. If "death" from running out of ATP is recoverable and my reputation carries forward, in what sense did anything actually *die*? What's the difference between that and just "waiting until I earn energy again"?
2. Why are the energy units named ATP/ADP — is the biology metaphor intentional, and if so, should I be reading it as "the internet has a metabolism"?
3. Who decides whether my work was "quality" (and thus refunds my ATP)? It's "recipient attestation" — but what stops a clique from only ever validating each other? (The 5% transfer fee is mentioned, but does that fully prevent collusion?)
4. The site is honest that there's "no live network with real users." So how do I know any of these economics actually hold up with *humans* rather than simulated agents? What would convince me?
5. There's clearly a much bigger vocabulary under the surface (Synthon, SAL, Law Oracle, Epistemic Proprioception, R6/R7...). How much of that do I *need* to understand the core idea, versus what's research depth I can ignore?
6. What does V3 (the value tensor) actually do, and where on the path am I supposed to learn it?

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely good explainer site, and clearly one that has been iterated on hard — the analogies (driver's license for LCT, cash/receipt for ATP/ADP, the surgeon for trust tensors) are some of the best I've seen for abstract systems concepts, and the willingness to name the project's own weaknesses (no live users, GDPR conflict, untested governance) made me trust it rather than dismiss it.

My honest experience as a blank-slate visitor: I left understanding the *core five* concepts at a "good" level, which is a real achievement for a topic this abstract. The site *taught* me ATP/ADP, LCT, and T3 — I didn't have to infer them. That's the headline win.

The two things that stood between me and "solid":

First, **the acronyms accumulate instead of resolving.** Every page does the right thing locally — plain words first — but globally the pile grows. The TL;DR left me holding more letters than I arrived with, and the glossary revealed an iceberg. I never felt the vocabulary was *finite and conquerable*; it kept expanding. A visitor needs a sense that "there are exactly 5 things and you now know 3 of them," and the site doesn't quite give that reassurance.

Second, **"death" is the wrong word for what it describes.** It's the most evocative term on the site and also the most misleading, because the common case (run out of energy) is recoverable, and "death" makes a naive reader think deletion/permanence. The drama is doing storytelling work, but it's costing comprehension.

Neither is fatal. The bones are excellent. If the site (a) gave me a persistent sense of how few core concepts there actually are and how many I'd mastered, and (b) fixed the "death" vocabulary so the scary word matches the scary outcome, I'd have walked away at "solid" instead of "good." As is: I'd come back to actually *run* the simulators (which I only previewed), and I'd recommend it — but only to someone I knew wouldn't bounce off the acronyms in the first thirty seconds.
