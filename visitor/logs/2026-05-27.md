### LCT Explainer (17:00)
- The phone-as-driver's-license analogy worked. I get that an LCT is hardware-
  bound identity that can't be copied because the private key is generated
  inside the chip.
- Three hardware types (TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2) with a trust-ceiling table
  was useful. Knowing that "most laptops have TPM" and "most phones since 2018
  have Secure Enclave" anchored the abstract to my actual devices.
- Recovery scenarios are addressed directly: multi-device = minutes/hours,
  single-device = days via community vouching, lose everything = 3-7 day
  social-recovery. The deliberate slowness ("easy recovery would mean easy
  identity theft") is the right framing.
- The hardware fingerprint example (0x4c8f…a3f2) with mutable label vs
  immutable fingerprint clarified the "your name is changeable, your identity
  isn't" point.
- Slight friction: The distinction between "device witnesses" (your devices
  co-sign each other) and "infrastructure witnesses" (network validators)

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a research-stage framework with hardware-bound identity, energy-based participation, multi-dimensional reputation, and 3-hop trust neighborhoods.
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — interactive simulations of the protocol so visitors can *see* the mechanics, not just read about them.
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token, hardware-bound identity anchored in TPM / Secure Enclave / FIDO2 chips, with multi-device co-witnessing for security and recovery.
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — ATP is the energy budget you spend on actions; ADP is the receipt of expenditure; recipients confirming value is what recharges your ATP. 5% burn on peer transfers to deter collusion.
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional T3 (Talent / Training / Temperament) measuring *who you are* in a specific role. V3 exists as a sibling but I only understood it via the glossary.
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — three simultaneous conditions for aliveness (ATP > 0, trust > 0.5, CI coherent). Two death modes: energy-death (recoverable bankruptcy) and trust-death (permanent license loss). Karma carries forward through energy-deaths.
- [x] Why any of this matters — the four root causes (spam zero-cost, platform-trapped reputation, unlimited fresh starts, AI deception) are real and untouched by current architectures. Web4 names a coherent mechanism-level response.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **V3 specifics, in the place I'd naturally look.** I left the Trust Tensor
   page knowing V3 exists and "grades output" but not its three dimensions.
   The glossary closed this for me but only because I went looking. Most
   visitors won't.
2. **What does "rebirth" mean as a UX event?** When Alice "dies and is reborn"
   in First Contact, does she log back in tomorrow and find her account
   reinstated with the karma bonus? Does she get an email? The mechanics of
   the rebirth event in deployed terms (vs simulation terms) was vague. The
   Aliveness page hand-waves with "automatically reborn" but doesn't show me
   the experience.
3. **AI agents are mentioned as entities — but where do they get hardware-bound
   identity?** A human's LCT lives in their phone's Secure Enclave. An AI
   agent doesn't have a phone. The site says AI agents get LCTs tied to
   "hardware-bound API access" but I'd like a 1-paragraph explanation of how
   that works — does the agent's identity live in the server hardware running
   it? In its owner's hardware? In a delegated credential?
4. **Synthons** (group entities) — mentioned on the Aliveness page in a single
   sentence ("Synthons (group entities) also have aliveness — they can die and
   dissolve too") but never explained. Is a synthon a team? An organization?
   A federation? A swarm of AI agents? The single sentence left me curious but
   not informed.

## Honest Assessment

This was a smooth first visit. Coming in with zero Web4 knowledge, the
landing → TL;DR → Why-Web4 → First Contact path delivered me to "I get the
basic shape" in about 15 minutes, and the per-concept explainers (LCT, ATP,
T3, Aliveness, MRH, Karma Journey) each closed specific gaps without piling
on new ones.

The thing that worked best: **honest framing about deployment status,
applied consistently**. Every page that uses present tense for a Web4
scenario seemed to also flag that nothing ships today. That repeated honesty
let me engage with the mechanisms as a thought experiment without feeling
manipulated. I trust the project more for telling me what isn't done yet
than I would if it had over-claimed.

The thing that worked second-best: **interactive simulations as the
explainer medium**. First Contact's 5-act simulation taught me ATP and trust
dynamics in a way no static prose could have. Watching Alice's numbers move
made the abstractions tangible. The Karma Journey CI² mechanic was similarly
clarifying through play.

The friction that surprised me most: **V3 deferral**. The site has gotten so
good at glossing acronyms inline (ADP, LCT, ATP) that I was caught off-guard
to hit V3 multiple times without ever seeing its expansion at the point of
reading. The glossary saves you, but only if you happen to go look. This
is exactly the kind of friction the prior visitor logs (per the project's
own pattern library: "ghost-concept friction = prominence not absence")
have been catching — the answer isn't missing, it's just not at the spot
the reader needs it. (I notice from the project's memory index that this
exact pattern has appeared 6+ times before. This is its 7th appearance,
specifically for V3.)

Beyond V3, the friction is small and the site is in good shape. The
concept sequence (Landing → TL;DR → Why-Web4 → First Contact → LCT → ATP →
T3 → MRH → CI → Aliveness) is well-tuned. Coming back for a second visit,
I'd dig into the Society Simulator, the Playground experiments, and the
FAQ deep-dives I skipped. That's a sign that the first visit succeeded —
it left me curious about more, rather than exhausted by the material I'd
already encountered.

Would recommend to a friend who's been complaining about spam, bot
accounts, or AI-generated nonsense — Web4 is the most coherent
mechanism-level response to those problems I've seen.
