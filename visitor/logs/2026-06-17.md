## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clear and confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the one-liner — *"a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor"* — told me what this is and, importantly, what it is NOT. Pre-empting "is this crypto?" was smart; that was my first suspicion.
- The problem statement landed: *"Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos."* I felt that. Those are real frustrations I recognize.
- **Clicked**: The big primary button, **"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →"** — exactly what a newcomer wants.
- **Confusion points**: The five core concepts teaser shows "Identity" and "Energy" in plain words, which is friendly. No jargon dumped here. Good.

### "Why Web4?" Page (02:30)
- I clicked the "2-minute TL;DR" button and landed on what felt like a **much longer page than promised**. It opens by telling me it has *"the problems (~5 min), what Web4 proposes (~2 min), and honest Q&A."* That's 7+ minutes plus a 50-question FAQ — not the "2-minute" thing I clicked.
- The page itself even says, near the top, that first-time visitors are *"better served by the Web4 in 2 Minutes link."* So there are TWO pages — a real short one (`/tldr`) and this long diagnostic — and the landing-page button labeled "2-minute TL;DR" seems to send me to the long one. **That's a bait-and-switch from a naive reader's view.**
- Content quality itself is high: the four mechanism cards (Energy/ATP, Identity/LCT, Trust/T3, Neighborhood/MRH) are each a one-liner I could follow.
- **Confusion**: Four acronyms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) appear at once. The page acknowledges it ("Lost in the acronyms? Quick index →") and tells me they "land much easier after First Contact." Honest, but it means the page admits I should be somewhere else.

### TL;DR Page (06:00)
- I found `/tldr` separately. **THIS is the actual 2-minute version** I expected from the button. Problem → Idea → Result → Status, plain language, terms defined on first touch.
- The "Result" framing is punchy: *"Spam dies because it's too expensive. Scammers can't escape their history."*
- It honestly labels itself: *"active research with working simulations, not a deployed product."* I appreciated not being oversold.
- **Confusion**: A "witness constellation" phrase appears with no explanation. Minor.

### First Contact (09:00)
- This is where it clicked for me. An interactive ~7-minute story controlling "Alice," tracking two simple meters: **🔋 Energy (starts 100)** and **🤝 Trust (starts 0.50)**. Two numbers I can watch — that's a great onboarding device.
- The promise that each acronym is explained *"the moment Alice uses it"* is the right model. This should arguably be the FIRST thing the landing page pushes, not the TL;DR.

### Karma Journey (14:00)
- Fun and concrete: "Live multiple lives," 100 energy, 8 action choices (cooperative/selfish/bold/neutral).
- **Death was clear here** — and this surprised me because I expected it to be muddy. The page cleanly splits: **Energy death** (ATP hits 0 while trust ≥0.5 → suspension, recoverable) vs **Trust collapse** (raw trust <0.5 → permanent rejection). Leading with *which meter failed* made it instantly graspable.
- **Confusion**: The **raw vs effective trust** split (you survive on raw 0.50 but your next-life karma tier uses effective = raw × CI²) made me re-read. And **why CI is squared** is asserted with a ratio comparison that felt like math I was supposed to trust rather than understand.

### ATP Economics (19:00)
- Solid. "ATP as cash, ADP as the receipt" is a clean analogy. The page works hard to reassure me I never touch ADP: *"You never hold, present, or manage the ADP yourself."*
- **Confusion**: The ADP material still arrives *after* the 60-second version, so by the time it tells me "you don't manage this," I'd already spent effort trying to. Also the **5% transfer fee** is explained as preventing "circular farming" — a term I don't know, so the justification didn't justify anything for me.

### LCT Explainer (23:00)
- "A digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip" + "your phone becoming your driver's license" — great metaphors.
- The real-vs-simulation boundary is stated (*"TPM2 binding has been validated on real hardware"* but *"the trust, witness, and economic dynamics… are still simulation"*). Honest, though it took a careful read to separate what's real from what's pretend.
- **Confusion**: "Witness" means two different things (my own devices vouching for each other vs. network nodes). The page admits this. Still had to re-read.

### Society Simulator (26:00)
- Ambitious — 12 agents, no central authority, watch cooperation emerge. The framing question is genuinely intriguing.
- **Confusion**: Hit me with six acronyms at once (ATP, T3, LCT, CI, MRH, V3). It catches itself with "👋 Never seen these Web4 terms before?" and points to First Contact — but if I'd landed here cold I'd have bounced.

### Aliveness (27:30)
- Three survival criteria (ATP>0, Trust>0.5, CI coherent), and the **two-deaths split matches Karma Journey exactly** (energy death = recoverable / bankruptcy; trust death = permanent / lost license). The consistency across pages was reassuring — I trusted the site more because the two pages agreed.

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-built explainer, and noticeably more coherent than I expected from a research site. The two things that worked best: (1) **First Contact's two-meter interactive story** — watching Energy and Trust move is what made the abstractions real for me, and (2) the **two-deaths distinction**, which several pages now state identically and clearly. The fact that Karma Journey and Aliveness *agreed* with each other on what death means actually increased my trust in the whole site.

The one thing that bugged me as a naive visitor is structural, not conceptual: I clicked a button that promised a "2-minute TL;DR" and got a 15-minute diagnostic, then later discovered the real 2-minute page existed separately. For a first-timer that's a small breach of the implicit promise the button made — and the first 30 seconds are where you keep or lose someone. Everything downstream is good enough that I'd come back; but I'd recommend this site with the caveat "skip the long Why-Web4 page at first and just do First Contact." The site almost says that itself — it should just *do* it.
