## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, calm, not screaming at me. The headline "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" is a hook I immediately get — spam, fake accounts, scammers who just make a new account. I felt seen.
- It quickly told me what this is NOT (not blockchain, not crypto, not Web3), which I appreciated because "Web4" made me brace for a crypto pitch.
- It was honest that this is a *research project*, not a product I can sign up for. Mild disappointment, but the honesty bought trust.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →"
- Confusion points: The sidebar is a wall of acronyms (LCT, T3, ATP, MRH, CI) before I know what any of them are. I also had to re-read to understand that "Web4" is the idea and "4-Life" is the lab demonstrating it. That distinction is important and easy to miss.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (03:30)
- The three-part promise landed: actions cost energy, identity is tied to your device, reputation follows you. I could repeat that back to a friend.
- BUT: the page says "only two acronyms needed" and then immediately throws ATP and LCT at me without really earning them yet. Telling me there are only two acronyms doesn't make two acronyms feel like zero.
- "Energy refills" was waved at but not explained. I left with a cliffhanger: refills *how*?
- Clicked: "Why Web4?" — I wanted the problem nailed down before the simulations.

### Why Web4? (07:00)
- This was the strongest page for me. The five failures (free spam, siloed reputation, easy resets, AI deception, platform lock-in) are all things I've personally experienced. Convincing diagnosis.
- The FAQ is genuinely impressive — it picks fights with itself ("Isn't this just social credit?", "Can a rich attacker buy 50 devices?"). That self-skepticism made me trust the authors more than a glossy pitch would.
- Hand-wavy bits I noticed: "energy backs ATP via received value" felt circular — who decides the *first* value before anyone has trust? And the hardware requirement quietly raises a "what about people without fancy phones?" equity question that's acknowledged but not solved.
- Clicked: "First Contact" — I wanted something concrete and human.

### First Contact / Alice (12:00)
- This is an interactive walkthrough where I drive Alice's first actions and watch her numbers move. Good instinct — show, don't lecture.
- It did make ATP and trust feel real because I saw them change as Alice acted.
- Problem: it introduces FOUR concepts at once — ATP, Trust Score, LCT, AND a new one I hadn't seen yet, "Coherence Index." That's a lot of new vocabulary in one breath. I felt the density.

### LCT Explainer (16:00)
- The "your phone becomes your driver's license, but one that can't be photocopied or faked" analogy is excellent. That one sentence taught me more than the homepage did.
- Then it lost me: TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2, "cryptographic attestation," "manufacturer-burned key." I don't need those to get the idea, and they made me feel like the page stopped talking to *me* and started talking to engineers.

### ATP Economics (19:00)
- The cell-biology metaphor (spend ATP → get an ADP "receipt" → recharge when others confirm your value) finally made the energy idea click. This is where "energy refills" from the TL;DR got answered — I wish this clarity were closer to the front.
- The "7× quality gap" example (low-quality work earns ~6 back, high-quality ~42) was a great concrete number.
- Still circular-feeling: quality is judged by community confirmation, but confirmation depends on existing trust. Chicken-and-egg for a brand-new person.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (22:00)
- I was scared of the word "tensor." The page literally says "you don't need the math," which relaxed me immediately. Good call.
- Talent / Training / Temperament is memorable and the "brilliant-but-erratic surgeon vs. kind doctor with shaky hands" example is sticky.
- I didn't fully grasp how the numbers actually *move* — that part got formula-ish.

### Aliveness (24:30)
- This is the emotionally gripping page. "When ATP reaches zero, you die." Whoa.
- The two-deaths idea is clear and fair: energy death = recoverable (like bankruptcy), trust death = permanent (like losing your license). That analogy did a lot of work.
- The honesty about the 0.5 threshold being "calibrated, not derived — no theorem fixes the number" was disarming. They're telling me the seams.
- Unsettling: "death," "rebirth," and "karma" for what is ultimately an account is heavy language. As a metaphor in a simulation it's fine and even fun; I'd flinch if this were my real online life.

### Society Simulator (26:30)
- Genuinely interactive — pick a scenario, hit Run, watch 12 agents cooperate/defect over 10 rounds. Cool to watch defectors get isolated.
- But by this point the acronyms (ATP, T3, LCT, CI, MRH, V3) were stacked high, and this page also introduces "scalar reputation" vs. the tensor — a distinction that only means something if you already know T3. Felt like it assumed I'd done homework.

### Glossary (28:00)
- I came here as a fallback when overwhelmed. Most acronyms (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI, synthon) are defined in plain language. The "Essential only" filter (7 core terms) is a kind feature for someone drowning.
- Annoyances: no plain A–Z index — I had to scroll categories. And definitions reference *other* undefined acronyms (ADP's definition uses "VCM" / Value Confirmation Mechanism, which has no entry). R6/R7 "Action Framework" is mentioned in core terms but never unpacked.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — an internet where actions cost energy, identity is hardware-bound, reputation is permanent and portable
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — a live lab/simulation of those rules, not a product
- [x] What LCT means — a device-anchored, unfakeable digital ID
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — an energy budget you spend to act and earn back via confirmed value (ADP = the spent-energy receipt)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — trust split into Talent/Training/Temperament, per role
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive requires energy + trust + consistency; energy death is recoverable, trust death is permanent
- [x] Why any of this matters — the spam/scam/fake-account/AI-deception problem is real and relatable

(Notable: I checked all the boxes, but several were *inferred from analogies* rather than fully taught — and I still couldn't tell you whether the system actually *works*, only what it claims.)

## Unanswered Questions
1. **The cold-start problem, for a person**: If a brand-new user has no trust and no confirmers, how do they earn their *first* ATP and *first* trust? Every page gestures at this; none nailed it for me.
2. **What about people without modern phones/security chips?** The equity issue is acknowledged but the fixes (FIDO2 keys, community witnessing) sounded incomplete.
3. **Does any of this work with real humans?** Everything I saw is simulation. The site is honest about that, but I left wanting one piece of evidence beyond "the agents in our sim cooperated."
4. **Who decides what "quality" is?** If communities define quality, can't a community enforce groupthink and starve dissenters of ATP?
5. **Where did 0.5 come from?** They admit it's calibrated not derived — so how fragile is the whole life/death system to that one knob?

## Honest Assessment

This is the most *intellectually honest* site I've browsed of its kind. It repeatedly argues against itself, admits what's unproven, and never tries to sell me a token. That alone made me stay for 28 minutes instead of bouncing.

The teaching strategy is "give an analogy, then let you play." When the analogies are good — phone-as-driver's-license, ATP-as-cell-metabolism, two-kinds-of-death — concepts genuinely click. The problem is **pacing and acronym load**. Concepts arrive faster than they're grounded: the TL;DR name-drops ATP/LCT before earning them, First Contact stacks four ideas at once, and by the Society Simulator I was carrying six acronyms. The glossary is a good safety net but has gaps (undefined terms inside definitions, no A–Z).

If I'm being a true naive human: I understood the *story* of Web4 well, but I could not tell you whether it would actually work, and the one thing I kept wanting — "show me it surviving contact with real people" — is the one thing the site honestly can't give yet. That's not a UX flaw; it's the nature of the project. The UX flaws are smaller and fixable: introduce acronyms more slowly, hide the engineer-grade hardware jargon, answer the personal cold-start question early, and make the "lost? start here" escape routes louder on the dense pages.

Who would I recommend it to? A curious skeptic who likes ideas and doesn't need a product. Not yet my non-technical friend who'd hit the LCT page's TPM/FIDO2 paragraph and close the tab.
