## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Calm, confident framing. The headline "A Trust-Native Internet Lab" was paired immediately with a one-line disambiguation ("Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor"). That last clause anticipated my exact suspicion — "is this another crypto thing?" — and defused it before I had to ask.
- **Hook**: "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" landed. Three concrete pains (free spam accounts, trapped reputations, unlimited fresh starts) I instantly recognized.
- **Five foundational ideas**: Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency — in plain English, with acronyms as "Shorthand:" subtext. This was a deliberate choice and it worked. I learned the concepts before the jargon arrived to compete for attention.
- **Clicked**: The "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" button. The presence of an explicit recommended path was reassuring — I didn't have to pick from a dozen entry points.
- **Confusion points**: Mild. "Trust-Native" itself isn't a phrase I'd encountered. The inline gloss helped, but the word "native" took a second to parse — I think it means "built in at the protocol level rather than added on top by a platform." That's never said exactly, but it's inferable from context.

### TL;DR Page (02:30)
- **Structure**: Problem → Idea → Result. The framing line "every term is spelled out the first time it appears" earned my trust quickly.
- **Three pillars**: Energy-Based Actions (ATP), Hardware-Anchored Identity (LCT), Portable Permanent Reputation. Plus a fourth: "Equal Rules for Humans & AI."
- **Wait — three pillars or five foundational ideas?**: The landing page just told me there were *five* foundational ideas (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency). The TL;DR collapses these into three pillars (Energy, Identity, Reputation), with Neighborhood and Consistency absorbed into Reputation. It's not contradictory but it's a different shape. Counting concepts is a small thing, but inconsistent counts across consecutive pages slightly eroded the otherwise tight framing.
- **CTAs at bottom**: First Contact (7 min), Why Web4 (5 min + FAQs), A Day in Web4 (5 min), Karma Journey (15 min), Society Simulator (15 min), Learning Journey. I picked First Contact because "interactive" + shortest interactive option.

### First Contact (07:30)
- **Welcome**: Cleared up a worry I'd been holding — "model of a proposed system, rules running here are real but nothing to sign up for." The simulation/reality boundary was explicit. Good.
- **Concept cards** before the walkthrough explained ATP, T3, LCT, CI with one-sentence definitions. Acronyms in parentheses *after* plain English. Consistent with TL;DR.
- **"Step 1 of 5 · Simulation"** counter visible. I felt oriented.
- **Confusion point**: CI's card read "Authenticity (CI)" — but CI stands for Coherence Index. "Authenticity" is what it measures, not what it is. Minor.
- I trusted the page enough to want to actually run through Alice's arc. Visualizing this site, the simulation would be where I'd spend real time.

### Why Web4 (12:00)
- Hit the page expecting the "problem" deep dive. Got that and a lot more.
- **Acronym intro**: Plain English first ("Every action costs energy from a personal budget…"), then "Shorthand: ATP (Allocation Transfer Packets)." Excellent pattern.
- **40+ FAQs grouped by topic** with a topic index. I sampled three: "Is this deployed?" (honest research-prototype answer), "What if I lose my hardware?" (clear multi-device recovery path), "Isn't this just a social credit system?" (distinction between bottom-up reputation and top-down state control).
- **Length**: Very long. I noticed the "stop reading at any point" prompts and the topic jumps. The page is acknowledging its own size. I skim-bailed about halfway through, feeling I'd gotten the gist.
- **"Allocation Transfer Packets"**: This phrase tripped me. ATP in biology = adenosine *triphosphate* (a molecule, three phosphates). The Web4 backronym substitutes "Allocation Transfer Packets" — but what's being allocated, and what's a packet here? In computer networking, "packet" already means an IP datagram. The choice feels backronymed: ATP because the biological cycle is the *real* analogy, with the expanded form awkwardly retrofitted. The biology metaphor is great. The Allocation/Transfer/Packets wording is the weakest link.

### Karma Journey (18:00)
- **Frame**: "Live multiple lives. Make choices. Watch how trust and karma compound across reincarnations." Striking. The reincarnation framing is unusual for an internet protocol explainer but it makes sense: hardware-bound identity → can't restart → karma persists.
- **Stats panel**: Trust 0.500, Energy 100, Consistency 0.85. I noted Consistency starts at 0.85, not 1.0 — new accounts pay a tax until they prove a pattern. That's fair but the *reason* isn't on this page; I had to wait for the Coherence Index page to find it.
- **Formula introduced**: Effective trust = raw trust × CI². Why squared? Not explained here. The text says "good work carries forward; harm carries forward too" but the *mechanics* of why CI² (not CI) is hidden behind the link to /coherence-index.
- **Friction point**: At the moment of making a *choice*, the player is shown raw trust prominently. But effective trust is what actually seeds the next life — so it's effective trust that drives long-term consequences. There's a buried-lede feeling: the most consequential number is computed but secondary to display.

### LCT Explainer (24:00)
- **Definition**: Linked Context Token. "A digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip — hardware-bound, witnessed by your other devices."
- **Analogy**: "Your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied or faked from a stolen photo." Good and concrete.
- **Witnesses**: "Your phone and laptop independently confirm your identity is real — like co-signers on a document." This was the single best on-site sentence for the witness concept.
- **Hardware terms in collapsible section**: "What are TPM, Secure Enclave, and FIDO2?" with plain definitions. The reassurance "You don't need to memorize these to use Web4" was kind.
- **Recovery**: Multi-device users recover in minutes-to-hours; solo-device users wait 3–7 days for community vouches. Plausibly explained.
- **Confusion in deeper sections**: Phrases like "tamper-resistant," "cryptographic attestation," "manufacturer-burned key," "vendor certificate chain" appeared without inline gloss. I'd already absorbed the core idea — but I bounced off these. They're in the deeper-detail zone of the page; a beginner-skim would not encounter them, but a "I want to know more" reader hits them suddenly.
- **"Linked Context Token"** — the *name* itself is opaque. "Token" I get. "Linked" I get (multi-device linking). "Context" — what context is being linked? It's never quite explained why "context" is the right middle word. I'd guess "the context of who you are, where you are, what role you're playing" — but I'm inferring, not being taught.

### ATP Economics (29:00)
- **Biology metaphor explained**: "Adenosine triphosphate is the molecule your cells spend to do work — they burn ATP, get ADP back, and recharge ATP from food." Clean. Doesn't require chemistry.
- **Mechanics**: Quality ramp (7× difference between mediocre and excellent contributions). Confirmers automatically determine recharge. No central judges. I followed all of this.
- **Bitcoin distinction**: "It's a usage budget, not a tradeable asset. No market, no price, no speculation." This is the line that should be on the *landing page*, honestly. It would have addressed my unspoken "is this crypto?" suspicion even more directly than the landing's "no blockchain" did.
- **5% transfer fee → community pools**: clean and the anti-collusion reasoning is given.

### Trust Tensor (32:30)
- **T3 = Talent + Training + Temperament**. Clean mnemonic. Each defined in one sentence.
- **Tensor definition**: "A tensor generalises vectors and matrices to any number of dimensions." That was generous of the page — most "tensor" appearances on the internet just assume you know.
- **Surgeon example**: "You might trust a brilliant surgeon who's unreliable differently from a steady surgeon with less raw talent." Concrete and immediate.
- **Decay half-lives**: Talent 365d, Training 180d, Temperament 30d. The differing rates make sense (temperament is more present-state, talent is more durable). Decent.
- **V3 mentioned but not explained**: "Sibling tensor measuring *what* you produce, but doesn't explain it here." Okay, but if V3 is on the same site at the same hierarchy, why isn't it linked from here? Or is it? I didn't see a "V3 →" link. (Might be there, but didn't surface in my read.)

### Coherence Index (36:00)
- **Maria's stolen phone scenario**: A thief in Madrid with valid credentials, correct device, right city — but CI drops to 0.55 because temporal continuity is broken (phone was off) and relational patterns don't match. Traditional security grants access; Web4 doesn't. Genuinely good.
- **CI² justification**: "Small drops in coherence should hurt more than linear — inconsistency breaks trust *and* creates ambiguity about which version of you is acting." Plus worked numbers: 10% dip costs 19% effective trust; 40% dip costs 64%. That's the right level of detail.
- **Four dimensions of consistency**: spatial, capability, temporal, relational. Each gets a one-liner.
- **The CI explanation is the strongest concept page I've seen so far.**

### MRH Explorer (39:00)
- **MRH = Markov Relevancy Horizon**. Visceral reaction: who decided this should be called that? "Markov" is a math term most people only encounter in stats class. "Relevancy" feels mid-Latinate. "Horizon" is the only friendly word in the phrase.
- The page itself refers to it as "trust neighborhood" — which is the term used on the *landing page* and is much friendlier. Why isn't "Trust Neighborhood" the page title with "(formally: Markov Relevancy Horizon)" as a subtitle?
- **The 3-hop visualization** works mechanically (you → d1 contacts → d2 contacts) but lacks a real-world *scenario*. I want a sentence like: "When you message someone 3 hops away — a friend of a friend of a friend — your trust signal is mostly noise, and the action costs more energy."
- The page *does* give the reason (exponential ATP cost beyond ~3 hops) but it lives in mechanics-speak, not story-speak.
- This is the page where my naive-visitor patience ran lowest.

### Aliveness (41:30)
- **Three criteria for "alive"**: ATP > 0, Trust > 0.5, CI coherent. Clean.
- **Energy death (recoverable, karma-carrying) vs Trust death (permanent, "like losing a professional license")**. The professional-license analogy is great. The page is honest that some failures are unrecoverable.
- **AI agents**: explicitly addressed separately — "death" means suspending the agent's identity credentials while the *operator's* standing remains intact. Good. I hadn't thought about whether the operator and the agent are the same trust subject; the page addressed it before I asked.
- **Karma rebirth math**: "If you die with 145 ATP and T3 = 0.72, you're reborn with 145 ATP karma bonus and 0.72 trust." Concrete.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed protocol layer that makes trust costly to fake, portable across platforms, and tied to hardware
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — that a trust-native society can self-organize without central authority, and that good/bad behavior compounds across "lives"
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored identity, witnessed by your other devices
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — an energy budget for actions; ADP is the receipt of a spent action
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — multi-dimensional trust (Talent + Training + Temperament), role-contextualized
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive = ATP>0 AND T3>0.5 AND CI coherent; energy death is recoverable, trust death is not
- [x] Why any of this matters — because today's internet's zero-cost identity is the substrate of spam, fake reviews, and platform lock-in; Web4 proposes a different substrate

## Unanswered Questions
1. **What's the path from "research prototype" to a thing I can use?** The site is honest about not being deployed, but I don't see a roadmap or a "join the waitlist / follow the protocol" pathway. If I'm convinced and want to be early, where do I go?
2. **What about people without smartphones or modern hardware?** TPM/Secure Enclave/FIDO2 implies recent devices. The site says "most phones since 2018" — but globally, that excludes a lot of people. Is software fallback (~0.50 trust ceiling) enough for the system to work for them? I'd like that addressed for fairness.
3. **MRH and 3 hops — what about scale?** If I can only reliably trust ~3 hops, can Web4 actually power a global system? Or are there "trust bridges" between disconnected neighborhoods? The MRH page hints at exponential ATP cost beyond 3 hops, but doesn't explain how strangers across the world ever connect.
4. **Why "Markov" specifically?** I'm not asking for a math lesson, but if the word is in the protocol's vocabulary, one sentence on *why that math framework* fits the trust problem would help.
5. **Is there a single number I should track?** The site has 7+ metrics (ATP, T3 [×3 dims], V3 [×3 dims], CI, MRH, karma, effective trust). For a casual user, what's the "battery icon"? The system might decide for me, but as a player of Karma Journey I wanted a single dashboard summary number.

## Honest Assessment

This is the most carefully designed technical explainer I've encountered on a research protocol. Several things stand out:

**What works**: The plain-English-first / acronym-second pattern is applied consistently and is the single biggest accessibility win. The simulation/reality boundary is clear (research prototype, thought experiment, not a product). The biology metaphor for ATP is intuitive and doesn't require background. The CI/Maria example is the kind of concrete-grounded explainer that makes abstract security ideas click. The acknowledgment that the page is long, with "stop reading at any point" prompts, was generous — it told me I was being met where I am, not lectured at.

**What still trips me**: 
- The acronym surface area is large (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, MRH, karma, effective trust). Each is explained well *individually*, but holding them all in working memory while playing Karma Journey was a strain. The Aa glossary panel (I noticed it but didn't open it during my flow) is probably meant to address this — but the friction is real.
- MRH is the page where my "this is for me" sense slipped. The name "Markov Relevancy Horizon" is unfriendly enough that I'd actively recommend renaming the page (not the protocol concept) to "Trust Neighborhood."
- The "Allocation Transfer Packets" expansion of ATP is the weakest naming on the site. The biology metaphor is strong; the literal expansion is awkward filler.

**Net**: I'd send this to a smart curious friend who asks "what's Web4?" The TL;DR + Why Web4 + Karma Journey path is well-curated. I'd add an asterisk: "skip MRH on first read, come back to it." That asterisk shouldn't be necessary on a site this well-built — and removing the need for it is the most actionable thing I could point at after this session.
