## Unanswered Questions
1. **What happens if I lose my phone?** The LCT page sells "hardware-bound" as a feature; the /what-could-go-wrong page admits recovery is an unsolved problem. The two pages don't talk to each other.
2. **Who decides "quality" of my work?** Recipient attestation is the answer given, but that creates a power structure where the already-popular can withhold attestations from newcomers. How is this prevented?
3. **If I'm wrongfully driven below 0.5 trust by a coordinated campaign or honest mistakes, am I genuinely banished forever?** With my hardware? Can I get a new phone and start over (a fresh start the system is designed to prevent)?
4. **Does "spatial coherence" require location tracking?** If yes, the privacy story needs to be told. If no, the page should clarify what's actually measured.
5. **Why "Web4" if not a Web3 successor?** The naming choice creates immediate friction.
6. **How do "trust neighborhoods" (MRH, 30% decay per hop) avoid creating filter bubbles?** If I only see what my 3-hop network trusts, that's literally a recommendation algorithm — just a peer-weighted one. What's the difference from an echo chamber?
7. **What is the actual path from simulation to deployment?** "47,000 lines of code and simulations" is referenced but I don't have a sense of who is running this, where, or for what.
8. **Why is Temperament half-life 30 days?** Real human life involves illness, caregiving, parental leave, sabbaticals. The system seems to penalize having one.
9. **What makes this "not blockchain" when it uses burned-token transfer fees and redistribution pools?** Naïve readers will conflate.
10. **How does the very first user get 3 witnesses?** Bootstrap problem unaddressed.

## Honest Assessment

The site is doing something hard: explaining a multi-layered protocol with novel mechanics to people who have no background. It mostly succeeds at the conceptual layer — the analogies (cellular ATP, driver's license LCT, Alice-as-analyst-vs-mechanic) are well-chosen. The "What Could Go Wrong" page is the strongest piece of the project; I have rarely seen a research site this candid about its failure modes.

But there are three structural issues that hurt a naive first-time visit:

**One — the most prominent navigation link is broken.** "Start Here" 404s. This is not a minor issue. It's the literal first signpost a visitor sees, and it dead-ends. I considered leaving the site. If I had been a real human with no commitment to the topic, I would have. This needs to be the top priority fix.

**Two — the system's harshest mechanics are introduced without enough softening.** "Agents die when ATP hits zero." "Trust death prevents rebirth entirely." "Newcomers pay 40% more ATP per action." Each of these is technically accurate, but encountered in sequence by a naive reader, they paint a picture of a hostile system that punishes ordinary mistakes and excludes outsiders. The /what-could-go-wrong page does great work acknowledging this — but the explainer pages themselves should soften and contextualize, not just state the rules. The mathematical sharpness of a spec is a poor fit for an onboarding page.

**Three — acronym density.** Nine acronyms, introduced one at a time, deferred to specialized pages, never consolidated visually until the glossary. The cumulative cognitive load by the time I reached the Aliveness page (with its formulas and CI = (spatial × capability × temporal × relational)^(1/4)) was high enough that I'd predict most visitors stop reading. A single "vocabulary at a glance" graphic on the landing page would help enormously.

The site clearly knows it's research. The honesty is refreshing. But the question I kept asking — "would I want to live in this system?" — wasn't answered by the time I'd seen the core concept pages. I saw a lot of rules and consequences and very little of what a good day in Web4 looks like for an ordinary, well-meaning user. The negative mechanics (death, decay, costs, witnesses) are described in much more detail than the positive ones (community, recovery, growth, reward). That imbalance shapes the read.

Two suggestions:

1. **Lead with someone living well**, not the rules of survival. Show a "day in Web4" from Alice's perspective — Alice posts, Alice's contribution gets validated, Alice's reputation grows, Alice helps a newcomer, Alice's friend Bob has a bad day and recovers. Then introduce the mechanics that make this possible.
2. **Fix "Start Here"** before anything else, and make sure every link on the landing page goes somewhere. The visitor's first 30 seconds shape the rest.

The site is assembled from many well-made pieces, but the joins between them are where I stumbled: the TL;DR over-promises "no prerequisites," "A Day in Web4" can't decide if it's a product tour or a thought experiment, the Society Simulator's own glossary contradicts itself by omitting MRH/V3, and ADP/death-metaphors arrive out of the order a first-timer needs them. None of these are comprehension-blockers individually; collectively they cost the site its "solid" rating and leave it at "good."

The single most persistent thing I could not resolve as a naive visitor: **is this real, simulated, or imagined?** The site is admirably honest that "you can't install it" — but the *experiences* it gives me (drive Alice, download the app in 30 seconds, watch a society) all speak in the present tense of a working system, while the disclaimers speak in the conditional tense of research. I believe the system is real research; the site's own framing kept undercutting that belief. Tightening tense/framing consistency on the experiential pages would do more for trust than any new content.
