## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: "4-Life" — momentarily wondered if this was an insurance product or a fitness brand. "Trust-Native Internet Lab" is intriguing but jargon-flavored. The parenthetical "Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" lands oddly: if it's not the successor to Web3, why is it called Web4? Felt like the name was fighting itself.
- The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" framing is well-trodden but lands. Spam, siloed reputation, unlimited fresh starts for bad actors — these are real, recognizable pains.
- Five Core Ideas in plain language (Identity / Energy / Trust / Neighborhood / Consistency) is the best framing on the page — but each one points at something with its own dedicated acronym I haven't met yet.
- Clicked: "Start Here" in the navigation, because the page told me to.
- Confusion points: Why "Web4" if it's not Web3's successor? What does "47,000 lines of code" prove? Why is the brand "4-Life"? (Took me a beat to land on "for life" as in lifelong.)

### Start Here (00:05) — **404 NOT FOUND**
- The navigation link the landing page invited me to click does not resolve.
- This is the most prominent CTA in the header. As a naive visitor, my first reaction is "is this site abandoned?" — I almost left.
- Backed up to the landing page and tried "Web4 in 2 Minutes" instead.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TLDR (00:07)
- "Acronyms will be explained upon first use, no prior knowledge needed" — appreciated the promise.
- The four numbered points were the clearest summary I got all session: actions cost ATP, identity binds to device hardware, reputation is multidimensional/portable/permanent, humans and AI follow the same rules.
- But then: "Three additional concepts — T3, MRH, and CI — receive brief introductions with links for deeper exploration." This is where the acronym tax kicked in. The page promised "no prior knowledge needed" then immediately deferred three more acronyms to other pages.
- "Active research with working simulations" — honest framing, appreciated.

### First Contact (00:11)
- Promised "you'll drive Alice's choices" across 7 minutes of interactive walkthrough.
- The Alice frame is a good move — concrete person, watchable metrics.
- Mapped four foundational ideas to four acronyms (ATP, T3, LCT, CI). Two new ones (LCT, CI) without expansion on this page.
- The simulator is interactive (play/pause/scrub) and I couldn't actually run it. So I bounced to a static page.

### Why Web4 (00:14)
- Strong problem framing. The five problems (spam wins, reputation siloed, unlimited fresh starts, AI deception, platform control) match what an average person already feels about the web.
- Solutions section presents ATP / LCT / T3 / MRH cleanly, finally introducing MRH ("Trust Neighborhoods") without expanding the letters.
- "Your trust should follow you across platforms" — this is the single most compelling sentence on the site for me. Better than the landing page tagline.

### LCT Explainer (00:18)
- Finally: **LCT = Linked Context Token**.
- The driver's license analogy was the best concept-anchor I encountered. "Your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied or impersonated from a remote computer." That landed instantly.
- But the page sidesteps the question every human will ask within 30 seconds: **what if my phone breaks, gets stolen, or falls in a lake?** I had to find the answer on a different page (and that answer turned out to be: it's an open problem).
- "Witnessed by multiple devices simultaneously" — unclear what this means. Witnesses = other phones? Other people's phones? Specialized hardware? The notary metaphor isn't drawn.
- "Pseudonymous" was at least defined.

### ATP Economics (00:22)
- **ATP = Allocation Transfer Packets**. The biology analogy (adenosine triphosphate) is the conceptual win; the expansion reads as a backronym ("allocation transfer packet" doesn't really mean anything on its own). Same problem with ADP = Allocation Discharge Packet.
- Quality Ramp (under 30% quality earn ~0 ATP, above 70% earn near-full): clear and useful.
- "Transfer fees ... burned amount routes to community redistribution pools" — wait. Burned tokens, redistribution pools — these are blockchain motifs, but the landing page said "no blockchain." If it walks and quacks like a token economy, what specifically distinguishes this? I couldn't tell.
- **"Agents reaching zero ATP die."** This sentence stopped me cold. "Die" is strong language for a naive reader. Even with the rebirth caveat, my first reaction was: my account dies if I post too much? The framing is harsher than the actual mechanic warrants.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (00:26)
- **T3 = Trust Tensor: Talent / Training / Temperament**. Finally — three Ts.
- The Alice-as-analyst-vs-mechanic example was excellent. Same person, different scores per role. I got this immediately.
- "Recipient attestation" — those receiving work confirm value. But I kept asking: who decides quality? Could a popular clique freeze out newcomers by withholding attestations?
- Half-lives: Talent 365 days / Training 180 days / Temperament 30 days. The temperament one feels punitive. "Take a 6-week vacation and your reliability score halves" is a strange consequence — life happens.

### Aliveness (00:30)
- **This page is where I would bounce off as a casual visitor.** It is a reference document, not an explainer. Formulas, multi-step processes, transitional state tables, 10 numbered takeaways. Reads as a spec.
- **Trust Death is the scariest sentence on the site:** "T3 drops below 0.5, indicating society rejection. This resembles 'losing a professional license' and prevents rebirth entirely." Combined with hardware-bound identity, this reads as: "make enough enemies and you can never participate again, ever, with this device." The harshness isn't softened.
- The "Appeals Mechanism" exists, but filing costs ATP — and if I'm in trust death I'm likely also low on ATP. Catch-22 not addressed.
- "Hasn't been tested with real humans" — appreciated honesty.
- "Witnessed into existence: minimum of 3 independent witnesses." So to *exist* in Web4 I need 3 vouchers. What if I'm new and know nobody?
- **CI = Coherence Index** is introduced here with formula: spatial × capability × temporal × relational, geometric mean. "Spatial coherence" — does this require location tracking? Because that's the obvious surveillance concern, and the page didn't address it.

### What Could Go Wrong (00:36)
- **The strongest page on the site.** Eight risks, each with severity/likelihood. Honest about unsolved problems. "Lose device, lose everything" listed as serious/high/unsolved — exactly the concern I had on the LCT page.
- "Excludes disabled users" being acknowledged as a serious risk speaks well of the project.
- "A project about trust that hides its weaknesses is undermining its own thesis" — best line on the entire site.
- This page should be linked more prominently. It does more for credibility than any other page.

### Glossary (00:40)
- Finally all acronyms in one place:
  - LCT, ATP, ADP, T3, MRH (Mutual Relation Horizon — at last), CI, V3 (Value Tensor — new to me), R6 (Action Framework — also new).
- **9 acronyms is a lot.** Even with patient sequencing, the cognitive load is high. The site introduces them gradually, but never consolidates them in a single visual map. I had to assemble the mental model myself.

### Karma Journey (00:43)
- "Karma is mechanically defined, not morally judged" — useful disclaimer.
- "Effective trust = raw trust × CI²" — the squaring penalty is striking but also alarming. A small dip is fine, but a serious incident "nearly halves you." Combined with the 0.5 trust death threshold and the 0.85 starting CI penalty for newcomers, the system felt heavily weighted against making any mistake.
- Newcomers pay 40% more ATP per action because their CI is "unproven." Plus they need 3 witnesses to even exist. Bootstrap is a steep hill.

### Society Simulator (00:48)
- This is straightforward Axelrod-style game theory: cooperators, defectors, reciprocators, cautious, adaptive. Watch coalitions form. Watch ATP-depleted agents die.
- The framing here is much friendlier than the Aliveness page. If the site led with this kind of concrete simulation, I'd have gotten the gist faster.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust-native internet using hardware-bound IDs, energy budgets, and multi-dimensional reputation
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab / explainer for Web4 concepts and simulations
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token, hardware-rooted verifiable presence (driver's license analogy)
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budgets that deplete on action and recharge on validated contribution
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional, role-specific trust scores (talent / training / temperament)
- [~] How agents "live" and "die" — got the mechanics (ATP zero, T3 below 0.5), but the framing felt harsher than the system probably is in practice
- [~] Why any of this matters — the problem framing landed, but I'm not yet convinced the cure isn't worse than the disease for ordinary users

## Unanswered Questions
1. **What happens if I lose my phone?** The LCT page sells "hardware-bound" as a feature; the /what-could-go-wrong page admits recovery is an unsolved problem. The two pages don't talk to each other.
2. **Who decides "quality" of my work?** Recipient attestation is the answer given, but that creates a power structure where the already-popular can withhold attestations from newcomers. How is this prevented?
3. **If I'm wrongfully driven below 0.5 trust by a coordinated campaign or honest mistakes, am I genuinely banished forever?** With my hardware? Can I get a new phone and start over (a fresh start the system is designed to prevent)?
4. **Does "spatial coherence" require location tracking?** If yes, the privacy story needs to be told. If no, the page should clarify what's actually measured.
5. **Why "Web4" if not a Web3 successor?** The naming choice creates immediate friction.
6. **How do "trust neighborhoods" (MRH, 30% decay per hop) avoid creating filter bubbles?** If I only see what my 3-hop network trusts, that's literally a recommendation algorithm — just a peer-weighted one. What's the difference from an echo chamber?
7. **What is the actual path from simulation to deployment?** "47,000 lines of code and simulations" is referenced but I don't have a sense of who is running this, where, or for what.
8. **Why is Temperament half-life 30 days?** Real human life involves illness, caregiving, parental leave, sabbaticals. The system seems to penalize having one.
9. **What makes this "not blockchain" when it uses burned-token transfer fees and redistribution pools?** Naïve readers will conflate.
10. **How does the very first user get 3 witnesses?** Bootstrap problem unaddressed.

## Honest Assessment

The site is doing something hard: explaining a multi-layered protocol with novel mechanics to people who have no background. It mostly succeeds at the conceptual layer — the analogies (cellular ATP, driver's license LCT, Alice-as-analyst-vs-mechanic) are well-chosen. The "What Could Go Wrong" page is the strongest piece of the project; I have rarely seen a research site this candid about its failure modes.

But there are three structural issues that hurt a naive first-time visit:

**One — the most prominent navigation link is broken.** "Start Here" 404s. This is not a minor issue. It's the literal first signpost a visitor sees, and it dead-ends. I considered leaving the site. If I had been a real human with no commitment to the topic, I would have. This needs to be the top priority fix.

**Two — the system's harshest mechanics are introduced without enough softening.** "Agents die when ATP hits zero." "Trust death prevents rebirth entirely." "Newcomers pay 40% more ATP per action." Each of these is technically accurate, but encountered in sequence by a naive reader, they paint a picture of a hostile system that punishes ordinary mistakes and excludes outsiders. The /what-could-go-wrong page does great work acknowledging this — but the explainer pages themselves should soften and contextualize, not just state the rules. The mathematical sharpness of a spec is a poor fit for an onboarding page.

**Three — acronym density.** Nine acronyms, introduced one at a time, deferred to specialized pages, never consolidated visually until the glossary. The cumulative cognitive load by the time I reached the Aliveness page (with its formulas and CI = (spatial × capability × temporal × relational)^(1/4)) was high enough that I'd predict most visitors stop reading. A single "vocabulary at a glance" graphic on the landing page would help enormously.

The site clearly knows it's research. The honesty is refreshing. But the question I kept asking — "would I want to live in this system?" — wasn't answered by the time I'd seen the core concept pages. I saw a lot of rules and consequences and very little of what a good day in Web4 looks like for an ordinary, well-meaning user. The negative mechanics (death, decay, costs, witnesses) are described in much more detail than the positive ones (community, recovery, growth, reward). That imbalance shapes the read.

Two suggestions:

1. **Lead with someone living well**, not the rules of survival. Show a "day in Web4" from Alice's perspective — Alice posts, Alice's contribution gets validated, Alice's reputation grows, Alice helps a newcomer, Alice's friend Bob has a bad day and recovers. Then introduce the mechanics that make this possible.
2. **Fix "Start Here"** before anything else, and make sure every link on the landing page goes somewhere. The visitor's first 30 seconds shape the rest.
