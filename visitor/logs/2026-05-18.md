## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident framing. "A Trust-Native Internet Lab" with an immediate, accurate disclaimer: "a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor." That last clause preempted exactly the wrong assumption I was about to make (I assumed this was a blockchain/Web3 thing from the name "Web4").
- The problem statement landed hard: "Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos. Bad actors get unlimited fresh starts. AI deception outpaces verification." I felt that. This is a real itch.
- It explicitly says "not a product, not a company, not a blockchain" and "Right now, you can't install it." Good — I knew where I stood immediately.
- Clicked: the prominent "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" (clearly the recommended path).
- Confusion points: Five ideas with shorthand "LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI" flashed by. Five acronyms before I'd read anything — I noted them but didn't retain them.

### 2-Minute TL;DR (03:00)
- The hook — "What if every online action had a cost, every identity was tied to physical hardware, and your reputation followed you everywhere — permanently?" — is genuinely intriguing and concrete.
- Four pillars (energy/ATP, device identity/LCT, portable reputation, humans=AI=agents) are explained in plain language with acronyms defined on first use. This was the clearest single page so far.
- **Friction**: The page says "No prerequisites" but then introduces ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, *and* CI. By the end of a "2-minute" page I'd been handed five acronyms. It's not that any one is hard — it's the cumulative load. "No prerequisites" set an expectation the content didn't quite keep.
- Clicked: "Why Web4?" — I wanted the *why* before more *what*.

### Why Web4? (08:00)
- Opens strong: "The Internet Has a Trust Problem. You already know this. You feel it every day." It tells me upfront it's 3 parts (~5 min problems, ~2 min proposal, optional Q&A). I appreciated knowing the shape.
- The four problems each have a stated "root cause" — diagnosis feels systematic, not ranty. The "Honest caveat:" pattern in the Q&A built real trust with me; a sales pitch that admits its own tradeoffs reads as honest.
- **Friction**: This page is *enormous*. The FAQ is the bulk of it. As a first-timer right after the TL;DR, going from a 2-minute page to what feels like a 15,000-word reference is a cliff. I skimmed and bailed to the FAQ topic index rather than read linearly — which I think is intended, but the transition felt abrupt.
- **Friction**: The four mechanism cards say "Learn more →" but the page doesn't show how they *interlock* until a small "All four, in one moment" section near the bottom. I wanted that synthesis earlier — ideally a diagram.
- Clicked: "First Contact" — I wanted to *see* it, not read more.

### First Contact (14:00)
- This is the right idea: drive a character ("Alice") through a 5-act arc (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn). "Show, don't tell" — exactly what I wanted after two text-heavy pages.
- The promise that each concept is explained "the moment Alice uses it" is a great design principle.
- **Friction**: Still couldn't tell if this is a thing that exists or a research thought-experiment. The simulation is vivid and present-tense; the "research prototype" caveat is muted. I left slightly unsure whether I was watching a *demo of a real system* or a *narrative illustration of an idea*.

### Karma Journey (19:00)
- Strong clarification I needed: "karma is **not** a moral judgment system" — instead, "your actions in this life become starting conditions for the next." That reframing made it click.
- The raw vs. effective trust distinction (raw = (T+T+T)/3 for survival ≥0.5; effective = raw × CI² for next-life seed) is elegant but dense. I had to read it twice. The "why squaring?" explanation comes *after* the formula — I wanted the intuition ("punishes inconsistency harder") first.
- **Friction**: The death/rebirth metaphor here vs. the note that real Web4 is "license suspension" created a small jolt. Which mental model do I hold?

### Society Simulator (24:00)
- Big payoff page: "12 agents... form alliances, betray each other, and self-organize — no central authority, just trust dynamics." The core question ("Can self-interested agents develop cooperation without central authority?") is compelling.
- Good: there's an explicit "New to these terms? Start with First Contact" entry point, and a collapsible key glossing ATP, T3, LCT, CI inline.
- **Friction (recurring-feeling)**: The acronym key covers four terms — but the page *also* uses **MRH** and **V3** in the "what's simplified" note ("doesn't fade with social distance (MRH), isn't scored per individual contribution (V3)"). The parenthetical phrasings *are* a gloss, but the acronyms still feel raw because they're the only two NOT in the key the page itself provides. A page referencing terms its own glossary omits is jarring — it implies I missed something.

### LCT Explainer (28:00)
- The "your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied or impersonated" analogy is the best single analogy on the site. "The chip's private key is generated inside the silicon and physically can't leave it" — clear, no crypto background needed.
- **Friction**: I couldn't quickly tell if I need to *buy hardware*. The reassuring answer ("most modern phones/laptops already have these chips") is buried below the chip-type list. For a beat I assumed I'd need special equipment.
- **Friction**: "Witness" is explained three ways and explicitly means two different things (my own devices co-signing vs. optional infrastructure nodes). The Tokyo example is great, but the dual meaning of one word made me reread.

### ATP Economics (32:00)
- "In one line: ATP is your energy budget" — consistent gloss, repeated. Good. The clarifier that "attention/usage budget" are *rewordings, not alternate expansions* directly answered a question I was forming.
- **Friction**: ADP shows up ~30% in ("you get an ADP — a receipt"). I'd been reading about spending ATP with no mention of the receipt/recharge half of the loop. The cycle felt incomplete until ADP finally appeared. Introduce ADP alongside ATP, not a third of the way down.
- The continuous-recharge explanation ("rolling window, hours not seconds, 50% in an hour, 80% in six") is concrete and good.

### A Day in Web4 (36:00)
- The interactive mockups (Web4 Mail/Talent/Reviews/Social/Market) are the closest I got to *picturing* using this. Valuable — this is what I'd been wanting since "Why Web4?".
- **Friction (felt strongly)**: Tense whiplash. "Download the app ~30 seconds" / "Your trust wallet" reads like a product I can get *today*, then "This is a conceptual walkthrough — Web4 is active research" pulls it back. Within one page I flip-flopped on whether this exists.
- **Friction**: Scenario 1 ("Your Phone Wakes Up") drops "-3 ATP" and trust scores (0.50, 0.91) and "security chip" with no inline link to "what's ATP?" / "what's the 0–1 scale?" right there at the moment of confusion. The concept explainers exist in a sidebar but the scenario text itself didn't hand me a definition when I needed it.

### Aliveness (39:00)
- Clear, important distinction: **energy death (recoverable, "like bankruptcy")** vs. **trust death (T3<0.5, permanent, "like losing a professional license")**. The upfront "your reputation isn't erased" reassurance is the right thing to lead with.
- **Friction**: Three death metaphors (bankruptcy / revoked license / suspended account) are introduced in *separate* sections under separate "What does death mean?" headers. They describe one concept at different scales, but split apart they read like three different systems. I had to actively stitch them together.

## Unanswered Questions
1. Is this a *demo of a running prototype* or a *narrative illustration of an idea*? The site says "research prototype, ~47k lines tested code" but everything I can touch is a simulation. I never saw the actual code/system do anything — only stylized simulators. The First Contact and A Day in Web4 pages especially left me uncertain.
2. What does adoption realistically look like — who runs the witness infrastructure, and why would the first platform switch before there's a network? (I saw this teased but didn't chase the FAQ.)
3. On the Society Simulator: if MRH and V3 are "simplified out," what would the simulation show differently *with* them? The note tells me they're absent but not what I'm missing.

## Honest Assessment
This is, genuinely, one of the most carefully built explainer sites I've seen for an abstract technical idea. The "problem before solution" discipline, the "Honest caveat:" pattern, the plain-English-first glosses, and the interactive arcs all work. By the end I could explain Web4 to a friend in a few sentences and mean it.

The friction is no longer "I don't understand" — it's "the *seams* show." The site is assembled from many well-made pieces, but the joins between them are where I stumbled: the TL;DR over-promises "no prerequisites," "A Day in Web4" can't decide if it's a product tour or a thought experiment, the Society Simulator's own glossary contradicts itself by omitting MRH/V3, and ADP/death-metaphors arrive out of the order a first-timer needs them. None of these are comprehension-blockers individually; collectively they cost the site its "solid" rating and leave it at "good."

The single most persistent thing I could not resolve as a naive visitor: **is this real, simulated, or imagined?** The site is admirably honest that "you can't install it" — but the *experiences* it gives me (drive Alice, download the app in 30 seconds, watch a society) all speak in the present tense of a working system, while the disclaimers speak in the conditional tense of research. I believe the system is real research; the site's own framing kept undercutting that belief. Tightening tense/framing consistency on the experiential pages would do more for trust than any new content.
