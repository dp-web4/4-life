## Honest Assessment

The site has **one truly excellent page** (`/what-could-go-wrong`) and **one excellent example** (the Birth → Life → Death → Rebirth numeric lifecycle on `/how-it-works`). Both are buried beneath a layer of mechanism explainers that fail the naive reader.

The site's biggest UX problem isn't any one page — it's the **acronym shock** that hits at the TLDR-to-mechanism transition. Every page from there onward assumes I'm comfortable carrying ATP/LCT/T3/MRH/CI/ADP in working memory. I'm not, and I doubt anyone is on a first visit.

The site's biggest UX strength is its **honest tone**. Whenever the writing admits unknowns ("the question is whether the real world behaves like the simulations," "no amount of good engineering solves a coordination problem"), trust goes *up*. The site should borrow this voice everywhere — particularly in mechanism explainers, which currently read as declarative ("here's how X works") when they could read as confessional ("here's our current theory of how X works, and here's what we don't know").

The interactive elements (`/first-contact`, `/society-simulator`, `/karma-journey`) are the genuine draw of this site. I never reached them through reading because the reading wore me down. **The reading-to-doing ratio is wrong.** I should have been clicking into a 30-second mini-sim by minute 3, not minute 25.

If I could change one thing: put a working, embedded, no-decision-required mini-sim on `/tldr`. Show me a single agent burning ATP, getting confirmations, leveling up T3, in 60 seconds. Words afterward.

Productive ambivalence: I came in skeptical, left somewhat trusting the project but unconvinced it'll work outside simulation. Which is exactly what `/what-could-go-wrong` claims is the right state to be in. If the rest of the site achieved that page's tone, I'd recommend it without hesitation.
