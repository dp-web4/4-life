## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: immediately credible. The headline "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" felt like an actual claim, not marketing fluff. The single recommended path ("Start here: 2-minute TL;DR") removed the usual where-do-I-click paralysis.
- The five-acronym box (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI) with one-line plain-English glosses was a great preview — I knew what I was about to wade into.
- The three disclaimers ("not installable, not a company, no profit motive") landed especially well — they pre-empted my skepticism.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes — /tldr (02:30)
- Hit the four-mechanic overview: actions cost energy, identity is hardware-bound, reputation is permanent, humans/AI follow the same rules.
- ATP and LCT got proper plain-English glosses. T3 was mentioned but not unpacked. MRH and CI weren't mentioned at all — fine for a 2-minute page, just noting it's not really "all 5 acronyms" yet.
- It probably takes ~4 minutes to read at a normal pace, not 2. Not a big deal.

### A Day in Web4 — /day-in-web4 (06:00)

- ATP got used as both "energy" and "currency" without a clean callout for the dual role. Trust scores like 0.50 and 0.91 appeared without a "what does 0.7 mean" anchor here (the trust-tensor page handles this, but late).

### Why Web4 — /why-web4 (10:00)

- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level — a research proposal that makes identity hardware-bound, actions costly, reputation portable, and humans/AI symmetric)

## Honest Assessment

The site is in genuinely good shape — better than most "research project" sites I've encountered. The journey from landing → TL;DR → Why Web4 → First Contact → concept pages → simulators is well-scaffolded. The honest disclaimers ("research prototype, not deployed", "designed but untested with humans", "calibrated, not derived") are the strongest credibility signal here, more than any technical detail.

But two acronym-expansion inconsistencies stuck out as exactly the kind of "wait, which is right?" moment that makes a visitor briefly distrust the source:

1. **ATP on First Contact** ("Attention Economics") vs. the canonical "Allocation Transfer Packets" on /atp-economics and /why-web4.
2. **CI on Karma Journey** ("Consistency Index") vs. "Coherence Index" on /coherence-index.

These are not small drift issues — they're the same inferential-misread failure mode flagged on Apr 28, just in different acronyms. The fix on Apr 28 anchored ATP correctly on /atp-economics, but the fix didn't propagate to the *first-touch* pages where naive visitors actually meet ATP (First Contact, Day in Web4). The same pattern now applies to CI: the dedicated explainer is consistent, but a high-traffic interactive page (Karma Journey) uses a different expansion.

The general lesson from Apr 28 still applies: **a literal-string check on the canonical page is necessary but not sufficient. The check has to be done across every page where the term appears for the first time in a visitor's journey.**

Beyond those two, the experience was excellent. The MRH page is the best math-flavored explainer on the site. The CI page's "coding tutorial at 10am vs. crypto spam at 3am" is the strongest concrete example I saw. The Why Web4 problem-statement section is genuinely good rhetoric without being marketing-y. And the disclaimers throughout — "no real users yet", "appeals untested", "this is calibrated, not derived" — are exactly the right tone for a research lab that wants to be taken seriously.

If the two acronym inconsistencies get reconciled site-wide, this becomes one of the cleanest research-explainer sites I've used.
