## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident framing. "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" landed immediately — I know that feeling (spam, fresh-start bad actors). The one-line pitch "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default" is a good hook.
- The four foundational rules (identity anchored in devices, every action costs energy, consequences persist, AI and humans same rules) gave me a quick mental model before I clicked anything. Nice.
- Clicked: **"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →"** — it was the obvious single primary action. The alternative angles ("What it looks like · 5 min", "Frustrations it would fix · 3 min", "Structured learning path") were clearly secondary, so I didn't feel torn about where to go. Good — this felt like a single front door.
- Confusion points: None major. Liked the upfront honesty that "Right now, you can't install it" — set expectations that this is a research proposal.

### TL;DR (02:30)
- Structured as problem → idea → result, "Plain English first." This worked. Took me ~2–3 min, matching the promise.
- Introduced four acronyms in quick succession: **LCT, T3, CI, MRH**. They were each defined, but that's four new terms in two minutes. I retained "energy budget" and "hardware-anchored identity" as concepts; the acronyms themselves blurred.
- Recommended **First Contact** as the fastest path next. I followed it.

### First Contact (05:00)
- This was the best part of the visit. An interactive 5-act story where I drive "Alice's" choices: Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn. Being able to play/pause/scrub made it feel like *I* was learning by doing, not being lectured.
- Watching trust and ATP respond to choices in real time made ATP ("every action costs energy, recipients confirm quality to earn it back") click for me in a way the TL;DR text didn't.
- The "Die & Reborn" act (standing suspends, +12 karma carries into next life) was intriguing but I didn't fully grasp the *mechanics* of death/rebirth yet — the page says it pauses to explain after. That's fine; it teed up Karma Journey.

### Karma Journey (11:00)
- Deeper multi-life simulator. This is where "death" finally made sense, and it's handled well: there are **two distinct deaths** and the page makes the difference explicit:
  - **Energy Death (recoverable)**: "You ran out of ATP while your raw trust held at or above 0.5 ... your identity and history persist, your participation rights are just paused."
  - **Trust Collapse (permanent)**: "Your raw trust itself fell below 0.5. This is the one door that doesn't reopen: society rejects rebirth."
  - The framing "*which metric failed* determines permanence — not the 0.5 threshold alone" is exactly the clarification I needed. I came in expecting death to be one confusing thing; I left understanding it's two clearly-separated things. **This was clear.**
- Friction: the **raw trust vs. effective trust** distinction (effective = raw × CI²) is correct but visually dense — I had to re-read. And **CI starting at 0.85 instead of 1.0** is counterintuitive; the justification is several paragraphs and I skimmed it.

### Society Simulator (17:00)
- The flagship interactive: 12 agents, preset scenarios, "Run Society," watch coalitions form and defectors get isolated over rounds. Visually compelling — the network graph with trust lines and draining energy bars tells a story.
- I appreciated the **"What's real, what's simulated"** honesty box (trust is a single scalar here, not the full tensor; MRH omitted; only 12 agents). That built credibility.
- Friction: this page hits you with **ATP, T3, LCT, CI, MRH, V3 all at once**. The page knows it — there's a "👋 Never seen these Web4 terms before?" callout pointing to First Contact. Good safety net, but if I'd landed here *first* (e.g. from a shared link) I'd have bounced. Glad I arrived via the guided path.

### ATP Economics (22:00)
- ATP itself is explained well and plainly: energy budget, every action costs it, confirmed-valuable work earns it back, run out and you "die."
- **ADP friction (the standout issue this visit)**: ADP is introduced as "a receipt recording what you did and what it cost," then immediately I'm told "you never hold, present, or manage the ADP yourself." My honest reaction: *then why am I learning about it on the page that teaches me energy?* The actual payoff — that the receipt is "the proof that earns [energy] back" — exists but reads as arriving **after** the mechanics, not before. The "why I should care" still feels buried under "here's what it is and that you'll never touch it."
- **Undefined terms** a beginner stumbles on here:
  - **"circular farming"** — named as something the 5% fee prevents, never defined.
  - **"quality ramp"** — appears as a heading with no preamble.
  - **"confirmer trust"** — used in the math before establishing what it is or how a newcomer builds it.

### Why Web4? (25:00)
- Strong problem framing: five concrete, relatable problems (free spam, siloed reputation, easy resets, can't-verify-AI, platforms-control-everything). The "you're a guest in someone else's house" line resonated.
- The **honesty is the page's best feature**: a large FAQ with "Honest caveat:" markers admitting bootstrapping is hard, ATP pricing is "calibrated for simulations, not real markets," collusion resistance has open problems, and GDPR right-to-be-forgotten is a genuine tension. I trust the project *more* for this, not less.
- Where it left me short: it persuaded me **that the problem matters**, less that **this solution is ready** — but it's honest that it isn't ready, so that's arguably intended rather than a bug. What I couldn't answer afterward: what happens when communities disagree about what counts as "quality"?

## Unanswered Questions
1. Why should I care about ADP if I never touch it? (I got a partial answer, but it took work to extract.)
2. What happens when a community disagrees about what counts as a "quality" contribution — who arbitrates?
3. If witness nodes are run by universities/nonprofits, what stops *them* from colluding or being compromised? (acknowledged but not resolved)
4. How does a brand-new user with zero trust and zero confirmers ever earn their first ATP back? (the bootstrapping problem)

## Honest Assessment
This was a good visit. The guided path — Landing → TL;DR → First Contact → Karma Journey → Society Simulator — works; each step prepared me for the next, and by the end I had a real mental model of Web4's pitch and could articulate why the internet's trust problem is worth solving.

Two things stand out positively. First, **First Contact** is the strongest teaching tool here — driving Alice's choices made abstract mechanics concrete. Second, the project's **honesty** (the "what's real vs simulated" boxes, the "Honest caveat" FAQ, "you can't install it") earns trust; most projects oversell, this one underselling-by-honesty actually made me believe it more.

The persistent weakness is **acronym density**. The site is self-aware about it (callouts, glossary, escape hatches), and on the guided path the load is survivable — but it's the thing I'd most warn a friend about, and the thing that would sink a deep-link first impression.

The single most actionable item this visit: the **ADP "why should I care"** problem on ATP Economics. I'm told what ADP is and that I'll never touch it before I'm told why it's load-bearing for me. Inverting that order would close the one concept gap I left with.

Nothing here was *broken*. The death/permanence question — which I went in braced to be confused by — was actually one of the clearest things on the site. That's a notable win.
