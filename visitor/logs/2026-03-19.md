## Unanswered Questions
1. **Who is building this?** The site mentions "active research" and "open-source" but I never learned about the team, their backgrounds, or what institution (if any) supports this work. There's no "About" or "Team" page.
2. **What's the actual adoption plan?** The 5-tier adoption pathway (wrapper → native) is mentioned in FAQs but never given a concrete timeline, milestones, or first target community. For a project that lists "Nobody Adopts It" as its #1 risk, the adoption strategy feels underdeveloped on the site.
3. **How accurate are the simulations?** The site says "working simulations" with 47,000 lines of tested code, but the simulations run 4-20 agents. How do I know these dynamics hold at 1,000 or 1,000,000 agents? The threat model admits this is unknown, but it would help to see more explicit discussion of simulation→reality gap.
4. **What would it feel like to use this daily?** "Day in Web4" has great scenarios but they're textual descriptions. I want to see a prototype UI, even a rough one. The mockup images are described but I want to click through them.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thorough and intellectually honest technical sites I've browsed. The commitment to transparency — publishing 400+ attack vectors, listing seven major failure modes, admitting what's unsolved — builds genuine trust in the project's credibility.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- The TL;DR → Why Web4 → First Contact progressive disclosure pipeline is excellent. I went from zero knowledge to solid understanding in about 15 minutes.
- "Day in Web4" and "Your Internet" ground abstract concepts in real experiences. These are the pages that made me care.
- "What Could Go Wrong" is the most impressive page — its honesty is the strongest argument FOR the project.
- The featured narratives (especially the Tidepool/Garden comparison) demonstrate genuine emergent insight, not just techno-optimism.

**What still creates friction:**
- The site has an enormous amount of content (22 pages deep and I still haven't visited everything). Some pages (Federation Economics, Threat Model) are individually as long as academic papers. This is impressive for credibility but overwhelming for a visitor trying to understand the basics.
- There's a difficulty cliff between the "Getting Started" pages (TL;DR, Why Web4, First Contact) and the concept pages (Federation Economics, Threat Model). The middle ground exists (concept sequence pages) but even those can get dense.
- The project feels somewhat anonymous — no team page, no institutional affiliation visible. For a project about trust, knowing who's behind it would help.

**Overall:** I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left with a solid understanding of the core ideas, genuine appreciation for the intellectual rigor, and curiosity about whether this could actually work. The site does what it sets out to do: explain complex ideas to newcomers while being honest about limitations. The main area for improvement is managing the depth gradient — the jump from "accessible introduction" to "academic depth" can be jarring.
