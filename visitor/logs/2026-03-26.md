## Unanswered Questions
1. **Who determines "quality"?** The system rewards quality contributions with ATP, but the measurement mechanism isn't explicitly described on the ATP Economics page. I inferred it's community voting from Alice's story, but it could be algorithmic. This is a fundamental question that should be answered clearly and early.
2. **What happens with shared devices?** Family tablets, library computers, school Chromebooks — the LCT model assumes personal device ownership. How does Web4 handle shared hardware?
3. **How does the 5% transfer fee work across communities?** If different communities have different ATP values (mentioned on the Society Simulator page), does the 5% fee apply to the raw ATP number or the adjusted cross-community value?
4. **What prevents buying multiple devices for multiple identities?** The LCT page explains hardware binding but doesn't address someone who buys 10 phones to create 10 identities. Is this addressed by the witness requirement? By cost? Both?
5. **How does Web4 handle VPNs and Tor?** The Coherence Index tracks spatial consistency, but many legitimate users mask their location. Does this permanently cap their CI?
6. **If trust is permanent, how does a teenager escape youthful mistakes?** The "consequences are permanent" principle is strong, but what about developmental growth? Is there a statute of limitations on early-life trust damage?
7. **What does "ADP" stand for?** ATP is well-explained (Allocation Transfer Packets), but ADP appears in the site title and some page references without clear definition. Is it the "spent" form of ATP (like biological ADP)?
8. **How would Web4 handle pseudonymous whistleblowing?** If your reputation follows you permanently, can you safely expose wrongdoing without retaliation being attached to your identity?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoughtfully constructed explainer sites I've encountered. The progression from "here's the problem" (landing page) to "here's the idea" (TL;DR) to "see it in action" (First Contact, Day in Web4) to "here's what could fail" (What Could Go Wrong) is genuinely well-designed.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- The honesty. "This is research, not a product." "Recovery from total device loss is unsolved." "Nobody adopts it is the #1 existential risk." This level of transparency builds more trust than any marketing could.
- The "Day in Web4" app mockups. Abstract concepts become immediately tangible when I see a mock inbox with trust scores next to sender names.
- Progressive disclosure. I could read the TL;DR and leave with a decent understanding, or go 15 pages deep and still find new layers.
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page. Publishing your own failure analysis is the strongest credibility signal on the site.

**Where it struggles:**
- Acronym density. ATP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI, SAL — seven acronyms for a newcomer to absorb. Each makes sense individually but the cognitive load of tracking all of them simultaneously is high.
- The jump from intuitive (Day in Web4, First Contact) to technical (Aliveness, Trust Tensor deep sections) is steep. The learn page structures this well, but if you browse organically (as I did), you can stumble into dense material before you're ready.
- The quality measurement gap. The entire system rests on "quality contributions earn ATP back" but exactly how quality is measured is left vague across multiple pages. This is arguably the most important implementation detail for a skeptical visitor.

**Overall:** I left the site understanding what Web4 proposes, why it matters, and where it stands. I also left knowing what's unsolved, which is rare and valuable. I'd return to try the interactive simulations and explore the intermediate/advanced content. The site respects my intelligence without assuming expertise — a difficult balance that it mostly achieves.

The biggest risk I see isn't in the technology — it's in the "who decides quality" question. If that's community-driven, it inherits all the problems of community governance (mob dynamics, majority bias, niche content undervalued). If it's algorithmic, it inherits all the problems of algorithmic content moderation (opacity, bias, gaming). The site doesn't fully address this tension, and it's the one that would make or break real-world adoption.
