## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean and confident. The headline *"4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations"* told me this is a place to *play with* an idea, not buy a product. I appreciated the immediate honesty: *"not 'Web3 + 1' — no blockchain, no tokens, no speculation"* and *"a research proposal, not a product or company."* That defused my "is this a crypto scam?" reflex within seconds.
- The problem hook — *"Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos"* — landed clean. I understood the pain before any jargon.
- **Clicked**: The recommended *"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR."*
- **Confusion points**: The "five core ideas" block shows plain-English names (identity, energy, trust, neighborhood, consistency) *plus* their acronyms (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI) all at once. As a newcomer this was my first hint that I'd be memorizing an alphabet soup. It didn't hurt yet, but it foreshadowed the friction to come.

### Why Web4 (03:00)
- The four-part problem framing (spam is free, reputation is siloed, bad actors get fresh starts, AI makes it worse) was genuinely excellent. *"Actions have no cost"* is a sentence I could repeat to a friend.
- The solution section maps four mechanisms cleanly to the four problems. Good structure.
- **Confusion**: The acronyms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) show up in the *problem/solution prose* before the *"This Is What Web4 Proposes"* section formally introduces them. So on first read I hit "ATP" and "T3" as unexplained tokens, then found their definitions a scroll later. I also met **V3**, **CI**, and **SAL** in the FAQ *before* they were properly defined. There's a *"Lost in the acronyms? Quick index"* link — which is a kind acknowledgment, but it sends me *away* from the page to decode rather than defining the term where I'm standing.

### First Contact (08:00)
- This was my favorite page. An **interactive 5-act story** where I make choices for "Alice" and watch two numbers move: 🔋 Energy Budget (ATP, starts 100) and 🤝 Trust Score (0.00–1.00, starts 0.50). Concrete, controllable, not a video. This is the moment Web4 stopped being abstract.
- Acronyms here **are** defined inline as they appear (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) — a noticeable and welcome contrast with Why Web4.
- **Confusion**: The mention of "hestia" and "the hub" (deployed code) versus the in-browser simulation required careful reading to separate "what runs in my browser right now" from "real deployed Web4 code." As a naive visitor I don't yet care about that distinction and it briefly muddied things. "Karma carries forward" was named but its mechanics stayed vague until I later found the Karma Journey page.

### A Day in Web4 (13:00)
- Ten dated scenarios across one day ("📱 Your Phone Wakes Up," 7:30 AM…). The everyday framing is a smart idea — it's the most "so what does this feel like to live in?" page.
- **Confusion (this was my biggest friction of the session)**:
  1. **ATP is used as the energy currency but is never actually defined on this page** — I only knew what it meant because First Contact and ATP Economics had taught me. A visitor who lands here first (e.g., via the "What it looks like" button, which is a *primary* landing CTA) would be lost.
  2. Same *"Lost in the acronyms? Quick index →"* escape hatch instead of inline definitions.
  3. **Conditional tense whiplash**: the setup section keeps saying what Web4 *"would"* look like, so I couldn't tell whether the daily scenarios describe something that exists today or a hypothetical future. Given the landing page already told me this is a research proposal, the scenarios reading as present-tense-but-actually-hypothetical was disorienting.
  4. "Hardware ceilings (0.85 from phone chips)" appears with no explanation of *why* a phone would cap your trust.

### ATP Economics (18:00)
- Strong page. "Every action costs ATP. Every contribution others find valuable earns it back." The biological analogy (cells burn ATP → ADP → recharge from food) genuinely helped me *feel* the loop rather than memorize it.
- The **ATP Simulator** (spam −5, low-quality net −5, meaningful +10, high-value +30) made the incentive structure tangible. The ~7× quality gap is a memorable headline number.
- **Confusion**: ADP's "it's not a second currency, just a receipt" distinction arrives *after* my brain has already filed it as "a second token." The recharge-vs-payment split (confirmations refund what you spent; commissioned work pays what it's worth — only the latter is net gain) is important but buried in the math section. And "who actually confirms my work, and how many confirmations do I need?" was never answered — I don't know if one person's click recharges me.

### Trust Tensor (23:00)
- Clear three-dimensional model: **Talent** (aptitude), **Training** (learned skill), **Temperament** (reliability/ethics), each assessed *within a role*. The "Alice as data analyst ≠ Alice as mechanic" example is excellent.
- The **decay half-lives** have a genuinely satisfying rationale: *"Character has to be shown fresh each month; skill stays earned for a year"* (Temperament 30d, Training 180d, Talent 365d). That one sentence justified numbers that would otherwise feel arbitrary.
- **Interactive** Trust Tensor Simulator (pick a role, apply a scenario, watch dimensions shift) reinforced the "trust is not one number" thesis.
- **Confusion**: The word "tensor" is intimidating and, for me, still doesn't feel earned — it's really "three numbers in a role context," and calling it a tensor added awe without adding understanding. **V3** gets name-dropped mid-page before it's explained, adding load.

### Karma Journey (26:00)
- Good payoff for the "death & rebirth" language teased earlier. Two death types are cleanly distinguished: **energy death** (ATP exhausted, trust still ≥0.5 → recoverable suspension) vs **trust collapse** (trust <0.5 and stays → permanent revocation). The honesty that "real Web4 wouldn't offer rebirth after collapse, but the sim does for teaching" is the kind of candor I trust.
- The reframe *"The word [karma] here is a handle, not a moral score"* is helpful.
- **Confusion**: **Two trust numbers on screen at once** — raw trust 0.500 (survival) vs effective trust 0.361 (karma tier) — with no immediate on-screen explanation of why they differ or which one "I" am. This is the single most cognitively expensive thing I hit on any interactive page. The CI² penalty ("a 10% dip costs ~19%, a 40% dip costs 64%") is clever but needs me to do algebra to feel it. LCT is mentioned but never defined here.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposal to bake portable, behavior-based trust and an energy cost into the internet
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab to *feel* those mechanics via simulations
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound identity token (learned it, though it was orphaned on 2 pages)
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget + spend-receipt cycle; the biological analogy sealed it
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three role-scoped dimensions (Talent/Training/Temperament), not one score
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — energy death (recoverable) vs trust collapse (permanent); karma carries state forward
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam/siloed-reputation/fresh-start/AI-impersonation all trace to "actions have no cost"

Notably: I got a **good** understanding, but I earned much of it by *cross-referencing pages* (ATP was only defined for me on First Contact and ATP Economics, not on Day in Web4 where I also needed it). A visitor who took a different path could easily end up with holes.

## Unanswered Questions
1. Who actually confirms my contributions, and does a single confirmation recharge me or do I need many?
2. On Karma Journey, which number is "really me" — raw trust or effective trust — and why is the gap so large right at the start?
3. Are the "A Day in Web4" scenarios describing something that exists now or a hypothetical future? The conditional tense left me unsure.
4. Why does a phone chip cap trust at 0.85 — what's the underlying reason a device limits how trusted I can be?
5. What is V3 (Value Tensor)? It's name-dropped on multiple pages but I never got a clean standalone explanation on the pages I visited.

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely good site — better than most explainers of a novel technical idea. The honesty is its superpower: telling me upfront it's a research proposal with no blockchain, and admitting what's unproven, made me *trust the trust site*, which is a nice bit of practicing-what-you-preach. The interactive pages (First Contact, ATP Simulator, Trust Tensor Simulator) are where I actually learned; the prose pages set up the *why*.

The one friction that runs through the whole experience is **acronym density on the front-door pages**. First Contact does it right — every acronym is glossed inline as you meet it. But Why Web4 and especially A Day in Web4 use ATP/T3/MRH/V3/CI/SAL in prose before (or without) defining them, leaning on a "Quick index →" link that pulls me off the page to decode. Because "A Day in Web4" is reachable directly from a primary landing button, a real newcomer can land on a page that uses "ATP" as if I already know it. The fix isn't a redesign — it's making each acronym's *first appearance on a front-door page* carry a 3–5 word inline gloss, the way First Contact already does.

Second friction: the Karma Journey showing raw 0.500 and effective 0.361 side by side with no on-screen legend. That's the one interactive moment where I felt genuinely lost about what I was looking at.

If those two things were smoothed, I'd move from "would recommend to a curious technical friend" to "would recommend to anyone who's ever been annoyed by spam."
