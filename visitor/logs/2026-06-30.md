## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Strong, honest hook — *"The Internet Has a Trust Problem."* That's a problem I actually feel (spam, bots), so I wanted to keep reading. The line *"Identity costs something, every action costs energy, and consequences follow you forever"* is intriguing but also a little ominous on first read.
- **Immediate friction**: Right under the hook the page throws five acronyms at me — *Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI)*. I don't know what any of them mean yet. The plain-English words are there, which helps, but seeing five unexplained capital-letter codes on the *very first screen* made me brace for a dense, jargon-heavy ride.
- **Clicked**: The obvious entry point, *"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR."*

### Web4 in 2 Minutes (/tldr) (03:00)
- This page rescued my first impression. The opening promise — *"Plain English first, so you can read this cold — every term is spelled out"* — is exactly what a confused newcomer needs, and it delivers.
- Each acronym is finally unpacked in a sentence I can follow: ATP = an energy budget that refills when others confirm your work was useful; LCT = an identity record anchored to your devices; T3 = three trust dimensions; MRH = your trust "neighborhood"; CI = whether you stay consistent.
- **Honest note**: This is the page that should arguably be the *first* thing I see, not one click down. The landing page front-loads the codes; this page front-loads the meaning. If they were swapped (or the landing used zero acronyms above the fold), I'd never have braced for jargon at all.

### Why Web4 (/why-web4) (06:00)
- Best-structured page so far. *"Why Web4? The Problem Before the Solution"* frames five concrete frustrations I recognize: spam winning, reputation trapped in silos, bad actors getting infinite fresh starts, AI making it worse, platforms controlling everything (*"guests in someone else's house"* landed for me).
- Then it maps each problem to a mechanism (ATP / LCT / T3 / MRH). Problem-before-solution ordering works really well here.
- The **"Honest Questions" FAQ** (~75 Q&As, including hardware loss, "powerful liars," social-credit fears) genuinely impressed me — it pre-empts the suspicious questions a skeptic like me would ask. This is a trust-builder.

### First Contact (/first-contact) (10:00)
- *"Your First Web4 Experience"* — an interactive walkthrough where I drive a character named **Alice** through five acts and watch her Energy (starts 100) and Trust (starts 0.50) respond.
- This is the kind of concrete, grounded example the abstract pages need. Watching a number move when Alice spams vs. contributes is far more convincing than a definition.

### LCT Explainer (/lct-explainer) (13:00)
- Defines LCT as *"a digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip — hardware-bound, witnessed by your other devices."* The core idea (an identity that can't be faked) lands fast.
- **Friction**: the *mechanics* lose me. "TPM," "Secure Enclave," "cryptographic" — the page does circle back to define these, but as a non-technical visitor I had to be patient. The **Device Witness slider** (1→5 devices, showing attack difficulty) is a nice touch that makes the abstract concrete.

### Karma Journey (/karma-journey) (16:00)
- *"Live multiple lives. Make choices. Watch how trust and karma compound across reincarnations."* Actually playable — a character sheet (Trust raw 0.500 / effective 0.361, Energy 100, Consistency 0.85) and seven choices (cooperative / selfish / bold).
- Fun and clarifying. **Minor confusion**: "Trust raw 0.500 vs effective 0.361" — I don't know why they differ or which one matters. The gap appeared without explanation on the sheet.

### ATP Economics (/atp-economics) (19:00)
- *"ATP: Allocation Transfer Packets — Every Action Has a Cost."* The battery/charge metaphor (*"glance at your balance the way you'd check your phone's charge"*) is the clearest analogy on the whole site. The "it's not money, you can't hoard it" framing answered my immediate "is this crypto?" suspicion.
- ADP = "Allocation Discharge Packet," a background receipt. Clear enough.
- **Friction**: The acronym *expansions* feel arbitrary to me. "Allocation Transfer Packets" / "Allocation Discharge Packets" only make sense if you already know the biology pun (ATP/ADP = adenosine tri/di-phosphate in cells). As a naive visitor I don't, so the letters look like they were reverse-engineered to fit. The page mentions the cell-biology origin, but briefly.

### Aliveness (/aliveness) (21:00)
- *"Aliveness: When Existence is Measurable."* Alive = ATP > 0 AND Trust > 0.5 AND Coherence coherent. Miss one = death.
- Good clarification that **death ≠ deletion** — energy death is recoverable (like bankruptcy), trust death is permanent (like losing a license).
- **Friction**: The death language is *heavy*. Bankruptcy, license revocation, "society refuses rebirth" — stacked together it tips from "stakes are real" toward slightly morbid. A casual visitor might find it off-putting before they're invested enough to appreciate the nuance.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a trust layer where identity costs something and actions cost energy
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive simulation of agents living/dying under these rules
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored identity that can't be faked
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — a refillable energy budget for actions (ATP) and its background receipt (ADP)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3: Talent/Training/Temperament, scored per role
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive requires energy + trust + coherence; death is suspension, not deletion
- [x] Why any of this matters — the "Why Web4" problem framing made the motivation concrete

## Unanswered Questions
1. The "raw vs effective" trust distinction showed up in the simulator but I never found where it's explained.
2. If identity is bound to my device's chip, what *actually* happens when I lose/upgrade my phone? (The FAQ apparently addresses this — I didn't open it — but it's a question I had the moment I read the LCT page.)
3. Who decides my contribution was "useful" enough to refund my ATP? The mechanism of *who confirms value* stayed fuzzy.
4. Is any of this real/running today, or purely a simulation? The site is honest that real-world deployment is untested, but I wasn't 100% sure what's live vs. theoretical.

## Honest Assessment
This is one of the more genuinely well-built explainer sites I've browsed. The information architecture is sound: problem-before-solution, plain-English definitions, interactive sims to ground the abstractions, and a strikingly honest FAQ that pre-empts skeptical questions instead of dodging them. By the end I understood all seven core concepts — and the site *taught* me most of them rather than leaving me to infer.

My single biggest friction is the **first impression**. The landing hero is excellent at stating the *problem* but immediately hits me with five capital-letter codes before defining any of them — which made me brace for a dense, jargon-heavy slog. The very next page (/tldr) does the opposite and is wonderful. The site's best onboarding asset is one click too deep. Swap the emphasis — plain words first, codes earned later — and the "would recommend" jumps from *maybe* to *yes*.

The secondary theme: small unexplained numbers and heavy metaphors. The "raw vs effective trust" gap, the arbitrary-feeling ATP/ADP expansions, and the stacked death analogies are each minor, but together they're the difference between "clear" and "effortless." The bones are excellent; the remaining work is polish on the seams.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (02:00)
- This is exactly what a newcomer needs. "Plain English first, so you can read this cold — every term is spelled out the first time it appears." That promise was kept.
- Structure (Problem → Idea → Result) is the right shape. The line "Web4 is the protocol — the rules and math; 4-Life (this site) is the lab where you explore those rules" finally cleared up the relationship between "Web4" and "4-Life," which had been a low-grade question in my head since the landing page.

### Why Web4 (06:00)
- Strong. Five concrete problems (spam is free, reputation trapped in silos, banned users get fresh starts, AI agents make it worse, platforms control everything). Each rang true.
- The four solution cards (ATP / LCT / T3 / MRH) each glossed the acronym in plain language right where it appeared — e.g. "ATP (Allocation Transfer Packets) — just means your energy budget." This is exactly what I needed; I never felt stranded by an acronym.

### First Contact / "Your First Web4 Experience" (11:00)
- An interactive 7-act simulation following a character "Alice." Two live metrics: 🔋 Energy Budget (ATP) and 🤝 Trust Score (0.00–1.00, starts at 0.50). Both were explained inline before being used. Good.
- Reassuring note: "you don't need to memorize these. Each one is explained in the story." That lowered my anxiety about the acronyms.
- Minor: the landing page's "2-minute TL;DR" and this "7-minute walkthrough" are different things; I briefly wasn't sure which was the *real* starting point. Not blocking — both are good — but the menu of entry points (2-min / 5-min / 7-min / structured path) is itself a small decision I had to make before learning anything.

### How It Works (16:00)
- Honest framing up front: "Nothing here is live — this is a research prototype." I appreciated not being oversold.
- This is the dense, full-picture page, and it shows. The acronyms arrive in a cluster ("LCT = Identity, ATP = Energy budget, T3 = Trust score, MRH = context boundary, CI = behavioral consistency") and then the page gets mathy fast.
- Friction: formulas appear with no plain-language translation beside them. `base_delta = 0.02 × (quality − 0.5)` and a chain-decay example `0.9^5 = 0.59` both showed up cold. As a non-math reader I skipped right past them — they read as decoration, not explanation.
- I also wasn't sure whether "rebirth" needs *each* trust dimension ≥ 0.5 or just the average. The threshold is stated but the scope isn't.

### ATP Economics (21:00)
- Opens well: "ATP is your energy budget" and a genuinely helpful biology analogy ("adenosine triphosphate is the molecule your cells spend... they burn ATP, get ADP back, and recharge ATP from food"). The analogy did the heavy lifting.
- The hard math (`Δrecharge = confirmer_trust × received_value_fraction`) is tucked behind a "Show me the math" toggle, so it's opt-in — good design; I didn't have to look.
- Small snag: "ADP" gets carried by the biology analogy but never gets its own one-line plain gloss the way ATP does ("the spent-energy receipt"). I inferred it from context rather than being told.

### Glossary (25:00)
- Excellent safety net. 40+ terms, scannable, with an acronym table at top and an "Essential only (7 terms)" filter — that filter is perfect for a first-timer who doesn't want to drown.
- Each entry has a plain-English line plus an analogy. LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, SAL, CI were all clear.
- The Advanced/Research sections (Synthon, "heterogeneous review," D-numbers, Synchronism) are over my head, but they're correctly walled off in their own sections, so they didn't hurt.

### Karma Journey (29:00)
- Interactive "live multiple lives" simulation. The disclaimer "The word [karma] here is a handle, not a moral score" was a smart pre-emptive clarification — I'd otherwise have read moral judgment into it.
- The core idea (your actions in one life become the *starting conditions* of the next) is intuitive and kind of poetic.
- Friction (familiar by now): there are **two** trust numbers doing different jobs — "Raw trust" (survival, must stay > 0.5) and "Effective trust = raw × CI²" (your karma tier at death). Keeping them straight took real effort, and the CI² squaring felt like magic-math I had to take on faith.

### Coherence Index (32:00)
- Best opening analogy on the site: "The Coherence Index (CI) works like a credit check — it asks not just 'who are you?' but 'does your behavior make physical sense?'" That clicked instantly. The four consistency dimensions (location, capability, timing, relationships) were concrete.
- Then it escalated: "Effective trust = T3 × CI²" appears as fact before the "Why is it CI², not just CI?" justification much lower down. So I met the formula before its reason — same CI² snag as Karma Journey.

### Society Simulator (34:00)
- Premise is instantly graspable: "watch 12 agents... form alliances, betray each other, and self-organize — no central authority." One-click presets (Cooperative Majority, Hostile World, Reciprocity Rules, Trust Scarce, All Adaptive) make starting frictionless.
- It tells me *what to watch for* (4 phases: Exploration → Coalition Building → Defector Isolation → Equilibrium) — helpful.
- Friction: pressing "Run" is easy, but understanding *what I'm looking at* afterward isn't. The intro fires off ATP/T3/LCT/CI/MRH/V3 before the button, and the live view (colored network nodes, energy bars) has no inline legend telling me what a node's color/size or a bar means. To decode my own screen I'd have to leave the simulator for the glossary. The "see something happen" part works; the "understand what happened" part sends me away.
