## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: A serious research site. The pitch is clear: today's internet has a trust crisis, and here's a proposed redesign ("Web4"). The four mechanics are numbered and plain-English. I like that they lead with "Identity tied to your devices" rather than an acronym.
- **Clicked**: I was drawn to "2-minute TL;DR" because I have no idea what I'm looking at and 2 minutes feels like a low-commitment promise.
- **Confusion points**: LCT/ATP/T3 appear in subtext on the landing page. I know they're explained later, but I already feel like I'm being asked to track three acronyms before I understand the thing.

### TL;DR (02:30)
- The problem-solution-proof pattern is effective. "What if every online action had a cost" is a crisp hook.
- I appreciate the honesty: "active research with working simulations, not a deployed product." That lowered my skepticism instead of raising it.
- **What stuck**: Actions drain an energy budget; identity is tied to hardware; reputation is permanent; AI and humans follow the same rules.
- **What didn't**: "Linked Context Tokens" and "Trust Tensor" still felt like vocabulary I was being asked to memorize rather than ideas I understood.
- **Clicked**: "Why Web4?" because I wanted to be convinced this matters.

### Why Web4? (06:00)
- The frustrations are painfully relatable: bot spam, captive reputations, unlimited fresh starts for bad actors, AI deception. I nodded along.
- The dismissal of prior approaches (moderation, CAPTCHAs, Web3 wallets, social login) felt earned, not dismissive.
- **But**: the acronym load spiked here. In one run I encountered ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI, SAL, VCM — some with plain-English siblings, some without. The "don't worry about acronyms" nudge appears, but I still felt the cognitive tax.
- **Clicked**: First Contact, because "watch Alice's arc" sounded more concrete than more text.

### First Contact (12:00)
- A 5-act narrative with Alice — build quality, stumble with spam, recover, experience "karma carries forward." This is the right pedagogy.
- Interactive pause/scrub/replay sold me that this is a lab, not a brochure.
- I didn't actually sit through the 7-minute walkthrough fully — I skimmed the setup. A human with more time would benefit more than I did.
- **Clicked**: Society Simulator, to see the promise in action.

### Society Simulator (17:00)
- "12 agents, different strategies, watch them interact" is a compelling demo premise. The scenario presets (Cooperative Majority, Hostile World) lowered the barrier to running something.
- **Confusion**: The simulator itself assumes I already know what ATP and trust mean. The breadcrumb politely suggests starting elsewhere if I'm new, which is honest but also a sign the page can't quite stand alone.
- **Clicked**: LCT Explainer, because that was the acronym I understood the least.

### LCT Explainer (22:00)
- The Web2→Web3→Web4 progression (passwords → crypto keys → hardware verification) is a really clean analogy. I got it.
- TPM / Secure Enclave / FIDO2 examples landed because they're things I've heard of and already own.
- **What worked**: "Your other devices can recover your identity — no 'forgot password' needed." That's the reassurance I needed.
- **What didn't**: "Witnessed presence" is named a lot but remains fuzzy. Also I noticed a pseudonymity callout but didn't quite connect how hardware-bound identity doesn't mean real-name identity.
- **Question left hanging**: Why does a single-device user start at trust 0.50 and not higher? The number appears without the reasoning.

### ATP Economics (27:00)
- Costs and rewards are shown (posting -15, contribution +30). Good.

- **Unanswered in-context**: Does the 5% transfer fee come out of the sender or receiver? I had to guess. "Does ATP decay if I go on vacation?" was unanswered here.
- **What I liked**: The quality ramp (30% quality earns zero, 70% earns full) is a concrete mechanism. The zero-budget-equals-death framing is vivid.

### Day in Web4 (31:00)
- **This is the page I wish I'd found earlier.** Ten scenarios — mail, hiring, reviews, social feeds, marketplace — each with explicit ATP costs (-3 post, +20 helpful answer, 5 to post a review, 2 reduced escrow for high-trust seller). This is what I wanted on the ATP page.
- Relatable framing: "What would your day look like if the internet had trust built in?" Yes, that's exactly the question I'd ask.

### Aliveness (34:00)
- The three conditions for being "alive" (ATP > 0, Trust > 0.5, CI coherent) is the first time the whole system snapped together for me.

- **Still fuzzy**: Synthons. I got that they're emergent groups and members keep individual identity, but I don't have a mental picture of when one "forms" or how it dies.

### Trust Tensor (37:00)
- Role-specific trust (high Talent as surgeon, low Talent as mechanic) is the intuition that sold me on the 3-dimension model.
- **Confusion**: V3 (output scoring) gets introduced mid-explanation. I thought I was learning T3 and suddenly I'm learning V3. These probably need clearer visual separation.

- **"Half-life: 365 days"** for Talent decay — I don't know what a half-life means *practically* here. Does my trust drop by half after a year of inactivity? After a year regardless? That matters.

### How It Works (40:00)

- Virtuous Cascade and Death Spiral with concrete numbers are memorable.

### Karma Journey (42:00)

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a redesign of the internet where trust, identity, and action costs are built into the protocol rather than bolted on by platforms
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — a live lab showing the mechanics working in simulation, honestly flagged as not-yet-deployed
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound identity verified cooperatively by your devices, not by a company's server
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — an energy budget that actions drain and quality work replenishes (with gaps on real-life specifics)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional, role-specific trust across Talent / Training / Temperament
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive requires ATP > 0, trust > 0.5, and coherent behavior; energy death is recoverable, trust death is not
- [x] Why any of this matters — because spam and identity abuse are structural, not bugs; today's fixes are reactive

## Unanswered Questions
1. **What does ATP feel like to spend day-to-day as a user?** Day in Web4 shows numbers in specific scenarios, but I don't have an intuition for a typical day. Do I budget ATP like dollars? Do I get a visible meter?
2. **How does pseudonymity actually work when identity is hardware-bound?** I read the callout on LCT that these aren't real names, but I'd like one worked example: "Your LCT is lct:…#alice.assistant1. I can verify you're the same entity across sessions without learning your legal name."
3. **Who decides the parameters?** MRH decay rate, CI multiplier, trust thresholds — these are numbers in a simulation. In a deployed Web4, who sets them? Each community? A global spec? I don't know if this is governance, physics, or configuration.
4. **When Alice "dies" in the simulator, what's the real-world analogue?** A banned account? A frozen wallet? A deleted identity? I get the metaphor but I can't project it onto a real product.

## Honest Assessment

This is a thoughtfully built research site. The honesty about its status ("active research, not a deployed product") makes me trust the claims more, not less. The architecture — problem framing → interactive lab → concept pages → big-picture integration → governance/threats — is genuinely well-designed, and the TL;DR → First Contact → Society Simulator → concept-page loop feels like it knows what it's doing.

The single biggest friction is still **acronym density**. Individual pages do a good job explaining their own acronyms, but when I move between pages I'm carrying a growing vocabulary. The plain-English-first pattern is already adopted on Why Web4's solution section; it could extend further (e.g., "energy budget" as the primary noun on the ATP page, with "ATP" as a subtitle).

The second friction is that **ATP is the weakest concept page relative to how central the concept is.** Day in Web4 has the concrete examples ATP should have. That's a one-line fix: prominent link, or better, embed a scenario or two inline.

On the flip side, **Aliveness is the strongest page I visited** — the three conditions snapped the whole system together for me. If I were redesigning the visitor flow, I'd consider routing curious visitors through Aliveness earlier, because it's the page that made me understand that this isn't a collection of mechanisms — it's a single coherent system.

As a naive visitor, I left knowing what Web4 *proposes*, why it matters, and roughly how it would work. What I still don't know is what living in it would *feel* like — and that's probably the honest limit of a simulation-stage project, not a documentation failure.
