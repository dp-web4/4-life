## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00 – 03:30)
- First impression: clean, calm, doesn't try to sell me anything. The headline "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" is concrete and grounded — spam, fake accounts, AI verification — these are problems I actually feel.
- I appreciated that it explicitly says **"Right now, you can't install it"** and frames itself as a "research proposal." That immediately set realistic expectations and made me trust the rest.
- Clicked: the prominent "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" button. (This is well-placed; I didn't have to hunt.)
- Confusion: the formula-style summary in the middle (visual breakdown of "Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency") was a *lot* to process in one glance. I saw 5 boxes with new words and started to feel like I was being quizzed before I'd been taught.

### /tldr (03:30 – 06:00)
- The 2-minute pitch is clear at a high level: cost-per-action + hardware-bound identity + portable reputation.
- But it threw **4 new acronyms at me in 2 minutes** — ATP, ADP, LCT, MRH. That's not a TL;DR, that's a vocabulary list.
- Quick hot take: "Allocation Transfer Packet" is a forced backronym for ATP — clearly chosen so the abbreviation matches biology's ATP. As a naive reader I have no idea why I should care that it matches biology yet. The page doesn't earn the analogy here; it just leaves it.
- "MRH = Neighborhood" — but the letters M-R-H don't spell "neighborhood." So what does MRH actually stand for? The TL;DR didn't tell me. (Spoiler: the glossary doesn't either — see below.)
- Clicked: "First Contact" since it was billed as the fastest entry point.

### /first-contact (06:00 – 11:00)
- Liked the framing: "drive Alice's choices … watch trust and reputation respond in real time." This is the kind of *show-don't-tell* the rest of the site needs more of.
- Two more acronyms appeared (T3, CI). Now I'm tracking 6.
- The 5-act structure (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) is intuitive and gives the simulation a narrative spine. Good design.
- I didn't actually see whether the simulation runs server-side or what "real-time" means in practice — I just read the description. From this read I couldn't tell how rich the interactivity is.
- Honest note: I came out of First Contact understanding the *plot* but not the *mechanism*. I knew Alice would lose ATP for spam — I didn't know why a trust score would change or who decides.

### /why-web4 (11:00 – 16:00)

- But: the page kept implying that ATP/LCT/MRH/T3 do something useful, while still expecting me to believe that they cohere. I didn't feel like I'd been handed the *picture* of how they fit together — only the *list* of pieces.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level): a proposed protocol layer where actions cost energy, identity is hardware-bound, and reputation travels with you
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate: interactive simulations of those dynamics with toy populations
- [x] What LCT means functionally (hardware-rooted identity), though I still don't know what "Linked Context Token" buys me as a name vs. just "device-bound identity"
- [x] What ATP/ADP means: energy budget + spent-receipt, biological metaphor, anti-spam mechanism
- [~] What Trust Tensors are: I know T3 has three dimensions (Talent/Training/Temperament) but the *tensor* math is hand-waved
- [~] How agents "live" and "die": below 0.5 raw trust = death, karma carries forward via CI² — I get the rule but not the gameplay feel
- [~] Why any of this matters: the case is made but feels like *a research bet that the internet would be better this way*, not *urgency I personally feel*

## Unanswered Questions
1. What does **MRH** actually stand for as a phrase? The site never says.
2. Is **CI** "Coherence Index" (glossary) or "Consistency Index" (karma-journey)? They disagree.
3. **MCP** and **RDF** appear in the very formula that defines Web4 on the landing page. What are they?
4. How do all the pieces fit *into one picture*? I have a list of mechanisms; I don't have a system diagram I could explain to a friend.
5. If everything costs ATP and new joiners get 100 ATP, what stops a brand-new society from grinding to a halt before anyone earns enough back? The bootstrap problem is acknowledged but I don't know how it's actually solved.
6. Who decides what counts as "high-quality" content that earns ATP back? Validators? Witnesses? Some kind of democratic vote? Algorithms? This was the biggest unanswered "but how" of my session.
7. What does "death" actually mean for an agent in the simulation? Their data deleted? Their identity recycled? Their LCT revoked? The word "die" is used freely but the operational meaning is fuzzy.
8. The landing page lists "tier 1 through tier 5 adoption path" (per the why-web4 page). What do those tiers actually look like in practice? I came away with no concrete picture of an early-adopter experience.

## Honest Assessment

The site is **better than I expected** at the meta level — it's intellectually honest, it doesn't oversell, it has a "what could go wrong" page that any startup would refuse to publish, and the writing voice is consistently calm and adult. That alone earns my respect.

But the **on-ramp is steep**. The single biggest pattern I noticed: every page assumes you'll learn the acronyms by osmosis from the *previous* page, but the previous page assumed the same. By the time I had ATP, ADP, LCT, MRH, T3, V3, and CI all in working memory I was tired, and I'm someone who *enjoys* this kind of reading. A normal curious human would bounce off /tldr. The TL;DR is the most overloaded page and probably the leakiest funnel.

The **consistency problems** (CI's two names, MRH's hidden expansion, MCP/RDF in the formula but nowhere else) are small individually but collectively they make the site feel like a research project that hasn't gone through copy-edit pass yet. For a project whose entire selling point is *coherence as a measurable virtue*, getting acronym names to agree across pages is table stakes.

What I wish existed: a single one-page map showing **how all the mechanisms compose into one experience.** Right now each mechanism has its own page and its own pitch, but the *gestalt* — what does it feel like to live a day on Web4? what's the user story end-to-end? — is missing. The "Day in Web4" page apparently exists (mentioned in CLAUDE context but not surfaced in my journey from the landing page) — make it more discoverable. Also: surface `/what-could-go-wrong` higher. It's the most credibility-building page on the site, and I only got there because I went looking for skepticism.

Net: I left understanding the *shape* of the proposal but not the *system*. I'd come back to read more, but I'd want either a smoother onboarding sequence or a willing human to walk me through the mental model on the first pass.
