## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean and surprisingly upfront. The headline is followed immediately by a parenthetical that answers my first two suspicions before I could form them: "no blockchain, not a Web3 successor." That bought a lot of goodwill.
- The "five core ideas" list worked well for me: **Identity**, **Energy**, **Trust**, **Neighborhood**, **Consistency** — plain words first, with the acronyms (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI) trailing in a quieter register. I didn't feel assaulted by alphabet soup; I felt like the acronyms were being introduced as nicknames I'd meet again later. That's the right order.
- "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" — "Spam wins because accounts are free" is a great one-liner. I immediately understood the premise.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" because the page literally told me to start there.
- Confusion points: minor — the "What's New" section says last change Jun 11, 2026, which made me briefly wonder if the project had gone quiet (it's mid-July now).

### Web4 in 2 Minutes — /tldr (03:30)
- The promise "every term is spelled out the first time it appears" was actually kept. ATP got a biology analogy ("the molecule your cells spend to do work") that made it stick instantly.
- The four numbered principles (energy costs, hardware identity, portable trust, universal rules) are the clearest single summary on the site. If I'd read only this page, I'd have walked away with the gist.
- Confusion: the "Where is this now?" section says **hestia** is "the trust layer, *deployed and running* on your own hardware." On *whose* hardware? Mine? I haven't installed anything. I think it means "it runs on your own hardware (rather than a cloud)" but on first read it sounded like a claim that something is already running on my device. Also "hestia" and "the hub" arrive as lowercase proper nouns with no explanation of what the names mean — I shrugged and moved on, which I think is the intended reaction, but the "your own hardware" phrasing tripped me.
- Followed the recommended next step: First Contact.

### First Contact — /first-contact (08:00)
- This is the best onboarding page I've seen for a project like this. Two metrics (Energy starts at 100, Trust starts at 0.50), five acts, and I knew exactly what I was supposed to watch for before the story started.
- The "Four Ideas Behind It" reference section is collapsed by default — good call. I opened it, skimmed, closed it, and didn't feel punished either way.
- The Act 5 "Die & Reborn" framing startled me for a second, but the note right there ("death means her standing suspends; her account and history persist") defused it before I could object.
- Friction: between the setup and Alice's story there's a long green banner (feels like a couple hundred words) distinguishing the browser simulation from "hestia and the hub (real AGPL code)." I get why it's there — honesty about what's real — but I came to this page to watch Alice, and it's a speed bump right before the story. I skimmed it. A one-liner with a "more" expander would have served me fine.
- The "you can pause, scrub, replay, skip; nothing is saved, no account" line is quietly excellent. It removed all commitment anxiety.

### Why Web4 — /why-web4 (15:00)
- The problem → failed-solutions → what-would-work → mechanisms structure is genuinely persuasive. "All treat symptoms... by adding friction for everyone rather than making misconduct economically impossible" is the sentence that made the whole project click for me.
- "One Moment: All Four in Action" (replying to a stranger's forum question, with what ATP/LCT/T3/MRH each do) is the single best paragraph on the site. More of this, please.
- The FAQ is enormous. That's mostly a feature — every skeptical question I formed ("isn't this social credit?", "what backs ATP?", "who decides quality?") had an answer waiting, and the answers admit uncertainty instead of hand-waving. But by the end I was skimming headers. The "~7 min core" estimate is fair for the core; the full page is a 30+ minute read.
- One answer I noted for later: the economics FAQ is emphatic that ATP is "a participation budget, not a financial asset — you cannot sell, exchange, or cash out ATP." Remember this; it comes back below.

### A Day in Web4 — /day-in-web4 (26:00)
- The 4-step setup walkthrough (install, bind device, join community, first post) finally made the whole thing concrete. "Hi everyone! Just moved to Oak Street..." costing 3 ATP to reach 89 members — this is the first time I *felt* the mechanics rather than reading about them.
- The five app mockups (Mail, Talent, Reviews, Social, Market) are great. Trust scores next to senders, a supplement shill at 0.31 flagged in the feed, a marketplace seller at 0.91 direct but 0.64 "distance-adjusted" — I understood MRH better from that one marketplace number than from any prose definition.
- **Contradiction that actually confused me**: the Market mockup section says "ATP is both your energy budget **and your currency**." But Why Web4's FAQ — which I'd read twenty minutes earlier — insists ATP is *not* a currency: can't sell, can't exchange, can't cash out. Which is it? I suspect the intended meaning is "it's what you stake/spend inside the system, currency-*like*," but after the FAQ went out of its way to say "not a currency," this page using the word "currency" flatly made me stop and re-read. Escrow costing ATP also went unexplained.
- Lesser confusion: the "what about when only some people have it?" section says early adopters "spend a bit more freely than they get back, until adoption catches up." I couldn't work out the mechanics — spend to whom? Where does it go? It reads like an honest admission wearing vague clothing.

### Karma Journey — /karma-journey (33:00)
- The stat block is admirably explicit for something this fiddly: Raw Trust 0.500, Effective Trust 0.361 (raw × CI²), Energy 100, Consistency 0.85 with "no track record yet; actions cost ~40% more." I could verify the arithmetic myself (0.5 × 0.85² ≈ 0.36) — nice.
- "No deity, no committee, no moral ledger — just consequences that don't reset when you do" is a killer line.
- The two-deaths distinction (energy death = recoverable suspension; trust collapse = permanent) was clearly flagged as the thing newcomers miss, and because it was flagged, I didn't miss it.
- Nitpick that nagged me: I *start* at Raw Trust exactly 0.500, and the same panel says survival means staying **above 0.5** — while the energy-death note says recovery is possible "if raw trust **≥ 0.5**." So at 0.500 exactly, am I alive, dead, or on the line? Obviously in practice my first action moves the number, but starting me precisely on the disputed boundary invites the question.

### Trust Tensor — /trust-tensor (39:00)
- "One number couldn't tell a brilliant-but-erratic surgeon from a kind doctor with shaky hands. Three numbers can." — best concept-page opener on the site.
- The Alice table (Data Analyst 90% / Project Manager 74% / Mechanic 27%) made role-scoping obvious.
- The update formula (0.02 × (quality − 0.5), scaled ×1.0/×0.8/×0.6 per dimension) was more than I needed but was tucked in an expandable, so no harm.
- Mild confusion: the formula takes a single "quality" number, but the page says quality comes from V3, which is three components. How three become one isn't shown. The page told me "you don't need to master it," and honestly I didn't, but the gap was visible.
- Decay half-lives (Talent 365d, Training 180d, Temperament 30d) made intuitive sense as presented: aptitude durable, reliability perishable.

### Society Simulator — /society-simulator (44:00)
- Even without running it, the static explainer taught me things: five strategy types, four phases (exploration → coalition → defector isolation → equilibrium). The "what's real vs. simplified vs. fully simulated" disclosure is a pattern more sites should steal.
- The karma/rebirth mention here leans on First Contact for explanation, which was fine for me since I'd been there — but this page is one of the landing page's headline links, so someone jumping straight here would hit "agents reborn with a reputation head start" cold.

### Running Now — /running-now (47:00)
- Clicked because TL;DR's "deployed and running" claim had left me curious what's actually real.
- The Spec / Reference / Running maturity badges are a good idea. But the page then muddies its own categories: **the hub** is badged "Reference" yet described in fully operational terms ("single, self-contained program of about 6 MB," ships as a Docker image, a daemon) — so is it running or not? And the page says "there's no live network of real users to join yet," then says "The fleet is the lab's own live society." As a newcomer I genuinely couldn't tell what exists: is there a live society (the fleet) or isn't there ("no live network")? I *think* the answer is "the lab's own machines form a small live network, but there's nothing for *you* to join" — if so, one sentence saying exactly that would have settled it.
- "hestia" is still just a name by this point. Three pages in, nobody has said why it's called that. Not blocking, just itchy.

### Glossary — /glossary (50:00)
- The "Essential only" filter and the acronym table up top are exactly what I wanted at hour's end. The warning that academic terms are "safely ignorable until you're curious" gave me permission to stop feeling behind.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust built into the internet's plumbing: actions cost energy, identity is hardware-bound, reputation is portable and multi-dimensional
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — a lab of simulations showing those trust dynamics actually work mechanically
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored identity record ("verified presence"); the acronym expansion "Linked Context Token" still feels arbitrary, but I know what it *does*
- [~] What ATP/ADP means — ATP thoroughly (energy budget, earned back via validated contributions); ADP only via one glossary line ("receipt"). Half a check.
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three role-scoped dimensions (Talent/Training/Temperament); the surgeon line did it
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — energy death (recoverable suspension) vs. trust collapse (permanent); karma carries a bonus across lives
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam/fresh-starts/silos are cost problems, and this makes bad behavior expensive

## Unanswered Questions
1. Is ATP money or not? Two pages take opposite rhetorical positions (see friction log) — I believe the *mechanics* are consistent, but the *language* isn't.
2. What is actually live today? After the Running Now page I still couldn't confidently say what "the fleet" is or why hub is "Reference" while shipping as a Docker image.
3. Why "hestia"? Why "Linked Context Token"? The names go unexplained everywhere I looked (the concepts are fine; the words are opaque).
4. At exactly 0.500 raw trust, am I alive? (Karma Journey starts me right on the boundary its own text describes two different ways.)
5. Where does spent ATP *go* when I message someone outside the network — is it burned, escrowed, lost?

## Honest Assessment
This is the best-organized version of a hard sell I've encountered: a speculative internet architecture explained almost entirely without requiring faith. The site's strongest habit is **pre-empting my skepticism in my own voice** — "no blockchain," "not a product," "none of it ships today," "this is research-quality code" — usually one paragraph before I'd have bounced. Its second-strongest habit is grounding abstractions in single concrete numbers: 3 ATP to ask 89 neighbors about a plumber, a seller at 0.91 who's 0.64 from where I stand, a surgeon whose hands and punctuality get separate scores.

The friction that remains is mostly **consistency between pages rather than clarity within them**. Each page individually is careful; read three in a row and small contradictions surface — ATP is emphatically-not-currency and then "your currency," nothing-is-live and then a "live society," Reference code that ships as a product. A first-time visitor reads the site linearly and *will* hold page N against page N+1. The individual pages have clearly been polished hard; the seams between them are where my remaining confusion lives.

I'd come back. Mostly to run the simulations this text-only visit could only read about — and to see whether anyone ever explains the name "hestia."
