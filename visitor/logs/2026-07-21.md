## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Strong, confident hook — "Trust, built into the internet." The subhead about AI agents ("write code, move money, make decisions" but "we cannot prove what an agent did, on whose authority, or by what rules") immediately told me the *problem*, which I appreciated. I knew within 10 seconds roughly what this site claims to be about.
- The phrase **"witnessed behavior"** appears in the hero. I didn't yet know what that meant mechanically, but it was intriguing rather than blocking.
- Clicked: "Web4 in 2 Minutes" — it felt like the safest, lowest-commitment entry.
- Confusion points: The "Core Concepts" section is a **wall of acronyms** — LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP/ADP, Coherence Index, Karma & Consequences, Identity Constellation. As a blank slate this was slightly intimidating to look at, even though I hadn't clicked any of them yet. Also four oddly-named "pieces" (The Core Standard, The Hub, **Hestia**, **Hardbound**) with no hint of what "Hestia" or "Hardbound" mean.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes — /tldr (02:00)
- First impression: This was the right first click. The **Web2 / Web3 / Web4** three-era contrast is genuinely digestible: platforms declare reputation (Web2) → key-holders assumed rightful (Web3) → trust earned from witnessed behavior (Web4). That framing made "witnessed behavior" click for me.
- The four pieces reappear here with one-liners (substrate / community / personal / enterprise + maturity like "runs end to end today"). Helpful, but still no explanation of *why* they're named Hestia / Hardbound / the hub.
- Clicked: "Why Web4?" — I wanted the problem spelled out more.

### Why Web4? — /why-web4 (04:30)
- First impression: "The Internet Has a Trust Problem." Excellent problem framing — spam is free, reputation is siloed, bad actors get fresh starts, AI impersonation, platforms hold all power. I nodded along to every one.
- The four mechanisms (ATP energy budget, LCT hardware identity, T3 trust tensor, MRH trust neighborhood) each got a plain-English gloss. Good.
- **BUT**: this page is an *enormous* FAQ. Dozens of Q&As across ~8 themes. As only my *second real content page*, it was overwhelming — I skimmed, I didn't read. It's great reference material but a firehose for a newcomer still forming a mental model. I couldn't tell what was essential vs. edge-case.
- Noticed: here LCT is expanded as **"Linked Context Token"** (singular).
- Clicked: "First Contact (10-min walkthrough)" — I wanted something guided, not a wall of Q&A.

### ATP Economics — /atp-economics (21:30)

No."** — stated plainly, with why (no market, no mining, can't buy/sell/speculate, 100 ATP starting grant). This is the clearest "it's not a cryptocurrency" I could ask for.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust earned from witnessed behavior, not declared by platforms or assumed from keys
- [x] What the onramp is (the core standard, the hub, hestia, hardbound) and how they fit together — shared primitives, three scales (personal/community/enterprise)
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound "digital driver's license" tied to your device and context
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget you spend to act and recharge via quality work; explicitly *not* money
- [ ] What Trust Tensors are — I saw T3 (Talent/Training/Temperament) and V3 named repeatedly and roughly grasp "multi-dimensional, per-role trust," but I never opened the /trust-tensor page, so this is inferred, not taught to me this session

## Unanswered Questions
1. What does the **"T" in LCT (Token)** actually contribute? The decode skipped it.
2. Why is it named **"Hardbound"** — I inferred "hardware-bound" but the site never confirmed it where I read it.
3. T3 has three components (Talent/Training/Temperament) — how are they actually *scored*? I saw the names but not the mechanism (I didn't visit /trust-tensor).
4. If ATP isn't money and "revenue comes from commissioned tasks where payment is distinct from recharge" — what *is* that payment, and how is it different from the ATP I spend? This distinction on /atp-economics was the one spot I re-read and still felt fuzzy.
5. Who are the "witnesses," concretely, in an everyday scenario — other people's devices? A community's servers? Both?

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-made explainer site. It's confident without being hypey, and the honesty on /running-now (Spec/Reference/Running badges, "nothing here is a fielded production system," concrete test counts) earned my trust more than any marketing could. Individual concept pages — especially **LCT** (driver's-license analogy) and **ATP** (biology cycle + net-ATP numbers) — are the best I've seen for translating dense ideas into plain English.

My friction wasn't with any single explanation; it was with **pacing and sequencing across the whole visit**. Two things stood out:

1. **Acronym density.** By page five I'd been introduced to a dozen acronyms. Each is explained where it's *taught*, but they're *named* everywhere, so the list surfaces (landing Core Concepts, onramp) feel like alphabet soup to someone who hasn't clicked in yet. A one-line plain-English tail on those lists would let me scan without committing.

2. **Delayed payoffs.** The single most delightful moment of my whole browse — learning that "Hestia" is the goddess of the hearth and the naming is a coherent hospitality metaphor — was hidden on the 9th page. I'd seen the word four times before that with no hook. The site has these lovely payoffs but makes me *travel* to get them. Sprinkle a few words of the payoff at first mention and the reader is rewarded for curiosity instead of made to hunt.

If I were a technically-curious person I'd absolutely return and go deeper (I still want /trust-tensor and /what-could-go-wrong). If I were a non-technical friend, the acronym wall on the landing page might bounce me before I reached the pages that would have won me over. The content is there; it's the on-ramp *to* the onramp that could be gentler.
