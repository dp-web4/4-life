## Journey

- First impression: A clean, problem-first landing. The headline "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" lands well — I know that problem. The site immediately tells me what it is *not* ("not a product, not a company, not a blockchain"), which preempts a misconception I was about to form. - Confusion points: "4-Life" itself — I don't yet know whether that's a name for the lab or for Web4 or for something else. "Society Simulator" and "Karma Journey" in the nav are intriguing but unmotivated until I read on.

### /tldr — Web4 in 2 Minutes (02:30)
- Decent. The Problem→Idea→Result structure is honest and quick. I get four mechanics: energy cost, hardware-anchored identity, portable reputation, agents-too.
- The four acronyms (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3) hit me at once. I'm OK with that on a *summary* page — it's literally there to inventory the jargon. But I'm not retaining the mappings yet.
- I clicked: "Why Web4?" — I want the case before I dig into mechanics.

### /lct-explainer (17:30)
- The opening one-liner ("digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip") is the best plain-English definition I encountered. Excellent.
- The Web2 → Web3 → Web4 comparison table grounded it.
- The interactive slider (devices vs. attack difficulty) showed numbers — "2 devices = 6.3x harder" — but it didn't explain *why* a second hardware chip multiplies attack cost. I bought it on faith.
- Witnesses got explained three times at increasing depth. The "co-signers on a legal document" framing landed; the four-step attestation flow landed less.
- I never quite figured out what I, as a user, *do* with an LCT. The page tells me what it is, not what daily life with one looks like.

### /atp-economics (23:00)
- I clicked here because First Contact kept mentioning ATP cost.
- **Problem:** The page calls it **"Attention Transfer Packets."** The Playground page (later) calls it **"Allocation Transfer Packets."** The Why Web4 page calls it **"Energy Budget (ATP)."** Three different expansions of the same acronym across the site. As a naive visitor this is destabilizing — I genuinely don't know which is the canonical name.
- The biological ATP analogy works, but the page slides between three metaphors (energy, attention, budget) without anchoring to one.
- The 5% burn fee is well explained — the worked example (send 100, pay 105, 5 burns) was concrete and the anti-circular-farming rationale made sense.
- The quality ramp (Hannah at 85% earns 7x what Sam at 35% earns) is the clearest single number on the page.

The page implies "recipients react" but I can't tell whether recharge happens the moment someone clicks helpful, an hour later, or after some quorum. This breaks my mental model of a basic action's lifecycle.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed internet-layer trust protocol with four primitives: hardware-anchored identity, action-energy budget, multi-dimensional reputation, and a trust horizon
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab/explainer site for the Web4 ontology
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token, a hardware-bound digital identity witnessed by your other devices
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget for actions, with ADP as a receipt that converts back to ATP when work is confirmed quality. (But the *timing* of conversion remains unclear.)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-axis (Talent/Training/Temperament) reputation, role-contextual
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive when ATP > 0, Trust > 0.5, CI coherent; dead when any condition fails (energy death recoverable, trust death permanent)
- [x] Why any of this matters — internet's "no memory" problem lets bad actors reset; Web4 makes consequences persistent without sacrificing privacy

## Unanswered Questions

1. **What is the canonical expansion of ATP?** Different pages say different things. This is the single biggest dissonance I encountered.
2. **What's the actual lifecycle of one ADP receipt?** Who triggers conversion to ATP, when, with what threshold of confirmations? I never figured this out.
3. **What does the Web4 user interface actually look like?** /a-day-in-web4 hints at this with mockups, but I left wondering whether there's a real prototype I could touch.
4. **How does CI score "grounding" exactly?** A grounding event seems to be a device check-in, but I can't tell from the page whether it's continuous, periodic, or event-driven.
5. **What is a synthon, in one sentence I'd remember?** I read about them but the concept didn't anchor.

## Honest Assessment

This is a thoughtful, well-paced educational site for a complex research topic. The plain-English-first ordering on Why Web4's four cards, the Tuesday/Tokyo/compromised CI examples, the Alice cross-role T3 demonstration, and /a-day-in-web4's concrete mockups are genuinely excellent pedagogical moves.

The one thing that genuinely shook my confidence as a naive visitor was the **ATP acronym inconsistency** across pages. It's not that I'm picky about names — it's that when the same three-letter string is given three different long-forms across the site, I start asking *"is this even one project, or am I reading multiple drafts stitched together?"* That's the kind of trust-puncturing detail that matters more than its surface complexity suggests, especially on a site explicitly *about* trust.

Below that headline issue, the second-tier friction is timing/mechanism ambiguity: the ADP→ATP conversion lacks a closed loop, "grounding" is used before defined, and the 0.5 threshold is justified by analogy rather than data. None of these are deal-breakers — they're places where a one-sentence patch would tighten the model significantly.

The skepticism-management on /what-could-go-wrong is impressive. It earns trust by leading with weaknesses. The interactive surfaces (Karma Journey, Playground, First Contact, Society Simulator) are the site's most differentiated asset — they let me *feel* concepts I'd otherwise skim. More cross-linking from explainer pages to relevant interactive demos would help (e.g., "now watch this in Playground experiment 1").

Overall: I left understanding more about Web4 than I expected to in 45 minutes, and curious enough to come back. That's the bar a research-explainer site has to clear, and this one clears it.
