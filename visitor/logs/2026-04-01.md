## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who decides the magic numbers?** Trust threshold 0.5, ATP transfer tax 5%, V3 weightings (0.30/0.35/0.35), trust decay rates — these are all consequential design decisions but I don't know if they're tunable per community, derived from theory, or chosen empirically.

2. **What happens to people with one cheap phone?** The hardware identity system inherently disadvantages people with fewer/cheaper devices. The site acknowledges this but the mitigation ("lower trust ceiling") means permanent second-class digital citizenship. How does this square with the equity goals?

3. **How does the bootstrap problem actually get solved?** Tier 1 (browser extension over existing platforms) sounds feasible, but the jump from Tier 1 to Tier 2+ (standalone apps) requires users to leave existing platforms. That's where every federated system has died.

4. **Is there a working demo I can actually try?** The simulators exist on the site but they're pre-scripted (Alice's journey) or abstract (society simulator with fictional agents). Can I create an identity and interact with something?

5. **What's the relationship between the consciousness research (SAGE, D5/D9) and the trust infrastructure?** The AI Agents section feels like a separate research track bolted onto the site.

6. **How does Web4 handle the "small town" problem?** In small communities, pseudonymity is thin — behavioral patterns identify people. The MRH + CI combination could enable social surveillance even without revealing real names.

7. **If trust is permanent and cross-platform, what about rehabilitation?** The site says "bad behavior persists" but also mentions trust decay. How long until a reformed actor can effectively participate? Is there a path to redemption beyond waiting for half-lives?

8. **Why "Web4" and not something more descriptive?** The name implies a successor to Web3 (blockchain), but the relationship is oppositional. This naming creates false associations.

## Honest Assessment

This is a remarkably well-constructed explainer site for a genuinely ambitious research project. The progressive disclosure mostly works — starting from TL;DR and working through First Contact, the concepts build on each other. The "What Could Go Wrong" page is the single best thing on the site because it demonstrates the intellectual honesty that a trust-based system should embody.

**What works well:**
- Problem framing is relatable and immediate (spam, fake reviews, platform lock-in)
- Alice's journey in First Contact is the best teaching tool on the site
- Day in Web4 mockups make abstract concepts tangible
- Honest risk assessment builds credibility
- The glossary and learning journey provide structure for deeper exploration

**What doesn't work as well:**
- The site tries to serve too many audiences simultaneously. A curious non-technical visitor, a developer evaluating the protocol, and an AI researcher studying consciousness thresholds are all visiting the same pages. The LCT page is the worst offender — it goes from "your phone has a security chip" to RDF triples in the same scroll.
- Jargon accumulation is steep. By page 3, I'm tracking: ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, MRH, SAL, R6, D5, D9, FIDO2, TPM, RDF, SPARQL. That's 15 acronyms. Some are essential; some could be deferred.
- The AI Agents section feels like it was written by a different team for a different audience. The consciousness research is interesting but its connection to "fixing the internet's trust problem" is unclear.
- The "magic numbers" problem is pervasive. Many critical parameters (0.5 threshold, 5% tax, 0.7 decay) appear as givens without sufficient motivation. For a research project, "we chose this based on X" would be more credible than presenting them as natural constants.

**Bottom line:** I came in not knowing what Web4 was. I left understanding the core pitch (energy costs + hardware identity + multi-dimensional trust = self-regulating internet). I believe the problem is real. I'm less sure the solution is feasible at scale, but the honest acknowledgment of that uncertainty is itself persuasive. I'd come back to try the simulators more carefully and watch how the project evolves.

The site's biggest competitor isn't another project — it's visitor attention span. The path from "what is this?" to "I get it" takes about 20 minutes of focused reading. That's a lot to ask. The Day in Web4 page should probably be in the top-level navigation more prominently — it's the fastest path to "oh, I see why this matters."
