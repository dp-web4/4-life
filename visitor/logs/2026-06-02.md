### Landing Page (00:00)

- **First impression**: Clean, organized, calm. It reads like a research lab, not a sales pitch — I appreciated that. The headline "A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations" told me roughly what I was looking at.
- **What worked**: The parenthetical right under the headline — "(Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor)" — preemptively answered my first two questions ("is this crypto?" / "is this Web3?"). Good instinct to put that there.
- **What worked**: "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" section. Spam, silo'd reputation, free fresh starts, AI deception. I felt all four of those personally. That's a strong hook.
- **Clicked**: The clear primary CTA, "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" (labeled "Recommended for first-time visitors"). Exactly what a confused newcomer needs — one obvious door.
- **Confusion points**: The "Core Concepts" menu immediately throws five acronym-laden links at me (LCT, T3, ATP, MRH, Aliveness) before I know what any of them are. I knew to ignore them and take the TL;DR, but a more impatient visitor might bounce off the wall of jargon in the nav.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (03:00)
- The page promises "Plain English first" — and mostly delivers on the *problem* ("the internet has no memory… a scammer banned from one platform signs up on another").
- **But the promise breaks on the solution.** Within the "idea" section I was hit with ATP (Allocation Transfer Packets), LCT, T3, MRH, and CI — five acronyms in a "2-minute" explainer. That's the opposite of plain English. I understood the *shapes* (energy costs money, identity is hardware-bound, reputation is portable) but the labels piled up faster than I could absorb them.
- **Naive reaction to "ATP"**: I vaguely remember ATP from high-school biology = energy. Fine, the energy metaphor lands. But "Allocation Transfer Packets" feels like a backronym someone reverse-engineered to keep the biology letters. It didn't help me; it made me wonder if the name came first and the meaning second.
- **Next step taken**: "First Contact (7-min interactive following Alice)" — a story about a real person sounded like the antidote to acronym soup.

### First Contact (08:00)
- This is a *teaser* page for an interactive simulation, not the simulation itself. It describes Alice's five acts (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) and a "reference map" of the four core ideas.
- **Honest friction**: As someone just reading the page (I didn't launch the embedded walkthrough), I got marketing copy about the experience rather than the experience. "Each one is explained in the story the moment Alice runs into it" is a *promise* of teaching, not teaching. I want to believe it, but the static page didn't actually teach me anything new — it described a lesson I'd have to click into.
- **What I liked**: Five acts is a clear arc, and "Die & Reborn" made me curious — death in a website is an arresting idea.

### Why Web4? (13:00)
- **This was the strongest page.** The four-part problem diagnosis ("Actions have no cost. Bad actors can operate indefinitely at zero expense.") was sharp and felt true.
- **Unusually honest.** The FAQ took on the exact objections I was forming: "Isn't this just a social credit system?", "What if someone powerful lies about me?", "Can't someone buy 50 devices?", "Do youthful mistakes follow you forever?" It even admits "This is research, not production," gives no shipping timeline, and says "whether the economics survive real human behavior is an open question." That candor made me trust the site *more*, not less.
- **Friction — why-before-what gap**: The problem section nails *why*. But the solution section reintroduces ATP/LCT/T3/MRH as labels without drawing the line from each problem to its fix. The mapping I wanted spelled out was: "actions have no cost → energy budget (ATP)"; "identity is cheap → hardware identity (LCT)." The page implies these links but never states them plainly, so the solution still felt like a leap of faith.
- **Friction**: "trust-native" is used before it's defined, and "society" / "SAL rules" get name-dropped casually.

### A Day in Web4 (18:00)
- **This page did the most to make Web4 feel real.** Ten concrete scenarios across one day: a phone that recognizes you via its security chip, an inbox where spam *costs the sender* energy, a hiring platform with verified trust scores, restaurant reviews only from people with real history.
- The five clickable wireframe mockups (mail, talent, reviews, social feed, marketplace) are the first time the abstractions snapped into a picture I could see myself using. "Posting costs 3–15 ATP depending on your trust level" finally made ATP feel tangible.
- **Still abstract**: The "what if only 1% of people use this?" mixed-adoption reality. The scenarios assume everyone's already on Web4, which is the easy case.
- **Suggestion to myself**: I almost wish *this* page were the first stop after the TL;DR, before any acronym explainer. Seeing the thing before learning its parts would have made the acronyms stick better.

### LCT Explainer (22:00)
- "A digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip — hardware-bound, witnessed by your other devices." The phone-as-unforgeable-ID-card analogy landed immediately. Good.
- "Your reputation follows you, but your real name doesn't have to" — this reassured me about the privacy fear the social-credit objection had raised.
- **Friction**: TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2, "cryptographic attestation," "manufacturer-burned key" show up early. There's a "For non-crypto readers" collapsible, but I have to *click* to get the plain version — the default text assumes I'll tolerate the crypto terms.
- **Still confusing**: What "recovery" looks like if I lose my phone (this is the scary practical question for hardware-bound identity, and it stayed fuzzy). Also: the page warns that "witness" means two different things (device witnesses vs. infrastructure witness nodes) and then the distinction stays blurry — flagging a confusion isn't the same as resolving it.

### ATP Economics (26:00)
- **Best concept page of the session.** It explicitly teaches the biology analogy ("cells spend ATP, get ADP back, recharge from food") instead of assuming it, and backs it with real numbers: a 50-ATP task returns ~6 ATP at 35% quality but ~42 ATP at 85% quality — a 7× difference. That single example taught me more than three paragraphs of prose.
- "ATP is not cryptocurrency (no trading, mining, or price)" — clears up the inevitable crypto suspicion head-on.
- "Run out of energy budget? You die." — blunt and memorable.
- **Friction**: The page openly says the exact math of how "confirmer trust" and "received value fraction" combine is "still settling," and recharge timing is "hours to a day." I respect the honesty, but it left me unsure how the core loop actually closes.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (29:00)
- I braced for math because "tensor" sounds intimidating, but the page disarmed it well: "You don't need the math — one number couldn't tell a brilliant-but-erratic surgeon from a kind doctor with shaky hands."
- The three dimensions (Talent, Training, Temperament) are intuitive, and the Alice-as-analyst-vs-mechanic example made role-specificity click ("85% Talent as a data analyst but 20% as a mechanic").
- **Friction**: V3 (value tensor) gets introduced mid-page and feels like scope creep even though the page says I don't need it. And the decay half-lives (Talent 365 days, Temperament 30 days) drop specific numbers with exponential math but no intuition for *why* those numbers — why does temperament fade 12× faster than talent?

### Aliveness (31:00)
- The "professional license" analogy is excellent: you need resources to operate (ATP), a track record people trust (T3), and consistent behavior (CI) — "lose any one and you can't participate, like a doctor who loses their license." That tied the three core concepts together better than any other page.
- **Emotional reaction**: Two kinds of death — "Energy Death" (recoverable, like bankruptcy) and "Trust Death" (permanent, like losing credentials). The *permanent* one made me flinch. The social-credit fear from the why-web4 FAQ came rushing back: a permanent death from a trust score is exactly the dystopia people worry about, and seeing it stated flatly here, away from the reassuring FAQ context, felt heavier than intended.
- **Friction — scary math**: Formulas appear — `effective_trust = base_trust × CI²` and `atp_cost = normal × 1/CI²`. For a page about an intuitive idea (staying "alive"), the squared-CI formulas are a wall. I trusted that it means "inconsistency hurts you twice over," but I couldn't tell you *why squared*.

### Glossary (33:00)

When I'm mid-page elsewhere and hit "MRH," I want to type M and jump to it. Instead I have to know which *category* MRH lives in. A glossary's #1 job is emergency lookup, and the structure optimizes for browsing instead.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **What actually happens if I lose my phone?** Hardware-bound identity sounds great until the hardware is gone. Recovery stayed vague on the LCT page and it's the question that would decide whether I'd ever use this.
2. **What is MRH, really?** I saw "trust neighborhood" and "3 hops max" repeatedly but never the actual explanation. The intimidating full name (Markov Relevancy Horizon) appeared without ever being unpacked on the pages I visited.
3. **Is permanent "Trust Death" appealable?** The why-web4 FAQ said mistakes decay and it's not social credit, but the aliveness page said trust death is permanent. Which is it? These two pages seem to disagree.
4. **Who decides "quality"?** ATP recharge depends on others confirming your work's value. In a brand-new community with no trusted peers, how does anyone earn their first ATP back? (The ATP page admitted this is unsettled.)
5. **Why those specific numbers?** 0.5 trust threshold, CI squared, 30 vs 365 day half-lives — they're presented as settled-ish but never motivated.

## Honest Assessment

This site is **honest, well-structured, and genuinely trying to teach** — and that earned my trust faster than slick marketing would have. The why-web4 FAQ openly answering "isn't this social credit?" and admitting "this is research, not production" is the kind of candor I almost never see, and it's the main reason I'd come back.

But the experience has one dominant friction, and it's the same one the whole site is built to avoid: **acronym overload arrives too early**. The TL;DR — the page most responsible for not scaring people off — front-loads five acronyms in its solution section. I survived it because I'm patient and curious. A normal person would feel talked-down-to and bounce.

The cure is already on the site: **"A Day in Web4"** and the **ATP Economics** worked-numbers were where the abstractions finally became real. The site teaches best when it shows a concrete scene or a single number *before* naming the concept. It teaches worst when it leads with the label. If the default path were "feel the problem (why-web4) → see it in use (day-in-web4) → *then* learn the named parts," the acronyms would have somewhere to stick.

My emotional arc: hooked by the problem, reassured by the honesty, briefly alarmed by "permanent trust death," and left curious but not converted. I understand what Web4 is. I don't yet understand what happens to *me* when my phone breaks or when someone powerful decides I'm untrustworthy — and until a site like this answers that calmly and concretely, the dystopia-fear sits right next to the optimism. The site knows this (the FAQ proves it), but the fear and the reassurance live on different pages, and a real visitor feels the fear before they find the answer.

**One-line verdict**: The most honest explainer of a hard idea I've read in a while — held back by leading with its vocabulary instead of its pictures.
