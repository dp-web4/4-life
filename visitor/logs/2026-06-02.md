## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean and confident. The headline "4-Life | Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the one-line framing — "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default" — told me roughly what I was looking at without jargon.
- The problem statement "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" with concrete bullets (spam is free, reputations are siloed, bad actors reset, AI deception) landed immediately. I recognized my own daily frustrations.
- I appreciated being told upfront this is "not a product, company, or blockchain" but "an active open-source research project... currently non-functional on the real internet." That set expectations honestly.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" (it's explicitly flagged as the first-timer path, so I trusted it).
- Confusion points: Almost none. Four "rules" (device identity, energy cost, permanent consequences, equal rules for AI/humans) are stated before I know what any of them mean, but they read as a teaser, not a wall.

### TL;DR — "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (03:00)
- This was the best page for me. The promise "Plain English first... every term is spelled out the first time it appears" actually held up.
- Four headings (The Problem / The Idea / The Result / Where is this now?) gave me a clean mental scaffold.
- The line "Web4 is the protocol — the rules and math; 4-Life (this site) is the lab where you explore those rules" finally resolved the two names for me. I'd been mildly unsure whether "Web4" and "4-Life" were the same thing.
- Every acronym (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI) was expanded inline. I didn't feel behind.
- Clicked: "First Contact."

### First Contact — Alice walkthrough (07:00)
- A 5-act interactive story following "Alice" with two live numbers: Energy Budget (ATP, starts 100) and Trust Score (0.00–1.00, starts 0.50). The play/pause/scrub framing is engaging.
- The arc (Building → Stumbling → Recovering → Death/Rebirth with karma) is a strong way to teach consequences.
- Confusion point: the real-vs-simulated tension is still palpable here. "Real rules, shown by simulation — not a demo of a shipped product" is a good line, but the present-tense storytelling ("Alice posts...", her trust climbs in real time) pulls me toward thinking I'm watching a live system, then the caveat pulls me back. It's a mild but recurring dissonance, not a blocker.

### Why Web4? (14:00)
- The opening — "You already know this. You feel it every day." — is the most emotionally effective writing on the site. The four problems each with a "Root cause" are excellent.
- BUT this page is enormous. The visible portion alone felt like 10,000+ words, and the FAQ has 80+ collapsed questions across topic groups. As a naive visitor I bounced off it — I skimmed rather than read, which I think is intended, but it's intimidating rather than reassuring.
- Jargon escalates sharply in the FAQ. "VCM" appeared with no expansion, and "FIDO2" assumes I know what that is. After the clean TL;DR, this felt like a different, harder site.
- "Why it's called Web4 (not Web3)" arrives late — if I'd never heard of Web1/2/3 I'd be a little lost early.

### Karma Journey (24:00)
- Interactive: starting stats Trust (Raw) 0.500, Energy 100, Consistency 0.85, with eight action buttons for Turn 1. Fun and clear.
- I liked the honesty: "The word here is a handle, not a moral score" — it heads off the religious/cosmic reading of "karma."
- The formula "Effective trust = raw × CI²" is shown and the squaring is explained as penalizing inconsistency.
- **Genuine confusion**: the page tracks TWO trust numbers — raw trust (drop below 0.5 and the life ends) and effective trust (raw × CI², which sets your karma tier at death). As a newcomer I couldn't answer: *if survival is decided by raw trust, why should I care about effective trust during this life?* The answer is presumably "it seeds your next life," but the page never connects "effective trust → karma tier → next life's starting conditions" as one explicit sentence at the point where both numbers are on screen. I had to infer it.

### LCT Explainer (30:00)
- Strong page. The "your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied or faked" metaphor nailed the concept before any tech.
- "A digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip — hardware-bound, witnessed by your other devices" is a keepable one-liner.
- The 0–1 scale is well anchored ("0.5 is the alive/dead line") and the ceiling grid (TPM 0.90 / Secure Enclave 0.85 / FIDO2 0.75 / Software 0.50) is clear. Device vs. network witnesses explicitly distinguished — good.
- Minor friction: the single-device recovery section ("I just installed the app on my one phone") took me a couple reads, and "community vouching" stayed abstract — I wanted a concrete "Alice texts Bob, Bob co-signs" example.

### Trust Tensor (36:00)
- T3 (Talent / Training / Temperament, always role-specific) is explained well, and the simulator (pick Surgeon/Analyst/Leader, apply a scenario, watch trust change by role) is the best teaching tool on the page.
- The "what does 0.7 mean" anchor ("0.5 = newcomer, 0.7 = ~70% cooperative, 0.9 = consistently exceptional") is helpful.
- Friction: a "[Deeper: how calibration is measured ↓]" link points to a "#calibration-meaning" section, but I couldn't find an actual explanation there — the link felt like it led nowhere. V3 is mentioned ("T3 = who you are; V3 = what you produce") but deferred, so the T3↔V3 feedback loop ("high-V3 work raises your T3") is asserted without being shown.

### ATP Economics (41:00)
- Well-structured with multiple entry points (one-liner, 60-second version, full breakdown, biology origin, crypto comparison, simulator).
- "ATP is your energy budget — a finite per-agent allowance, not money you accumulate" is clear and consistent with the rest of the site.
- "Think of ATP as cash and ADP as the receipt you get when you spend it" is a great keepable line.
- **Friction**: ADP's role felt contradictory. It's introduced as a "receipt" (a record), but later the page says ADP "converts back into fresh ATP." A receipt that turns back into spendable energy isn't a receipt — so I couldn't tell whether ADP is a *record* or an *asset*. That muddied an otherwise crisp section.
- "Does ADP expire?" is answered ("unconfirmed slices decay over a window of weeks") but it's buried in the math and stated once without emphasis.

### Aliveness (45:00)
- The professional-license framing is good. Three survival criteria (ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, CI coherent) are stated cleanly.
- The two death paths are the clearest part: Energy Death (recoverable, "like recovering from bankruptcy") vs. Trust Death (permanent, "like losing a professional license"). This finally made the stakes concrete.
- "The 0.5 threshold is calibrated, not derived... we picked the midpoint" — I appreciated this honesty.
- Friction: "synthon" (a group that itself becomes a living entity) is introduced very late, under an "advanced" fold. I almost missed it, and there's no threshold for when a synthon "emerges" — just "trust becomes dense and mutual," which is vague.

### Society Simulator (47:00)
- Big interactive payoff: "watch 12 agents... form alliances, betray each other, and self-organize" across phases (Exploration → Coalition → Defector Isolation → Equilibrium). Run button, scenario picker, speed control.
- Good onboarding nod: "Never seen these Web4 terms before?" pointing back to First Contact, plus an expandable acronym key.
- Friction: the page calls MRH "math-flavored jargon you can ignore." Telling me to *ignore* a term it just used felt like it slightly undercut its own credibility — I'd rather have a one-line "here's what it means and why it's safe to skim" than "ignore this."

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-made educational site, and the recommended path (TL;DR → First Contact → concept pages) works. The TL;DR is the standout — I'd tell a friend to read just that first. The interactive widgets (Karma Journey, Trust Tensor simulator, Society Simulator) make abstract mechanics tangible, and the site is consistently honest about what's proven vs. speculative, which earns trust.

My understanding landed at "good" rather than "solid" because of a handful of specific conceptual snags, all clustered around the system's *second-order* mechanics — the moments where two numbers interact (raw vs. effective trust) or one thing plays two roles (ADP as receipt vs. refill). The first-order concepts (identity is your device, actions cost energy, trust is earned and follows you) all clicked cleanly. It's the relationships *between* concepts that left small gaps.

The Why Web4 page is the one place where the site's "explain everything" instinct works against a naive reader — it's a reference document wearing a landing-page costume, and I bounced. A clearer "skim this, skip that" signpost at the top would help first-timers like me.

None of my friction was a dead end. I left understanding the core idea, mildly curious to explore the simulators more, and trusting that the people behind this are being straight with me. That last part matters more than any single explanation.
