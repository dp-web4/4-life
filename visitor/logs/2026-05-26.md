## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
**First impression:** Clean, confident, well-organized. The headline "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" works as a hook — concrete, not abstract. I immediately noticed and appreciated the parenthetical "no blockchain, no tokens, no speculation" — they're preempting my suspicion that this is another crypto pitch.

**The "5 Core Ideas" plain-English list is good but raised friction:**
- "Identity — your hardware-bound digital ID" → bound to *what* hardware? My phone? Old laptop?
- "Trust — your three-dimensional trust score" → three what dimensions?
- "Neighborhood — who can reach you (3 hops max)" → why 3 specifically?
- "Consistency — does your behavior add up?" → measured how?

I appreciated that the page openly says "this is a proposed framework" rather than pretending to be a shipped product. That honesty made me more willing to keep reading.

**Clicked:** "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" because the page explicitly labels it as recommended.

### TL;DR (02:30)
Dense. In ~2 minutes of reading I'm hit with: ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI — six acronyms. The 4 plain-English framings (Energy-Based Actions, Hardware-Anchored Identity, Portable Reputation, Universal Rules) are good. But the additional "T3, MRH, CI" stub paragraph at the end felt like a deferred-payment trick — "we'll explain these later."

**Quote that caught me:** *"Web4 is the protocol — the rules and math; 4-Life (this site) is the lab where you explore those rules."* Good positioning.

**The "permanent" reputation claim** triggered immediate suspicion. Permanent sounds either revolutionary OR terrifying — what if I make a mistake at 22? Is that with me at 60?

**Clicked:** First Contact, because watching Alice through 16 concrete actions sounded more digestible than more theory.

### First Contact (06:00)
Described as a 5-act interactive simulation (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn). I see Alice's ATP (starts 100) and Trust (starts 0.50). Behavioral Consistency listed as a core concept.

**Friction:** "Die & Reborn" with "karma carries forward" immediately raised the question my brain has been holding: if Alice **dies** but karma persists, is reputation tied to her *person* or her *LCT*? The TL;DR said reputation is "permanent" but death-rebirth muddies that. I had to leave this question hanging and hope a later page resolved it. (It did — see Aliveness.)

### Why Web4 (10:00)
Long page — feels like a manifesto + 67-question FAQ. I skimmed.

The **4 concrete problems** land well: spam (free accounts), reputation silos (your Reddit karma doesn't transfer), bad-actor fresh starts (banned → new account), AI deception. These are visceral, real, things I already feel.

I really liked phrases like *"Honest caveat,"* *"This is an open question,"* and acknowledging *"appeals processes are designed but not yet deployed."* That kind of self-awareness builds trust.

**Friction:** 67 FAQ questions across 7 categories is intimidating — useful as reference but I wouldn't read this cold. The categorical grouping helps; if I had a specific worry, I could find it. But discovery surface is large.

### LCT Explainer (16:00)
**This page was the best so far.** Clear plain-English definition upfront: *"a digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip — not a password you type and not a key file you store, but a presence your hardware proves."*

The hardware tiers + trust ceilings (TPM=0.90, SE=0.85, FIDO2=0.75, Software=0.50) are concrete numbers. Single-device support explicitly addressed: *"Only have one device? You're still in"* but with a lower ceiling.

**What happens if I lose everything?** Answered: social-recovery path, 3–7 days. Not erased.

**Trust scale anchored**: 0–1.0, with **0.5 = alive/dead line**. That answered a question I'd been holding since the landing page (where do the decimal trust numbers I keep seeing come from?).

**Lingering friction (LOW):** The page describes recovery vouching ("trusted witnesses confirm you're you") but the *mechanism* — what does a stranger actually do? how does their attestation become binding? what threshold of vouches is needed? — is left vague. Conceptually OK; operationally unclear.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (22:00)
**T3 = Talent + Training + Temperament**, scored *per role*. The Alice example landed beautifully:
- As data analyst: 85/90/95 = 90% overall
- As project manager: 65/70/91 = 76% overall
- As mechanic: 20/15/50 = 27% overall

This single example resolved a confusion I'd been carrying — "ban evasion" isn't about losing one global score; it's role-contextualized. Alice can be expert-coder-but-novice-mechanic, and that's fine.

The math (0.02 × (quality − 0.5), so ~+0.007 per good attestation) means climbing from 0.5 to 0.9 takes ~50 quality actions. Slow build, can't shortcut. Makes sense as anti-gaming, though the page didn't explicitly call out *why* it's slow.

**Friction (LOW-MED):** V3 is mentioned ("T3 measures who you are; V3 measures what you produce") but deferred. This is the second time V3 was punted — once on TL;DR, once here. There's no obvious "V3 explainer" page in the nav. V3 feels like a **ghost concept** — referenced but never grounded.

### ATP Economics (28:00)
**ATP = Allocation Transfer Packets**. The "60-second version" box upfront is the right move.

The biological analogy (adenosine triphosphate, the cell energy molecule) is acknowledged — good, because someone with a science background will immediately wonder if the acronym is borrowed. The page treats it as inspiration, not literal physics.

**ADP = Allocation Discharge Packet** = receipt. Cash/receipt metaphor: clean.

The 5% transfer fee for sending ATP between agents is explained as anti-collusion: you pay 105 to send 100, the 5 goes to community redistribution. Makes sense.

**Friction (LOW):** Naming nit — ATP = "Allocation **Transfer** Packets" but the page repeatedly says ATP is "not money you accumulate" and that transfer is rare (and penalized). If transfer is the *uncommon* operation, why is "Transfer" the centerpiece of the acronym expansion? "Allocation Token Packets" or "Action Throughput Packets" would feel less mismatched. Not a blocker, but it's a tiny cognitive snag.

### MRH Explorer (32:00)
**MRH = Markov Relevancy Horizon.** The "Trust Neighborhood" framing is the right teach-first frame. The "Markov" formal name is correctly relegated.

**Friction (LOW):** "Markov" is jargon. A naive reader who doesn't know Markov chains (probability theory) is going to bounce off that word. The page handles it by leading with "neighborhood" and treating "Markov Relevancy Horizon" as the formal name in an aside — good. But if someone Googles MRH first, they hit "Markov" first.

**3 hops is empirically calibrated, not derived.** Good honesty. Trust decay 1-hop=0.85, 2-hop=0.65, 4-hop=noise feels intuitive (friend-of-friend you sort of trust; friend-of-friend-of-friend-of-friend you don't).

### Coherence Index / CI (36:00)
**The credit-check analogy clicks immediately:** *"asks not just 'who are you?' but 'does your behavior make physical sense?'"*

**Four dimensions concrete and well-named:**
- Spatial (impossible travel)
- Capability (hardware plausibility)
- Temporal (activity continuity)
- Relational (relationship history)

**Maria's stolen phone scenario** (CI drops to 0.55 because temporal continuity broken + relational patterns don't match, even though physical device is in the same city) is exactly the kind of concrete grounding I want. This is the strongest example on the site so far.

The H1 ("Coherence Index: Behavioral Consistency") anchors well — the subtitle makes the technical name reachable.

### Aliveness (40:00)
**Finally, the death question got resolved.** Two kinds of death, cleanly distinguished:

- **Energy death** (ATP=0): recoverable. *"Like recovering from bankruptcy."*
- **Trust death** (T3<0.5): permanent. *"Like losing a professional license."*

The doctor-license analogy holds: energy = overhead, trust = client relationships, coherence = clean practice record. All three must hold.

**Karma carries forward starting conditions**: die with 145 ATP and T3=0.72, rebirth keeps that. So a new identity isn't a fresh start — *that's* the anti-spam mechanism.

This resolved the lingering question from First Contact perfectly. The page is honest enough that the contradiction I felt earlier ("permanent reputation" vs "rebirth") was real and acknowledged — they're two different things (energy can reset, trust can't).

### A Day in Web4 (42:00)
Present-tense thought experiment, **openly labeled**: *"Everything below is narrated in the present tense as a thought experiment — none of it ships today."* Good — the present tense is a deliberate device, not deception.

Ten scenarios across a day. Mockups feel concrete (specific ATP costs, trust scores 0–1). The page reminds you it's research.

**Friction (LOW):** Dense terminology even with the safety net. If you arrived here first (e.g., from a Google search for "trust simulation"), the "go take First Contact first" suggestion is right but feels like an offramp from the thing you came to see.

## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed trust-native internet protocol with hardware-anchored identity, role-contextualized trust scores, an energy budget that recharges via community validation, and a notion of "neighborhood" that bounds whose signals reach you.
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — the live lab for poking at the Web4 model: simulations, day-in-the-life scenarios, interactive concept explorers.
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored identity, multi-device witnessed, with social recovery if you lose everything.
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget (ATP) + spent-receipt (ADP); recharges through community validation of your work.
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — 3D vector (Talent, Training, Temperament) scored per role, not globally.
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — energy death (ATP=0) is recoverable, trust death (T3<0.5) is permanent; karma carries forward starting conditions.
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam, reputation silos, ban-evasion, AI deception are real problems the current internet doesn't solve, and the proposed mechanism addresses them at the protocol level (not by hiring more moderators).

## Unanswered Questions

1. **What is V3, concretely?** Referenced on TL;DR and on the T3 page as "what you produce," but never grounded with an example or explained in its own right. Is V3 a separate score I have? Is it attached to artifacts I create? Does it factor into ATP recharge?

2. **What does the actual UI look like for everyday actions?** Day-in-Web4 narrates "you spend 5 ATP to post a comment" — but what does *spending 5 ATP* look like as an interaction? Click a button? See a confirmation dialog? Watch a number tick down? The site is rich on the model and the math; thinner on the felt experience.

3. **How does community vouching for recovery actually become binding on my LCT?** The LCT page describes the social-recovery path conceptually but the operational mechanism (who signs what, in what order, with what threshold) is opaque.

4. **What is the relationship between T3 trust and CI coherence, mechanically?** Both are in [0, 1]. Aliveness mentions "all three must hold simultaneously" (energy, trust, coherence). But does CI directly modify T3? Does CI gate ATP spend? Or are they independent signals that the rest of the system multiplies together?

## Honest Assessment

This site is in really good shape. After ~45 minutes of browsing, I came away with a solid mental model of Web4 — not just buzzwords, but actual relationships between the concepts: how ATP recharges through validation, how T3 is role-contextualized, why CI matters as an anomaly-detection layer, why "death" comes in two flavors, what an LCT actually proves.

**What the site does well:**
- Plain-English first, acronyms second (mostly).
- Honest about status — "designed but not yet deployed," "research not product," "open question."
- Concrete examples that ground abstract concepts (Alice across roles, Maria's stolen phone).
- Specific numbers (trust ceilings per hardware tier, ATP recharge math, hop decay rates).
- Multiple entry points (2-min TL;DR, 7-min Alice, 15-min society sim).
- Two-minute alive/dead anchor (0.5 line) makes all the decimal trust numbers across pages legible.

**What still trips:**
- V3 is a ghost — referenced but never explained. Either ground it or stop mentioning it cold.
- "Markov" in MRH is unexplained jargon (small fix: one inline gloss).
- "Transfer" in ATP's acronym oversells the rare operation (small fix: clarifier).
- Operational details of social recovery / vouching are conceptually clear but mechanically vague.

**Biggest insight as a naive visitor:** The two-tier death model (energy death recoverable, trust death permanent) is the single most clarifying piece of the whole framework. It resolves the apparent tension between "permanent reputation" (which sounds scary) and "rebirth" (which sounds like a free escape hatch). Once you see that energy can reset but trust can't, the anti-spam mechanism becomes obvious without being authoritarian. Lead with this more.

**On the question "is this real?":** The site handles this well. By the time I got to A Day in Web4, the present-tense framing felt earned because earlier pages had explicitly said "designed but not deployed," "Web4 is the protocol, 4-Life is the lab," and "none of this ships today." The honesty compounds across pages.

Overall: yes, I would return. Yes, I would recommend it — especially to someone curious about what "trust on the internet" could mean beyond "more KYC" or "another crypto wallet." It's one of the most thoughtful technical explainers I've encountered.
