## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean and confident. "Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations." The one-line definition ("a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default") actually told me what this *is* before I had to dig. Good.
- The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" lead resonated immediately — spam, siloed reputation, unlimited fresh starts, AI deception. I felt the problem before any jargon.
- One clearly recommended door stood out: **"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR."** I didn't feel torn between options this time — there's an obvious primary path and everything else is visibly secondary. That's a relief; a lot of sites throw five equal buttons at you.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR"
- Confusion points: A cluster of acronyms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI) is visible in the "five fundamental ideas" area, but they're framed as ideas (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) so it didn't sting.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (03:00)
- The problem framing ("the internet has no memory; bad actors reset by making new accounts") is crisp and human.
- The four core ideas use friendly metaphors: energy budget, hardware-bound ID card, multi-dimensional trust, "humans and AI follow identical rules." This landed.
- Confusion: "Witness constellation" and "Markov Relevancy Horizon" show up as supporting concepts and add weight without much grounding — but the page is honest that I don't need to memorize them yet.
- Clear next steps offered. Clicked: **First Contact** (the "7 min interactive").

### First Contact — Alice (07:00)
- Following Alice through 5 acts watching two numbers (🔋 ATP energy, 🤝 Trust 0.00–1.00 starting at 0.50) is a genuinely good teaching device. Watching her build → spam → backfire → recover → "die" (suspended, not deleted) → rebound made the abstract concrete.
- The death framing was handled gently — "standing suspended, not deleted" — and I appreciated that distinction being made explicitly.
- Mild friction: four acronyms (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) are introduced up front with a "you don't need to memorize these" note... while listing them anyway. Minor. The story carried me past it.

### Karma Journey (12:00)
- This is the meatiest page and the most rewarding once I got through it. The rebirth/karma framing (consequences persist across "lives" without moral judgment) is interesting.
- **The two-trust-numbers distinction is now stated clearly and EARLY** — "Raw trust keeps you alive now (your plain reputation). Effective trust — raw dampened by how consistent you've been — is what you inherit next life." That sentence did a lot of work and I'm glad it leads rather than hides at the bottom. The color-coded "two numbers" framing helped me hold both in my head.
- The death section ("Both crossings involve 0.5...") cleanly separates **Energy death** (ATP→0, raw trust ≥0.5, recoverable, suspension) from **Trust collapse** (raw <0.5, permanent). This was clear. I could tell recoverable from permanent without re-reading. That used to be the kind of thing I'd expect to trip on, and it didn't.
- **Where I DID stumble**: the **"What the numbers mean"** section. The conceptual distinction is fine, but the math clusters densely — "effective trust = raw × CI²", "CI starts at 0.85", "1/0.85² ≈ 1.38 — a ~40% surcharge." I had to re-read the CI² / surcharge arithmetic to parse what it was actually saying. The *idea* (new identities are dampened until they prove consistency) is intuitive; the *arithmetic* presented inline interrupted the flow. I understood it eventually, but it's the one spot on the page that felt like homework rather than reading.

### Why Web4? (17:00)
- Strong problem-first structure: five concrete problems each with a plain-language "root cause" before any solution. This is the page I'd send a skeptic to.
- The FAQ is huge (48+ questions, collapsible). Good that it's skimmable; I didn't read all of it.
- It honestly warns that the acronyms "land much easier after First Contact" — which matched my experience exactly. Glad I did First Contact first.
- Felt like a 12–15 minute page for the main content. Long, but it's upfront about that and offers the TL;DR alternative.

### ATP Economics (20:00)
- ATP = energy budget (battery metaphor, clear). ADP = a receipt/proof that an action happened — "background accounting, not currency you control."
- Good news: when ADP comes up, the page leads with **why I should care** ("when others confirm your work was valuable, that confirmation is what unlocks fresh ATP — and the ADP is the proof that earns it back") *before* the mechanics. I didn't have the "why am I learning about something I never touch?" reaction I might have expected.
- Late-page math (`Δrecharge = confirmer_trust × received_value_fraction`) is for people who want it; it's after the plain explanation, so skippable.

## Unanswered Questions
1. In "effective trust = raw × CI²", why *squared* specifically? The page tells me CI is squared but not the intuition for why squaring (vs. just multiplying once) is the right penalty shape.
2. CI "starts at 0.85" for new identities — does it climb automatically with time, or only through consistent action? I inferred the latter but wasn't taught it at that spot.
3. Who actually runs the "society" that accepts or rejects a rebirth? Is there a central authority, or is it emergent from peers? (Why Web4 says no central authority, but the rebirth-acceptance language sounds like a gatekeeper.)

## Honest Assessment
This was the smoothest visit I can imagine having as a newcomer. The site clearly knows where it wants to send a first-timer (TL;DR → First Contact), the problem is framed before the solution, and the two things I'd most expect to trip on — the death/permanence distinction and the raw-vs-effective trust split — were both handled well and *early* on Karma Journey. The two-numbers framing in particular is doing real work now; I held both concepts in my head instead of conflating them.

The one remaining snag is narrow and specific: the **CI² surcharge arithmetic** in Karma Journey's "What the numbers mean" section. It's not that I couldn't understand it — it's that the inline math broke an otherwise readable flow and made me re-read. The conceptual version of that same point (raw trust dampened until you prove consistency) is already stated clearly above it, so the arithmetic feels like it could live behind a "show the math" toggle for the people who want it. That would let the page stay intuition-first without losing rigor.

Net: good understanding, would return, would recommend. The site is in a strong place; the friction now is polish on one dense paragraph, not a structural gap.
