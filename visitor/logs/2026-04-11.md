## Unanswered Questions
1. **What does the actual UI look like?** The app mockups on "A Day in Web4" are conceptual descriptions, not visual prototypes. I still can't picture the interface I'd actually use. A few wireframes or screenshots (even low-fidelity) would make this much more concrete.
2. **How does quality measurement actually work in practice?** The system "observes confirmation speed, recipient diversity, and confirmer trust" — but who confirms? Is this automatic or manual? What stops people from rubber-stamping confirmations? The V3 scoring is described but the human workflow is unclear.
3. **What happens during the adoption gap?** Tiers 1-2 add trust to existing platforms, but what's the experience like when only 1% of users have Web4 identity? Do they just see trust scores for each other and nothing for everyone else? What's the value proposition at low penetration?
4. **AI Identity pages seem broken** — the nav lists "AI Identity", "AI Trust Limits", "AI Learning" but /ai-identity returned a 404. Are these pages built yet?
5. **How does cross-federation trust work in practice?** Federation Economics explains the mechanics, but what happens when two communities have fundamentally different trust calibrations? If community A's "0.7 trust" means something different than community B's "0.7 trust", how is that reconciled beyond MRH decay?

## Honest Assessment

This is an exceptionally well-constructed educational site for a complex technical topic. The information architecture — starting with a quick TL;DR, progressing through narrative (Alice's story), interactive experiences (Karma Journey, Society Simulator), and deep concept explainers — respects different learning styles and time commitments.

**What works brilliantly:**
- The "plain English first, acronym second" pattern throughout. I never felt acronym-bombed.
- The honest self-assessment pages (What Could Go Wrong, Threat Model). These build genuine trust in the project by admitting what isn't solved.
- Interactive elements that make abstract concepts tangible — the Karma Journey and Society Simulator especially.
- The "What is this, really?" section on the landing page that immediately addresses skepticism.
- Progressive disclosure — I could go as deep as I wanted on any topic without being forced.

**What could improve:**
- The /ai-identity 404 is the only real broken thing I found. Everything else worked.
- The effective trust vs raw trust distinction on Karma Journey could trip up visitors. The 0.361 starting effective trust looks alarming when you've just learned 0.5 is the survival threshold.
- Visual UI mockups remain the biggest gap. After 45 minutes, I understand the *mechanics* deeply but still can't picture the *experience*.
- Quality measurement workflow is the conceptual gap I couldn't close. The system measures quality, but the human side of "who confirms and why" is underdeveloped in the explainers.

**Overall:** I went from knowing nothing about Web4 to understanding six interconnected systems (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI, Aliveness) and how they compose into a trust-native internet framework. That's remarkable for 45 minutes. The site earned my trust by being transparent about its limitations — which, fittingly, is exactly what Web4 itself is designed to reward.
