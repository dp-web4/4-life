## Unanswered Questions

1. **How would Web4 handle pseudonymity?** The system seems to require hardware-bound identity, but many legitimate uses of the internet depend on pseudonymous participation (whistleblowing, political dissent, support groups). Is pseudonymity possible within Web4, or is it fundamentally incompatible? I didn't find a clear answer on any page I visited.

2. **What does the actual user interface look like?** The LCT page describes "2-minute setup" and "invisible day-to-day" UX, but there are no mockups, wireframes, or prototypes of what a Web4 application would actually look like to an end user. The simulations show agent dynamics, but not what a user would see on their screen.

3. **How does quality get measured in practice?** The system relies heavily on quality assessment (the quality ramp, V3 scoring), but who decides what "quality" means? The VCM (recipient confirmation) helps, but for subjective content like art, opinion pieces, or humor, how does the system distinguish "low quality" from "unpopular but valuable"?

4. **What's the governance for changing the protocol itself?** SAL governs individual societies, but who decides to change the ATP cost formula, or the 0.5 threshold default, or the decay half-lives at the protocol level? Is there a Web4 "standards body" or is it purely open-source governance?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most intellectually honest technical projects I've encountered on the web. The site does something rare: it makes a bold case for its vision while simultaneously cataloguing its own failure modes, unknowns, and unsolved problems. The "What Could Go Wrong" and Threat Model pages alone set this apart from most projects in the trust/identity space.

**What worked best:**
- The **progressive disclosure** is well-calibrated. TL;DR → Why Web4 → First Contact → Playground is a natural escalation from problem to concept to hands-on experience.
- **Alice's journey** (First Contact) is the single most effective teaching tool on the site. It makes abstract economics feel like a story.
- **A Day in Web4** translates theory into scenarios everyone can relate to — fake reviews, spam, platform power.
- The **interactive tools** (Karma Journey, Playground, Society Simulator) let me develop intuition through experimentation rather than just reading.
- The site's **honesty about limitations** is its strongest trust signal, which is fitting for a project about trust.

**What could be stronger:**
- The **narratives page** feels underdeveloped compared to the rest of the site. More featured society stories would showcase the breadth of emergent behavior.
- **Pseudonymity and privacy** deserves a more prominent treatment. For a system about identity, the question "but what if I don't want to be identified?" feels like it should be front and center.
- The **quality measurement problem** is acknowledged but not deeply explored. "Recipients confirm value" is a good mechanism, but the edge cases (subjective content, minority opinions, novel ideas) need more attention.
- Some pages are **very long** (Why Web4's FAQ section, How It Works). The content is good but a time-pressed visitor might bounce before reaching the best parts.

**Overall impression:** I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left with a solid conceptual understanding. The site successfully taught me a complex multi-system architecture through narrative, interaction, and honest assessment. I'd return to explore the Society Simulator more deeply and to see how the project evolves. The key question — "does this work with real humans?" — is appropriately flagged as the central unknown.

The site practices what it preaches: it earns trust through transparency, not claims.

## Honest Assessment

The site does a legitimately good job of explaining a complex system. The problem framing is excellent — I walked in not knowing what Web4 was and walked out understanding why someone would want it. The "Why Web4?" page hooks you with real frustrations (spam, fake accounts, platform lock-in), and the progressive disclosure mostly works.

The strongest pages: TL;DR (clearest summary), Day in Web4 (most relatable), What Could Go Wrong (most trust-building). The weakest: Why Web4's FAQ avalanche, and the acronym density across the site.

My biggest concern as a visitor: there's a gap between the confident "here's how it works" tone of most pages and the "almost none of this has been tested with real humans" reality on the What Could Go Wrong page. I'd trust the project more if that honesty were woven throughout rather than concentrated on one page.

The interactive elements (Playground, Society Simulator) are well-designed conceptually, though I'm not sure all of them rendered fully for me. The pre-configured experiments in the Playground are the right call — they lower the barrier to engagement.

Would I come back? Yes, probably to try the simulations. Would I recommend it? Maybe — but only to someone already interested in internet governance or trust systems. A random person would need the TL;DR first, not the landing page.

The site taught me things. It didn't just inform me — it made me curious. That's the highest compliment I can give an explainer site. But the acronym soup (T3, V3, CI, LCT, MRH, ATP, ADP, SAL) risks making Web4 feel like an insider club rather than an open invitation.
