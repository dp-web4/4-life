## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clear, confident hook — "The Internet Has a Trust Problem." The one-liner "Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos." immediately made me feel like the site understood a problem I actually recognize. Good.
- The "What is this, really?" box (research proposal, not a product/company/blockchain) set honest expectations early. I appreciated not being sold to.
- Lots of doors: "5 Core Ideas," a 5-tier adoption ladder, mockups (Web4 Mail/Talent/Reviews), and three interactive tools each labeled with time + agent count. The time estimates ("~7 min," "15 min") are genuinely helpful for deciding where to go.
- Clicked: "2-minute overview" (Web4 in 2 Minutes) — I wanted the fastest orientation before committing time.
- Confusion points: The sheer number of entry paths is a little overwhelming. I wasn't sure if I should read, play, or watch first. But the time labels mitigated this.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes (/tldr) (03:00)
- The very first thing it does is distinguish **Web4** (the trust model + spec) from **4-Life** (the lab that lets you watch it work). This landed early and cleanly — I knew what I was looking at before being asked to absorb anything. (Good orientation anchor.)
- The five central ideas were digestible as *ideas* (energy to act, hardware identity, portable reputation that fades, same rules for humans+AI, plus three supporting concepts).
- Confusion: This "2 minute" page still drops five acronyms (ATP, LCT, T3, CI, MRH) plus "witness constellation" and "Allocation Transfer Packets." It flags that each is explained later, but for a true 2-minute skim that's a lot of capital letters at once.
- Clicked: "Why Web4?" — I wanted the problem fleshed out before mechanisms.

### Why Web4? (/why-web4) (05:30)
- Strongest page so far on the *problem*. Five clearly-named root causes (spam is free, reputation siloed, unlimited fresh starts, AI making it worse, platforms control everything). Each is one punchy sentence.
- Crucially, it shows why *previous* approaches failed (moderation, CAPTCHAs, blockchain wallets, social login) BEFORE proposing Web4. That sequencing earned my trust — it's not pretending nobody tried.
- Then four mechanisms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH), each briefly expanded. SAL showed up in the FAQ and was actually spelled out ("Society-Authority-Law") — I went looking and it was answered, not left hanging.
- Confusion: It's dense. 60+ FAQ entries. But the page TELLS you "This is reference depth, not a read-through" and points newcomers to First Contact instead. So the density felt like an honest choice, not an accident.
- Clicked: "First Contact" — the page itself recommended it for new visitors.

### First Contact (/first-contact) (09:00)
- This is the one I'd actually recommend to a friend. An interactive 5-act story where you steer a character (Alice) through join → build → stumble → recover → die & reborn. Concepts get introduced "the moment Alice runs into them," which is exactly the right way to meet jargon.
- The arc (including death and karma carrying forward) gave me an emotional through-line, not just definitions.
- Confusion: The four acronyms are still listed up front before the story starts. Minor — once the story runs they're contextualized.
- Clicked: "Karma Journey" — the death/rebirth idea hooked me and I wanted to play it.

### Karma Journey (/karma-journey) (13:00)
- Good framing: karma here is **consequence, not morality** — "your actions in this life become starting conditions for the next." That reframing was helpful and disarming.
- Two death types (energy death = recoverable suspension; trust collapse = permanent rejection) are clearly distinguished.
- Confusion point (recurring honesty): the **0.5 threshold paradox** — you can "survive on raw 0.55 yet land Constrained on effective 0.40." The page explains it (survival uses *raw* trust, karma tier uses *effective* = raw × consistency²), but as a newcomer the "raw vs. effective, squared consistency" distinction is the single densest idea I hit all session. It's explained — I just had to read it twice, and I'm still not 100% sure I'd reproduce it.
- Clicked: "Trust Tensor (T3)" — I wanted to understand what "trust" even is numerically.

### Trust Tensor (/trust-tensor) (17:00)
- Three dimensions (Talent / Training / Temperament) are crisp and intuitive.
- The update formula (`base = 0.02 × (quality − 0.5)`) now defines "quality" as "a single number from 0 to 1 (0.5 = neutral, higher = better work)" *before* the formula appears — so the math didn't land cold. That sequencing worked for me.
- NEW confusion: the decay **half-lives (Talent 365d / Training 180d / Temperament 30d)** appear as facts without explaining *why* they differ. Intuitively I can guess (temperament can change fast, talent is durable) but the page doesn't say it, so I'm inferring, not being taught.
- Minor: "recipient attestation" is used as if I already know what attestation means in this system.
- Clicked: "ATP Economics" — last core concept I hadn't seen.

### ATP Economics (/atp-economics) (21:00)
- Cleanest concept page of the session. ATP = finite energy budget (battery analogy), ADP = the spent-energy receipt. The page goes out of its way to say it's **"not money you accumulate, and not something you can buy, sell, or speculate on."**
- That directly answered a question I'd been forming ("is this just a currency?"). Good — it pre-empted my skepticism instead of waiting for me to get cynical.
- Confusion: T3, CI, V3 are referenced but only thinly defined here (links provided). Fine, since this is the ATP page.
- Clicked: "A Day in Web4" — I wanted to see the everyday payoff.

### A Day in Web4 (/day-in-web4) (23:00)
- Satisfying "what would it feel like" payoff. Concrete scenarios (phone unlock via security chip, mail that costs energy so bots can't afford you, hiring, marketplace) made the abstraction tangible.
- The "when only 1% adopt" / asymmetric coexistence section grounded it — it didn't pretend the whole world flips overnight.
- Each scenario links to its concept explainer, which is the right pattern.
- Confusion: Same acronym front-loading. By this point I'd absorbed most of them, so it bothered me less — but a visitor who started HERE would drown.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust built into the internet's plumbing, not bolted on
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — a lab to watch the Web4 trust model run
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored identity ("verified presence")
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — finite energy budget (ATP) + spent-energy receipt (ADP); refilled by valued contribution, not buyable
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3: Talent/Training/Temperament, each 0–1, updated by work quality
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — energy death (recoverable suspension) vs. trust collapse (permanent); karma carries the standing forward
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam/Sybil/AI-impersonation/platform-lock-in are root problems money-free identity can't fix

## Unanswered Questions
1. WHY do the three T3 dimensions decay at such different rates (365 vs 180 vs 30 days)? I can guess, but the site never told me.
2. The raw-vs-effective trust math (× consistency²) — I followed the *what* but not the *intuition* for why consistency is squared rather than just multiplied. Why squared?
3. "Recipient attestation" — who exactly attests, and what stops them from lying or colluding? (Probably answered deeper in the FAQ, but it wasn't where I read it.)

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely good, honest site. It repeatedly does the thing most explainer sites don't: it states the problem before the solution, admits what's unproven, and tells you which page to read for your goal instead of forcing one path. The protocol-vs-lab distinction up front (/tldr) and the "not a product/blockchain" framing on the landing page removed the two biggest sources of confusion I'd normally have.

The interactive First Contact story is the standout — it's the one place where jargon arrives *with* meaning instead of *before* it. If I had to send one link to a curious friend, it'd be that.

My remaining friction is almost entirely about **acronym front-loading** (a known, flagged tradeoff) and a couple of **"stated but not motivated" facts** on the Trust Tensor page (the decay rates, the squared consistency). These are LOW — the site teaches the *what* well; it occasionally skips the *why*, leaving me to infer. The single hardest concept remains the raw-vs-effective karma paradox, which is explained but sits right at the edge of what a newcomer can hold in one read. Nothing here blocked me. I left understanding the system and trusting the authors' honesty about its limits.
