- First impression: Clean and confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus "explore Web4 through simulations" told me roughly what I was looking at within a few seconds. ### TL;DR (02:00)
- This is a strong page. It led with the problem ("The internet has no memory. A spammer can create a new account in seconds.") before the solution, which kept me oriented.
- The four mechanisms (energy budget, hardware identity, permanent multi-dimensional reputation, same rules for humans and AI) were stated plainly.
- Confusion points: This is where the acronym fire-hose started. ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI all appeared. ### First Contact (04:00)

## Unanswered Questions
1. Where does my *initial* ATP come from, and who controls the "society pool" that grants it? (Found a buried hint, never a clear answer.)
2. If reputation is permanent and hardware-bound, what happens when I lose/replace/sell my phone? Does my identity die with the chip?
3. What stops a wealthy actor from buying thousands of TPM-equipped devices to manufacture "witnessed" identities at scale?
4. When two communities with opposing values both claim me, whose trust score do I carry? The "they don't federate" answer didn't resolve this for me.

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely well-made explainer site — markedly better than most "new internet paradigm" pitches, mostly because of its honesty. It says "this is research, not a product," it admits its numbers are calibrated rather than derived, and its FAQ volunteers unsolved problems instead of hiding them. That candor is the site's superpower; it made me trust the authors even when I doubted the design. I left with a *good* working understanding and I'd come back.

The core friction is **acronym load and assembly cost**. The ideas themselves are intuitive when grounded (the surgeon analogy, Alice's energy bar, "spam burns out"). But the site teaches them in a vocabulary (ATP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI) that arrives faster than the definitions do. I built my understanding by stitching fragments across eight pages — the glossary that would have made this painless wasn't put in front of me until I went looking. A persistent "confused by a term? → glossary" affordance, plus inline first-use expansions, would do more for a naive visitor than any new content.

The second friction is **the practical-grounding gap**. I understand the mechanics, but the two questions a real person asks first — "where does my energy come from?" and "what happens when I lose my phone?" — are either buried or unaddressed. The site is strong on *why the problem matters* and *how the mechanisms work*, weaker on *what this means for me, concretely, on a Tuesday.*

Net: solid foundation, honest voice, real ideas — held back from "solid" understanding only by vocabulary pacing and a few missing everyday answers. Fix the glossary's discoverability and answer the "where does ATP come from / what if I lose my device" questions up front, and a naive visitor would leave with confidence instead of assembled-but-shaky comprehension.
