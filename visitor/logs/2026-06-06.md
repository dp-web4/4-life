## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean and confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus "explore Web4 through simulations" told me roughly what I was looking at within a few seconds. I appreciated the immediate honesty: *"this is research, not a live product or company"* and *"no blockchain, not a Web3 successor."* That defused my instinctive "is this a crypto scam?" reflex right away — which matters, because the word "trust-native internet" pattern-matches to crypto pitches.
- The section headers read like the questions I'd actually ask: "The Internet Has a Trust Problem," "What is this, really?," "So what can you actually do right now?," "How would this ever get adopted?" That framing earned my attention.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" — it was the most prominent CTA and 2 minutes is a low commitment.
- Confusion points: The nav has both "Start Here" and a TL;DR link and a "Structured learning path" and "Pick your path based on how much time you have." That's *several* front doors. As a visitor I briefly wasn't sure which was THE starting point. Not a blocker, but a moment of "okay, where do I actually go?"

### TL;DR (02:00)
- This is a strong page. It led with the problem ("The internet has no memory. A spammer can create a new account in seconds.") before the solution, which kept me oriented.
- The four mechanisms (energy budget, hardware identity, permanent multi-dimensional reputation, same rules for humans and AI) were stated plainly.
- Confusion points: This is where the acronym fire-hose started. ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI all appeared. They're acknowledged as shorthand with links, but seeing five unfamiliar capitalized initialisms in a "2-minute" page made me feel like I was already behind. I didn't *need* them to follow the gist, but they created low-grade anxiety.
- Clicked: "First Contact (7 min interactive)" — following a named character (Alice) sounded like the most concrete way in.

### First Contact (04:00)
- This was my favorite concept on the site: drive Alice's choices across 5 acts and watch two numbers (🔋 Energy/ATP starting at 100, 🤝 Trust starting at 0.50) respond. Concrete, low-stakes, human. "Spam burns out" and "No fresh starts" stuck with me.
- It IS interactive (play, pause, scrub, replay), though the outcomes are scripted — I'm observing rules, not making novel ones. That's fine for teaching.
- Confusion point: This is where I noticed T3 defined as "talent, training, temperament" — the TL;DR had used T3 without spelling it out, so I only learned what the T's stand for two pages in.

### Why Web4? (10:00)
- The single most persuasive page. Five concrete, relatable problems (spam is free, reputation is siloed, identity is disposable, AI agents accelerate abuse, you're a guest on someone else's platform), each with a root-cause diagnosis.
- The FAQ is unusually candid — it admits unsolved problems (bootstrapping is hard, GDPR vs. permanent reputation tension, "newcomers ARE more vulnerable" for their first ~30 actions, numbers are "calibrated, not derived"). That honesty made me trust the *people* even where I doubted the *system*.
- Hand-wavy bits I noticed: what happens when federated communities have genuinely opposing values (answer is basically "they don't federate" — feels like a dodge), and whether energy-cost economics actually deter abuse at billions of users (page admits it's untested). The gap between "works in simulation" and "works in the real world" is real and the page is more honest that the gap exists than it is about how wide it is.

### LCT Explainer (15:00)
- I could summarize it after reading: an identity that lives in your device's security chip, can't leave the silicon, co-witnessed by other devices so it can't be faked — letting you build reputation without revealing your real name.
- I appreciated that it proactively raised privacy concerns (7 leakage channels, timing/graph-structure metadata) rather than hiding them: *"Web4 trades centralized data collection for decentralized attestation patterns."* Honest.
- Confusing: "device co-witnesses" (my own devices) vs. "network witnesses" (infrastructure) use the same word for two jobs, and the trust ceilings (0.50 / 0.75 / 0.90) needed a logical leap I didn't make on first read.

### ATP Economics (19:00)
- Clear on WHAT and WHY ("ATP is your energy budget — a finite per-agent allowance," every action costs, quality earns it back). The spam-is-free contrast is intuitive.
- The biology metaphor (ATP/ADP from cell metabolism) added vocabulary overhead. I had to juggle ATP (energy), ADP (spent receipt), and confirmation (recharge) at once before any concrete example landed.
- **My biggest unanswered question lived here:** where does the energy *come from* initially? If it's finite per agent, who funds my starting balance? The answer ("starter grant from the society's pool") is buried in a footnote/tooltip. As a newcomer that's the first practical question I'd ask, and it's the hardest to find.

### Trust Tensor (22:00)
- I was braced for math ("tensor" is intimidating) and the page disarmed it well: *"You don't need the math — one number couldn't tell a brilliant-but-erratic surgeon from a kind doctor with shaky hands."* The surgeon/mechanic role examples are excellent.
- T3 (Talent/Training/Temperament — grades the person) vs. V3 (Valuation/Veracity/Validity — grades the output). The "T3 grades Alice; V3 grades the tutorial" line is the key, and it arrives a bit late/buried.
- Confusing: the V3 digression shows up before I'd digested T3, and a stray update formula (`0.02 × (quality − 0.5)`) plus arbitrary-seeming decay half-lives broke the plain-English spell.

### Aliveness (25:00)
- "Death" is metaphorical (suspension of participation, not erasure) and the page says so up front: *"your reputation isn't erased. What ends is the current life-cycle."* Aliveness is a measurable state: ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, CI coherent. "Spam dies naturally (energy exhaustion). No moderators needed."
- Confusing: the relentless death/rebirth framing risks burying the "it's really just suspension" caveat, and there's no concrete "why should *I*, a user, care" example. Heaviest acronym density of any page I visited.

### Glossary + Society Simulator (peeked, 27:00)
- The glossary is genuinely good and I wish I'd been pointed to it on page ONE. It defines all my pain terms (ATP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH = "Trust Neighborhood," CI) in plain English, with an "Essential only" filter for the 7 core terms. MRH in particular only got a real definition here — every other page used it raw.
- The Society Simulator has solid onboarding ("Never seen these Web4 terms? ... First Contact is a 7-minute guided intro"), scenario presets, and a "What to Watch For" four-phase guide. I'd probably hit "Run" first and backfill understanding — which the page anticipates.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed trust model where actions cost energy, identity is hardware-bound, and reputation is permanent + portable
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — interactive simulations that show those rules playing out
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-rooted, device-witnessed pseudonymous identity
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget spent on actions, refilled by valued contributions (ADP = the spent receipt)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — multi-dimensional trust (T3 person, V3 output) instead of one meaningless average
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive = ATP>0, T3>0.5, CI coherent; "death" = suspension, not erasure
- [x] Why any of this matters — the "internet has no memory / actions are free" problem is real and well-argued

(Notably: I checked every box, but several were *inferred across multiple pages* rather than taught cleanly on one. The understanding is real but assembled, not handed to me.)

## Unanswered Questions
1. Where does my *initial* ATP come from, and who controls the "society pool" that grants it? (Found a buried hint, never a clear answer.)
2. If reputation is permanent and hardware-bound, what happens when I lose/replace/sell my phone? Does my identity die with the chip?
3. What stops a wealthy actor from buying thousands of TPM-equipped devices to manufacture "witnessed" identities at scale?
4. When two communities with opposing values both claim me, whose trust score do I carry? The "they don't federate" answer didn't resolve this for me.
5. Concretely, what does using Web4 *feel like* day-to-day? (There's "A Day in Web4" I didn't visit — but I shouldn't have to hunt for the everyday picture.)

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely well-made explainer site — markedly better than most "new internet paradigm" pitches, mostly because of its honesty. It says "this is research, not a product," it admits its numbers are calibrated rather than derived, and its FAQ volunteers unsolved problems instead of hiding them. That candor is the site's superpower; it made me trust the authors even when I doubted the design. I left with a *good* working understanding and I'd come back.

The core friction is **acronym load and assembly cost**. The ideas themselves are intuitive when grounded (the surgeon analogy, Alice's energy bar, "spam burns out"). But the site teaches them in a vocabulary (ATP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI) that arrives faster than the definitions do. I built my understanding by stitching fragments across eight pages — the glossary that would have made this painless wasn't put in front of me until I went looking. A persistent "confused by a term? → glossary" affordance, plus inline first-use expansions, would do more for a naive visitor than any new content.

The second friction is **the practical-grounding gap**. I understand the mechanics, but the two questions a real person asks first — "where does my energy come from?" and "what happens when I lose my phone?" — are either buried or unaddressed. The site is strong on *why the problem matters* and *how the mechanisms work*, weaker on *what this means for me, concretely, on a Tuesday.*

Net: solid foundation, honest voice, real ideas — held back from "solid" understanding only by vocabulary pacing and a few missing everyday answers. Fix the glossary's discoverability and answer the "where does ATP come from / what if I lose my device" questions up front, and a naive visitor would leave with confidence instead of assembled-but-shaky comprehension.
