## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who's actually building this?** I see a GitHub org (dp-web4) but no team page, no about section, no indication of who's behind the project or what their credentials are. This matters for trust (ironic, given the topic).
2. **Is there any real-world deployment or adoption?** The "Honest Questions" section on the Why Web4 page says it's theoretical with simulations. But has anyone actually tried to build on this protocol?
3. **How does this handle the cold-start problem in practice?** The bootstrapping section acknowledges it's hard. But the "partner with universities/employers" suggestion feels hand-wavy. How do you convince the first 1,000 users to buy hardware tokens?
4. **What's the relationship between the educational model (5D trust) and the production model (3D trust)?** The site uses a 5-dimensional trust model for teaching but mentions the real spec uses 3 dimensions. How do competence/reliability/integrity/alignment/transparency map to Talent/Training/Temperament?
5. **How does ATP prevent wealth concentration?** If good actors accumulate ATP across lives, don't they become untouchable elites? What prevents a "rich get richer" dynamic?
6. **What about edge cases like shared devices?** Families sharing a tablet, public library computers, borrowed phones — hardware-bound identity doesn't work well here.
7. **How does MRH handle new relationships?** If I need to contact someone outside my horizon (e.g., a stranger who's a specialist), how do I reach them?
8. **What's EP (Epistemic Proprioception) exactly?** It's mentioned several times but never fully explained on the pages I visited.

## Honest Assessment

This is a surprisingly well-built explainer site for a genuinely complex topic. As a first-time visitor with zero Web4 knowledge, I went from "what is this?" to "I understand the core architecture" in about 25 minutes. That's impressive.

**What worked well:**
- The "Why Web4?" page is outstanding. Problem-before-solution, honest caveats, real engagement with criticisms. I'd share this page with skeptical friends.
- The "How It Works" lifecycle walkthrough (Birth → Life → Death → Rebirth) was the single most effective teaching tool. Following an agent through multiple lives made the abstract concrete.
- Interactive elements (trust tensor simulator, coherence scenarios, MRH explorer, playground sliders) invite experimentation. Even without running them, their presence signals "you can touch this."
- The biological metaphor (ATP = metabolic energy, death = real) is genuinely intuitive. Much better than typical crypto/token jargon.
- Honesty throughout. "This is research, not production." "Bootstrapping is genuinely hard." This builds trust in the project.

**What needs work:**
- The landing page has broken/loading elements ("Loading...", "Analyzing simulation data...") that make the site feel unfinished.
- First Contact is a dead end without JavaScript. For a page that's supposed to be the "10-minute introduction," it offers nothing to visitors who can't run the simulation.
- The educational model (5D trust) vs canonical model (3D trust) inconsistency is confusing. Pick one for the site or explain the mapping clearly.
- Lab Console is intimidating for casual visitors. Simulation modes have no descriptions. Two of three buttons require Python. This should be the "wow, look at it run!" moment, but it's gated behind setup.
- There's no "Why This Matters To You" page — something connecting the abstract Web4 mechanisms to concrete everyday scenarios. "Imagine you're on social media and..." type narratives.
- No team/about page. For a project about trust, the lack of information about who's building it is a notable gap.

**Overall:** I'd give this a solid B+. The conceptual content is excellent — among the best I've seen for explaining a novel protocol. The site structure and progressive disclosure work well. The gaps are mostly around polish (broken loading states), accessibility (JS-dependent features), and the missing "last mile" of connecting abstract concepts to everyday human experience. I would return to play with the simulators if they work. I'm not sure I'd recommend it to a non-technical friend yet — they'd bounce off the jargon on the landing page before reaching the excellent Why Web4 page.
