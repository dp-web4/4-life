## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean, confident, and immediately oriented me. The heading "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the subtitle "explore Web4 through simulations" told me what I was looking at. The one-line definition right under it did a *lot* of work: "Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor." Two things I'd usually have to guess (is this blockchain? is this a Web3 thing?) were answered before I could even form the question. Good.
- The problem statement landed cleanly: "Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos. Bad actors get unlimited fresh starts." I felt that — it's a real problem I recognize.
- **Clicked**: I had four obvious entry points (TL;DR · 2 min, What it looks like · 5 min, Frustrations it would fix · 3 min, Structured learning path). The "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" was clearly marked as the recommended first stop, so I took it.
- **Confusion points**: None at this stage. The five plain-language ideas (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) were a nice low-jargon teaser.

### TL;DR — "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (02:30)
- This is a genuinely good on-ramp. Five tidy sections: The Problem, The Idea, Three More Concepts, The Result, Where Is This Now.
- **Every acronym was expanded on first use**: ATP = Allocation Transfer Packets, LCT = Linked Context Token, T3 = Talent/Training/Temperament, MRH = Markov Relevancy Horizon, CI = Coherence Index. I appreciated not being made to guess.
- The "Where Is This Now?" section was honest: "active research with working simulations," not deployed. That set my expectations correctly so I wasn't confused later about whether I could sign up.
- **Confusion point (minor)**: "Identity anchored in device security chips" — I nodded along but didn't really *picture* what that meant yet. Filed it away.
- It pointed me to "First Contact" as the fastest next step. Followed.

### First Contact (05:00)
- An interactive story: I steer a character named Alice through five acts (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn). It tracks just two numbers — 🔋 Energy (ATP, starts at 100) and 🤝 Trust (0.00–1.00, starts at 0.50). Limiting it to two numbers was a smart teaching move; I never felt swamped.
- The "is this real?" framing was very clear and repeated calmly: "a model of a *proposed* system," "Real rules, shown by simulation — not a demo of a shipped product." I never wondered whether Alice was a real person or whether I was using a live product.
- Each concept (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) got introduced *when Alice hit it*, which made them feel earned rather than dumped.
- **Confusion point**: What actually counts as "quality" vs "spam" stayed abstract. The sim decides, but I couldn't tell you the rule. Minor.

### Day in Web4 — "What it looks like" (10:00)
- This was the page I most wanted: it tries to show what *using* Web4 would feel like, with clickable mockups (Mail, Talent, Reviews, Social, Market). Seeing a mock inbox and trust-scored vendors was the most concrete the whole site got. Strong.
- **Confusion point (recurring-feel)**: The verb tense wobbles. The intro says "narrated in the present tense as a thought experiment," but then the page swings between "Your device's security chip quietly proves it's really you" (sounds real, now) and "this is what it *would* feel like" (hypothetical). The setup steps ("Download the app," "Bind your device") read like literal instructions even though they're labeled as imagined. For a beat I wondered if there was something to download.
- The "None of this is downloadable yet — Web4 is active research" disclaimer exists but sits mid-page, after I'd already been picturing it as real.

### Why Web4? (15:00)
- The problem framing here is the best on the site. Four named problems (free spam, trapped reputation, disposable identity, AI at scale, platform control), each with a root cause. It diagnoses before it sells, which made me trust it.
- The four-part solution (ATP / LCT / T3 / MRH) is clean and each has a "Learn more" link.
- **Confusion point (real)**: By the time I reached the FAQ I was tracking ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI, V3, *and* SAL. Definitions are given at first mention, but that's a lot of three-letter codes to hold at once.
- **Confusion point (real, recurring-feel)**: The FAQ is enormous — something like a dozen categories and ~60+ questions, and the collapsed answers hide whether each is 100 words or 1000. It's clearly meant as a reference manual, and the topic index helps, but as a *first-time reader* trying to read top-to-bottom it's exhausting. I read "Most Asked," opened 3–4, and bailed from fatigue rather than confusion. The honesty ("we don't attach timelines because honest research doesn't have them") was disarming.

### ATP Economics (24:00)
- This page is excellent for a newcomer. There's a "The 60-second version" box right at the top with five bullets, and it answered my actual questions immediately:
  - What ATP is in one sentence: "your **energy budget** — a finite per-agent allowance, not money you accumulate, and not something you can buy, sell, or speculate on."
  - ATP vs ADP: "cash" vs "the receipt you get when you spend it."
  - Who confirms value: "the recipients you helped, not you and not a central rater."
  - When energy comes back: a worked example (~50% in a few hours, ~80% by six hours, fully within a day).
- I came in braced for the energy metaphor to be fuzzy and left actually understanding it. Best-explained concept on the site.
- **Confusion point (minor)**: "confirmer_trust" and "quality ramp %" hint at machinery underneath — for a second I wasn't sure if peers or an algorithm score me (the page does say peers).

### LCT Explainer (29:00)
- The driver's-license analogy is good: "your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer."
- Hardware question answered well: yes, my existing phone works; the chips (Secure Enclave / TPM / FIDO2) are "already in devices you probably own." Reassuring.
- **Confusion point (real, my #1 of the session)**: The trust numbers. A table of ceilings (0.90 / 0.85 / 0.75 / 0.50) shows up *before* the little "New to the scale?" explainer that tells me 0 = none, 1.0 = max, 0.5 = the alive/dead line. So I met the numbers cold and had to scroll back to make sense of them. The ordering is backwards for a first-timer.
- **Confusion point (real, related)**: The page uses **both** percentages (1 device = 50%, 2 = 75%, 3+ = up to 90%) **and** decimals (0.50–0.75, "the remaining 0.10 is the gap") for what seems to be the same scale, but never says out loud that 90% = 0.90. I *inferred* they're the same thing; the page didn't *tell* me. A single "(90% = 0.90 on the trust scale)" would have removed all doubt.
- **Confusion point (minor)**: "Witness" means two things (your own devices co-signing each other vs. optional outside infrastructure), and that's flagged — but the two meanings aren't fully untangled until later.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (34:00)
- Clear. The "tensor" word is defused immediately ("you don't need the math"), and the surgeon-vs-doctor example sells *why* one number isn't enough.
- I finally got an answer to "what does 0.7 mean?": "0.5 = newcomer baseline, 0.7 = ~70% cooperative behavior in this role, 0.9 = consistently exceptional." This is the kind of anchoring I wished the LCT page had near its table.
- V3 is deliberately deferred ("You don't need it to follow this page"), which felt like good pacing, not a gap.
- **Confusion point (minor)**: The update formula ("0.02 × (quality − 0.5)") and the three decay half-lives (365/180/30 days) appear without much motivation in the main flow. I skipped them, which the page seems to expect.

### Coherence Index / CI (38:00)
- My favorite explainer after ATP. The credit-check framing ("does your behavior make physical sense?") plus two concrete scenarios did it: a "Normal Tuesday" at CI ~0.97 vs a compromised account posting "crypto spam at 3am from a different country" crashing to ~0.4. I instantly got it.
- The heading uses the plain name ("Behavioral Consistency"), and the math (geometric mean, squaring) is tucked behind "Show the math" toggles, so it never intimidated me.
- **Confusion point (minor)**: New accounts start at CI ~0.85; the page reframes it as "you just haven't been witnessed yet," but it still reads like a penalty for being new.

### MRH / Trust Neighborhood Explorer (41:00)
- The decay idea is intuitive via examples (direct friend ≈0.85, friend's colleague ≈0.65, 4-hops = below the noise floor). The "why 3 hops?" question is met head-on: "Calibrated, not derived" — chosen because past 3 hops the signal collapses in real networks. I liked that honesty; it didn't pretend the number was a law of physics.
- **Confusion point (minor)**: The 4D horizon profile (spatial/temporal/complexity) added complexity without examples I could hold onto.

### Karma Journey (44:00)
- Fun and the formulas are explained *right where I play*. CI² even justifies the squaring inline: "a small dip barely shows (0.9² = 0.81); a serious one nearly halves you (0.6² = 0.36)." That's the kind of thing I'd expect to be offscreen, and it wasn't.
- Raw trust (survival) vs effective trust (karma tier at death) is a subtle two-metric distinction, but the play area defines both.
- **Confusion point (minor)**: I start at CI 0.85 with a 1.4× cost multiplier and the *why* lives behind a link, not in-game.

### Society Simulator (47:00)
- Big interactive: 12 agents with strategies cooperating/betraying autonomously, or I can "play as an agent." There's a collapsible "Acronyms: what do ATP, T3, LCT, CI, MRH, V3 mean?" key and an early "Never seen these Web4 terms before?" → First Contact link. Both helped.

So I couldn't tell which numbers the *running* simulation actually computes vs. which are just described in passing. A small "what this demo actually updates" note would help.

### Glossary (visited for lookups)

As someone with no security/crypto background, "TPM" and "Secure Enclave" stayed black boxes for me. I'd have liked a one-line plain definition (or a link) for each of those.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust verified by behavior, not assumed; not blockchain, not Web3
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab/simulation of the Web4 trust model
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound, witnessed proof of presence ("your phone as an un-fakeable ID")
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — ATP = energy budget (cash), ADP = receipt; recipients confirm value to recharge it
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three role-scoped trust dimensions (Talent/Training/Temperament), 0.7 ≈ 70% cooperative
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive = ATP>0 + trust>0.5 + coherent; energy-death recoverable, trust-death permanent (license analogy)
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam, trapped reputation, disposable identity, AI at scale

All seven boxes checked. The site *taught* me almost all of this rather than making me infer it — the one place I had to infer was that LCT's percentages and decimals are the same scale.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **On the LCT page, do "90%" and "0.90" mean the same thing?** I'm 95% sure they do, but the page never confirmed it, and being unsure about the single most important number system on the site is uncomfortable.
2. **What exactly does the Society Simulator compute vs. narrate?** The "Not modeled in this scalar demo" caveat next to MRH left me unsure which numbers on screen are "live."
3. **What actually *is* a TPM / Secure Enclave, in plain terms?** The whole identity story rests on these chips, but they stayed black boxes for me. Is the chip doing something I should understand (or worry about) privacy-wise?
4. **If my reputation is permanent and tied to my hardware, what happens if I lose all my devices, or my phone dies?** I saw hints that single-device users hit a trust ceiling, but not a clear answer to "I dropped my only phone in a lake — am I locked out of my whole identity forever?"

## Honest Assessment
This is, genuinely, one of the most carefully built explainer sites I've worked through. The on-ramp (Landing → TL;DR → First Contact) is paced beautifully: it never throws more than two numbers at me at once, it expands every acronym on first use, and it is *relentlessly* honest that this is research, not a product — which paradoxically made me trust it more. The ATP and Coherence Index pages are model explainers: a short summary box up top, a concrete relatable scenario, and the scary math tucked behind a toggle.

My friction was concentrated in two spots. The bigger one is the **LCT page's trust numbers**: I hit a table of ceilings before anyone told me what the 0–1 scale means, and then the page freely mixes "90%" and "0.90" without ever saying they're the same thing. That's the only place on the whole site where I had to *infer* something load-bearing instead of being taught it — and it's about the most important number system Web4 uses, so it matters more than its size suggests. The second is the **Why Web4 FAQ**: it's a magnificent reference, but as a first read it's a wall, and I left it fatigued rather than enlightened.

A smaller theme: the site is excellent at defining *its own* vocabulary but assumes I already know the *borrowed* vocabulary it stands on — TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2, blockchain. Those stayed fuzzy for me, and since the identity story leans entirely on "security chips," that fuzziness sat right under the foundation.

Net: I came in knowing nothing and left able to explain Web4 to a friend in a couple of sentences. That's a real win. Would I return? Yes — I'd go straight back to the Day-in-Web4 mockups and the Society Simulator to play. Would I recommend it? Yes, with the note: "start at the TL;DR, do First Contact, and don't try to read the Why-Web4 FAQ in one sitting."
