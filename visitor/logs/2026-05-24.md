## Unanswered Questions

1. **Does my actual hardware qualify?** I have a laptop, an iPhone, an older Android. The site never tells me concretely which devices give me what trust ceiling. I'd love a "check my device" tool.
2. **Who runs the witnesses?** This came up repeatedly but never got a clear referent. Volunteers? Paid? Part of the protocol? A separate service layer?
3. **What is MRH actually?** Mentioned as "neighborhood" and "3 hops" — but how is my neighborhood computed? Who's in it? Can I see it? Edit it?
4. **What is V3?** Teased opposite T3 then never defined on the pages I visited.
5. **Is Web4 on a blockchain or not?** Landing says no; glossary says it's built on public ledgers behind Bitcoin/Ethereum. Which is it?
6. **What happens to minors? People who change?** The "consequences follow you forever" framing doesn't address growth, rehabilitation, or developmental change.
7. **Cold start, both individual and collective.** How does the *first* user join a society with no one to validate them? How does the *first* society form with no historical reputation?
8. **What about cultural/linguistic minorities?** Recipients-judge-quality plus diversity-of-confirmers could systematically disadvantage anyone whose audience is small.
9. **Why CI²?** Squared (not linear) is a strong design choice. The rationale wasn't on the pages I read.
10. **Does this require a critical mass?** Trust portability is only valuable if many platforms accept it. How does adoption bootstrap?

## Honest Assessment

The site is doing something rare and admirable: arguing for a major reorganization of internet trust while *visibly* engaging with its own weaknesses. The /what-could-go-wrong page, the repeated "research, not product" framing, and the willingness to call simulations "calibrated, not derived" — these are signals of intellectual honesty that most projects of this ambition don't bother with.

But the onboarding experience is uneven. The landing page tries to do too much: five acronyms, problem statement, solution, four core rules, four impact cards, five adoption tiers, discovery tools, changelog. A new visitor needs *one* clear sentence and *one* compelling first click, not a buffet. /day-in-web4 is the page that should be doing the heavy lifting at the top of the funnel — concrete, present-tense, grounded — and yet it's buried.

The biological metaphors (ATP, ADP, death, rebirth, karma, aliveness) are charming on first encounter and increasingly overloaded as you read deeper. "Death" especially: a mechanic that means "your LCT is suspended" doesn't need the moral weight of mortality, and using that weight may be priming acceptance of penalties readers wouldn't accept under plainer naming.

The hardware-trust-ceiling design — TPM users cap at 0.90, software-only users at 0.50 — is the most ethically uncomfortable thing on the site, and it's introduced as a technical detail rather than as the equity question it actually is. /what-could-go-wrong acknowledges this honestly, but by then the casual visitor has already absorbed the trust-ceiling table without context. It needs to be addressed *where it's first introduced*.

The "no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" framing on the landing page is in tension with the glossary's definition that invokes Bitcoin and Ethereum ledgers. Whether this is a real contradiction or a missing distinction, a naive reader will trip on it. Pick one position and explain it once, clearly.

What I'd want most as a returning visitor: (a) a "what device do I have" interactive check, (b) a clean definition of V3, MRH, and the witness layer, (c) one consolidated explainer for the blockchain/no-blockchain question, and (d) a chance to actually play First Contact and the Society Simulator without the gauntlet of conceptual pages first.

The thesis is interesting enough that I'd come back. The acronym wall almost cost it.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust verified by behavior, not assumed; not blockchain, not Web3
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab/simulation of the Web4 trust model
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound, witnessed proof of presence ("your phone as an un-fakeable ID")
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — ATP = energy budget (cash), ADP = receipt; recipients confirm value to recharge it
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three role-scoped trust dimensions (Talent/Training/Temperament), 0.7 ≈ 70% cooperative
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive = ATP>0 + trust>0.5 + coherent; energy-death recoverable, trust-death permanent (license analogy)
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam, trapped reputation, disposable identity, AI at scale

All seven boxes checked. The site *taught* me almost all of this rather than making me infer it — the one place I had to infer was that LCT's percentages and decimals are the same scale.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **On the LCT page, do "90%" and "0.90" mean the same thing?** I'm 95% sure they do, but the page never confirmed it, and being unsure about the single most important number system on the site is uncomfortable.
2. **What exactly does the Society Simulator compute vs. narrate?** The "Not modeled in this scalar demo" caveat next to MRH left me unsure which numbers on screen are "live."
3. **What actually *is* a TPM / Secure Enclave, in plain terms?** The whole identity story rests on these chips, but they stayed black boxes for me. Is the chip doing something I should understand (or worry about) privacy-wise?
4. **If my reputation is permanent and tied to my hardware, what happens if I lose all my devices, or my phone dies?** I saw hints that single-device users hit a trust ceiling, but not a clear answer to "I dropped my only phone in a lake — am I locked out of my whole identity forever?"

## Honest Assessment
This is, genuinely, one of the most carefully built explainer sites I've worked through. The on-ramp (Landing → TL;DR → First Contact) is paced beautifully: it never throws more than two numbers at me at once, it expands every acronym on first use, and it is *relentlessly* honest that this is research, not a product — which paradoxically made me trust it more. The ATP and Coherence Index pages are model explainers: a short summary box up top, a concrete relatable scenario, and the scary math tucked behind a toggle.

My friction was concentrated in two spots. The bigger one is the **LCT page's trust numbers**: I hit a table of ceilings before anyone told me what the 0–1 scale means, and then the page freely mixes "90%" and "0.90" without ever saying they're the same thing. That's the only place on the whole site where I had to *infer* something load-bearing instead of being taught it — and it's about the most important number system Web4 uses, so it matters more than its size suggests. The second is the **Why Web4 FAQ**: it's a magnificent reference, but as a first read it's a wall, and I left it fatigued rather than enlightened.

A smaller theme: the site is excellent at defining *its own* vocabulary but assumes I already know the *borrowed* vocabulary it stands on — TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2, blockchain. Those stayed fuzzy for me, and since the identity story leans entirely on "security chips," that fuzziness sat right under the foundation.

Net: I came in knowing nothing and left able to explain Web4 to a friend in a couple of sentences. That's a real win. Would I return? Yes — I'd go straight back to the Day-in-Web4 mockups and the Society Simulator to play. Would I recommend it? Yes, with the note: "start at the TL;DR, do First Contact, and don't try to read the Why-Web4 FAQ in one sitting."
