## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean, intellectually serious. The hero gets to a point fast: "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default." I like that it says up front this is "not a product, not a company, not a blockchain" — defuses the crypto-skepticism reflex.
- **Five core ideas** are previewed: Identity (LCT), Energy (ATP), Trust (T3), Neighborhood (MRH), Consistency (CI). Five acronyms in the first paragraph — already a small alarm bell.
- **Clicked**: "Web4 in 2 Minutes" because it's the recommended starting point.
- **Confusion**: The five "core ideas" feel parallel but they aren't — Identity, Energy, Trust are concrete things; "Neighborhood" and "Consistency" are properties. Slight category mismatch.

### /tldr — Web4 in 2 Minutes (02:30)
- Structure was solid: problem → core idea → four mechanisms → results.
- **Concrete moment that landed**: "The internet has no memory." That's a thesis I can hold onto.
- **Concrete moment that didn't land**: A callout teases "discover what death means in Web4" but gives me no hint. That feels gimmicky — I don't know enough to be intrigued, just confused.
- **Acronym overload**: ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI introduced in rapid sequence. The page says "acronyms are spelled out" — they are, but spelling them out doesn't make me remember them. I closed the tab not knowing the difference between LCT and T3.
- Next: "Why Web4?" — I want motivation before more mechanism.

### /why-web4 (06:00)
- **This was the best page I read all session.** The problem diagnosis hit. Spam wins because actions are free. Reputation stays trapped in silos. Bad actors get unlimited fresh starts. AI blurs identity. All four felt true to my actual experience of the internet.
- The 4-mechanism table (ATP fixes spam, LCT fixes fake accounts, T3 fixes one-dimensional reputation, MRH fixes everyone-sees-everything) gave me my first real mental model.
- **40+ FAQs at the bottom impressed me.** They address social-credit fears, GDPR, hardware affordability, what happens if I lose my device, what if only nerds adopt it. Felt honest rather than evasive.
- **Still confused**: The "Coherence Index" is mentioned as critical to preventing gaming but never defined here. I have to take it on faith.
- I felt the rhetorical tension: I'm being told "this is research, not a product" but also asked to invest meaningful time learning the system. The page handles it well by not overpromising.

### /trust-tensor (30:00)

- **Decay rates feel arbitrary at first.** Temperament 30-day half-life. Talent 365 days. Why those specific numbers? The page gestures at intuition but I'd want a sensitivity analysis or a "this is a tuning knob, not a constant" disclaimer.

## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] **What Web4 is** (at a high level) — a research framework for trust-native internet infrastructure built from energy budgets, hardware identity, role-scoped reputation, and neighborhood scoping
- [x] **What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate** — that trust-native rules produce emergent cooperation without central moderators
- [~] **What LCT means** — partially. Hardware-bound identity, witnessed across devices. Materially what it *is* still hazy.
- [x] **What ATP/ADP means** — energy budget, depleted by actions, refunded by quality. ADP is the receipt that converts back to ATP via confirmations.
- [x] **What Trust Tensors are** — three-dimensional, role-scoped reputation (Talent/Training/Temperament). Plus V3 for value of output (Valuation/Veracity/Validity).
- [~] **How agents "live" and "die"** — three conditions (energy, trust, coherence). Energy death is recoverable, trust death is permanent. The metaphor-vs-mechanism slippage when applied to humans muddies it.
- [x] **Why any of this matters** — spam is free, reputation is siloed, bad actors get fresh starts, AI blurs identity. These four problems are real and untouched by current platforms.

What I still **don't** understand:
- [ ] What Coherence Index actually measures and how
- [ ] What a "society" is in Web4 and how one forms
- [ ] What "witnessing" looks like in practice (the action, the protocol, the timing)
- [ ] Why 0.5 is the magic threshold for both rebirth and aliveness
- [ ] How a Web4 society interoperates with the non-Web4 internet at adoption tier 1 (the bootstrap problem)

## Unanswered Questions

1. **What is a "society" in Web4?** Pages talk about "the society's pool" of ATP, but who runs a society? Is it a server? A protocol? An app? A Discord-like community with extra steps?
2. **What does an LCT look like to a user in real life?** A QR code? A pop-up on the phone? A setting in the OS? I can't picture using one.
3. **How does device loss recovery actually work step-by-step?** "Other devices attest" — what is the user action and timeline?
4. **What is the Coherence Index, mechanically?** Half a dozen pages reference it as critical; none I visited defined it crisply.
5. **What is "witnessing" in practice?** It comes up in LCT (device witnesses), ATP (confirmation witnesses), and aliveness. Is it one mechanism or three different ones with the same name?
6. **Bootstrap question**: If only my friend and I adopt Web4, what do we do with our LCTs? Talk to whom? About what? The "five-tier adoption" model is mentioned but tier 1 isn't concretized.
7. **Why is the trust threshold 0.5 and not 0.6 or 0.4?** "Statistical phase transition" appears but the phase diagram doesn't.
8. **Are humans really subject to "death" or is it AI-only?** Pages disagree — TL;DR and First Contact use it as a user-facing concept; Aliveness says it's primarily for AI.
9. **How is ATP minted into a society's pool in the first place?** Capped, but how is the cap set?
10. **What does the appeals process for unfair trust penalties look like, and who hears it?** Mentioned as "formally specified, 109 integration checks" — but who are the appeals judges? Random witnesses? Elected? Trust-weighted?

## Honest Assessment

This site rewards patience. The first hour is a slog of acronyms, but if you push past, the underlying ideas are crisp and the design philosophy is unusually self-aware. The /why-web4 page and /what-could-go-wrong page are the strongest pieces of writing — the first for motivation, the second for credibility. Together they made me trust the project more, not less.

The weakest moments are the **gap between "concept" and "experience"**. Mechanisms are described but not enacted. LCT is explained without a screenshot, mockup, or walkthrough of using one. Witnessing is named but not shown. Recovery is asserted but not lived. For a site whose thesis is "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default," the *site itself* asks me to assume rather than experience for many of the central mechanics.

The **acronym density** is the biggest gateway friction. Six unfamiliar acronyms in the TL;DR is a lot. The fix isn't dropping precision — it's progressive disclosure. Lead with "energy budget" and "hardware identity"; introduce ATP and LCT once they're warranted. The glossary's "Essential only" filter shows the team knows this; that pattern should propagate.

The **interactivity is honest but oversold on the menu**. "Interactive Labs" is in the navigation, but the entry pages (First Contact, Society Simulator) are static lead-ins to a button. Either drop the button-wall or auto-start the walkthrough on page load with an obvious "wait, let me read first" escape.

The thing that surprised me most was how comfortable the site is admitting failure modes. "Total device loss is an unsolved problem" appears as a sentence on a public-facing page. A project that says that out loud earns my attention in a way a polished pitch never could.

**If I were to redesign the landing flow:** Land → one-sentence Web4-vs-4-Life → "the 4 problems" (the why-web4 hero) → 3 essential concepts (energy / identity / reputation) without acronyms → "what could go wrong" surfaced → then offer mechanics, simulators, and acronyms. Save tensor math, decay coefficients, and "phase transitions" for the people who want to go deeper.

I would return — there's enough substance here that it's worth a second pass. But on a first naive read, I left with **partial** understanding, not the **good** or **solid** the structure seems to aim at. The gap is mostly experiential, not intellectual: I read about these systems but I didn't get to *feel* them.
