## Journey

- First impression: Clean, bold. "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" — yeah, I agree with that. Good hook.
- The four pillars (hardware-bound identity, metabolic attention, permanent consequences, AI coexistence) are intriguing but feel like buzzwords at this stage. I don't yet know what any of them *mean*.
- The recommended path (Why Web4 → First Contact → Playground) is helpful — I appreciate being told where to start.
- Confusion points: What is "4-Life" exactly? Is it a product? A research project? A game? The subtitle says "Fractal Web4 Societies" which doesn't clarify anything. I see "experimental lab" in the footer but I'm still not sure what I'm looking at.

### Why Web4 (01:30)
- This page is *significantly* better than the landing page. It starts with problems I actually recognize (spam, silo'd reputation, unlimited fresh starts, AI impersonation, platform tyranny).
- The "Previous Solutions That Failed" section is excellent — it builds credibility by acknowledging that CAPTCHAs, moderation, blockchain identity etc. have all been tried.
- Then it introduces four acronyms at once: ATP, LCT, T3, MRH. I can sort of follow along because each has a one-sentence explanation, but it's a lot of new terminology.
- The FAQ section is genuinely useful. The question "Is this just theoretical?" and the honest answer "yes, with working simulations" earns trust. The acknowledgment that Sybil attacks are "expensive, not impossible" feels honest.
- Confusion points: I still don't understand what "metabolic" means in this context. Why are they using biology words for internet infrastructure?

## Honest Assessment

The site has real intellectual substance. The problem diagnosis (spam, silo'd trust, unlimited fresh starts) is spot-on, and the proposed solutions are genuinely novel. The biological metaphor (metabolism, life, death, rebirth) is creative and mostly works once you spend time with it.

But there's a fundamental tension: the site is trying to be both an onboarding experience for curious newcomers AND a technical reference for people deep in the Web4 rabbit hole, and it's not quite succeeding at either.

**For newcomers**, the recommended path doesn't work well. "First Contact" is a meta-page that doesn't actually teach anything. The Playground throws parameters at you before explaining concepts. The best explanatory content ("How It Works," the comparison table on "Aliveness") is buried deep in pages you wouldn't naturally reach first.

**For experts**, the technical details are scattered across many pages rather than consolidated, and the "simplified for teaching" disclaimers undermine confidence in what's being shown.

**The biggest missing piece** is human motivation. The entire site explains *how* the system works mechanically, but never addresses *why I should care* in personal terms. There's no "A Day in Your Web4 Life" narrative. No concrete scenario showing me struggling with a real problem today and how Web4 would change that experience. The closest is the spam/bot problem statement on "Why Web4," but even that stays abstract.

The acronym density is also a real barrier. By the time I'd visited 5 pages, I was tracking ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, CI, MRH, EP, V3, and R6. That's nine new acronyms for a newcomer. Even technically savvy visitors will hit cognitive overload.

I'd come back if someone told me "check out the How It Works page specifically" — but if I'd bounced after the landing page and First Contact, I probably wouldn't have stuck around long enough to find the good stuff.
