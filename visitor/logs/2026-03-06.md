## Honest Assessment

This site has improved significantly in terms of onboarding clarity. The recommended path (Why Web4 -> First Contact -> Playground) works well, and the landing page does a good job of framing the problem before introducing the solution.

The strongest pages are **Why Web4**, **What Could Go Wrong**, **A Day in Web4**, and the **TL;DR**. These are concise, honest, and grounded. They explain things in human terms.

The weakest area is **acronym management**. By my count, the site introduces at least 9 significant acronyms (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, MRH, SAL, DID), most within the first few pages. For a site targeting people with "zero Web4 background," this is a lot to absorb. Each individual acronym makes sense when explained, but the cumulative effect is overwhelming.

The second issue is **page length**. Several core pages (Trust Tensor, How It Works, Aliveness, LCT Explainer) are essentially long-form articles. A visitor browsing casually will not read all of them. Progressive disclosure — showing the simple version first with "go deeper" options — would help enormously. Some pages do this (collapsible FAQs on Why Web4), but the concept explainers tend to dump everything at once.

The intellectual honesty is the site's greatest strength. The "What Could Go Wrong" page and the honest status notes ("designed but untested with real humans," "aspirational UX") build genuine credibility. This is rare and valuable.

Overall: I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left with a solid understanding of the core ideas and a genuine appreciation for the project's self-awareness. I'd return to explore the interactive tools (Playground, Society Simulator) if I had more time. I'd recommend it to someone interested in internet governance or trust systems, but probably not to a casual friend — it's still too dense for truly casual browsing.
