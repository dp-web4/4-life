## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean and confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the subtitle "Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" told me almost immediately what I was looking at and, helpfully, what it is *not*. The "no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" clause preempted my two biggest assumptions before I could form them. Good.
- The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" section ("Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos. Bad actors get unlimited fresh starts.") landed — these are problems I actually feel.
- The honesty box ("Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain… nothing is live on the real internet yet") set expectations well. I never once wondered "is this a thing I can sign up for?"
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" (the recommended path). The four-tier entry menu (TL;DR / What it looks like / Frustrations it'd fix / Structured path) is a genuinely good way to let different visitors self-sort.
- **Confusion points**: None serious at this stage. The interactive tools list (Society Simulator, Karma Journey, Trust Explorer, Sandbox) plus concept explainers is a lot of links, but they're below the fold and clearly secondary to the "start here" path.

### TL;DR (04:00)
- The one-sentence summary is excellent: "Web4 proposes a trust-based internet where actions cost energy, identity ties to your hardware, reputation follows you everywhere, and both humans and AI play by identical rules." That single sentence is the most keepable thing I read all session.
- **Friction**: The page (per its framing) leans on "only two acronyms you need," but in practice I met four — ATP, LCT, T3, CI — on this very page. They're each glossed, so it wasn't painful, but the "only two" promise slightly undersells what's coming. Minor.
- The biology origin of ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is mentioned. As a non-scientist this is a fun fact but momentarily made me wonder if I needed to know cell biology. I didn't — the page recovers quickly with "energy budget."
- Next-step menu (First Contact / Why Web4 / A Day in Web4 / Learning Journey) is clear.

### First Contact (09:00)
- This is the highlight. An interactive 5-act story where I drive Alice's choices and watch ATP and Trust respond. The promise that concepts are "explained in the story the moment Alice runs into it" is exactly the right pedagogy — I never felt lectured.
- The two tracked numbers (ATP starting at 100, Trust starting at 0.50 on a 0–1 scale) are anchored before they move. Good.
- **Friction**: LCT, T3, and CI are *named* in the "what you'll learn" framing before the story reaches them. As a naive reader I saw three undefined acronyms in a list and braced. This is a deliberate design choice (defer to the moment of need), and it mostly works, but the bracing reflex is real.

### Why Web4? (16:00)
- Strong problem framing: "The Internet Has a Trust Problem. You already know this. You feel it every day." The four problems (spam/bots, siloed reputation, unlimited fresh starts, AI making it worse) are concrete and resonant.
- The four-mechanism solution (ATP / LCT / T3 / MRH) maps cleanly onto the four problems.
- The FAQ is enormous (50+ questions). It IS skimmable — topic index, collapsed by default, each section stands alone — so length didn't hurt me. But I'd never *read* it; I'd only ever search it.
- **Friction (recurring-feeling)**: This page is where the acronym load peaks. In one stretch I hit ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, V3, CI, SAL. The page even self-deprecates ("math-flavored jargon you can ignore"), which is honest but doesn't actually reduce the load. A true first-timer hitting this page *before* First Contact would be underwater. The page order (TL;DR → First Contact → Why Web4) protects against this if followed, but the landing page also offers "Frustrations it'd fix" as a direct path here.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (23:00)
- Clear core idea: trust isn't one number, it's three dimensions (Talent / Training / Temperament) weighted differently per role. The Alice-across-three-roles example (Data Analyst 90%, Project Manager 76%, Mechanic 27%) made the role-weighting click instantly. Best concrete example on the site.
- The 0–1 scale is anchored well here: 0.5 = newcomer, 0.7 ≈ "70% cooperative behavior," 0.9 = exceptional. Calibrated-probability framing is satisfying.
- V3 (Value Tensor) distinction is crisp: "T3 grades Alice; V3 grades Alice's tutorial."
- **Friction**: The "Base = 0.02 × (quality − 0.5)" update formula appears without much motivation — I don't know *why* 0.02, and as a beginner I don't need the formula at all. It reads as a detail that leaked up from a spec. The measurement mechanism ("recipient attestation") is named but I couldn't picture how a recipient actually submits dimension scores.

### LCT Explainer (30:00)
- Thorough and, for the most part, well-sequenced. "A digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip… a presence your hardware proves" is a good plain-English anchor. The 0–1 scale and the 0.5 alive/dead line are stated *before* the ceiling numbers (TPM 0.90 / Secure Enclave 0.85 / FIDO2 0.75 / Software 0.50). The "no hardware is unbreakable" candor about why the ceiling isn't 1.0 builds trust.
- Recovery story (multi-device = minutes, single-device or total-loss = 3–7 day social recovery, "deliberately slow… easy recovery would mean easy identity theft") is reassuring and well-motivated.
- **Friction**:
  - The "alive/dead line at 0.5" is repeated but I never learned what *concretely* happens below it — can't post? can't vote? both? It's an abstraction without a consequence I can picture.
  - The device-witness numbers ("1 device 50%, 2 devices 75%, 3+ up to 90%") sit near a widget showing both "Presence Trust Score: 0.55" and "Anomaly Detection: 30%." I couldn't tell if these are the same metric or two different ones. Genuinely confusing.
  - "Witness" is overloaded: my first device is "witnessed" by its manufacturer key, but elsewhere "witness" means *other devices* co-signing. Two different things, one word.

### ATP Economics (36:00)
- The ATP/ADP relationship is finally clear here: ATP = available energy, ADP = the "spent energy" receipt ("a record of an action, not a second currency you can spend"). The "60-second version" box is excellent and exactly what I'd want to keep.
- 5% transfer fee (sender pays, fee → community pool to deter collusion farming) and the ~7x quality ramp (≤30% earns ~zero, ≥70% earns ~full) are both well-explained with examples.
- **Friction (recurring + verified)**: The page body consistently says "energy budget" — but the **breadcrumb / route label reads "Home / Core Concepts / Attention Economics."** So the page that goes to lengths to call ATP an *energy* budget is itself filed under *Attention* Economics. A naive visitor who notices the breadcrumb (or the URL/route metadata) gets a contradictory signal about whether ATP is about energy or attention. This is the exact "ATP glossed multiple ways" drift that the body text has otherwise stamped out — it survived in the navigation/breadcrumb layer. Single highest-leverage small fix this session.

### Aliveness (41:00)
- The three-condition definition of "alive" (ATP > 0, Trust > 0.5, CI coherent) with the doctor-license analogy (overhead + patient trust + clean record) is a strong, memorable frame.
- The energy-death vs trust-death distinction is the key payoff and it's clear: energy death = recoverable (like bankruptcy), trust death = permanent for that LCT (like losing a license). This directly answered the "is death permanent?" question I'd been carrying since First Contact.
- Karma ("good carries forward; harm carries forward too… just consequences that don't reset") and cross-life patterns ("a rule like 'when ATP runs low, act conservatively'") are well-distinguished — patterns are *learned behavior*, not a number.
- **Friction**: CI's four dimensions (spatial, capability, temporal, relational) stay abstract — I'd want one concrete example of what *violates* each. Synthons (group aliveness) arrive late and thin. The appeals mechanism is flagged as "designed, not deployed," which is honest but left me slightly uneasy about fairness in practice.

### Karma Journey (44:00)
- Interactive multi-life simulation. The three numbers (raw Trust, ATP, CI) are reintroduced cleanly. CI starting at 0.85 → "actions cost 40% more" until consistency normalizes it is a nice tangible hook.
- **CI² is explained right where it appears**: "Effective trust = raw × CI² — sets karma tier at death," immediately followed by "Squaring makes inconsistency compound… 0.9² = 0.81 barely shows; 0.6² = 0.36 nearly halves you." This is a genuine improvement — the formula no longer floats without interpretation. The raw-vs-effective split (raw → survival, effective → next-life inheritance) is explained, though it needs careful reading.
- **Friction**: The turn interface shows seven action options but no walkthrough of how a sequence of turns actually plays out. As a first-timer I wasn't sure what a "good" first move was.

### A Day in Web4 (47:00)
- Ten present-tense daily scenarios. The framing is impeccably consistent: "Everything below is narrated in the present tense as a thought experiment — none of it ships today." I never once mistook it for a shipped product. The 0–1 scale is anchored up front (0.5 neutral / 0.85 well-trusted), and scenarios link out to concept pages (LCT, ATP). Exemplary.
- **Friction**: The ten scenarios aren't listed upfront — you discover them only by clicking "Next scenario." I'd have liked an overview of the day before walking through it.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **What concretely happens below the 0.5 alive/dead line?** Every page invokes it; none told me which specific abilities I lose. ("Can no longer act" is too abstract.)
2. **On the LCT page, are "Presence Trust Score" and "Anomaly Detection" the same number or two different ones?** The widget shows both and I couldn't reconcile them with the "1/2/3 devices → 50/75/90%" ladder.
3. **How does a recipient actually *submit* a T3 dimension score?** "Recipient attestation" is named but I can't picture the act — do they rate talent/training/temperament separately? Is it a button, a vote, automatic?
4. **In small communities, how are "diverse confirmations" measured?** The ATP recharge depends on diverse confirmers, but a brand-new society has few people — does recharge just stall?

## Honest Assessment
This is, again, an unusually well-made educational site. The guided path (TL;DR → First Contact → Why Web4 → concept pages) is genuinely effective: by the time I reached Aliveness, the energy-death-vs-trust-death distinction *clicked* because First Contact had already let me feel Alice spend, stumble, and recover. The single most valuable thing I took away was the TL;DR's one-sentence definition; the second was First Contact's "drive Alice and watch the numbers move."

The site has clearly absorbed a lot of past feedback — present-tense thought-experiment framing is airtight on "A Day in Web4," the 0–1 scale is anchored before numbers on most pages, ATP/ADP is finally crisp, and CI² is now explained right where it appears. I came in cold and left with solid understanding and no sense of being misled about what's real.

The remaining friction is almost entirely at the seams. The one that bugged me most as a careful reader was the **"Attention Economics" breadcrumb on a page whose entire body insists ATP is an *energy* budget** — the kind of small contradiction that makes a thoughtful visitor wonder which they should believe. The acronym pile on Why Web4 is survivable *if* you arrive in order, but that page is also reachable directly from the landing menu, so order isn't guaranteed. And the 0.5 threshold — invoked everywhere as the line between life and death — is never cashed out into a concrete "here's what you can't do anymore." That's the gap I'd close first if I were teaching this to a friend.

Would I return? Yes — I'd come back to actually play the Society Simulator and Karma Journey rather than just read about them. Would I recommend it? Yes, with the note: "start at the TL;DR and do First Contact before anything else."
