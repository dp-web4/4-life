## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Confident, calm headline. "A Trust-Native Internet Lab" — I don't really know what "trust-native" means yet, but the immediate sub-line "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default" gives me a foothold.
- The most reassuring sentence on the whole site was here: *"Web4 is a research proposal for making trust work on the internet. Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain."* The "not a blockchain" hit hardest — I assumed it was one.
- The "Internet Has a Trust Problem" framing (spam, siloed reputations, fresh-start bad actors, AI deception) is concrete and felt true.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — the largest CTA. I noticed the navigation also offered "Core Concepts" with 5 subtopics and "Interactive Labs" with 5 sims. I ignored those for now to follow the recommended path.
- **Confusion points**: Five named mechanics (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) flash by very quickly. I don't yet know which I'm supposed to remember.

### /tldr (02:30)
- The opening line — "every online action had a cost, every identity is tied to physical hardware, and your reputation followed you everywhere — permanently" — gave me a clear mental picture in ~6 seconds. Good.
- Three mechanisms (energy/identity/reputation) reinforced what I half-remembered from the landing page. The repetition helped.
- **Friction**: For a page promising "2 minutes," I still saw the acronyms LCT, MRH, CI fly past without being unpacked. The page then said MRH and CI are "outside scope" of the TL;DR. That's honest, but it made me feel like I'd been handed half a map.
- **Clicked**: "Follow Alice through her first 16 actions" — that's a story. I want a story.

### /first-contact (06:00)
- The pitch is good: 7 minutes, 5 acts, you control Alice. The act titles are punchy and tell a tiny narrative arc on their own:
  1. Join — Equal start for everyone
  2. Build — Quality earns trust back
  3. Stumble — Spam costs more than it pays
  4. Recover — Consistency rebuilds
  5. Die & Reborn — Karma carries forward
- I got a definition of T3 here that the TL;DR hadn't given me cleanly: **talent, training, temperament**. I had been wondering what the "3" stood for, so this felt like a quiet aha.
- **Friction**: The actual walkthrough is hidden behind a button I'd have to click through. As a naive visitor I'm now ~6 minutes in and I still haven't seen the *thing* the site is showing me. (Note: I'm a fetcher and can't easily execute the interactive simulation. A human visitor would probably push through. But it does mean the explainer pages have to carry most of the comprehension load.)
- **Decision**: rather than wade into the sim, I went sideways to read about ATP, which Act 2 implies is the core mechanic.

### /atp-economics (08:30)
- **This page was the best one so far.** Borrowing from biology — "your cells spend [ATP] to do work — they burn ATP, get ADP back, and recharge ATP from food" — was the first metaphor that made me *feel* I understood why "energy" applies to internet behavior.
- Concrete numbers helped a lot: 100 ATP start; spam costs 5 and earns 0; meaningful contribution costs 15 and earns 25 back. The 7× advantage of high-quality work over spam is the kind of fact I'd remember in a week.
- **Confusion that lingered**: *When* does the "earn back" happen? Who decides what's quality? "Recipients confirm" — but how, mechanically? In the real world a spammer's recipients are themselves spammers. The page describes shape but not timing or adjudication.

### /lct-explainer (12:00)
- "Digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip" + "you probably already own this hardware" was an excellent one-two. I was bracing for "you'll need a $200 dongle" and instead got "your iPhone already has this." That defused real anxiety.
- The driver's-license-that-can't-be-photocopied analogy worked.
- **Friction**: The page shifts gears abruptly. One moment it's accessible analogies; a few paragraphs later it's TPM attestation, certificate chains, and proof batching. As a naive reader I lose the thread when the prose stops explaining and starts citing. (This is the page I'd most want a "Show me less / Show me more" toggle on.)
- The interactive slider (1–5 witnessing devices showing trust score change) is the kind of thing I want more of — small, fast feedback. Trust at 0.55 with one device vs. higher with many: that grounded the abstraction in one click.

### /trust-tensor (16:00)
- I clicked this nervously because "tensor" is a word that has scared me away from ML pages before. The page literally has a heading "Why 'tensor'?" and reassures: *"You don't need the math."* That was disarming.
- "One number couldn't tell a brilliant-but-erratic surgeon from a kind doctor with shaky hands. Three numbers can." — this is the kind of one-liner I'd quote to a friend.
- **Friction**: Decay mechanics (365 / 180 / 30 day half-lives) and formulas like `base = 0.02 × (quality − 0.5)` appear without grounding. The page has earned my trust by this point so I forgive it, but I'd want the formulas tucked behind a "Show me how the score updates" disclosure.

### /why-web4 (20:00)
- Five reinforcing problems: spam economics, reputation silos, disposable identity, AI deception, platform control. Each rings true. The "you already know this; you feel it every day" framing earned a small wince from me.
- I was impressed that the page **considers and rejects alternatives** rather than pretending Web4 is the only idea: moderation armies, CAPTCHAs (AI solves them now), blockchain wallets (no behavior history), social login (central control).
- The page is also honest about limitations: hardware cost as a barrier, bootstrapping is hard, "whether the economics survive contact with real human behavior is an open question," and the risk that communities could define "low quality" as "ideologically disagreeable."
- **Friction**: The "you feel it" rhetoric doesn't actually cite anything. No numbers on spam cost, AI-generated fraud, account recovery friction. The page is emotionally convincing but empirically thin — it persuades by appeal, not by data.
- I also wanted (and didn't find) a serious engagement with: does role-scoped trust really prevent reputational damage from spreading? If Alice is a great surgeon but cheats at poker, can Web4 actually keep those two separate?

### /aliveness (25:00)
- The three criteria — ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, coherent Coherence Index — give "aliveness" a crisp definition. The professional-license metaphor (resources + record + consistent behavior) was apt.
- **Friction (genuine)**: I had to read the page twice to be sure it wasn't claiming the agents are *literally* alive. The disclaimer ("death = temporary loss of participation rights") is there, but the framing — "Die & Reborn," "agents live and die" — is dramatic enough that I needed to hunt for the deflation. A clearer hierarchy of "what we mean by alive" vs. "what we don't mean" would help.

### /society-simulator (28:00)
- This is the page I most wished I could actually run. As a fetched page it's mostly setup text describing what the sim does.
- The page is **commendably honest** about what's simplified: "Trust is a scalar value (not the full T3 tensor)," "only 12 agents," "no full witness layer," "inside the sim, every agent both acts and observes" — i.e. it's a behavioral demo, not a real Web4 deployment.
- **Friction (mild)**: That distinction (demo vs. real protocol math) could easily be missed. I almost missed it. A small "DEMO" badge or one-sentence disclaimer at the top would help.

### /what-could-go-wrong (31:00)
- **This page surprised me, in a good way.** The opening — "Hiding them doesn't make them go away — it just means you discover them at the worst time" — is the kind of sentence that makes me trust the rest of the site retroactively.
- Categorized risks: existential ("nobody adopts it" is the #1 risk), serious-but-unresolved (witness network corruption, hardware vendor capture), and policy choices (accessibility exclusion). The author distinguishes design tensions from partial mitigations from unvalidated mechanisms. That's grown-up writing.
- This is also the page that made the rest of the site feel intellectually honest in retrospect. Without it, the optimistic tone elsewhere would have read as sales-y. With it, the optimism reads as "we know what we don't know."

### /glossary (33:30)
- I came here because MRH and CI had been mentioned several times and I still couldn't have explained them.
- Got useful: MRH (Trust Neighborhood, "trust decays 30% per hop"), CI (Coherence Index, "behavioral consistency across time, space, capability, relationships"), SAL (Society + Authorities + Laws), ADP (the "discharged" form of ATP).
- **Friction (real)**: The glossary explains ATP by introducing ADP, VCM, and "recipient attestation." VCM is mentioned but never defined as its own entry. So I'd go to the glossary to resolve an unfamiliar term, and end up with a *new* unfamiliar term. That's circularity, and it breaks the promise of a glossary.

## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:

- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a research proposal for making internet behavior costly to fake and reputation portable, anchored in device hardware
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — the social dynamics that would emerge if the above were real
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token, your hardware-bound identity that lives in a TPM-style chip
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy you spend to act; recharge by being attested to as having produced quality
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — talent, training, temperament; role-scoped; multi-dimensional so one bad axis doesn't tank you everywhere
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive = ATP>0 AND T3>0.5 AND coherent; death = temporary loss of participation rights
- [x] Why any of this matters — current internet trust mechanisms (moderation, CAPTCHAs, wallets, social login) all fail under AI scale; Web4 is one attempt at an alternative

What I **don't** feel I understand:
- How the "recipient confirms quality" loop actually closes without becoming a popularity contest or a circular spammer ring
- Whether MRH (the "neighborhood" idea) is a graph that I navigate, or just a label on me
- What VCM actually is — defined nowhere I looked
- Whether the simulator data is encouraging because the system would actually work, or because the sim is calibrated to make it work

## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who pays for the witness network?** If witnessing is what makes attestations trustworthy, someone has to run witnesses, and witnesses presumably need ATP too. Where does the bootstrap energy come from?
2. **How does Web4 handle malicious *quality* signals?** If a clique of users all attest to each other's spam as high-quality, do they print ATP for each other?
3. **What is VCM?** It appeared in the glossary's ATP definition but never got its own entry.
4. **What stops a state actor from buying a million phones, generating a million LCTs, and farming reputation industrially?** Hardware cost is a barrier; "a million phones" is cheap for a state.
5. **What's the relationship between MRH ("neighborhood") and the witness network?** They sound related but were presented as separate things.
6. **If I lose my phone and have no witnessing devices, can I recover my identity?** /what-could-go-wrong says this is an unsolved problem — does that mean I'd lose my entire reputation permanently?
7. **Is anyone actually running this?** "Active research project, simulations not deployment" is clear, but it leaves me wondering whether Web4 is 6 months from a pilot or 10 years from a pilot. The site doesn't give me a roadmap.

## Honest Assessment

The site is good. Better than I expected. The structure — landing → TL;DR → choose your concept → optional simulators → honest-failures page → glossary — is a coherent curriculum, not just a pile of pages. Several individual passages are genuinely well-written: the biology metaphor for ATP, the "brilliant-but-erratic surgeon" line on trust tensors, the "you discover them at the worst time" opening of what-could-go-wrong.

The site's biggest strength is **intellectual honesty**. It says "not a product, not a blockchain." It rejects four alternative solutions before proposing its own. It lists existential risks. It distinguishes simulation from deployment. After a few pages I started trusting the prose, which is exactly the trick a site about *trust* most needs to pull off.

The site's biggest weakness is **the moment a curious-but-non-technical reader hits a concept that requires the next concept to make sense.** ATP requires "recipient attestation" to work. Recipient attestation requires understanding witnesses. Witnesses require MRH. MRH decays per hop, which is fine, but I'm now four concepts deep from "energy is good" and I've lost the thread. The glossary should be the place this resolves, but the glossary itself is circular. **Fixing the glossary's circularity is probably the single highest-leverage UX change on the whole site.**

Second-biggest weakness: the difference between "things that are real Web4 protocol behavior" and "things that are this-particular-demo's behavior" is genuinely hard to track. /society-simulator is honest about it but I had to read carefully. I worry that a less attentive visitor would walk away believing the 12-agent demo *is* Web4 and judge accordingly.

I came in cold and left with a real mental model of energy-based participation, hardware-bound identity, role-scoped trust, and graceful aliveness criteria. I also left with concrete suspicion about whether quality attestation actually closes the loop in practice — which I think is the right thing for a research site to leave me with. I am skeptical, but I am skeptical of *the right things*, which is better than being skeptical of everything.

If I were the dev team, my one-line prioritization would be: **fix the glossary first**, then **answer the "who decides what's quality, and when" question on /atp-economics**, then **add small "DEMO vs. PROTOCOL" badges on the simulator pages**. The site is closer to being onboarding-complete than to needing a redesign.
