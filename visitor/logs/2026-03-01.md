### First Contact (07:00)

- The ATP cost/reward asymmetry clicked: quality post costs 8, earns 12+. Spam costs 25, earns nothing. The economics enforce quality without moderation.
- Self-regulation emerged as the key insight: "The system regulates itself through economics."

### Karma Journey (11:00)

- Choice architecture is well designed. Cooperative actions cost more ATP but build trust slowly. Selfish actions give quick ATP but erode trust. Risky actions are unpredictable. This teaches the tradeoffs intuitively.
- The reincarnation mechanic is clever — dying with good karma gives you a better next life. This makes permanent consequences feel less punitive and more like compound interest.
- I got the message: short-term exploitation is possible but long-term cooperation wins.

### Trust Tensor (32:00)
- T3's three dimensions (Talent/Training/Temperament) with different weights (40/30/30) is more nuanced than any reputation system I've seen. The surgeon example (brilliant but unreliable ≠ average) made the case for multi-dimensionality.
- Role-specificity is the killer feature: Alice's trust as data analyst (0.85 Talent) is completely separate from her trust as mechanic (0.20 Talent). Trust doesn't leak across domains.
- V3 (Value Tensor) for outputs — Valuation/Veracity/Validity — is the complement. Truth and rigor (0.70 combined) outweigh popularity (0.30). This is a values statement encoded in math.
- Different decay rates per dimension (Talent: 365d, Training: 180d, Temperament: 30d) — recent behavior matters most for consistency, skills persist longer. Smart.

### Coherence Index (34:00)
- Four dimensions of consistency: Spatial, Capability, Temporal, Relational. Each catches different attack patterns.
- The multiplicative (not additive) combination is the key insight — one weak dimension tanks the whole score. This forces attackers to maintain coherence across ALL dimensions simultaneously.
- The Maria's stolen phone example is effective: even same-city theft triggers temporal continuity breaks and behavioral mismatches.
- The economic consequence (up to 10x ATP cost increase) means incoherent behavior becomes economically unsustainable before damage occurs. Prevention through economics, not punishment.

## Unanswered Questions
1. What does "EP" stand for? It appears on the Compare page and Lab Console but is never explicitly defined for newcomers. (I found "Cross-Life Learning (EP)" in the glossary, but "EP" itself is unexpanded — what does the abbreviation mean?)
2. How does Web4 handle the cold-start problem for brand new communities with zero trust history? The bootstrap convergence is mentioned but I'd like to see a concrete walkthrough of a community's first 100 interactions.
3. What's the actual user experience of "hardware-bound identity" on a practical level? Do I need a special device, or does my existing phone/laptop work? The LCT page mentions TPM and Secure Enclave chips that are already in devices, but the practical onboarding flow (what buttons do I push?) is unclear.
4. How would Web4 handle communities with fundamentally different values? The federation economics covers economic coordination, but what about a community that considers something ethical that another considers harmful?

## Honest Assessment

This is the most intellectually honest research project website I've browsed. The site practices what it preaches — it builds trust through transparency, not hype. Every page that claims something bold also acknowledges what's unproven.

**What worked brilliantly:**
- The problem-before-solution structure throughout. Every concept page starts with a recognizable pain point before introducing the mechanism. I never felt lectured at.
- Progressive disclosure is well-executed. Landing page → Why Web4 → First Contact → Concept pages is a natural escalation. Each layer adds depth without requiring the previous one to have been exhaustive.
- The interactive elements (Karma Journey, Playground, Society Simulator) transform abstract economics into felt experience. I *understood* ATP costs because I watched my budget deplete, not because I read a formula.
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page is the strongest credibility signal. Publishing your own failure analysis is rare and powerful.

**What could improve:**
- The Compare page and Lab Console use internal jargon ("EP Closed Loop," "Five Domain EP") that wasn't introduced in the main learning path. A visitor following the recommended route encounters these labels without context.
- The Narratives page being empty is a missed opportunity. Even one pre-generated example story would show visitors what the narrative engine produces and motivate them to try the simulator.
- The site has ~77 pages, which is impressive but potentially overwhelming. The Explore Guide's quiz helps, but I wonder if some pages could be consolidated. Do I need both "How It Works" and "First Contact"? They cover similar ground at slightly different depths.

**Overall:** I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left with a solid understanding of why it matters, how it works, and what's still unproven. The site taught me — I didn't have to infer. That's high praise for any educational resource. Would definitely return to try the Society Simulator's "Play as Agent" mode and explore the Playground experiments more deeply.
