## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
First impression: clean, dark theme, confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" lands, but I had to read it twice — "trust-native" is doing a lot of work for a phrase I've never seen. The subheadline "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" anchored me — okay, this is about something I recognize.

Then I scrolled and got hit with five acronyms at once: **LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI**. They each had a plain-English line next to them, which helped, but my immediate reaction was "this is a lot." I appreciated that the very next thing was a giant **"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →"** button. Genuinely felt like the site was reading my mind ("I don't know where to start"). Clicked that.

Confusion points:
- "Trust-native" — what does that mean concretely? (Got resolved later but cost a beat upfront.)
- Five acronyms in card format felt like flashcards before the lesson. I would've preferred to meet ATP and LCT first, then T3/MRH/CI later.

### TL;DR (`/tldr`) (02:30)
Good. The Problem → Idea → Result → Status structure is exactly what I needed. I now grasp:
- The internet has zero-cost identity reset → spam thrives
- Web4 wants actions to cost a budget that refills via community validation
- Identity hardware-anchored, reputation portable

Three new acronyms (ATP/ADP/LCT) introduced with full names in parentheses. Three more (MRH/CI/T3) **mentioned but not explained on this page** — I noticed the asymmetry. Why drop "T3" without saying it's the trust score?

The page told me to go to "First Contact" next. Fine — I followed.

### First Contact (06:00)
The "Alice five-act story" framing is strong. I love that there's a stated learning goal ("ATP, T3, LCT, CI in one walkthrough"). However, the page **describes** the simulation in prose more than it **shows** it. I read about a 5-act arc with sliders but I don't see the sliders until I click "Start the 5-act Walkthrough." I would have clicked sooner if the first act had been visible inline ("press Play to begin Act 1: Alice arrives").

Concept reinforcement on this page is good — ATP, T3, LCT, CI all introduced again with one-liners. By page 3 these acronyms are starting to feel familiar.

### Why Web4? (12:00)
Long page. Maybe **too** long. The "4 problems → 4 solutions" structure is excellent, and the "what previous solutions get right / what they fail at" treatment is the strongest persuasive argument on the site. The "all four mechanisms in one moment" forum-reply scenario was the moment Web4 finally clicked as a thing that could exist in user-space, not just whitepaper-space.

But then **40+ FAQs** under collapsibles. As a naive visitor I appreciate the depth being there but I started bouncing past sections. The page advertises "~5 min problems + ~2 min solutions + optional Q&A" — the first 7 minutes pay off; the last 30 are research-grade reading.

Strongest move: admitting "this is research, not vaporware." Earned my trust.
Weakest move: the page ends without a clear "now go do this" CTA. I had to go back to the nav.

### Karma Journey (22:00)
The hook ("Live multiple lives. Make choices.") is great. Then... I scrolled looking for the actual game and found:
- Stat cards (Trust 0.500, Energy 100, Consistency 0.85, Effective Trust 0.361)
- Eight text buttons (Help peer / Take shortcut / etc.)
- A "history" log

No animation, no visual reaction when I imagine clicking, no character art, no scene. For something called a "journey," it feels like a spreadsheet with verbs. I'd play 2-3 turns and want to see the **society** my choices affected. The "Watch your strategy play out in a multi-agent society" link to the Society Simulator was the right pull.

Also, "Effective Trust: 0.361" is shown without me ever earning the framework to read it. I get raw=0.5, consistency=0.85, but the effective number drops by 28% and I don't viscerally know why until I read the squaring explanation — which is a paragraph I'd have to click into.

### Society Simulator (28:00)
Better. Hit "Run Society" and you get a coalitional narrative. The acronym section is collapsed by default — for me that was right; I'd picked them up by now. For someone landing here directly from Google, the acronyms appearing inline (ATP, T3, etc.) before the toggle expansion would feel like a wall.

The strongest part: the simulator promises **a story** (character arcs, key moments) at the end, not just numbers. That's the right trade for a sim that runs ~30 seconds.

### LCT Explainer (32:00)
Strong analogies: "driver's license that can't be faked," "co-signers on a legal document." I felt I understood the **idea** by paragraph two.

But — the technical layer remains fuzzy. Words I encountered without firm grounding:
- "Manufacturer-burned key"

- "Endorsement Key (EK)"

The interactive slider ("Number of devices witnessing your LCT") was the highlight — I could feel the trust score shift as I added devices.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (37:00)
"Alice as data analyst vs. Alice as mechanic" finally made T3 click. Different roles weight Talent / Training / Temperament differently. The interactive role-picker (Surgeon / Data Analyst / Team Leader) sealed it.

Then **V3** appears mid-page — Valuation / Veracity / Validity. Wait, why do I need a second 3-letter trio with three V-words? At this point I'm ~35 minutes in, and the cognitive load of tracking T3 + V3 simultaneously (six dimensions, two roles each affects) felt like a steep ramp.

### ATP Economics (40:00)
The biology metaphor (cellular ATP→ADP) is clever and I appreciate it being called out as **a metaphor, not a literal currency**. Good quality-ramp explanation: <30% earns nothing, >70% earns full payment.

Honest disclosure: "the reference protocol is still settling" — I respect this but as a beginner it makes me wonder which numbers I just absorbed are load-bearing vs. provisional. (Same energy as a textbook saying "this chapter may change.")

Still vague to me: ADP. The page calls it a "receipt." Receipts are evidence; what's the receipt **for** mechanically? Does it loop back into anything I'd see as a user, or is it ledger plumbing I don't need to think about?

### Aliveness (42:00)
This page nailed it. Three concrete thresholds for "alive":
1. ATP > 0
2. Trust > 0.5
3. CI maintained

The **energy death (recoverable) vs. trust death (permanent)** distinction is one of the best two-sentence ideas on the site. I left this page understanding the death/rebirth metaphor in a way that earlier mentions had not given me.

### Glossary (43:30)
Good filtering ("Essential only" → 7 terms). 40+ terms total. Definitions read clearly: term, plain English, deeper context. I noticed it explicitly says "if a term doesn't make sense yet, that's normal" — this is the right tone for a glossary on a research site. Felt like a safety net.

### Day in Web4 (45:00)
Five interactive mockups (email, hiring, reviews, social, marketplace) — this is what I'd been waiting for. The simulated "what does this look like as a real user" is the answer to the question that's been nagging me: **does Web4 ever look like a thing I'd open on my phone?**

Honest framing: "These are interactive concept demos, not final designs" + the 5-tier adoption path (Wrapper → Native) gave me realistic expectations.

### What Could Go Wrong (47:00)
Last page. I almost didn't click — figured it'd be defensive. It wasn't. Eight failure modes including "nobody adopts," "witness capture," "false positive penalties," "accessibility gap." This is the page that earned the most trust from me.

The candor is structural, not performative — they list problems with severity ratings and admit the witness-surveillance tension is "fundamental and unresolved." If a research site can say that on its own front-facing page, I trust the rest of the claims more.

## Unanswered Questions

1. **Witness network bootstrap.** Why Web4 admits the bootstrap path is "circular" — initial witnesses come from existing identity providers, but those have the very centralization problem Web4 is solving. What does the bootstrap actually look like in practice today? Is the simulator using a fake witness network, or is there a real one?

2. **Initial ATP minting.** Who creates the first ATP pool a new agent gets? A community treasury? A genesis allocation per agent? The site repeatedly says "ATP isn't backed by crypto" but doesn't quite say what backs the issuance.

3. **What does ADP do, mechanically?** It's "a receipt" — does it stay with me forever? Does it ever get garbage-collected? Does anyone other than me read it?

4. **Pseudonymity tension.** Why Web4 says my reputation is portable across platforms but also pseudonymous. If my reputation follows my LCT, and my LCT is hardware-bound, then anyone who fingerprints my hardware can de-anonymize me. The site partially acknowledges this. How is the tension resolved in practice — software fallbacks? Per-context LCTs? Something else?

5. **Adoption story.** "Day in Web4" mocks up email/hiring/reviews/social/marketplace as if they're Web4-native. What does the *transitional* state look like — when 1% of users have an LCT and 99% don't? Do non-LCT users see something different on a Web4-aware site, or is it invisible to them?

## Honest Assessment

The site is genuinely good. By the end of 45 minutes I felt **oriented, not overwhelmed**, which is high praise for a research project with this much surface area.

The strongest assets:
- The TL;DR → First Contact → Why Web4 progression is a real path. Each page assumes I just came from the previous one and don't have to context-switch.
- "What Could Go Wrong" is the most credibility-earning page on the site. Putting it in the main nav rather than burying it as a footer link signals confidence.
- Aliveness's three-threshold framing is the cleanest one-paragraph mental model on the site.
- The interactive elements (LCT slider, T3 role picker, ATP simulator, Society Simulator) consistently outperform the prose. When the site stops telling me and shows me, I learn faster.

The weakest moments:
- **Acronym density** is the recurring drag. Each individual acronym is well-explained at first encounter; the cumulative load across pages still hits hard. By Trust Tensor I was holding 8+ in working memory.
- **Karma Journey** under-delivers on its name. As the *interactive* page right after First Contact in the visitor journey, it has the best opportunity for an emotional hook, and instead reads like a form.
- **Why Web4 length.** I scrolled past most of the FAQ even though I was curious. A page that long competes with itself.

One specific thing I'd flag: as a naive visitor I'd benefit from knowing earlier that **agents = humans + AI under the same rules**. The landing mentions it; First Contact uses Alice (human-coded) so I forgot. When I got to T3 and saw "the same tensor scores apply to AI agents" I had to recalibrate. If "humans and AI play by the same rules" is a core thesis (and it seems to be), it deserves a more persistent presence in the visitor journey, not just one mention upfront.

Net: I would come back. I'd want to actually run the Society Simulator and the Sandbox. I'd recommend it to a friend who's already curious about decentralized identity or AI trust — not to a friend without that pre-existing curiosity, because the on-ramp is still an investment of attention. A "trust-curious general public" version of this site would need to lose ~half the acronyms and double the visualizations.
