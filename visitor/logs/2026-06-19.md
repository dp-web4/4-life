## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clear and honest. "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations." I immediately liked that it tells me what it *isn't*: "Right now, you can't install it" and "Nothing here is 'live' on the real internet yet." That candor lowered my defenses.
- The "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" banner gave me an instant reason to care — spam, siloed reputation, endless fresh starts. I understood the pitch before any jargon hit me.
- Clicked: "2-minute TL;DR" — the obvious first door. There were a lot of options (TL;DR, "What it looks like", learning path, quiz, three interactive tools, deep dives), but the TL;DR clearly read as *the* primary entry, so I didn't feel torn about where to start.

### TL;DR (02:30)
- "Web4 in 2 Minutes" — promises plain English, "defining every term on first use."
- The four pillars (energy costs, device-anchored identity, portable reputation, universal rules for humans + AI) were genuinely clear. "Creating a fake identity means buying new hardware — expensive and slow" made the idea click instantly.
- **First friction**: the page promises to define every term on first use, but T3, MRH, CI, and "witness constellation" appear with only quick glosses. The promise sets an expectation it doesn't quite meet. Minor, but I noticed.
- Clicked: "First Contact →" (recommended for narrative learners).

### First Contact (06:00)
- Reassuring framing: "You don't need to [know Web4] — this page starts from zero." The Alice five-act story (join → build → spam penalty → recover → consequences carry forward) is a smart way to teach by consequence.
- It hands me four acronyms at once (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) in a reference map, but promises each is "explained in the story the moment Alice uses it" — which I'm willing to trust.
- The "death" spoiler was momentarily alarming until I gathered it means suspended standing, not deletion. Fine.

### Why Web4 (11:00)
- The five-problem diagnosis (spam is free, reputation is siloed, fresh starts, AI deception, platforms own everything) is the most convincing part of the whole site. "you're a guest in someone else's house, always" landed.
- It fairly critiques prior fixes (CAPTCHAs, Web3, OAuth) instead of strawmanning them.
- **Friction**: the "What Would Actually Work" solution section is abstract — four mechanism cards (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) with little concrete grounding, right after a vivid problem section. The energy drops. The page even admits "acronyms land much easier after [First Contact]," which is honest but means a linear reader hits abstraction unprepared.
- The "Honest Questions" FAQ is *huge* (~80 questions). Impressive but I skimmed/bounced — it reads as something to return to, not read through.

### Karma Journey (18:00)
- Interactive "play multiple lives" sandbox. Good hook: consequences persist across rebirths.
- **The death distinction was clear** — this is a highlight. Energy death (ATP runs out, raw trust ≥ 0.5) = recoverable suspension; Trust collapse (raw trust < 0.5) = permanent, "the one door that doesn't reopen." And crucially the page tells me *why* the number alone is confusing and resolves it: "Both crossings involve 0.5, so the number alone can't tell them apart — which metric failed does." That sentence did exactly the work I needed.
- **Friction (the day's biggest)**: the raw-trust-vs-effective-trust split plus CI² is a real cognitive load. Survival keys off raw trust, but karma inheritance keys off *effective* trust (raw × CI²). And a new agent starts at CI 0.85, so effective trust is ~0.36 even at raw 0.5 — while the "Honored" tier wants effective trust > 0.7. As a newcomer that gap felt daunting and the *why squared?* question lingered. Logically sound, but I had to re-read it twice and still wasn't fully sure I'd retained it.

### ATP Economics (26:00)
- The energy budget model is clear, and the Sam-vs-Hannah worked example for the 30%/70% quality ramp (~7x earnings difference) is excellent scaffolding.
- **ADP check**: the page now leads ADP with *why I should care* — "Here's why it matters to you even though you never touch it: when others confirm your work was valuable, that confirmation is what unlocks fresh ATP back into your budget." That's the right order (relevance before mechanics) and I understood it. One tiny nit: "even though you never touch it" is slightly defensive — it almost plants the doubt it's trying to dispel. But this was *not* a blocker this time; the why-first framing worked.

### Trust Tensor (30:00)
- T3 = Talent / Training / Temperament, always within a role. The surgeon-vs-mechanic example sells the "trust isn't one number" idea well.
- **Friction**: V3 is introduced *inside* the T3 page to explain how T3 moves (the `0.02 × (quality − 0.5)` formula needs "quality," which is V3). That's a small circular dependency — I needed a concept not yet taught to understand the one in front of me.

### Society Simulator (33:00)
- Clear CTA ("Run Society"), good "What to Watch For" phase breakdown (meet → coalitions → isolate defectors → equilibrium).
- **Friction**: ATP/T3/LCT/CI appear immediately with no inline explanation (there's a link to First Contact, which helps). Also the "what's real vs. simulated" section is long and clearly written for skeptical/technical readers — as a naive visitor I started skimming it; it interrupts the "just run the thing" momentum.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a trust-by-behavior internet model, no blockchain
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — simulations of those trust dynamics
- [x] What LCT means — device/hardware-anchored identity (got the gist; least hands-on of the concepts)
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget for actions; ADP = the receipt others confirm to recharge you
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — 3D trust (Talent/Training/Temperament) per role
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — energy death (recoverable) vs trust collapse (permanent), clearly distinguished
- [x] Why any of this matters — the five-problem framing on Why Web4 was the strongest part

## Unanswered Questions
1. *Why* is CI squared (not CI or CI³)? The page says it's a calibration choice but I never felt I understood the intuition.
2. What concretely counts as a distinct "role" for T3? The boundaries felt fuzzy in practice.
3. LCT stayed the most abstract for me — I understood "hardware = ID" but never got a hands-on feel for it the way I did for ATP and trust.

## Honest Assessment
This was a genuinely good visit — the best-organized version of this site I can imagine encountering cold. The landing page's honesty ("you can't install it yet") and the Why Web4 problem framing earned my attention before any jargon arrived, and the Karma Journey's two-deaths distinction — which I'd expect to be the most confusing thing here — was actually one of the *clearest*, because the page anticipated my confusion ("the number alone can't tell them apart — which metric failed does") and resolved it in one sentence. The ADP "why should I care" question, which I imagine trips people up, was answered up front.

My remaining friction is almost entirely **acronym load** and the **two-trust-numbers (raw vs effective) + CI²** complexity on Karma Journey. Those aren't framing failures so much as inherent density — by the time I'm on a concept page, four-to-five acronyms greet me before a single definition. The site clearly knows this (it links First Contact everywhere as the antidote), but a linear reader who skips First Contact still pays the tax. If I were to fix one thing, it'd be a single plain-English anchor near the top of Karma Journey naming the two trust numbers and why a newcomer starts low — that was the one place I had to re-read and still wasn't sure I retained it. Everything else is polish. I'd come back, and I'd send this to a technically curious friend.
