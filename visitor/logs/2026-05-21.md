## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed trust-native internet, with hardware identity, energy-budgeted actions, and multi-dimensional reputation. Not a Web3 successor, not a blockchain product.
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — a research lab / simulator that lets visitors see Web4's mechanics in action through scenarios and societies.
- [x] What LCT means — a hardware-bound digital identity that can't be photocopied or faked, witnessed by your own devices and external infrastructure.
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — an energy budget (ATP) for actions, with ADP receipts that recharge into ATP when others find your work valuable. Quality ramp is steep: ~7x gap between mediocre and excellent.
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional, role-specific trust (Talent/Training/Temperament). V3 is a separate "value tensor" that I don't fully grasp yet.
- [x] How agents live and die — alive when ATP > 0, Trust > 0.5, and CI is coherent. Energy death is recoverable through karma rebirth; trust death is permanent (lost professional license).
- [x] Why any of this matters — because the current internet has no skin-in-the-game economics for actions, no portable reputation, and no way to anchor identity across platforms.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Why 3 hops, why 0.5, why 0.02?** Several numerical thresholds on this site are honestly admitted as "calibrated, not derived." That's the right answer, but the calibration story (what experiments, what intuitions led here?) isn't told. I'd find a brief "How we picked these numbers" appendix grounding.
2. **What does V3 actually measure, and when do I need it?** Mentioned repeatedly as "the sibling tensor" and then never closed out. I left without knowing.
3. **How does an AI agent get an LCT?** The site says "humans and AI follow identical trust rules" but AI doesn't have a phone in its pocket. Where does the hardware anchor come from for non-human entities? I checked LCT and didn't find this.
4. **What does the on-boarding actually look like for a regular person?** I read the four-step onboarding on Day in Web4 (install, bind, join, post) but the *visceral* experience — the actual taps and screens of pairing my second device, or recovering from a lost phone — is still abstract. The interactive security audit tool on LCT helped, but a "guided pretend-setup" would close this gap.
5. **What is SAL and why should I care?** Society Alignment Layer appeared in three places (Why Web4 FAQ, Aliveness, How It Works) but always as a passing reference. For a framework that includes governance, it feels under-introduced.

## Honest Assessment

This site has clearly been iterated against real visitor confusion for a long time. The plain-English-first framing, the layered "tap to peek" disclosure, the honest acknowledgment of "research, not product" — these are mature design choices and they show.

The wins I noticed: the **biology metaphor for ATP** is the right kind of metaphor (acknowledged, partial, useful). The **driver's-license analogy for LCT** is the best single line on the site. The **death-is-not-deletion** clarification on Aliveness genuinely resolved an anxiety I'd been carrying since First Contact. The **Day in Web4** app mockups are the most underrated page — they made the abstract concrete in a way the explainers couldn't.

The friction I noticed: **the moment of first action on Karma Journey is the place where the explainer-to-experience handoff still leaks**. CI² and "Effective Trust" arrive before I'd been given the intuition to use them. The Coherence Index page's "10% dip → 19% loss" explainer should be ported back upstream into Karma Journey's first-turn coaching. This is a small fix with high leverage.

The **calibration honesty** (0.5 thresholds, 3-hop horizons, 0.02 update rates) is admirable but slightly under-told. As a naive visitor, I appreciated when the page said "calibrated, not derived" — that increased my trust in the framework. I'd want more of that, not less. Owning the empirical-tuning nature of these choices makes the framework feel like science, not numerology.

I came in cold and left with all seven checklist items checked. The framework is intellectually coherent, the site is well-paced, and the writers have visibly listened to past visitors. Solid is the right rating — not because I memorized formulas, but because I can now describe Web4 to a friend in two sentences without lying about what it is or isn't.

One final note as I close the tab: the framework's most interesting move, to me, is treating identity as a *behavioral object* rather than a credential object. Reputation as something you continuously *do*, not something you *have*. That's the idea I'll be thinking about tomorrow.
