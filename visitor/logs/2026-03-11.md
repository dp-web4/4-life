## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean. Immediately tells me the internet has a spam/fake account problem and this site is trying to show an alternative. That's good.
- **Clicked**: "Why Web4" — felt like the natural starting point
- **Confusion points**: "Web4" as a term is unexplained. I've heard of Web3 (crypto stuff). What happened to Web3? The site assumes I'm curious enough to click forward without explaining the lineage. Minor friction.

### Why Web4 (05:00)
- **What I got**: A clear problem statement. Spam is free. Reputation doesn't travel. Bad actors reset. AI makes deception easier. I believed all of this before reading it, so this landed well.
- **What confused me**: The "solutions" section introduces ATP, LCT, T3, and MRH as acronyms in quick succession without defining them inline. I now have four mystery terms I need to track.
- **Confusion points**: The acronym dump is a real wall. I noted the terms but had no idea what they meant yet. I had to trust the site would explain them. That's a cognitive load ask.

### First Contact (10:00)
- **What I got**: Alice's journey — she posts, earns energy, spams, loses energy, dies, gets reborn. Clever narrative. Made the lifecycle concrete.
- **What confused me**: "Attestation" — the page says quality is confirmed by recipients, but I don't know *how*. Do they click something? Does the system infer it? This matters a lot and got zero explanation.
- **Confusion points**: The distinction between Web4 and existing reputation systems like Reddit karma or eBay seller ratings isn't explained. I kept thinking "this sounds like Reddit karma but with extra steps."

### How It Works (15:00)
- **What I got**: Five systems: LCT (identity), ATP (energy), T3 (trust), CI (coherence), MRH (context). Birth/Life/Death/Rebirth cycle.
- **What confused me**: Numbers appear without equations. "7× quality ramp," "1.4× cost multiplier," "176 checks." I'm being given statistics with no way to verify or understand them. This felt like authority theater — throwing numbers at me to seem rigorous without actually explaining the math.
- **Confusion points**: CI (Coherence Index) is introduced as a concept but I have no mental model for what "behavioral consistency" actually looks like in practice. Is this based on timing patterns? Content similarity? Who decides?

### ATP Economics (20:00)
- **What I got**: ATP is your budget; ADP is the receipt. Actions cost ATP. Good contributions earn it back. You die when you run out.
- **What confused me**: Why do I need *both* ATP and ADP? The distinction is explained (one is budget, one is receipt) but the "why" of having two separate constructs is never motivated. It feels like technical architecture leaking into user-facing explanation.
- **Confusion points**: "Metabolic economics" metaphor — I understand biology, but not everyone does. The analogy might confuse people who don't know what cellular ATP is. The 5% burn/redistribution mechanics felt important but were buried and rushed.

### Trust Tensor (25:00)
- **What I got**: Trust is three-dimensional (Talent, Training, Temperament) and role-specific. Alice's trust as a data analyst doesn't bleed into her trust as a mechanic. That's actually a nice idea.
- **What confused me**: Decay half-lives and Brier scores. These are introduced in the middle of an explainer page without scaffolding. I'm a newcomer; I don't know what a Brier score is. Also, V3 (Value Tensors) appears near the end as if it's a natural extension of T3, but this is the first I've heard of V3 and it gets no real explanation.
- **Confusion points**: The page says trust "never leaks" across domains, which I like, but then doesn't explain how the system *knows* I'm operating in different domains. Who defines the boundaries of "data analyst" vs "mechanic"?

### Aliveness (28:00)
- **What I got**: You're "alive" if ATP > 0, T3 trust > 0.5, and your Coherence Index is strong. All three must hold. Death is ATP depletion or trust collapse. Rebirth carries karma forward.
- **What confused me**: Appeals mechanism — the page mentions it exists but admits it's "designed but untested with real humans." That's a significant caveat. If the appeals system is untested, the whole "consequences without moderators" pitch is shakier than the site implies.
- **Confusion points**: What does it *feel like* to have your CI (Coherence Index) fail? What triggers it? The page is abstract where it should be concrete.

### LCT Explainer (31:00)
- **What I got**: LCT = Linked Context Token. Your identity is bound to your hardware (phone's secure chip, YubiKey). Fake accounts require real devices, making mass-faking expensive.
- **What confused me**: "Witnesses" — this word means human people to me. It took several paragraphs to realize witnesses are other devices, not people. That's a naming problem.
- **Confusion points**: Device loss/recovery is described as "deliberately hard" — but deliberately hard for whom? For the user who lost all their devices through no fault of their own, "deliberately hard" is just "broken." The social recovery mechanism is hand-wavy in a place where it matters enormously.

### TL;DR (33:00)

- **What confused me**: This should have been my *first* stop, not my eighth. The navigation doesn't push newcomers here first. The TL;DR buried in a nav item isn't doing its job as an entry point.

## Honest Assessment

The site is clearly written by people who understand the problem deeply. The problem framing is excellent — I genuinely felt the frustration of reputation silos and cheap fake accounts as real problems by the time I finished the "Why Web4" page.

But there are two layers of friction that compound on each other:

**Acronym debt**: The site introduces ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, CI, MRH, T3V3, and MRH-SAL in rapid succession across its pages. Each page assumes I've already absorbed the ones introduced before, but there's no guarantee a visitor reads pages in order. By page four I was carrying five undefined acronyms and just trying to keep up. The glossary tooltip idea appears to exist in some pages but isn't consistent.

**Authority vs. explanation tension**: The site frequently cites numbers ("176 integration checks," "7× quality ramp") without showing the underlying logic. This pattern signals "trust us, it's rigorous" rather than "here's how to think about it." For a site about trust, that's an ironic friction point. A curious skeptic will feel lectured at rather than taught.

The best parts of the site are the narrative examples (Alice's journey) and the "What Could Go Wrong" page's honesty. The worst parts are the acronym walls in "How It Works" and the hand-wavy recovery mechanics in LCT.

If I were a normal person who stumbled onto this, I'd probably bounce after the "How It Works" page — not because the ideas are bad, but because the cognitive overhead of tracking all the new vocabulary while also trying to grasp new concepts is genuinely exhausting.

Fix the 404. Add inline acronym definitions. Make TL;DR the entry point. Show one concrete CI example. Those four things would probably double comprehension.
