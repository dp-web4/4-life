## Unanswered Questions

1. **What does the actual UI look like in practice?** The Day in Web4 wireframes are conceptual mockups, but I'm curious what a real prototype interface would look like — even a rough interactive demo of "Web4 Mail" or "Web4 Reviews" would be compelling.

2. **How does the 0.5 threshold handle cultural differences?** Different communities might have different norms for what constitutes "quality" or "cooperation." Does the 0.5 line move per society, or is it universal? The federation economics page hints at this but doesn't resolve it.

3. **What happens when two societies have incompatible trust standards?** Federation economics covers mergers and pricing, but what about fundamental value conflicts (e.g., a society that values radical honesty federating with one that values diplomatic tact)?

4. **How do you prevent the witness network from becoming a de facto surveillance system?** The LCT page addresses privacy through pseudonymity and ZK proofs, but if devices are constantly attesting to each other's presence, the metadata alone could be revealing. The threat model touches on privacy leakage channels but I'd like more on the concrete privacy guarantees.

5. **What's the actual adoption path?** The 5-tier adoption model (Wrapper → Observable → Accountable → Federated → Native) is mentioned but not deeply explored on the site. A concrete "here's how a Discord server or subreddit could start using Web4 ideas today" would make adoption feel tangible.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most well-structured educational sites I've browsed on a deeply technical topic. The progressive disclosure works — I was never overwhelmed, and the "problem before solution" philosophy paid off on every page.

**What worked exceptionally well:**
- The TL;DR → Why Web4 → First Contact → Karma Journey → Society Simulator progression is a masterfully designed learning funnel. Each page builds on the last.
- Honest about limitations: "research not deployment," "tested at small scale," "appeals untested." This builds credibility rather than undermining it.
- The four featured narratives on the Narratives page told a more compelling story about Web4 than any amount of technical explanation could. The Tidepool result (29% cooperators outperforming 63%) was genuinely surprising and stuck with me.
- Interactive elements at every layer — sliders, simulators, survival challenges. Not just reading, doing.

**What could be stronger:**
- The Compare page assumes too much prior knowledge. It's the weakest page I visited relative to the quality standard set by everything else.
- The Karma Journey starting trust of 0.361 contradicts the "neutral is 0.5" framing used everywhere else, which briefly broke my mental model.
- I kept wanting to see a working prototype — even a toy version of "Web4 Mail" that I could click through. The wireframes on Day in Web4 are good but static.
- The site oscillates slightly between two audiences: curious visitors who need intuition, and researchers who want formal verification numbers. The collapsible sections mostly handle this well, but sometimes the technical details leak into the main narrative (e.g., "176 formal verification checks" on How It Works feels like it's for a different audience than the surrounding text).

**Overall:** The site successfully taught me a coherent mental model of Web4 in 45 minutes. I came in knowing nothing and left understanding why trust-native infrastructure matters, how the core mechanisms (ATP, LCT, T3, CI, MRH) work together, and — critically — what the unsolved problems are. The intellectual honesty throughout was the single most persuasive element. I'd return to explore the interactive tools more deeply.
