## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed trust layer where actions cost energy and reputation is earned, portable, and persistent
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — that these mechanics produce cooperation without central authority, shown via simulations
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored identity that makes fresh starts expensive
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget spent to act, recharged by confirmed value; ADP is the automatic receipt (fuzzy for a minute, then solid)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — role-specific 3-axis trust (Talent/Training/Temperament), driven by V3 ratings of your work
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — run out of ATP → suspension/death; karma carries a head start into rebirth (First Contact taught this; the simulator barely reinforced it)
- [x] Why any of this matters — the five problem framings on Why Web4 are personally recognizable

## Unanswered Questions

## Honest Assessment
This is the most coherent multi-page onboarding I can remember experiencing for a technical project. The path the site steered me down (landing → TL;DR → story → problem/FAQ → concepts → simulator) built genuinely — each page assumed roughly what the previous ones taught, and by page 8 the acronyms that would have drowned me on day one read as vocabulary I owned. Three patterns did the heavy lifting: honest scoping everywhere ("calibrated, not derived"; "what's real, what's simulated"), analogies before mechanisms (surgeon, co-working space, battery meter), and permission to stop ("if this feels like enough, you can stop here").

The frictions left are mostly seams between pages rather than holes in pages: a concept gets a full act on one page and a clause on another; a term is disciplined at first use, then reappears naked five scroll-screens later; and one link — the fleet — casually gestures at the most exciting claim on the site ("a live society") without telling me what I'd find. That last one is the only friction that changed my behavior: I didn't click, because I couldn't tell if I was about to be impressed or bait-and-switched. I'm noting that a site this careful about the deployed-vs-live distinction should not leave its one "live" link unframed.

Would I return? Yes — to actually run the Society Simulator and First Contact interactively, and to find out what the fleet is. That's two return visits this session earned honestly.
