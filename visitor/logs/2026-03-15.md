### Trust Tensor (25:00)
- T3 = Talent (0.4), Training (0.3), Temperament (0.3). Role-specific. Alice example across Data Analyst (90% trust), Project Manager (76%), Mechanic (27%) made this very concrete.
- Decay half-lives make sense: Talent persists (365 days), Training fades without practice (180 days), Temperament is about recent behavior (30 days).
- V3 (Value Tensors) for outputs: Veracity + Validity (0.70) outweigh Valuation (0.30) — rewarding truth over popularity. This is a meaningful design choice.
- Evidence strength hierarchy: hardware attestation (1.0) down to gossip (0.3). Chain strength = product of links. Clear why 3-hop limit exists.
- Trust calibration section: 0.7 score should predict cooperative behavior 70% of the time. This grounds the numbers in something I can understand.
- Fractal composition (person → team → org → federation) is elegant but I'm taking it on faith — hard to verify without seeing it in action.

### Coherence Index (28:00)
- Four dimensions: spatial, capability, temporal, relational. Geometric mean (not average) so one weak dimension drags everything down.
- Maria's stolen phone example: even in same city, temporal continuity breaks + relational mismatch → CI drops to 0.55 → blocks access. This makes the abstract concrete.
- Economic consequence: low CI = costs multiplied by 1/CI², capped at 10x. CI of 0.3 = 11x normal costs. Attacks become unaffordable.
- The interactive slider tool sounds useful — I'd want to play with it.

### MRH - Markov Relevancy Horizon (30:00)

- Role-specific contexts: Alice-as-surgeon has different graph than Alice-as-car-owner. Prevents inappropriate trust transfer.
- This concept clicked quickly. The analogy to "you only hear about news through people you know" is intuitive.

## Unanswered Questions

1. **What would the actual UI look like for an end user?** The "A Day in Web4" page describes scenarios but I never saw a mockup or wireframe of what a Web4 app interface would look like. The inbox example mentions trust scores next to messages — I'd love to see that as a visual.
2. **How does the system handle cultural differences in trust?** Different cultures have very different norms around trust, directness, and collaboration. The T3 dimensions seem culturally neutral but the thresholds and weights might need calibration per society. Federation economics touches on this but doesn't address the cultural layer.
3. **What happens during the transition period?** If only some platforms adopt Web4, do users maintain parallel identities (Web2 + Web4)? The 5-tier adoption model is mentioned but the friction of living in both worlds simultaneously isn't explored.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most well-constructed educational sites I've encountered for a technical research topic. The progression from "2-minute TL;DR" through increasingly detailed concept pages is natural and never felt forced. Every time I had a question, I found the answer within one or two clicks.

The strongest elements:
- **Problem before solution**: Every page starts with "here's why the current internet fails" before introducing Web4's answer. This kept me motivated to understand the mechanics.
- **Honest transparency**: The "What Could Go Wrong" and threat model pages are unusually candid for a project advocating its own technology. Listing genuinely unsolved problems builds real credibility.
- **Concrete examples throughout**: Alice's trust scores, Maria's stolen phone, the restaurant review economics — abstract concepts are consistently grounded in relatable scenarios.
- **Progressive disclosure**: I could stop at any depth and have a coherent understanding. The TL;DR alone gave me the big picture; the concept pages filled in the mechanics.

The weakest elements:
- **No visual UI concept**: After 45 minutes understanding the mechanics, I still can't picture what using Web4 would actually look like. The "A Day in Web4" scenarios describe interactions but a single mockup of a Web4 inbox or profile page would make the whole thing feel more real.
- **Federation Economics assumes context**: This page jumped into dynamic pricing without grounding what a federation is or why they form. Most other pages nail the "start with why" pattern, but this one starts in the middle.
- **Narratives page below the fold**: The four featured society stories are excellent, but the "All Narratives" section with just one additional story feels incomplete. Either curate more or explicitly frame it as "more coming."

Overall: the site taught me a complex system from scratch in under an hour. The writing is clear, the structure is logical, and the honesty about limitations actually strengthened my trust in the project. I'd return to explore the interactive simulators more deeply, and I'd recommend this to anyone interested in internet governance or trust systems.
