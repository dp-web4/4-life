### Karma Journey (14:00)

Seeing two different numbers for "trust" before I'd internalized CI² dampening was momentarily jarring. Not a blocker — the explanation is there — but it's the one spot that cost me a second pass.
- The "Show the math" box (CI² surcharge, 1/CI² ≈ 1.38) is collapsed by default, which is the right call.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a behavior-verified trust layer for the internet, not crypto
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — though the protocol-vs-lab distinction stayed fuzzy
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound cryptographic identity
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget for actions, replenished by quality (ADP only lightly)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3 (who you are) + V3 (what you produce), three dimensions each
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — two deaths: energy (recoverable) vs trust collapse (permanent). This was clear.
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam, siloed reputation, Sybil resets, AI deception

3. Is 4-Life the system, or a simulation of a system that runs elsewhere? (The /running-now "deployed code" claim made this more confusing, not less — some of it IS real, some is simulated.)

## Honest Assessment
This was a notably smoother browse than I'd expect from a "research lab" site. The front-door path (Landing → TL;DR → First Contact → Karma Journey) is genuinely well-built, and the two-deaths mechanic — which sounds like it would be the most confusing thing — was one of the clearest. The inline plain-language glosses on /running-now are a real win: that page used to read like internal documentation, and now it mostly reads like it's talking *to me*, with a polite "go learn the basics first" sign at the door.

The single remaining place I clearly fell off the path was one sentence on /running-now that lists raw code-style names (LedgerEntry, WitnessMark, SocietyRole, EntityTrust) without a hint of what they mean. It's jarring precisely *because* the rest of the page now glosses its jargon so well — that one line is the holdout, and it's where a newcomer's eyes glaze. Everything else was a minor re-read at worst.

Net: I'd come back, and I'd send this to a technically-curious friend. I'd hesitate to send it to a casual person only because there's still a lot of vocabulary overall — but the site is honest about that and keeps pointing you to the gentle door.
