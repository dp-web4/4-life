## Unanswered Questions
1. How would this work with existing social media? The adoption tiers mention a browser extension but I can't picture the actual user experience of a Tier 1 wrapper on, say, Reddit.
2. What happens to children or elderly people who share devices? The hardware-bound identity model assumes one person = their own devices.
3. How does the system handle cultural differences in what counts as "quality"? A helpful contribution in one community might be spam in another.
4. What's the actual timeline? "Research takes as long as it takes" is honest but gives me no sense of whether this is 2 years or 20 years away.
5. Who funds this? The site says nobody profits yet, but research costs money. What's sustaining the work?
6. If my trust tensor is role-specific, how many roles can I have? Is there a practical limit?
7. How would Web4 handle whistleblowers or activists who need to act anonymously? Permanent reputation seems at odds with situations requiring disposable identity for safety.

## Honest Assessment

This is the most thoughtfully built research explainer site I've encountered. The progressive disclosure actually works — I went from "what is this?" to "I understand the core mechanics" in under 30 minutes, and at no point did I feel talked down to or overwhelmed.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- The honesty. The "What Could Go Wrong" page alone makes me trust this project more than any amount of marketing would. Listing your own failure modes with severity ratings is unusual and effective.
- Alice's story in First Contact. Concrete characters with numbers make abstract systems tangible.
- The Day in Web4 mockups. Seeing Web4 Mail and Web4 Reviews made me *want* this to exist.
- Problem-first framing throughout. Every page starts with what's broken, then proposes how to fix it.

**What needs work:**
- The naming is still slightly confusing. Web4, 4-Life, LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI, SAL, V3, R6 — the acronym density is high. The site manages it well through progressive disclosure, but a first-time visitor hitting the glossary sees 40+ terms and might bail.
- Some pages (How It Works, Aliveness) mix visitor-friendly explanations with specification-level detail. The transition isn't always smooth.
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page should be more prominent — it's the best trust-building page on the site and it's buried under "Start Here" rather than being a headline link.
- I couldn't clearly tell where the interactive elements were on the Society Simulator page vs. the explanatory text.

**Overall:** I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left with a solid understanding of the core mechanics, genuine appreciation for the intellectual honesty of the project, and several real questions I'd want answered. I'd come back to try the interactive simulations and explore the learning paths. Whether I'd recommend it depends on the person — someone interested in internet infrastructure would love this; someone looking for a quick practical tool would bounce off the density. The site is at its best when it shows rather than tells.
