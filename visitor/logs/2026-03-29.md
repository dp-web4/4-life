## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust-native internet infrastructure where identity costs something, actions cost energy, and consequences are permanent
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — it's the interactive lab where you can see Web4 mechanics in action through simulations
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Tokens, hardware-bound identity tied to device security chips
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — Allocation Transfer Packets (energy budget for actions) / Allocation Discharge Packets (receipts that convert back when work is valued)
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional trust scores: Talent, Training, Temperament
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive when ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, and CI is coherent; die when any fails; rebirth possible if trust was high enough
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam, fake accounts, siloed reputations, AI deception are real problems; making trust a native internet feature could solve them economically

## Unanswered Questions
1. Who runs the witness network in practice? The bootstrapping problem is acknowledged as unsolved, but I'm left wondering who the first witnesses would be
2. How does Web4 handle genuine second chances? "Consequences are permanent" sounds good until someone genuinely reforms — is there any path for people who've made real mistakes?
3. What happens during the transition period? If only some platforms adopt Web4, doesn't that just create another silo?
4. The 5-tier adoption path mentions "Tier 1 could work on Reddit today" — has anyone actually tried this? Even a proof-of-concept?
5. How would this work for children/minors? Device-bound identity assumes adult hardware ownership
6. If my trust score follows me forever, what about contexts where I'm legitimately a beginner? High trust in cooking shouldn't imply high trust in programming — the T3 dimensions help, but are they granular enough?

## Honest Assessment

This is a thoughtful, well-built site that takes a genuinely interesting idea seriously. The problem framing is strong — everyone has experienced spam, fake reviews, and reputation silos. The "Day in Web4" page alone almost sold me.

What impressed me most: the intellectual honesty. The "What Could Go Wrong" page, the "genuinely unsolved problems" list, the "no timeline commitments" stance — these build real trust (ironically appropriate for a trust project). Most projects would hide their weaknesses. This one leads with them.

What held me back from "would recommend: yes" (I said "maybe"): the acronym density and the gap between vision and reality. By page 3, I'd encountered LCT, ATP, ADP, T3, CI, MRH, SAL, V3, and more. Each makes sense individually, but the cumulative effect is jargon overload. And while the app mockups and simulations are compelling, the repeated reminder that this is "research with no deployment" creates a "so what can I actually do with this?" feeling.

The site is strongest when it's concrete and personal (Day in Web4, First Contact, the spam economics examples). It's weakest when it piles on acronyms or dives into technical depth before the reader has built enough context to care (How It Works, deep sections of Aliveness).

The single biggest friction point: **"death" is used throughout the site as a dramatic hook, but it actually means "temporary loss of participation rights."** This disconnect between the scary word and the mundane reality creates confusion. I didn't learn what death actually meant in practice until I was 39 minutes into my browse, deep in the Aliveness page. That clarification should be front and center the first time the word appears.

Overall: I came away understanding Web4 and finding it genuinely interesting. I'd check back in six months to see if anything's been deployed. But I wouldn't send this to a non-technical friend yet — they'd bounce at the acronym wall.
