## Unanswered Questions

1. **What does the actual developer integration look like?** If I'm a platform developer wanting to add Tier 1 (wrapper) to my existing app, what's the SDK/API? The adoption tiers describe what each level does but not the developer experience of implementing them. Is there a "Hello World" for Web4 integration?

2. **How does cross-device synchronization actually work in practice?** If my phone and laptop are both acting as witnesses for each other, what happens when one is offline for days? The outage FAQ on What Could Go Wrong mentions this as a "known distributed systems challenge" but doesn't give me intuition for how it would feel as a user.

3. **What's the energy model for passive consumption?** I understand posting costs ATP and quality earns it back. But what about reading? Browsing? Lurking? The ATP page mentions "reading costs 1 ATP" in one place but this isn't elaborated. If reading costs energy, does that mean I pay to consume content? That seems like it could be a major UX barrier.

4. **How would Web4 handle the transition period where some users are on Web4 and some aren't?** Tier 1 wrapper adds trust scores to existing platforms, but what do those scores mean when 95% of users don't have them? A trust score of 0.85 is meaningless if most people you interact with have no score at all.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most carefully constructed technical education sites I've encountered. After 50 minutes and 20 pages, I genuinely understand what Web4 is proposing, why it matters, and what the honest limitations are.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- The landing page gives you 4 different entry points calibrated by time commitment. This is rare and thoughtful.
- "Problem before solution" is followed consistently — every concept page starts with what's broken today.
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page is the single most trust-building piece of content on the site. A project about trust that publishes its own failure analysis walks its own talk.
- The Day in Web4 wireframes transform abstract concepts into something I can picture using.
- Progressive disclosure is excellent — I can spend 2 minutes (TL;DR) or 2 hours (full concept sequence) and both feel complete at their depth level.
- The Narratives page with the four featured stories makes game theory tangible. The Tidepool finding (reciprocity > raw cooperation) is genuinely surprising.

**What could be better:**
- The CI page's "Scientific Foundations" section with quantum mechanics is the one place the site overreaches. The rest of the content trusts the reader to understand without physics analogies — this section breaks that pattern.
- The Aliveness page tries to be comprehensive and becomes exhausting. It's doing the work of 2-3 pages.
- The landing page adoption duplication is a small but noticeable editing miss.
- A few pages (First Contact, Trust Dilemmas, Your Internet) rely heavily on JavaScript interaction but their static fallbacks are thin. For a site that values accessibility, this is worth addressing.

**Overall**: The site has clearly been through many iterations of visitor feedback. The plain-English-first approach, the honest FAQ, the "research not product" transparency — these all work together to build exactly the kind of trust the project is about. I'd come back to run the simulations and I'd share the TL;DR page with technically curious friends.
