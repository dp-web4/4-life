## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean and confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations" told me *this is a lab/toy*, and the very next line "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" gave me a reason to care. Good one-two punch.
- I liked that it pre-empted my skepticism: "no blockchain, not a Web3 successor." I had *already* assumed this was crypto, and it defused that in one line. Nice.
- The three time-boxed entry cards ("2-minute TL;DR", "What it looks like · 5 min", "Frustrations it would fix · 3 min") made me feel like I could dip a toe in without committing.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" — because it literally told me to.
- **Confusion**: The five core-concept tiles (Identity / Energy / Trust / Neighborhood / Consistency) were fine as *words*, but I didn't yet know why "Neighborhood (3-hop contact limits)" mattered. It read like a spoiler for a movie I hadn't watched.

### TL;DR (02:30)
- The framing "internet has no memory. A spammer can create a new account in seconds" is the single clearest sentence on the whole site. I *got* the problem instantly.
- The four ideas (energy costs, device-anchored identity, portable reputation, same rules for humans+AI) landed as *intuitions*.
- **Confusion**: The acronyms started stacking — ATP, T3, MRH, CI, LCT flashed by. The page even admits "these land much easier after First Contact." But if you *know* they'll confuse me, why show them all here before I've earned them? It felt like being handed a glossary as a greeting.
- The "energy budget → cellular biology (ATP)" leap felt clever-for-the-authors but abstract-for-me. I don't need to know it's borrowed from mitochondria to understand "actions cost energy."
- **Clicked**: "First Contact" (recommended next step).

### First Contact (05:00)
- The premise — "drive Alice's choices, watch trust and reputation respond in real time" — is exactly the right idea. A story beats a lecture.
- The 5-act arc (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) is genuinely compelling. "Die & Reborn" made me want to know what death means here.
- **Confusion #1**: Same acronym problem — ATP, T3, LCT, CI show up in the reference map *before* the walkthrough teaches them.
- **Confusion #2**: A line distinguished "simulation running in your browser" from "real deployed code (hestia, the hub)." That's the first time those two names appeared, unglossed, and I had no idea what "hestia" or "the hub" were. It planted a small seed of confusion I carried for the rest of the session.

### Why Web4 (10:00)
- **Best problem-framing on the site.** "Creating accounts is free. Sending messages is free. There's no cost to flooding platforms with garbage." I nodded at every line.
- The "Why Previous Solutions Failed" section (moderation, CAPTCHAs, Web3 wallets, social login) earned my trust — it admitted what each got *right* before saying where it broke. That honesty is persuasive.
- **Confusion #1 (the big one)**: The reality status is genuinely muddled. Up top: "the trust layer (hestia) and a Web4 society (the hub) are public, AGPL code you can run yourself." In the FAQ: "No live network with real users yet." So... can I *use* this or not? I read "code you can run yourself" as "I can join something." It took me two passes to understand: I can run a *local sandbox*, but there's no *network* to plug into. That distinction (deployed **code** vs. deployed **network**) is the thing I most wanted stated plainly and up front.
- **Confusion #2**: "Quality contributions should earn resources back. Bad actors should exhaust themselves." My immediate questions — *who decides quality? what stops a collusion ring?* — are answered in the FAQ, but the main narrative makes the promise without the answer nearby.

### ATP Economics (16:00)

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely good site — noticeably more polished than I expected from "a lab." The problem-framing (TL;DR and Why Web4) is the strongest part: it made me *care* before it made me *learn*, which is the right order. The concept pages with worked examples (Sam vs. Hannah on ATP, the surgeon on T3) and the interactive simulators are where I learned the most — those "show me" moments consistently beat the prose.

My two real stumbles were both about **expectation vs. reality**, not about the concepts themselves:

1. **"Can I use this?"** The repeated "code you can run yourself" phrasing kept making me think there was a live system to join, and the correction ("no network yet") always arrived a paragraph or a page too late. This is the friction I'd fix first — it's a trust issue, ironically, on a site about trust. One plain sentence beside each claim would fix it.

2. **The jargon cliff on "Running Now."** The *leads* are written for me (a normal human); the *bullets underneath* are written for an engineer. The order is right — plain-English first — but the technical layer arrives too deep, too fast. Tuck the daemon/Docker/Rust detail behind a "for the curious" fold and the page would serve both of us.

The V3 situation felt *close to solved* this time. On the ATP page it's no longer a mysterious ghost — the "measures your work, not you" framing genuinely clicked. What's left is small: the page can't fully answer "who computes it" on its own, and V3 doesn't have a dedicated home of its own (it's a roommate on the T3 page). That's a self-containment nit, not a comprehension wall.

Net: I left understanding *more than I expected to*, wanting to poke the simulators more, and slightly unsure whether I'd just read about a real thing or a well-built thought experiment. Clarifying that last point would take this from "impressive demo" to "I get it."
