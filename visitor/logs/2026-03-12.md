## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Catchy hook — "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" immediately resonates. The interactive challenge (10,000 fake spam accounts — what do you do?) was a smart entry point. Pulled me in.
- **Clicked**: Followed the recommended learning path: Why Web4 → First Contact. Also visited TLDR, Day in Web4, What Could Go Wrong, How It Works, ATP Economics, LCT, Trust Tensor, Aliveness, Your Internet, Society Simulator.
- **Confusion points**: "Trust-native" in the title isn't explained until you scroll. ATP appears in the four pillars section but isn't defined there — just says "Every action costs ATP (energy)" in parentheses. A newcomer who doesn't follow the learning path is left guessing what ATP stands for.

### Why Web4? (03:00)
- **First impression**: The five-problem framing is compelling and maps to real experiences (spam, trapped reputation, etc.).
- **What worked**: The "why previous solutions failed" section is refreshingly honest. Shows the team has thought about failure modes.
- **Confusion points**: A wall of acronyms appears — ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, SAL, V3 — all introduced briefly within a few paragraphs. I can follow the concepts individually but the acronym soup is overwhelming. By the time SAL and V3 appeared, I had stopped retaining the earlier ones.

### TLDR (05:30)
- **What worked**: Best entry point for a complete stranger. Four clean ideas, no acronyms dominating. Hardware-bound identity = you can't make fake accounts cheaply. Energy budget = spam burns out. Portable reputation. Universal rules for AI/humans. This clicked.
- **Confusion points**: "Hardware-bound" — I understand the idea but I don't know what this physically means. My laptop? My phone? Do I need to buy something? This question lingered through the rest of the session.

### First Contact (07:00)
- **What worked**: Alice's story is a nice narrative device. Watching her post, mentor, spam, die, and get reborn made the mechanics feel real.
- **Confusion points**:
  - Alice "earns 12 ATP from upvotes" — but ADP isn't mentioned here. The ATP → ADP → ATP confirmation loop that I later saw on the ATP page wasn't explained. I assumed ATP just refills automatically from upvotes.
  - "112 ATP at rebirth" — I don't know where the extra 12 comes from. The page doesn't explain karma math.
  - Trust numbers (0.48, 0.55) appear without anchoring. I know 0.5 is neutral but why does spam drop it so far? No formula visible.

### ATP Economics (10:00)
- **What worked**: The quality ramp example (30% quality = nothing, 85% quality = 42 ATP) made the incentive structure click. The Gini coefficient comparison (0.25 vs. 0.6–0.7 in real economies) was surprising and memorable.
- **Confusion points**:
  - ADP is introduced here as a "receipt" that converts back to ATP through community confirmation. This was confusing — earlier pages implied ATP refills directly. The two-step mechanism (ADP discharge via confirmation) isn't explained until deep in this page.
  - "Gratitude loops" — is this a button users click? A passive system? I couldn't picture what this looks like in practice.
  - Rebirth ATP threshold: the page says "trusted agents" get reborn but doesn't specify T3 ≥ 0.5 as the criterion here. Cross-referencing required.

### LCT Explainer (13:00)
- **What worked**: The Face ID comparison was helpful. The key-never-leaves-hardware concept made security sense.
- **Confusion points**:
  - "Witnesses" — who are they? The page says witnesses are "any device or platform" from a "federation-wide pool" but doesn't explain who runs witness infrastructure. This is a big gap. I'm being asked to trust witnesses I can't identify.
  - "2 devices = 75% trust, 3+ devices = 90%" — where does this math come from? It feels arbitrary.
  - Hardware requirements felt ambiguous throughout the session. Does every person need a device with a Secure Enclave or TPM? What about older phones, cheap Android devices, or people in low-income contexts?

### Trust Tensor (16:00)

## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who are the witnesses?** Every page assumes witness infrastructure exists but never explains who runs it, how you find witnesses, or what stops witnesses from colluding.
2. **What hardware do I actually need?** Is this a standard smartphone in 2026? Do I need to buy something? What about people with older or cheaper devices?
3. **Does this exist?** The site says "active research project" and "real-world validation is an open question" — but is there a real prototype I can try? The Society Simulator is the closest thing I found.
4. **What is a "federation"?** The word "federation" appears repeatedly but is never explained. Is it a company? A community? A server? A protocol?
5. **ADP timing:** When exactly does ADP convert back to ATP? Is there a delay? What if nobody confirms my contribution?
6. **What does "rebirth" mean practically?** Is this a game mechanic in the simulator, or is this what would happen to a real user's account in a deployed Web4 system?

## Honest Assessment

I came to this site as someone who relates to the problems (spam, fake reviews, platform lock-in) but has no Web4 knowledge. The landing page and "Day in Web4" were the standouts — concrete, relatable, and jargon-light. The problems are explained better than the solutions.

The site does a commendable job of being honest about what doesn't work yet. The "What Could Go Wrong" page and the repeated admissions of "not yet tested" or "open question" actually increased my trust in the project. Most tech projects hide their limitations.

The biggest friction for me was **acronym overload**. ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, SAL, V3, CI, ADP — introduced rapidly across multiple pages, not always with definitions, not always with links. By the time I hit MRH I had stopped trying to track acronyms and just vibed with the general idea. That's a signal something is wrong.

The second biggest friction was **"witnesses" as a vague concept**. The identity system hinges on witnesses but I never learned what they actually are. This matters because it's the load-bearing social/technical question: if witnesses can collude or fail, the whole identity system fails. The site acknowledges this risk but doesn't explain the mechanism enough to evaluate whether the risk is real.

The learning path (Why Web4 → First Contact → Playground) was helpful for structuring my journey. But someone who doesn't follow it and just clicks around will hit acronyms without context quickly.

**Bottom line**: I understand the big idea and I'm moderately convinced the problem framing is correct. I'm less convinced about the solutions because I couldn't fully grasp the witness/LCT mechanism. I'd return to try the Society Simulator and Karma Journey interactives. I'd recommend this to a technically-minded friend with the caveat: "follow the learning path, not random links."
