## Unanswered Questions
1. **What would the actual user interface look like at scale?** The Day in Web4 mockups are conceptual wireframes. What does a real app with Web4 integrated look like? How does a developer actually build on this? The 5-tier adoption pathway mentions "wrapper" as tier 1 — what does wrapping an existing app actually involve?
2. **How does cross-society trust transfer work in practice?** Federation economics and cross-society policy are mentioned but I never saw a concrete example of "I have trust in Community A, now I join Community B — what happens?" The MRH page says societies can federate, but the actual mechanics of my trust traveling between communities remained abstract.
3. **What's the minimum viable community size?** The cold-start walkthrough mentions ~100 actions to stabilize, but how many *people* does that require? Could a 5-person team use this meaningfully, or does it need 50+ members to generate enough signal?
4. **How would moderation of illegal content work?** The site focuses on spam and bad actors, but what about genuinely harmful content (CSAM, terrorism recruitment, etc.)? "No central authority" sounds good for spam but what about content that requires legal intervention? The governance section mentions SAL and law oracles but the concrete enforcement mechanism for illegal content wasn't clear.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoughtfully constructed educational sites I've encountered for a complex technical concept. The landing page hooks you with real problems, the TL;DR delivers on its promise, and the progressive depth works — I could stop at any point and still have a coherent understanding.

The standout pages are **Day in Web4** (makes abstract concrete through mockups), **First Contact** (Alice's story teaches by showing), **What Could Go Wrong** (builds credibility through honesty), and the **Karma Journey** (teaches through interaction).

The main friction pattern is **page length on concept explainers**. The LCT, ATP, and Trust Tensor pages each try to serve two audiences — curious visitors and technical reviewers — in the same page. For a first visit, I got the concept within the first third of each page and then waded through specification-level detail. The `<details>` collapsible sections help, but the sheer scroll height is intimidating. A "beginner view / full reference" toggle might help.

The **concept sequence** (LCT→ATP→T3→MRH→CI→Aliveness) is well-structured and the navigation between pages is seamless. The glossary panel is helpful and unobtrusive. The exploration progress bar is a nice motivational touch.

What impressed me most: the site's willingness to say "we don't know" and "this is unsolved." The What Could Go Wrong page, the "active research" disclaimers, the honest caveats on every technical claim — this isn't a marketing site, it's a research lab that's inviting you to understand what it's exploring and why. That intellectual honesty IS the trust-native approach they're proposing.

I would return. I would recommend this to anyone interested in internet governance, trust systems, or the future of online communities. The interactive tools (Karma Journey, Society Simulator, Playground) give me reasons to come back and experiment further.

**Bottom line**: The site succeeds at its core mission — making Web4 comprehensible to someone with zero prior knowledge. The concepts build on each other logically, the analogies are well-chosen, and the honest caveats prevent the kind of hype-driven skepticism that kills credibility. The main improvement opportunity is managing the depth — not reducing it, but layering it more aggressively so first-time visitors aren't swimming in reference material on their first read.
