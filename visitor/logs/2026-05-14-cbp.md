## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations" tells me there's a thing called Web4 and this site demonstrates it. The parenthetical "(trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default)" is the most useful line — it tells me the *idea* in plain English before any acronyms.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" — it's literally marked as the recommended path, so I did the obvious thing.
- **Confusion points**:
  - The page already shows six section headings ("The Internet Has a Trust Problem", "5 ideas, in plain English", "What is this, really?", "How would it work?", etc.) — I haven't clicked anything and there are already lots of branches. Mild decision overload.
  - "Karma Journey", "Playground", "Society Simulator", "Trust dilemma quiz" — four secondary entry points alongside the primary CTA. Why are there so many ways in?

### /tldr (02:00)
- **What worked**: The "internet has no memory" framing is sharp. "A spammer can create a new account in seconds" is concrete and instantly relatable. The "make bad behavior expensive" through-line is graspable.
- **What stuck out**: The page promises "Plain English first — the acronyms (ATP, ADP, LCT) are spelled out the first time they appear." Good intent. But by the end I had encountered ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI — six acronyms in a 2-minute read. That's not plain English, that's a glossary.
- **The ATP/biology problem**: I know ATP from biology (Adenosine Triphosphate). The site expands it as "Allocation Transfer Packets." This feels backronym-ish. Either the biology metaphor is intentional (ATP/ADP cycle = energy expenditure and replenishment, which fits the system) and should be embraced openly, or the term should be different. The current half-commitment is awkward.
- **Confusion point**: "77-page site" — does this mean there are 77 pages I should read? That's overwhelming for someone deciding whether to invest more time.
- **Clicked**: First Contact, since "interactive narrative following Alice's first 16 actions" sounded like the most beginner-friendly thing.

### /first-contact (06:00)
- **What worked**: The act structure (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) is clear and dramatic. Mapping acronyms to plain words ("Trust/T3", "Consequences/LCT", "Authenticity/CI") helps.
- **What didn't**: The actual interactive content is gated behind a "Start the 5-act Walkthrough" button. As a naive visitor browsing to decide whether to invest the 7 minutes, the static page gives me the *framing* but not the *feel*. I want a preview, a screenshot, a teaser of what happens in Act 1 before committing.
- **Confusion**: "Die & Reborn (karma carryover)" — why are there "lives"? In actual Web4 do I die? Or is "death" just a metaphor for a banned/depleted account? This isn't disambiguated.

The page implies *some* aspects persist (Talent: 1 year) and the LCT itself is permanent. But the half-life mechanism *partially contradicts* the "consequences follow you" promise. The teen-at-15 framing helps, but I needed to think hard to reconcile this.

## Unanswered Questions

2. **Is ATP a cryptocurrency or non-monetary energy?** The "5% transfer fee" line raised this and nothing resolved it.
3. **What's the relationship between Trust (T3) and Veracity?** Veracity was mentioned in the political-abuse FAQ as a separate scoring dimension but never defined.
4. **Who manufactures the security chips Web4 trusts?** Is this fundamentally Apple/Google/Samsung-dependent? The What-Could-Go-Wrong page says yes, but the LCT page doesn't flag it.
5. **What is "SAL" (Society Alignment Layer)?** Mentioned in Why-Web4 as a governance framework but not explained.
6. **What does "dying" mean in real Web4, not just the Karma simulation?** Is it LCT invalidation? Permanent? Recoverable?
7. **What is the difference between Trust dropping to 0 and an LCT being revoked?** They feel like they might be different things.
8. **Who runs this?** Is 4-Life / Web4 an academic project, a foundation, a startup? The "About" or "Who we are" page didn't surface in my browse, and that matters for whether I trust the project.
9. **What's the relationship between the spec and the simulations?** Is the site documenting a real protocol with reference implementations, or theorizing about one that could exist? The TLDR mentions both but the line is fuzzy.
10. **How do the first 5–10 members of a brand-new community establish any trust?** The bootstrap problem felt waved away.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the more thoughtful explainer sites I've encountered for an unfamiliar technical concept. The intellectual honesty on `/what-could-go-wrong` is unusual and earns real credibility. The Web3 contrast — "ownership vs trust" — is a memorable framing I'll carry away. The mockups on `/day-in-web4` are the single most effective artifact for showing what the system *feels* like.

What undermines the experience for a naive visitor:

- **Acronym density.** I encountered eight acronyms (ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI, SAL, Veracity) before I'd been on the site for ten minutes. The site says "plain English first" but doesn't keep that promise. By the third page my working memory was saturated and I started skimming rather than absorbing.
- **The ATP backronym.** "Allocation Transfer Packets" feels like it was reverse-engineered to match the biology term. Either the metabolism metaphor is the whole point — in which case say so — or pick a different name. The current half-commitment is a minor but persistent distraction.
- **Interactive content gated behind clicks.** First Contact, A Day in Web4, and Karma Journey all promise interactive experiences but show only framing on the static page. As a visitor browsing to decide whether to invest more time, I want a preview of what the interaction *feels* like before committing 7–15 minutes.
- **Voice slippage between research and product.** Sometimes Web4 is presented in present tense ("Messages cost energy"), sometimes as research aspiration. The truth is the latter, and the slippage costs credibility every time I notice it.
- **"Karma" as a frame that fights its own disclaimer.** The page leads with "karma here is not a moral judgment" — which raises the question: then why call it karma? The word imports religious connotations the system explicitly disclaims.

What would change my "maybe recommend" to "yes recommend":

1. A genuinely 2-minute TLDR — no acronyms, just the problem, the idea, and a one-line example. The current /tldr is closer to 5 minutes of dense reading.
2. Inline previews of the interactive pages (a screenshot of Act 1, a sample turn from Karma Journey).
3. Plain-language single-sentence definitions for "witness," "Veracity," and "death" as they appear.
4. An honest about-page surfaced in nav: who's behind this, is it academic/startup/foundation, what's the relationship between the site and the spec.

What I respect: the project is willing to publish `/what-could-go-wrong` and to write things like "Recovery from total device loss is an unsolved problem." That's research-honest, and it's the reason I'd be willing to come back to follow the work.

**Net read**: a thoughtful, dense, intellectually honest explainer that overestimates its own readability for genuinely cold visitors. Forty percent of the friction is acronym hygiene; the rest is preview-ability and voice consistency. If a curious friend asked me what Web4 is after this browse, I could tell them "make bad behavior expensive on the internet by tying identity to hardware and making reputation portable" — and that's a real takeaway. But I couldn't yet explain the mechanics with confidence, and I'm not sure the static site (without doing the interactive simulations) gets a visitor much further than that takeaway.
