## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a proposed framework where trust is built into the protocol itself, not managed by platforms
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — it's an interactive lab/simulation to make Web4 concepts tangible
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound identity using existing security chips in phones/laptops
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget (ATP) and receipts (ADP); internal accounting, not cryptocurrency
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional, role-specific trust score (Talent/Training/Temperament)

All 7 checked.

## Unanswered Questions

1. **What does the day-to-day UX actually look like?** The mockups are promised but I didn't experience them interactively. When I send a message, do I see an ATP cost before I hit send? Does the UI remind me to conserve energy? This is the #1 gap for me — I know the mechanics now but I don't know what *using* this would feel like moment-to-moment.

2. **Cold-start governance.** When a new Web4 community forms with no trusted members, who confirms the first quality contributions? The ATP page says "community decides via weighted confirmations" but that's circular in a fresh community. Why Web4 has an FAQ on this — but it's buried in an accordion 20+ questions deep.

3. **What happens when I lose my phone?** Device recovery via quorum (m-of-n) was mentioned but hand-wavy. If my phone is my identity anchor, losing it shouldn't erase me. How long does recovery take? What if I only own one device?

4. **Privacy specifics.** The site is honest that Web4 "trades centralized data collection for decentralized attestation patterns" — but what exactly leaks in attestation? If my devices witness each other, is there a graph of my device movements somewhere?

5. **Who runs the witness infrastructure?** The "like email" analogy is good but I never got a concrete answer. Is it volunteers? Communities? Paid nodes? Is there an incentive structure for witnesses?

## Honest Assessment

This site has improved dramatically on the fundamentals. The TL;DR is genuinely 2 minutes and digestible. The LCT page answers my big practical question ("do I need new hardware?") early and directly. "Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain" on the landing page sets expectations correctly. The research-prototype framing is consistent and honest.

My biggest gripe is **entry-point proliferation**. The landing page offers TL;DR, First Contact, Day in Web4, Your Internet, Why Web4, Karma Journey, Society Simulator, Trust Dilemmas — all as "starting points." They cover overlapping ground. I suspect these were added iteratively to address different visitor personas, but as a first-timer I experience it as "which door is the right one?" Decision fatigue before I've even started. I'd recommend picking ONE canonical path (TL;DR → Day in Web4 → Concept pages) and making others feel like side-quests, not parallel onramps.

The second issue is **Why Web4 is too big to be a gateway**. When I click "Why Web4?" I expect a tight problem statement, maybe 500-1500 words. I got 9,500 words and 40+ FAQs. That's a research paper in visitor's clothing. The problem diagnosis at the top is genuinely good — but it gets buried. If I were redesigning: keep the problem statement and top 5 FAQs on /why-web4, move the other 35 FAQs to a searchable /faq page.

The third observation is that **T3 + V3 on one page was cognitive overload**. T3 is hard enough — three dimensions, role-specific weights, decay rates. Piling V3 on top (with its own three dimensions) meant I left without either landing cleanly. Split the pages, or introduce V3 much later in the learning path.

Positive: the honesty is compelling. Every page says "this is unproven" or "this is research" or "this remains an open question." As a skeptical reader, I trust this site much more than I'd trust one that promises certainty. That tonal choice is a major asset — don't lose it.

Positive: the interactive elements (live trust engine, sandbox, society simulator, trust dilemmas, karma journey) are a huge differentiator. Even when I couldn't drive them via this browse, knowing they exist made the abstract ideas feel testable. A technical reader will treasure this.

I'd come back. I'd recommend it to a thoughtful friend — with the caveat "skip Why Web4, read TL;DR, then play with Day in Web4 and Society Simulator." The material is there; the path through it needs tightening.
