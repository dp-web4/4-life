## Journey

### Landing Page (0:00)
First impression: strong opener. "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" — I know this problem. The four bullets (spam, siloed reputation, second chances, AI deception) matched complaints I already have. I liked that the page explicitly said "experimental lab" and "not a product or company" — I didn't feel like I was being sold anything. The "2-minute TL;DR" button was the obvious next click for me.

Clicked: **Start here: 2-minute TL;DR** (I wanted to know what I was getting into before committing to a longer walkthrough.)

Confusion points: minor — "4-Life" as a name is a little opaque on first contact. I had to read past the headline to understand it's a lab for Web4. The relationship between "4-Life" and "Web4" wasn't immediate from the landing hero alone, though it becomes clear within a paragraph or two.

### TL;DR (2:00)
Very effective page. Three mechanisms (energy-based actions, hardware-anchored identity, portable reputation) framed with concrete relatable examples: "A spammer can create a new account in seconds" and "Your 10-year reputation on Twitter means nothing on Reddit." These were the moments I felt "oh, I see what this is trying to fix."

I liked that the page named the ATP mechanism by function ("energy budget") before loading up on acronyms. Managed expectations explicitly — "active research with working simulations, not a deployed product." I appreciated the honesty.

Clicked: **First Contact** — the "walkthrough" framing felt more newcomer-friendly than "Why Web4" which sounded like it'd be longer.

### First Contact (7:00)
I was expecting the ATP expansion to land here since it's the walkthrough. The page says ATP's function is "attention energy" — fine, functional. But I couldn't find the acronym expansion on this specific page; I had to click through to the ATP page later to learn it stands for "Allocation Transfer Packets."

Alice's 5-act arc (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) is a strong structure. I understood the story: mistakes cost you, consistency rebuilds, karma persists. The "7 minutes" time estimate and "nothing affects your real account" reassurance are nice touches.

Confusion: MRH and T3 appear as linked terms, but the page drops them without quite saying when I'd need to care about them. I made a note to check those later.

### Why Web4 (14:00)
After First Contact I doubled back to Why Web4 to get the deeper problem framing. This page works well. The three-part structure (Problems ~5min → Solutions ~2min → FAQ) felt well-paced. The four-acronym table (ATP/LCT/T3/MRH) as a "shorthand" compact reference is smart — I could skim it without feeling buried.

The FAQ section is genuinely impressive. "40+ questions" sounds like a lot but they're collapsed sensibly. The fact that the page acknowledges tensions rather than overselling built real credibility for me.

### LCT Explainer (20:00)
Good progression: Web2 (passwords) → Web3 (wallets) → Web4 (witnesses). The Tokyo login scenario worked — phone GPS + laptop TPM + smartwatch biometrics independently confirming the same session gave me a concrete picture.

Friction: the **Immutable/Characteristic/Dynamic tiers** section hit me abstractly. I wasn't sure what problem those tiers solve until I re-read it twice. A concrete "example per tier" would have helped.

Also: the **trust ceiling numbers** (TPM=0.90, SE=0.85, FIDO2=0.75, Software=0.50) — I don't know what behavioral thing a 0.75 means. "If my trust ceiling is 0.75, what can I do that someone with 0.50 can't?" wasn't obvious.

The "witnesses = my own devices" clarification is useful — early in my browse I wasn't sure if witnesses were third-party servers or peers. This page answered it, but I imagine someone skimming could still miss it.

### ATP Economics (26:00)
Now I found the expansion: **Allocation Transfer Packets**, with the adenosine triphosphate biology metaphor. This is where the vocabulary clicks: cells burn ATP, get ADP back, recharge. That's a rich and memorable mapping.

The interactive simulator (start with 100 ATP, try different actions) was the single most engaging thing I saw today. Concrete feedback beats prose.

Friction: the **5% burn on transfers** was introduced without motivating the anti-gaming reason up front. Felt punitive/bureaucratic before I understood why.

Minor mismatch with First Contact's framing: First Contact glosses ATP as "attention energy," ATP Economics expands as "Allocation Transfer Packets." Both are defensible but I had to reconcile them mentally.

### Trust Tensor (T3) (32:00)
Talent/Training/Temperament clicked immediately. Role-contextualized trust — "your code-review trust ≠ your financial-advice trust" — was a concept I actually appreciated and don't see elsewhere.

Friction: **V3 appears partway through** without a preamble. I was comfortable with T3 (who you are) when suddenly V3 (what you produce) shows up. The T3×V3×base = ATP formula was a "wait, there's a V3 now?" moment. This transition could be foreshadowed earlier.

The **decay half-lives** (Talent 365d, Training 180d, Temperament 30d) are elegant — skills persist, character must be re-demonstrated. I got that quickly.

### Coherence Index (38:00)
The "credit check that asks — does your behavior make physical sense?" analogy was my favorite one-liner of the whole site. It immediately clarifies what CI does.

The Maria-stolen-phone example (CI drops to 0.55 despite correct credentials) and the bot farm scenario (correlated temporal patterns → CI = 0.41) were both concrete and memorable.

Friction: **T3 vs CI distinction arrives late**. The formula `Effective trust = T3 × CI²` appears well after both are introduced. For a naive reader, getting the distinction **upfront** ("T3 is what you've earned; CI is whether you're acting like yourself right now") would have helped.

### Markov Relevancy Horizon (42:00)
MRH as "trust neighborhood" works conceptually. The three-hop social-graph bound + trust-decay-by-distance (0.70, 0.49, 0.34) is intuitive once I accepted 0.7 as the chosen factor.

Friction: **"Markov" is intimidating terminology**. The page defines "Markov property" but the name alone will scare non-technical readers away. A parenthetical plain-English alias in the page title ("MRH — Your Trust Neighborhood") would help.

The 0.7 decay being called a "design choice" rather than deriving from something felt slightly arbitrary — I'd want to see either "we tried X and Y and 0.7 worked best" or "this is tunable per federation."

### How It Works (46:00)
Strong page. The Birth → Life → Death → Rebirth narrative arc stitched things together in a way I hadn't gotten elsewhere. The explicit "each system handles one job" framing is helpful.

Friction: **CI in the pipeline arrives without its own prior introduction** if someone reads How It Works before the CI explainer. The "1.4× cost multiplier" datum dropped in without motivation. Suggest a one-sentence gloss inline.

Federation is mentioned at the end but deferred. If the scaling story is important, even a two-sentence teaser would close the loop.

### Society Simulator + Karma Journey (brief)
Society Simulator: ran it in ~30 seconds, saw clusters form and defectors isolate. The visualization works. It assumes I've read some concept pages though — I don't think this is the right first stop for a blank-slate visitor.

Karma Journey: attractive premise (multiple lives, choices carry forward). The "make cooperative choices then switch to selfish" strategy hint invites experimentation, which is good. But I was already tired by this point and the layered concepts (T3, ATP, CI, karma tiers, consistency multipliers) felt like a lot to absorb all at once.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a trust-native protocol layer for the internet
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — a working lab simulating Web4 societies so you can see trust mechanics in motion
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-anchored identity witnessed across your devices
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — attention/energy budgets you spend to act and earn back through quality
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional role-specific reputation (Talent/Training/Temperament)
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive if ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, CI coherent; energy death is recoverable, trust death is permanent
- [x] Why any of this matters — the current internet's free-identity-cheap-action architecture is what enables spam, fraud, and AI deception at scale; Web4 makes those costly

## Unanswered Questions

1. **When does the protocol ever coexist with Web2?** If my bank runs Web4 but Reddit doesn't, how does trust travel between? There might be an answer somewhere, but I didn't stumble on it naturally.

2. **Who runs the witness infrastructure?** My devices witness each other — but is there any server-side infrastructure involved in hardware attestation, and who operates it?

3. **What does the user-facing UI actually look like?** I understand the mechanics but not the experience. Do I see my T3 score? Do I get warnings before actions that would drop my CI? (Recurring note from prior logs per my sense of this, but I can't see past logs.)

4. **What actually happens at trust death?** If T3 < 0.5 and I'm locked out, is there any recovery path beyond "wait it out" or "start over with no karma"?

5. **How are the trust numbers (0.5 threshold, 0.7 decay, 1.4× CI multiplier) validated?** The site admits these are design choices. Is there any empirical basis, or is it all simulation-tuned?

## Honest Assessment

This is the best-sequenced explainer-style site I've browsed in a long time. The visitor journey has been thought through — landing → TL;DR → First Contact → concept pages → interactive simulators is a genuine arc, not a navigation dump. The honesty about "research prototype, not a deployed product" is refreshing, and the FAQ section tackles skeptical questions head-on rather than dodging them.

The main remaining friction is **late-binding of cross-concept relationships**. T3↔V3, T3↔CI, ATP↔CI, MRH↔everything — these relationships exist and are eventually explained, but they tend to surface partway through individual pages rather than being previewed up front. A naive reader gets a better picture from "How It Works" (which composes them) than from any single concept page alone. So a possible structural tweak: every concept page could open with a one-line "what this connects to" callout before diving into its own content.

The other recurring pattern: **concrete examples unlock comprehension, abstract definitions do not.** The Tokyo login scenario, Maria's stolen phone, the bot farm CI crash, Alice's 5-act arc — these are the moments I understood. The abstract tier tables and formulas — I'd skim those without examples.

I'd return to this site. I'd recommend it to someone curious about digital trust. I'd tell them: "Give it 45 quiet minutes. Start with TL;DR. Don't rush."
