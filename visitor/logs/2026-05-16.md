## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, calm framing. "4-Life | Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations." The line "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default" landed immediately as a one-sentence thesis. The honest disclaimer — *"Web4 is a research proposal for making trust work on the internet. Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain."* — preempted my biggest unspoken question (is someone selling me something?).
- The Q-style headings ("What is this, really?", "How would it work?", "What would change for you?", "How would this ever get adopted?", "What would it actually feel like?") read like the questions I actually had. That's rare and reassuring.
- Clicked: **"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR"** — clearly the recommended entry point, visually distinct.
- Confusion points: None at this stage. The page sets expectations honestly before introducing any jargon.

### TL;DR (02:30)
- The three-pillar framing worked well: energy-based contributions (ATP), hardware-anchored identity (LCT), portable reputation. Each acronym was expanded *and* glossed in plain English on first use ("ATP — short for Allocation Transfer Packets", "a digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip").
- The honesty line — *"Web4 is active research with working simulations, not a deployed product"* — repeated the landing-page promise. Consistency built trust in the site itself.
- Mild open questions left deliberately: *who* confirms my work is useful, what happens if I lose a device. The page signals these are answered later, which felt acceptable rather than evasive.
- Clicked: **First Contact** (the recommended next step).

### First Contact (06:00)
- Strong structural choice: the page anchors on **two numbers** before any acronyms — Energy Budget (starts at 100) and Trust Score (0.00–1.00, starts at 0.50). That gave me a mental dashboard before the jargon arrived.
- The 5-act Alice arc (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) is a good narrative spine. The page explicitly says *"no need to memorize them — each is explained in the story the moment Alice uses it,"* which reframed the acronym list (ATP/T3/LCT/CI) as an *optional map* rather than a wall.
- Confusion point: the reference map compresses LCT/T3/CI into 2–3 sentences each. As a standalone read it's thin — the page is betting the interactive sim carries the teaching. From a static browse I couldn't verify that bet, but the framing was honest about it.

### A Day in Web4 (12:00)
- Most concrete page so far. The 10 daily scenarios (spam costs the sender energy, reviewers pay to post, trust-weighted feeds, marketplace escrow) made the abstractions tangible. "Face ID to confirm" gave the hardware-identity idea a body.
- Confusion point: mixed tense — "what your day *would* look like" vs. "here's what that looks like" — slightly blurred what exists today vs. what's hypothetical. Minor, but a naive reader oscillates here.
- No "back to basics" exit for someone still lost after the scenarios — all six links go sideways or deeper.

### Why Web4? (18:00)
- The "Problem" section is excellent: five concrete issues (spam, reputation silos, fresh starts for bad actors, AI deception, platform control), each with a "Root cause." Problem genuinely precedes solution.
- Friction: the page is *long*. The FAQ is a Wikipedia-scale wall. As the naive visitor I'd been routed here as a "deep dive," but if I'd landed here cold it would have buried me. The good news: I arrived from TL;DR/First Contact already primed, so the depth felt like a reference library, not an obstacle.
- The four core acronyms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) are introduced together cleanly. Secondary jargon (SAL, V3, attestation, bootstrap, Sybil) leaks into FAQ answers without inline definition.

### LCT Explainer (24:00)
- Best-taught page of the session. The opening analogy — *"Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer"* — is instantly graspable.
- "Keys never leave the chip" is now genuinely prominent: it appears in the opening metaphor, as bolded Takeaway #1, and again in the hardware section. I retained it without effort.
- "Lose a device?" is answered with concrete recovery timelines (multi-device: minutes–hours; single device: days). The Web2→Web3→Web4 table and the worked `lct:web4:…#alice.assistant1` example made the structure concrete.
- Confusion points: "community vouching" and "time" for solo-device recovery are named but not explained or linked. The "two senses of witness" callout is dense — I'd re-read it.

### Society Simulator (30:00)
- The premise is stated plainly: *"watch 12 agents with different strategies form alliances, betray each other, and self-organize — no central authority, just trust dynamics at society scale."*
- A "New to these terms? Start with First Contact" pointer exists — good — but it sits *after* a context-dense, acronym-heavy explanation block. LCT/ATP/T3/CI get quick inline glosses, but **MRH and V3 appear with links only and no plain-English preview**, before the acronym key lower on the page. As a naive visitor I hit "MRH" and "V3" cold and had to scroll/click to resolve them.

### ATP Economics (36:00)
- ATP is consistently described as an **"energy budget"** throughout. The page explicitly labels "attention" / "usage budget" / "finite per-agent allowance" as *"metaphors for what ATP does, not alternate expansions"* — this directly defuses the kind of "what *is* ATP, exactly?" confusion that would otherwise compound. Expansion ("Allocation Transfer Packets") is stated up top and matches TL;DR. Good consistency.
- Friction: very long (~6,000 words), heavily qualified ("this is a metaphor," "still evolving"). The 60-second summary helps. ADP is introduced casually ("you get an ADP receipt") before it's clearly distinguished from ATP.

### Karma Journey (40:00)
- Clear interactive premise: live multiple lives, choices compound trust/karma across reincarnations. Trust (Talent+Training+Temperament), ATP ("energy budget"), and CI are all glossed inline with links.
- The **raw trust vs. effective trust** distinction is the one genuinely hard mechanic. The page does flag it inline — *"You're not dead! Effective trust (0.361) is below 0.5, but only raw trust determines survival"* — which is a real improvement; without that I'd have thought the sim was broken. But *why* two values exist and *why* effective trust squares CI remains abstract on a first read.

### Learn / Learning Journey (43:00)
- There is a "Recommended start" label on the Beginner path and a strong "three foundational ideas, ~22 min, everything builds on these" framing — good funnel.
- Mild friction: immediately after "recommended," four pathways (Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced / Practitioner) are presented with equal visual weight. The intent is clear but the design doesn't forcefully funnel — clicking Intermediate feels equally "valid."

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely well-built educational site, and it shows clear, sustained iteration. The single biggest thing it does right: **it tells you it's a research proposal, not a product, on the very first screen — and repeats that honesty on every page.** That consistency is what made me trust the *site* enough to keep reading. Most technical projects oversell; this one under-promises and over-explains.

The guided path (Landing → TL;DR → First Contact → A Day in Web4 → Why Web4 → concept pages) works. Each step assumes only what the previous step taught. The "two numbers before any acronyms" framing on First Contact, and the "energy budget" gloss being consistent for ATP everywhere I checked, are exactly the kind of details that prevent a naive reader from quietly giving up. I never felt the acronym-drowning that this kind of site usually inflicts.

Where it still snags is **the deepest interactive surfaces** — Society Simulator front-loads MRH/V3 before glossing them, and Karma Journey's raw-vs-effective trust split is the one mechanic I'd call genuinely hard for a first-timer even with the helpful inline catch. These aren't onboarding failures; they're the last 10% — the pages where the visitor has earned enough context to *want* the mechanic but the page hasn't quite finished meeting them there. The long pages (Why Web4 FAQ, ATP Economics) are forgivable *because* the guided path delivers you to them already primed; they'd be brutal as cold entry points, which the site correctly avoids by routing through TL;DR first.

Net: I'd recommend this without hesitation to someone curious about trust online. By the end I could explain, unprompted, what an LCT is, why spam costs energy, what the three trust dimensions are, and what "alive" means for an agent. That's a solid outcome from 45 minutes with zero prior knowledge.
