## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, bold headline — "The Internet Has a Trust Problem." That hooks me.
- The four principles (hardware-bound identity, metabolic attention, permanent consequences, AI/human coexistence) are intriguing but dense for a first glance.
- "Fractal laboratory for Web4 societies" in the subtitle means nothing to me. What's fractal about it? Why laboratory?
- The recommended onboarding path (Why Web4 → First Contact → Playground) is helpful — I know where to go.
- Clicked: "Why Web4?" as the first recommended step.
- Confusion points: What is Web4? The landing page says it but doesn't explain it — it just lists properties. The recommended path implies I should click through, which is fine.

### Why Web4? (01:30)
- This page is excellent. The five problems are all things I recognize from daily internet experience — spam, reputation silos, bad actors getting fresh starts, AI making it worse, platform control.
- "Why Previous Solutions Failed" table is sharp and persuasive. CAPTCHAs, moderation armies, blockchain wallets — I've seen all of these fail.
- "What Would Actually Work?" section cleanly bridges from problems to requirements.
- The Web4 mechanism table (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) introduces four acronyms at once. I can hold them, but it's a lot.
- The "Honest Questions" FAQ is *really* good. The caveats ("This is still early-stage research," "A sufficiently motivated adversary can still attack") build trust. This doesn't feel like a sales pitch.
- Clicked: "Start First Contact" as the page suggested.

### First Contact (04:00)
- "Step 1 of 5" — good, progress indicator. But I never found steps 2-5 anywhere.
- "What You'll Learn" section (ATP, Trust, Consequences, Self-Regulation) is a good preview.
- "Start Simulation" button — I clicked it but nothing navigable happened. As a visitor I'd expect this button to take me to a simulation. If it triggers a client-side animation/walkthrough that requires JavaScript interaction, I can't confirm it works, but the page itself is a dead end without clicking that button.

### Playground (06:00)
- This is the first interactive thing that actually works! Sliders for Number of Lives, Ticks per Life, Risk Appetite, Starting ATP, Starting Trust, and Karma parameters.
- "Run Simulation" button and preset buttons (Easy Mode, Hard Mode, Risk-Averse, Risk-Seeking) — I'd try these.
- The Key Insights cards below (Metabolic Balance, Trust Accumulation, Karma Compression, Risk vs Reward) explain what I'm supposed to learn. Good.
- "Intentionally simpler than the full Web4 engine" — honest and appreciated.
- This is where I'd spend time actually playing. Good entry point.
- Confusion points: The "Technical Details" section mentions "EP learning" and "MRH contexts" without explanation. I don't know what EP is yet.

### LCT Explainer (08:30)
- The Web2 → Web3 → Web4 identity evolution is crystal clear. Passwords → wallet keys → hardware-witnessed tokens. Each step addresses the prior weakness.
- "What Is a Linked Context Token?" section is accessible — hardware-bound, witnessed, contextual proof of presence.
- The example LCT (`lct:web4:agent:alice.assistant1@Thor#perception`) is helpful but the syntax is opaque. What's Thor? Why #perception?
- The interactive device witnesses slider is great — visual demonstration of how more devices = more security.
- Security comparison across attack scenarios is very effective. Seeing Web4 survive attacks that destroy Web2/Web3 is compelling.
- **Then it gets technical FAST.** The full JSON LCT object structure, the Supported Hardware Platforms table, Standardized Task Types, Dual Signature Verification — this feels like it shifted from "explainer for curious humans" to "spec document for developers." The transition is abrupt.
- Confusion points: The JSON blob in Technical Details is intimidating. "Binding proof: cose:Sig_structure" — I have no idea what COSE is. Same for "eat:mb64" in hardware_anchor. This section needs a "you can skip this" callout or should be hidden more aggressively behind a collapsible.

### ATP Economics (11:00)
- Opening framing is strong: "Traditional Web: Spam is free → Web4: Every action costs ATP."
- The interactive simulator is simple and effective — four action buttons showing ATP costs and rewards. I can viscerally feel that spamming bleeds you dry while quality contributions grow your balance. Great design.
- Multi-Life simulation data showing ATP trajectories across four lives — this tells a story. Life 3's crisis at 15 ATP and recovery is compelling.
- ADP explanation (Allocation Discharge Packet) — the metabolic discharge counterpart to ATP. The biology analogy works well.
- "Why 'Metabolic Economics'?" — comparing to biological ATP/ADP. This is stated as "not a metaphor" but a "rigorous economic framework." That's a bold claim. I'm not sure I buy "not a metaphor" — it's clearly inspired by biology and the naming is metaphorical even if the math is rigorous.
- Confusion points: The ATP vs Traditional Tokens comparison table is good but raises a question: if ATP is non-transferable and non-tradeable, how do economies actually work? Can I pay someone for a service?

### Trust Tensor (13:30)
- The problem framing works: single-number trust scores (7/10) lose nuance. I agree.
- Five dimensions (competence, reliability, integrity, alignment, transparency) make intuitive sense.
- The interactive simulator with scenarios is great — I can see how "missed deadline" tanks reliability more than competence, and how "openly admitted mistake" can actually raise transparency. This is the kind of nuanced insight the page is trying to teach.
- The Candidate A vs Candidate B comparison (Expert vs Reliable) effectively demonstrates why multi-dimensional trust matters for decisions.
- The educational note about "simplified 5D model" vs "production 3D tensors (Talent, Training, Temperament)" is confusing. Am I learning the wrong model? Will the real system behave differently?
- Confusion points: T3 vs V3 distinction is mentioned in Technical Details. V3 (Value Tensor) rates resources/outcomes — this is new and unexplained. Adding concepts in the technical section of another concept's page is disorienting.

### How It Works (16:00)
- This might be the best page on the site. The Birth → Life → Death → Rebirth journey is a narrative I can follow.
- Each stage is concrete: Birth gives you 100 ATP and neutral T3. Life shows action costs and trust evolution tables. Death happens at ATP = 0. Rebirth requires T3 ≥ 0.5.
- The three-life example (Novice → Maturing → Established) tells a compelling story of growth across lifetimes.
- EP (Epistemic Proprioception) is mentioned for the first time here as "learning across lives." The name is forbidding but the concept is clear: agents learn from their mistakes.
- "Why This Design Works" section with four quadrants is a strong summary.
- Confusion points: This page is so good that I wish it came earlier in the journey. It ties everything together better than any other page. Should this be the second page after "Why Web4?"?

## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust-native internet infrastructure where identity costs something, actions have metabolic weight, and consequences persist
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — a simulation lab showing how Web4 mechanisms create self-regulating societies
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound, multi-witnessed identity tokens that make fake accounts expensive
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — metabolic economics where every action costs energy and quality earns it back; running out means death
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — multi-dimensional trust scores (competence, reliability, integrity, alignment, transparency) that resist gaming
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — alive when ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, and CI coherent; death at ATP = 0; rebirth requires trust ≥ 0.5
- [ ] Why any of this matters — I understand the *technical* why (spam dies, quality compounds, trust is earned) but not the *human* why. Who needs this? What's the first real use case? When do real humans benefit?

## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who is building this?** Is this one person's research project, a team, an organization? The site never says.
2. **What's the first real use case?** The system makes theoretical sense but where would it actually be deployed first? A chat app? A social network? An AI governance system?
3. **If ATP is non-transferable, how do economies work?** I understand spending ATP on actions and earning it from quality, but can Alice pay Bob for a service? How does trade work?
4. **What's the relationship between this site and the whitepaper?** Are they the same content at different depth levels? Does the whitepaper have more detail?
5. **What happened to First Contact Steps 2-5?** The page promises a five-step experience but only shows step 1 with a button that doesn't lead to the next step.
6. **Why three separate simulation tools?** Playground, Lab Console, and Society Simulator seem to overlap. Which should a newcomer use?
7. **How does federation actually work?** Multiple pages mention societies connecting, but I didn't find a detailed federation page.
8. **What does "fractal" mean in this context?** It's used repeatedly (fractal laboratory, fractal composition, fractal RDF structures) but never concretely defined for Web4.
9. **Am I learning the right model?** Multiple pages note the educational model differs from the canonical spec (5D vs 3D trust, simplified vs production). Should I trust what I learned here?
10. **How far along is this?** The FAQ says "theoretical with working simulations" and deployment is "multi-year." Is there a roadmap?

## Honest Assessment

The **Why Web4?** page and the **How It Works** page are genuinely excellent. They do what great explainer content should do: start with problems I recognize, build to solutions incrementally, and use concrete examples. The honesty in the FAQ (acknowledging limitations, caveats, and unsolved problems) builds real credibility.

The **ATP Economics** and **Trust Tensor** pages are also strong — the interactive simulators make abstract concepts tangible. I could feel the ATP draining from spam and see why multi-dimensional trust matters.

Where the site falters:

1. **The interactive payoff is missing.** The site promises simulation experiences (First Contact, Lab Console) that either don't work in-browser or require local Python setup. The Playground and Society Simulator work, but they're not prominently positioned. A visitor clicks "Start Simulation" on First Contact and hits a wall. That's the moment you lose people.

2. **Three simulation tools is confusing.** Playground (simple sliders), Lab Console (needs Python), and Society Simulator (multi-agent) each do different things but the site doesn't guide you to the right one. Consolidate or signpost.

3. **The glossary's advanced section is a different project.** "Synchronism Consciousness Arc," "Modal Awareness," "Cumulative Identity Context" with references to specific research sessions — this is internal research vocabulary, not a visitor glossary. It feels like two audiences (curious newcomers and deep researchers) got mashed together.

4. **I never learned why this matters to *me*.** I understand the technical elegance — spam dies, trust compounds, identity is hardware-bound. But who is the first user? What problem does a real person solve tomorrow by using Web4? "The internet has a trust problem" is a great opening, but by the end I still don't have a concrete "this is what your life looks like *with* Web4" story.

5. **The onboarding path has gaps.** The recommended path (Why Web4 → First Contact → Playground) is good in theory, but First Contact stalls at "Start Simulation" and the Learn page (which has a beautifully structured progression) isn't in the path at all.

Overall, this site has strong bones. The conceptual framework is coherent, the writing quality is above average for crypto/decentralization projects, and the honesty about limitations is refreshing. But the visitor experience has a few walls that would cause many people to bounce — especially the broken First Contact simulation and the Python-dependent Lab Console. Fix those two things and this becomes a significantly more compelling first visit.
