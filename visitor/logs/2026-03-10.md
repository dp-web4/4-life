## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a protocol where identity, energy, and trust replace centralized moderation
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — that these mechanics can produce self-regulating societies
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token, hardware-bound identity verified by witness devices
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — attention energy budget; actions cost it, quality contributions earn it back
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — multi-dimensional (Talent/Training/Temperament) role-specific trust scores
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — ATP depletion, trust collapse, or coherence failure
- [ ] Why any of this matters — I understand the spam problem clearly, but I'm not convinced Web4 is deployed or deployable at scale. The "why this matters *now*" didn't land.

## Unanswered Questions
1. Is this a real deployed system or purely a research prototype? The site doesn't say clearly enough.
2. What does "death" mean for a real human user in a deployed Web4 community — temporary lockout, permanent ban, or something else?
3. How are T3 trust scores actually generated? What behaviors trigger trust gains/losses?
4. Who are the "witnesses" in the LCT witness network — other users, devices, or a trusted authority?
5. What does "quality" mean concretely? The ATP economics page says recipients validate quality, but gaming this seems straightforward between friends.
6. Does losing all ATP in one community affect your identity in other communities?
7. "CI" — what does the C stand for in CI? I found out it's Coherence Index on the /aliveness page, but I passed three pages where it appeared unexplained.
8. Who built this and why? I know it's an "open research project, no company/token" (from the FAQ) but that section is buried deep.

## Honest Assessment

The site does something rare: it starts with a problem I actually care about (spam, fake accounts, deception) and the problem framing is excellent. The "Why Web4?" page is the strongest content on the site — honest, specific, and self-aware about what doesn't work.

The drop-off comes in the solution half. Once the proposed mechanisms appear, the acronym density spikes suddenly. ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI, SAL, ADP, V3 — eight abbreviations I encountered in roughly 40 minutes. Each has an explainer, which helps, but the first-encounter experience feels like being handed a glossary before you've had a chance to want one.

The Alice narrative in /first-contact is the site's best teaching tool and should come earlier, possibly before the acronym lists. The Society Simulator is also intuitively accessible — watching agents cooperate or defect is universally legible.

The largest unresolved confusion: I never got a clear sense of whether this is real or imaginary. The site oscillates between "research prototype with 47,000 lines of tested code" (specific and real-sounding) and simulation language ("agents live and die"). By the end I was uncertain whether I was being taught about a working protocol or playing a thought experiment. For a skeptical visitor, that ambiguity is deflating.

The honest FAQ stance is a genuine differentiator. Saying "we don't know yet, here's the honest risk" builds more trust with me than false confidence would. More of that, earlier.

Would I return? Probably — to explore the simulator. Would I recommend it? Only to someone already curious about identity/trust infrastructure, not to a general audience yet.
