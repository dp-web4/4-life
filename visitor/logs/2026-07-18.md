## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident. Headline **"Trust, built into the internet."** with subhead **"A proposed open standard for agentic AI governance."** I liked that the very first paragraph names a concrete, believable problem: *"AI agents already take real actions: they write code, move money, and make decisions. Today we cannot prove what an agent did, on whose authority, or by what rules."*
- The navigation is *big* — four groups (Start Here / The Onramp / Core Concepts / Going Deeper) with ~26 links total. As a newcomer this is slightly intimidating but the "Start Here" group told me where to begin.
- Three CTAs: "Web4 in 2 minutes", "See how it fits together", "Read the Web4 whitepaper".
- Clicked: "Web4 in 2 minutes" (the least-scary option).

### "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (/tldr) (03:00)
- Delivered on the promise — short, staged (The Problem → The Shift → Four Pieces → The Result → Honesty). The Web2/Web3/Web4 contrast landed well: *"trust is earned through witnessed behavior, not granted from above."*
- I appreciated the honesty box up front: Hestia "runs end to end today"; core standard "installable now"; others earlier-stage.
- **BUT**: the page opens by pledging *"Plain English first"* and *"every term is spelled out the first time it appears"* — then uses **"ontology," "primitives," "substrate," "hardware binding"** with no plain-English gloss. For the flagship 2-minute page, that broke the promise it had just made. I knew "substrate" from context but a non-technical friend would stall.

### Why Web4? (/why-web4) (07:00)
- Best-argued page so far. Problem → Why Previous Solutions Failed → What Would Work → What Web4 Proposes → Honest Questions. The four trust failures (spam is free, reputation is siloed, bad actors reset, AI makes verification harder) are relatable and non-jargony.
- The **"Honest Questions"** FAQ is huge (50+ collapsible Qs, topic index). Impressive depth; felt trustworthy.
- Friction: this is where the acronym firehose really starts — T3, V3, MRH, LCT, CI, ATP, SAL all appear. Each *is* introduced with its plain name, but seeing 7 acronyms in quick succession, none yet "seen in action," is a lot to hold. I trusted I'd meet them later rather than truly absorbing them here.

### First Contact / Alice (/first-contact) (11:00)
- This is the page that made it *click*. Driving Alice through five acts (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) while watching just **two** numbers (🔋 Energy/ATP starting at 100, 🤝 Trust starting at 0.50) is the right call — two metrics, not seven acronyms. Good pedagogy.
- Reassuring clarification: *"death means Alice's standing is suspended — she can't act until rebirth — not that her account is deleted."*
- Minor confusion: the boundary between "this interactive walkthrough" and "the actual deployed hestia/Hub code" is stated but still a bit fuzzy — am I watching a real system or a teaching simulation? (It's the latter, but I inferred that.)

### The Onramp (/onramp) (16:00)
- "One shape at three scales" is a genuinely good framing. The table — **hestia** (one person/agent), **the hub** (a community/society), **hardbound** (enterprise oversight) — made the three-scale idea land. Core Standard = the shared substrate underneath all three.
- Friction: "**RBAC**" appears in the hardbound description un-expanded. Also "the pieces compose" isn't self-evident — it turns out to mean "they all import the same canonical libraries," which I only got after reading further.

### LCT (/lct-explainer) (19:00)
- Strong opening analogy: *"your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated."* Analogy-before-definition throughout; collapsible crypto sections let me skip depth. Well built.
- Confusion: "witness" is used two ways (your own devices co-witnessing vs. network witnesses) — the page flags it, but I still had to slow down. And software-only identity capping at **0.50** — the same number described elsewhere as the "survival line" — read as contradictory until the page explained it means "zero margin, not dead on arrival."

### ATP/ADP (/atp-economics) (22:00)
- Very clear that ATP is **not money**: *"It's a usage budget, not a tradeable asset. No market, no price, no speculation."* The cellular-energy metaphor anchors it. Good.
- Friction (mild): the 60-second summary says *"high-quality work recharges fully,"* which reads like a *net gain*. Only later, in "Show me the math," did I learn *"the cap means you can't profit on a single action, only recover its cost,"* and that net-positive comes from a **separate payment channel**. The two statements are reconcilable (and the page does reconcile them), but the cheerful summary line lands *before* the caveat, so for a few seconds I thought "recharges fully" meant "come out ahead." A one-clause hedge in the summary ("...up to what you spent") would close the gap.
- Also noted (not a bug): real action costs are only ever shown as simulator examples (−5 spam, −20 high-value); no canonical "posting costs X" — which is honest given numbers are "still negotiating."

### Running Now (/running-now) (25:00)
- My favorite for tone. Blunt: *"Web4 is R&D, not a shipped product. Nothing on this page is a fielded production system."* Three badges (Spec / Reference / Running). Only **hestia** is "Running"; the other three are "Reference." This is exactly the honesty the rest of the site earns credit for. *"You do not have to take any of this on faith."*

### Trust Tensor (/trust-tensor) (27:00)
- The three dimensions (Talent / Training / Temperament, always role-contextual) are clear; surgeon-vs-mechanic examples help. Role weights are shown per-role (Data Analyst example 40/35/25), not as a universal, which is the right message.
- Confusion: the decay half-lives (30-day / 180-day) arrive without any earlier hint that trust *decays* at all — it's a new idea dropped mid-page. And the T3↔V3 relationship ("T3 grades Alice; V3 grades the tutorial") is dense on first read.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust earned through witnessed behavior, built into the internet's plumbing
- [x] What the onramp is (core standard, hub, hestia, hardbound) and how they fit — "one shape at three scales" over a shared substrate
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound verifiable presence certificate (phone-as-unforgeable-ID)
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — an energy *budget* you spend and earn back (explicitly not money); ADP is the auto-receipt
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three role-contextual numbers (Talent/Training/Temperament), not one score
- [x] What is real today vs R&D — only hestia is "Running"; everything else is spec/reference; site is refreshingly upfront
- [x] Why any of this matters — spam is free, reputation is siloed, bad actors reset, AI breaks verification

Full checklist achieved this session — the site is in good shape.

## Unanswered Questions
1. On /first-contact, is Alice's walkthrough driven by the *real* hestia/Hub engine, or a teaching simulation? I inferred "simulation" but was never told plainly.
2. What does an action *actually* cost in ATP in a real deployment? I only ever saw simulator examples. (Understandably unsettled — noted as honest, not a complaint.)
3. If software-only identity caps at 0.50 and 0.50 is the "survival line," can a software-only identity ever actually *do* anything, or is it permanently on the knife's edge? The page hinted "zero margin" but I wasn't sure of the practical consequence.

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely strong explainer site, and notably *honest* — the "Running Now" page and the "still negotiating" ATP disclosures buy a lot of trust. The pedagogy is well-sequenced when you follow the intended path (tldr → why → First Contact → onramp → concepts): the two-number Alice walkthrough is the emotional and conceptual center that makes the acronyms survivable.

My honest friction is mostly **jargon-timing**, not jargon-presence. The site keeps its promise to spell terms out — *except* on /tldr, where it makes the plain-English pledge most loudly and then leans on "ontology/primitives/substrate" anyway. That's the one place the site's own standard slipped, and because it's the 2-minute page a lot of first-time visitors will hit it. If I were to fix one thing today, it's that: make the flagship 2-minute page actually meet the bar it sets in its own opening line.

Everything else is minor ordering/prominence (the ATP summary line, the T3 decay surprise, the un-expanded RBAC). Nothing was broken; no dead links on any path I actually clicked. I'd come back — mostly because the honesty made me *want* to see whether the "Reference" pieces graduate to "Running."
