## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident headline — "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" with "explore Web4 through simulations" and the parenthetical "(trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default)." I immediately knew this was about online trust, not a product pitch. The honest line "Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain" lowered my defenses — I trust a site that tells me what it *isn't*.
- The "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" framing landed instantly. "Spam wins because accounts are free" — yes, I feel that.
- Five concepts shown (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) with acronyms in parentheses. The plain-English names did the work; the acronyms were just sitting there harmlessly.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" because it explicitly said "Recommended for first-time visitors." That's exactly what I wanted to be told.
- Confusion points: None at this stage. Good entry.

### TL;DR (03:30)
- This was the best page of my visit. Plain English first, acronyms second. "The Problem / The Idea / The Result" structure is exactly the right shape for an impatient newcomer.
- ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI all got a one-line plain definition on first mention. I felt oriented in under two minutes, as promised.
- It correctly funneled me onward: First Contact, Why Web4, A Day in Web4, Society Simulator, Karma Journey.
- Confusion points: Minor — I understood the *gist* of T3/MRH/CI but knew I'd need the linked pages for real understanding. That's acceptable for a TL;DR; it set honest expectations.

### First Contact (07:00)
- Engaging premise: "What if the system itself made [spam] impossible?" I wanted to see Alice's story.
- The five-act interactive arc (join → build → consequence → recover → karma carries forward) sounds genuinely good — a story, not a spec.
- Confusion points: It front-loads four acronyms (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) before the story starts. I hadn't internalized them from the TL;DR yet, so seeing all four again as a wall felt like being asked to memorize vocabulary before the movie starts. The "two numbers to watch" (Energy Budget, Trust Score) was a good anchor that rescued it.

### Why Web4? (14:00)
- "You already know this. You feel it every day." — strong opening. The five problems each with a root cause felt diagnostic, not ranty.
- But this page is **long**. After the four-mechanism explanation it opens into a FAQ with 40+ collapsed questions across seven groups. As a newcomer the sheer count made me think "wow, a LOT of people have objections to this" — slightly anxiety-inducing rather than reassuring.
- Confusion points: The page literally says "Don't worry about acronyms — they're just shorthand" and then uses ATP/LCT/T3/MRH/CI extensively in shorthand boxes. Telling me not to worry while showing me the thing I'm worried about is a small contradiction. Also: this page repeats that Web4 is "research, not production" — honest, but it slightly deflated the urgency the opening built up.

### Karma Journey (21:00)
- "Live multiple lives. Make choices. Watch how trust and karma compound across reincarnations." — intriguing hook.
- The definition of karma here is genuinely clarifying: "your actions in this life become starting conditions for the next." Not moralizing — mechanical. I liked that.
- ATP and CI defined inline with the 1.4x newcomer multiplier shown numerically. Good.
- Confusion points: The "raw trust vs. effective trust" distinction (raw determines survival, effective seeds next life) required me to re-read twice. And "CI²" — squaring the consistency score — felt like math homework dropped into a game. I understood *that* it mattered, not *why* it's squared.

### A Day in Web4 (27:00)
- This made it concrete. The onboarding walkthrough (download app 30s → face scan 60s → join community 2min → first post costs 3 ATP) felt like a real product tour. "No crypto wallet, no blockchain, no 24-word seed phrase" — that's the line that made me believe this could be usable.
- The clickable mockups (Mail, Talent, Reviews, Social, Market) with visible trust scores (0.87, 0.91) answered the question I'd been forming: "but what does it actually look like?"
- Confusion points: Here ATP is called just "energy" / "currency." But on the Society Simulator I'd later see it called "attention budget," and on the ATP page it's "Allocation Transfer Packets, a usage budget, not a tradeable asset." Three different framings of the same thing across three pages. As a naive reader this made me genuinely unsure: *is ATP energy, attention, or money?*

- The headline does good bridging work: "You saw how one agent builds trust. Now watch 12 agents..." It positioned itself as the next step up.
- I can press Run and watch colored nodes cluster without understanding anything — that's a nice low floor.
- Confusion points: This page was the most jargon-dense of my visit. The setup text labels ATP as "(attention budget)" — which directly contradicts the ATP page. It also references "MRH hop-decay" and "V3 output scoring" with no explanation. The "New to Web4? Start with First Contact" pointer is buried mid-page, *after* the acronym wall — exactly when I'd already be confused, not before.

### ATP Economics (39:00)
- Clear definition up front: "every action costs ATP. Every contribution others find valuable earns it back." The biology origin (adenosine triphosphate) was a nice mnemonic.
- The Sam (35% quality, loses 44) vs. Hannah (85%, loses 8) worked example made the ~7x quality multiplier click. Concrete numbers beat abstract claims.
- The "usage budget, not a tradeable asset" / not-cryptocurrency contrast was helpful and directly addressed my "is this money?" confusion — but it only resolved it *here*, after Day in Web4 and Society Simulator had already muddied it.
- Confusion points: ~2,500 words, long. The recharge formula (`confirmer_trust × received_value_fraction`) jumped into notation I didn't need on a first read.

### LCT Explainer (44:00)
- Best opening analogy on the site: "Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer." I understood the *what* immediately.
- The Web2 (passwords) / Web3 (private keys) / Web4 (hardware + behavior + attestation) contrast was a clean mental model.
- The witness explanation (laptop + phone vouch for each other; Tokyo login example; 1 device 50% → 3+ up to 90%) gave me intuition.
- Confusion points: Very long (~4,500 words). The crucial reason hardware matters — "keys never leave the chip" — is hidden in an expandable section I'd likely skip, so "secure chip" stayed abstract. "Tamper-evident creation record" is asserted but never explained — *what makes it tamper-evident?* And "witness" seems to mean two different things (my own devices vouching for each other vs. infrastructure nodes) without the page flagging the shift.

### Coherence Index (47:00)
- The most cleanly executed concept page. "Like a credit check that asks not just 'who are you?' but 'does your behavior make physical sense?'" — instantly grasped.
- Maria's stolen phone (CI drops to 0.55) and the bot farm (50 accounts with "correlated activity bursts" — real humans don't synchronize) are excellent concrete examples. This is the model the other concept pages should follow.
- Confusion points: Minor — `Effective trust = T3 × CI²` reappears here. The squaring still felt arbitrary on first read, but the geometric-mean explanation ("attacker must fake all four dimensions at once") gave me a *reason* the math is harsh, which Karma Journey didn't.

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely well-built educational site, and I'd recommend it. The TL;DR and Coherence Index pages are exemplary — plain English first, concrete scenarios (Maria's stolen phone, the bot farm, Sam vs. Hannah), and analogies that actually land. The honest "not a product, not a blockchain" framing earns trust early. By the end I could explain the core idea to a friend: online actions cost energy, identity is anchored in your devices, reputation is multi-dimensional and follows you, and consistency is checked against physical plausibility.

My single biggest friction was **terminology drift on ATP**. It's the most-mentioned concept on the entire site, and it's described as "energy," "currency," "attention budget," and "Allocation Transfer Packets / a usage budget, not a tradeable asset" — depending on which page I'm on. The Society Simulator literally labels it "(attention budget)" while the ATP page insists it's a usage budget and explicitly *not* tradeable. As a naive visitor, this is the one place I felt the site contradicted itself rather than just being dense. Everything else is a matter of length or sequencing; this is a coherence problem in the explanation itself, and it's on the concept the site leans on hardest. If one thing gets fixed before the next visitor, it should be this: choose a single canonical gloss for ATP and enforce it everywhere it first appears.

Secondary theme: the long concept pages (LCT, ATP, Why Web4) bury their single most important sentence inside expandables or deep scrolls. "Keys never leave the chip" is *the* reason hardware identity works, and I'd have missed it. The shorter pages prove the team knows how to be concise — the long ones just need a 60-second version up top.

None of this stopped me from getting a good mental model. The site over-delivers on honesty and concrete examples; it under-delivers on consistency and brevity. Fix the ATP framing and I'd move from "good" to "solid."
