## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean and confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the one-line clarifier — *"Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor"* — was genuinely reassuring. The "no blockchain, not a Web3 successor" preemptively answered the exact suspicion I had.
- The "Start Here" box gave me a clear single front door: *"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →"* labeled "ideal for first-time visitors." I appreciated that there was ONE obvious choice instead of a wall of equal options. Good.
- Two alternative entries sat beside it: *"What it looks like · 5 min"* and *"Frustrations it would fix · 3 min."* The first one (running software) tempted me — that turned out to be a trap for a newcomer (see below).
- Clicked: the 2-minute TL;DR, as recommended.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (02:00)
- This was the best page of the session. Plain-language problem statement (*"The internet has no memory. A spammer can create a new account in seconds."*) followed by four concrete solutions.
- It explicitly promised *"every term is spelled out the first time it appears"* — and it kept that promise. ATP, LCT, T3, CI all defined inline. As a naive reader I actually understood the gist by the end: behavior-based trust, energy-cost actions, hardware identity, portable reputation, same rules for AI and humans.
- This is the page that did the teaching. Everything good about my understanding came from here.

### Running Now (05:00) — went here too early
- I clicked "What it looks like · 5 min" from the landing page because seeing real software sounded exciting. As a total newcomer, this was the wrong move and the page didn't stop me.
- The message is great in principle: *"parts of it have left the whiteboard. The trust layer, the witness chain, sealed channels, and a working Web4 society are deployed today."* I liked knowing it's not vaporware.
- But the page assumes I already know LCT, T3/V3 tensors, MCP server, witness chain, sealed channels. I'd had zero of those explained yet (I'd only done the TL;DR). It reads like release notes for someone already inside the project.
- The maturity badges (Spec / Reference / Running) were the one helpful newcomer-friendly element.

### First Contact (10:00)
- This felt like the *right* second step (it's recommended on both the landing and TL;DR pages — I just clicked the wrong tile first). An interactive 5-act story where I drive "Alice" and watch her Energy (ATP, starts 100) and Trust (T3, starts 0.50) move.
- The two-metric focus is smart scaffolding. The previews of Energy Budget, Trust, Permanent Record, and Coherence Index were clear enough.
- Minor: acronyms still arrive fast (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) and "hardware-bound" / "karma" are used before I really had a grip on them. But the glossary link is right there and I'd just been primed by the TL;DR, so it was tolerable.

### Karma Journey (16:00)
- Bigger, denser, but mostly landed. The raw-vs-effective trust distinction was stated clearly up front this time:
  - Raw = *"(Talent + Training + Temperament) / 3 — Decides survival. Drop below 0.5 and this life ends."*
  - Effective = *"raw × CI² — Decides your karma tier at death (Honored / Neutral / Constrained)."*
- The death distinction was genuinely clear and was a highlight: Energy death (ran out of ATP, raw trust ≥ 0.5) is **recoverable**; Trust collapse (raw < 0.5) is **permanent** — *"the one door that doesn't reopen."* And the honest line *"the number alone can't tell them apart — which metric failed does"* resolved the exact confusion I would have had.
- Two lingering small things: (1) why effective trust *starts low* reads like a penalty until you slow down and realize it's just "no history yet"; (2) the CI² *squaring* is asserted but not intuitively motivated on this page — I accepted it without feeling why it's squared rather than linear.

### Hestia (21:00)
- Same problem as Running Now, sharper. The opening framing is actually nice — *"a small daemon that gives any entity — a person, an AI agent, an autonomous service — a real cryptographic presence on its own machine"* — keys you own vs. an account you rent. I got that.
- Then it dives into LCT, T3/V3 tensors, MCP server, TUI, policy gate, vault, witness chain, delegation — seven features listed with almost no grounding. "Phase 2," "Spec still" maturity labels meant nothing to me yet.
- The honest "Lost in the acronyms? Quick index →" link is a band-aid, not a fix. By the end I still couldn't answer *"what does this do for ME, today?"*

### The Hub (25:00)
- *"a single ~6 MB Rust daemon that turns a real community into a sovereign Web4 society."* Slightly better than Hestia because it explicitly connects back to the simulations: *"a society in this site's simulations is a swarm of agents forming trust"* and the Hub is that made real.
- But same core issue — LCT, Trust Tensor, sealed channels, hash-chained ledger all assumed. "Law is witnessed, not dictated" is a great slogan but the page never shows me *how* witnessing works. The seven roles are listed without telling me how they differ.
- Net: I believe real software exists. I do not understand what it does at the level the TL;DR taught me about the concept.

## Honest Assessment
The conceptual on-ramp is genuinely good now. The TL;DR taught me Web4 in two minutes as promised, the landing page's single recommended door removed decision paralysis, First Contact and Karma Journey built on that cleanly, and the death distinction — recoverable energy death vs. permanent trust collapse — was one of the clearest things on the whole site.

The weak link is the new "deployed reality" branch (Running Now → Hestia → The Hub). The *idea* of these pages is fantastic — proving this isn't just a thought experiment is exactly what a skeptic like me wants. But the *execution* assumes I've already absorbed the entire vocabulary, and the landing page actively invites me to click into them early ("What it looks like · 5 min") before I've learned any of it. The result: I came away believing real software exists, but unable to say what it does, in stark contrast to how well I understood the *concept*. These three pages need either a "read the basics first" gate for newcomers, or a thin layer of inline plain-language definitions so they don't read like changelogs for insiders.
