## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident, and the headline **"Trust, built into the internet."** landed well. The framing — AI agents already write code and move money but we can't prove what they did, on whose authority, or by what rules — is a problem I recognize immediately. Good hook.
- The one-line thesis is: *"Trust is earned through witnessed behavior, not declared by a platform and not assumed because someone holds a key."*
- **First snag, and it turned out to be the theme of my whole visit:** I don't know what "witnessed" *means* here. Witnessed by whom? A person? A machine? A network? The entire pitch hangs on this word and I'm asked to accept it in the first sentence.
- Clicked: "Web4 in 2 minutes" (the obvious first move).

### TL;DR / Web4 in 2 Minutes (03:00)
- The Web2 / Web3 / Web4 contrast is genuinely useful — "platforms declare reputation" vs "key-holders assumed trustworthy" vs "trust earned through witnessed behavior." That three-way split is the clearest single thing on the site.
- The four pieces (core standard / hub / hestia / hardbound) are named as substrate + three scales. Fine as a map.
- **"Witnessed" again used repeatedly, still not defined here.** Also met "ontology," and the acronym wall (LCT, T3, V3, MRH, ATP/ADP) as bare links. I clicked onward rather than chase every link.

### Why Web4? (07:00)
- Best problem framing on the site: spam is free, reputation is siloed, bad actors get infinite fresh starts, AI deception outpaces verification, platforms hold all the cards. I nodded at every one.
- The FAQ (40+ questions, collapsed) is honest and skeptic-friendly — it openly says this is research-stage and that bootstrapping is the hard part. I respect that.
- **Friction:** the five *solutions* (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) get thrown at me in quick succession before I have any grip on them. It reads like a summary written for someone who already finished the site. The page itself told me to go to First Contact instead — so I did.

### First Contact (12:00)
- This is the highlight. An interactive 5-act story where I steer "Alice" — she joins, builds reputation, makes a mistake that costs trust, recovers, and then "dies" and is reborn with karma. Watching the ATP meter and trust score move in response to choices taught me more than any prose page.
- **Confusion:** Act 5's "death & reborn" with a *permanent record* mechanic arrives without foreshadowing. Even with a spoiler warning, the jump from "reputation" to "permanent immortal history" was abrupt.
- Also: the page mixes the browser walkthrough (simulation) with references to real deployed tools (hestia, the hub). I couldn't always tell what was a teaching sim vs. something that actually exists.

### ATP Economics (18:00)
- **Strongest concept page.** The cell-biology origin (ATP = the molecule your cells spend to do work) is a great mnemonic, and the page is emphatic and repetitive in the right way: *"budget, not wealth — not money you can hoard or cash out. No market, no price, no speculation."* After Alice's meter, this clicked completely.
- The quality ramp with the Sam-vs-Hannah worked example (identical 50-ATP task, −44 vs −8 net) made "quality pays" concrete. 7× gap is memorable.
- Minor: the recharge-vs-payment distinction is dense, and "confirmation" as a UX action stays a little abstract, but neither blocked me.

### Running Now (22:00)
- Refreshingly honest: *"Nothing on this page is a fielded production system, and this page will not pretend otherwise."* Maturity badges (Spec / Reference / Running) help.
- I now understand: core primitives + hestia are installable/runnable today; the hub is a complete MVP binary but not in production; hardbound and federation are prototype/spec.
- Slight blur: hestia is badged "Running" yet two of its core features are "honestly still early" (hardware binding deferred, AI vault initial). The badge felt a touch more finished than the fine print.

### The Onramp (26:00)
- The "one shape at three scales, all speaking the same vocabulary" line is a good synthesis of the four pieces.
- **Real friction — the word "onramp" wobbles.** On this one page it seems to mean (a) the whole 4-Life *website*, (b) *this specific page*, and (c) *getting started with Web4 itself*. And I still can't cleanly state the relationship between "4-Life," "Web4," and "the onramp." Is 4-Life the site that teaches Web4? Is the onramp the four pieces, or the site, or this page? Not stated plainly up front.
- **New jargon spotted:** "R6/R7" appears here with no gloss at all — it's not even in the acronym set the rest of the site has been teaching me (LCT/T3/V3/MRH/ATP). I have no idea what it is.

### LCT Explainer (30:00)
- Good opening analogy: "your phone becoming your driver's license — one that can't be photocopied or faked." The coffee-shop-login and device-loss examples are concrete.
- **Same core friction:** "witnessed presence" is in the title and used early, but its plain meaning arrives late — and here it splits into *two* senses (your devices co-witnessing each other vs. network witnesses) that I kept conflating.
- TPM/Secure-Enclave/FIDO2 do get a friendly collapsible section, but a naive reader meets "hardware attestation" / "cryptographic binding" before they'd think to open it. Rewards non-linear reading.

### Glossary + Trust Tensor (33:00)
- I went to the glossary specifically to resolve "witness," and **there it's defined well**: *"to witness is to observe and attest to an action, and witnesses stake their own reputation when they do."* That's exactly the sentence I needed on page one. The device-co-witness vs network-witness split is also cleanly separated here.
- Trust Tensor page is now strong: *"T3 measures who you are; V3 measures what you produce."* "You don't need the math… a tensor is just a 3-axis score." The Alice-as-analyst-vs-mechanic example nails role-specificity. (Notably, the T3/V3 confusion the *Why Web4* page warned me about is fully resolved *here* — it's just resolved two pages too late.)

## Unanswered Questions
1. At the moment I read "trust earned through witnessed behavior" on page one — **who or what is the witness?** I only found out at the glossary, near the end.
2. What are **R6 and R7**? They showed up on the onramp page like I should already know.
3. Is **4-Life** the same thing as **Web4**, or the site *about* Web4, or one of the four pieces? I inferred "the site that teaches it" but was never told.
4. In the story, "death" felt dramatic — but in practice, does an agent hitting zero ATP really *terminate*, or just go dormant? The story and the ATP page use "death" slightly differently.

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely good site now — noticeably more coherent than I expected from the acronym-heavy first impression. The arc that works is: **First Contact (feel it) → ATP Economics (the model clicks) → Running Now (it's honest about what's real)**. Those three pages carried my understanding.

But my whole visit had one quiet fault line running through it: **the word "witness."** It's the load-bearing beam of the entire pitch — it's in the headline, the TL;DR, the LCT page — and yet the crisp, perfect definition lives only in the glossary, which is the *last* place a linear reader arrives. Every page assumed I already knew what witnessing was; I only truly did at minute 33. Fix that one thing at point-of-first-read and the landing page goes from "intriguing but hand-wavy" to "oh, I get it" in a single sentence.

The secondary irritant is the site's own name-of-itself: "onramp" is doing too many jobs, and the 4-Life / Web4 / onramp trio is never disentangled in plain words up front. It didn't stop me, but it's the kind of low hum of confusion that makes a curious person slightly distrust their own comprehension.

Net: I'd come back, and I'd send a skeptical friend here — but I'd tell them "read the glossary entry for *witness* first, then start."
