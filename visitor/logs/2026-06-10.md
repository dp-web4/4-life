## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean and confident. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations" plus the sub "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" told me immediately this is about fixing online trust. The line "no blockchain, no tokens, no speculation" was *reassuring* — it pre-empted my biggest suspicion (is this another crypto thing?). Good move.
- **Clicked**: I saw "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" and went there. The explicit "Start here" is exactly what a confused newcomer needs — I didn't have to guess.

### TL;DR / Web4 in 2 Minutes (03:00)
- The problem hook landed hard: "The internet has no memory. A spammer can create a new account in seconds." That's a problem I *feel*, so I was hooked.
- The four-mechanism framing (energy to post, hardware identity, earned trust, same rules for AI and humans) is clean and concrete.
- **First friction**: acronyms started appearing — T3, MRH, CI — flagged with links but not all defined inline. As a true newcomer I noted them as "things I'll have to look up later," which is a small tax on every paragraph.
- The Web4 (protocol) vs 4-Life (lab) distinction took me a second read to parse.

### Why Web4? (07:00)
- The opening — "You already know this. You feel it every day." — is excellent. It validated my frustration before selling me anything.
- The five problems (free spam, siloed reputation, infinite fresh starts, AI making it worse, platforms control everything) all felt real, not theoretical.
- **Friction**: this page is *enormous* — a 66+ question FAQ across 7 categories. The page itself warns "This is reference depth, not a read-through," which is honest, but as a newcomer I wasn't sure how much I was *supposed* to read. I bounced off after the problem framing.

### First Contact (12:00)
- A controllable narrative ("Alice's story") with two anchor numbers: 🔋 Energy Budget (ATP, starts 100) and 🤝 Trust Score (0.00–1.00, starts 0.50). The two-number focus is the friendliest thing I've seen so far — concrete and trackable.

### ATP Economics (17:00)

- **Confusion**: ATP vs ADP took multiple reads. ADP as "spent-energy receipts" is a subtle distinction.
- **Possible inconsistency**: here CI seemed to be framed as a "Consistency score," but the glossary and TL;DR call it the "Coherence Index." Same acronym, slightly different gloss across pages — small but the kind of thing that erodes a newcomer's confidence they're learning one consistent system.

### LCT Explainer (22:00)

- LCT = Linked Context Token, explained part-by-part. The Web2/Web3/Web4 comparison (memorize / store on disk / witnesses verify) was helpful.
- **My biggest worry as a human — "what if I lose my phone?" — was directly addressed** (multi-device recovery in minutes-hours; social recovery in 3–7 days). That built a lot of trust. Privacy concerns are also acknowledged honestly rather than waved away.
- **Confusion**: two meanings of "witness" (my own devices vouching vs network nodes verifying) — the page admits this is confusing, and it is. Also crypto jargon (TPM, Secure Enclave, Endorsement Key) is behind collapsibles, which I might hit before realizing help is there.

### Glossary (28:00)

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely well-made explainer, and it gets two hard things right: it **leads with problems I actually feel**, and it's **radically honest about its own weaknesses** (the "What Could Go Wrong" page flipped me from skeptical to interested). The best individual moments — the ATP biology metaphor, the phone-as-driver's-license image, the surgeon trust example, the lost-phone recovery answer — are excellent and made concepts click instantly.

The dominant friction is **acronym density**. ATP, LCT, T3, CI, MRH, SAL, V3 are everywhere, and on most pages they appear *before* they're explained. The site clearly knows this — there are "no need to memorize" reassurances and a superb "Acronyms at a glance" glossary index — but that index is hidden on the glossary page, so I spent 28 minutes wishing for it before I found it. If that escape-hatch were one click from every concept page, the whole experience would feel dramatically lighter.

The single thing I'd fix first is the **Aliveness page's human-vs-agent ambiguity**. "Death" is the most dramatic, attention-grabbing concept on the site, and I finished the page genuinely unsure whether it was talking about *me* or about software agents. For a framing that emotionally loaded, leaving the "does this happen to me?" question open is the highest-leverage gap to close.

Net: a curious, patient person leaves with a real mental model and a favorable impression. An impatient one bounces on jargon before the payoff. The content is strong; the remaining work is almost entirely about **lowering the cost of the first 20 minutes** — surfacing the glossary, standardizing acronym expansions, and answering "is this about me?" sooner.
