## Understanding Gained

- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust-native protocol, hardware-anchored identity, energy economy, multi-dim reputation, all four properties must work together
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — a working interactive lab for these ideas, not a product
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound, witnessed identity token; expansion learned (Linked Context Token); format learned but two competing examples weakened it
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget and its spent-receipt counterpart; biology analogy understood; backing mechanism still fuzzy
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3 = Talent / Training / Temperament, role-specific, multi-dimensional
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — three criteria (ATP > 0, T3 > 0.5, CI coherent); recoverable vs. permanent death; synthon extension to groups
- [x] Why any of this matters — Five Problems / Previous Failures table on /why-web4 is the clearest motivation; I now have a one-sentence story I could tell a friend

- What M-R-H stands for as letters
- How "trust ceiling" and "trust score" relate (inferred: ceiling caps the score, but the page never says it directly)
- Why brand-new members aren't born already at the threshold of permanent death

## Unanswered Questions

1. **What does MRH stand for?** Every other acronym got told to me; this one didn't. The omission feels deliberate or embarrassed.
2. **How is ATP issuance actually pegged to "real resources"?** I get that the pool is fixed at society formation, but the "anchored to CPU, storage, attention" line needs a mechanism. Otherwise it reads as faith.
3. **What protects new members from instant permanent death?** If trust starts at 0.5 and 0.5 is the permanent-death floor, a single bad early interaction (or just being misjudged by recipients while you have no track record) would be terminal. The math as stated breaks the onramp.
4. **Are LCT formats `lct:web4:0x...#label` and `lct:web4:agent:name@device#scope` two views of the same thing, or different formats for different entity types?** The site uses both.
5. **What does the appeals mechanism actually look like, and what's the gap between "designed" and "deployed"?** The /aliveness page admits one exists in spec but isn't built. How big is that gap?
6. **Why "Web4" as opposed to "Web3.5" or just "the trust protocol"?** /why-web4 answers this in the FAQ but I'd want it surfaced earlier, given the natural skepticism toward serially-numbered web epochs.
7. **What's the relationship between T3 (trust) and V3 (value)?** Both are tensors with three components; they appear on the same pages but I'm not sure if they're independent, correlated, or one drives the other.

## Honest Assessment

This is the most intellectually serious project website I've browsed in a while. The "honest caveat" / "this is unproven" / "this hasn't been tested with real humans" voice is consistent across pages, and it pays off — by the time I hit the social-credit FAQ entry I trusted the site to actually answer the question rather than spin. That's rare.

The content quality is high and the page hierarchy is sane. The friction isn't conceptual confusion (the *ideas* are clear) — it's **acronym front-loading**, **inconsistent format examples**, and **a couple of math/onboarding paradoxes that any careful reader will notice**.

Specifically: the TLDR oversells how brief it is, the LCT page has two competing format examples on one screen, the ATP backing story has a hand-wave at exactly the spot a skeptic would press hardest, and the aliveness math creates a "new members are born already dead" paradox that should not exist on a site whose whole premise is hard-edged about consequences.

If I were the project's worst critic, I'd say: the site is overconfident about its acronym density (one of the project's better moves — the "no blockchain" framing — is undone by introducing more proprietary three-letter codes than crypto did) and slightly under-confident about its actual claims (the "honest caveat" tone is good, but a few of the caveats are doing the work that real specification should do).

If I were the project's best friend, I'd say: this is what an explainer site should look like. Most projects at this stage either pretend to be more polished than they are or vanish into jargon. 4-Life manages to be candid, motivated, and falsifiable, which is the rare and valuable combination.

I'd come back. I'd send it to someone who likes ideas. I would *not* send it to my non-technical relatives — the acronym wall in the first five minutes would lose them, and that's specifically the audience the "plain English" framing is courting. The TLDR and the landing-page acronym list are doing the most damage to the people the site most wants to reach. Pulling those back to two acronyms each and deferring the others to their dedicated pages would, I think, fix the worst of it.
