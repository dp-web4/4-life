## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean, confident. "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" — the tagline "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default" actually told me something concrete, which is rare for a landing page. The "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" framing (spam wins because accounts are free, reputation trapped in silos) immediately made me care.
- The five plain-language ideas (Identity / Energy / Trust / Neighborhood / Consistency) before any acronyms — good. I didn't feel jargon-slapped.
- I liked the time-budgeted entry points ("2-minute TL;DR", "What it looks like · 5 min", "Frustrations it would fix · 3 min"). It respected that I didn't know how much to invest yet.
- "Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain… a research proposal" — appreciated the honesty, set my expectations.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR".

### Web4 in 2 Minutes / TL;DR (03:00)
- This was the best page for me as a newcomer. Every acronym got a one-line plain definition: ATP = "personal energy budget", LCT = "digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip", T3 = Talent/Training/Temperament, MRH = trust neighborhood (3 hops), CI = "does your behavior add up over time".
- It explicitly says "active research with working simulations, not a deployed product" — clear.
- It pointed me at three next steps. I picked "First Contact" because "7 min interactive following a character" sounded like the gentlest on-ramp.

### First Contact (07:00)
- "Your First Web4 Experience". The Alice / 5-act framing is inviting. Two tracked numbers (🔋 Energy Budget / 🤝 Trust Score, starting 100 and 0.50) are a strong, simple mental model.
- Mild friction: the four acronyms (ATP, T3, LCT, CI) reappear in quick succession in the concept blurbs. Because I'd just read the TL;DR I was fine — but if I'd landed here first (a plausible path; it's recommended directly), I'd have been underwater. The page leans on the TL;DR having been read.
- Couldn't actually run the walkthrough (I only read pages), so I can't judge the interactive part — but the setup made me want to.

### Why Web4? (12:00)
- "The Problem Before the Solution". The four root causes (no cost to action, platforms own reputation, free resets, AI agents defy verification) and the "previous fixes treated symptoms" line landed well.
- Acronyms are introduced plain-English-first here, consistently. Good.
- Friction: this page is enormous. The FAQ alone has dozens of questions across seven categories. As a first-timer I bounced off it — it reads like a reference document, not a "why" page. I also noticed "CI" gets used heavily in the FAQ but I don't recall it being introduced in the main body here (I only knew it from the TL;DR). "V3" and "VCM" show up in answers cold.

### Verified Presence / LCT Explainer (17:00)
- Best analogy on the site: "your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated." Immediately got it.
- "The chip's private key is generated inside the silicon and physically can't leave it" — this is the sentence that made hardware identity click for me. Tamper-evident chaining and attestation were explained without drowning me in crypto.
- Lingering gap: it promises a setup visual / "first 5 minutes" but never shows what the actual experience looks like. I still can't picture using this as a real person — no screenshots, no UI. This is the one thing I most wanted and didn't get.

### ATP Economics (22:00)
- Heading: "ATP: Allocation Transfer Packets — Every Action Has a Cost". ADP-as-receipt, the quality ramp (7x for good work), the 5% burn, rolling-window recharge — all solid and concrete.
- **Friction (this surprised me):** every other page consistently told me ATP = "energy budget". This page opens by telling me "attention", "energy", and "budget" are *all* metaphors for the same thing and uses all three interchangeably. After being trained sitewide on one word, the canonical page reintroduces three. The upfront "they're all metaphors" disclaimer softens it, but it still made me re-ask "wait, so what *is* ATP, exactly?" — which is the exact question the dedicated page should put to rest.

### Trust Tensor (T3) (26:00)

### Society Simulator (30:00)

- Friction: that card uses **MRH** and **V3** as bare acronyms, and the page's own acronym key only covers ATP/T3/LCT/CI — so the page references two terms its own key doesn't define. If I land here directly (it's a top-nav Interactive Lab), MRH/V3 are unexplained.

### Aliveness (33:00)
- "Professional license" analogy for needing ATP + T3 + CI is excellent. Energy death (recoverable, "going bankrupt") vs trust death (permanent, "losing your license") is a clean, memorable split. The "suspended license, not account deletion" line answered the stakes question well.

### Karma Journey (36:00)

### How It Works (38:00)

- Friction (recurring feeling across the whole site): the tone oscillates. "You're born with energy and neutral trust" reads like a live system; "the simulations let you explore how it *would* work" reads hypothetical. The "research prototype, not a deployed product" line is here but buried mid-page, after several sections of mechanics described in the present tense. By the time I hit it I'd half-internalized this as a working product.

### MRH Explorer (40:00)

- Friction: "Markov property" and "O(d³) vs O(n)" appear without being earned. For a novice those are speed bumps; the analogy carried the concept fine without them.

## Honest Assessment
This is a genuinely well-built explainer. The on-ramp works: TL;DR → First Contact → concept pages is a coherent path, and the best pages (LCT's driver's-license analogy, How It Works' 3am-crypto-spam coherence example, Aliveness' professional-license framing, Karma's raw×CI² rationale) are excellent — concrete, honest, and they tell me *why*, not just *what*. I came in a blank slate and left able to explain Web4 to someone else at a high level. That's a real achievement for a topic this abstract.

The friction is no longer about *missing* explanations — it's about *consistency and entry-point independence*. Three things stood out:

1. The ATP page, the one page that should be the definitive source, slightly muddies ATP by leading with three co-equal metaphors right after the rest of the site has carefully trained me on one. It's a small thing but it's on the worst possible page to have it.
2. "Is this real or simulated?" still flickers, mostly because mechanics are narrated in the present tense before the prototype disclaimer appears.
3. Several pages assume I arrived via the recommended path. First Contact and Society Simulator are both promoted as direct entry points but lean on acronyms a cold visitor wouldn't have.

None of these are severe. The site is past the "is it comprehensible" stage and into the "is it consistent and robust to any entry point" stage — a good problem to have. Fix the ATP page's opening framing and add an upfront prototype badge on How It Works, and this would be close to airtight for a naive reader.
