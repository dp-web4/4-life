## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00–02:00)
- **First impression**: Clean. Headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" + "explore Web4 through simulations." I do not know what Web4 is and the page does not tell me above the fold. I see "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" — okay, that's a hook.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" because it was the smallest commitment.
- **Confusion**: "Trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default" sounds nice but I cannot picture what it actually means. Is this a browser? A protocol? A thought experiment? The "Lab" framing is the only signal that it's research, and I almost missed it.

### /tldr (02:00–05:00)
- I was promised "2 minutes." I got six acronyms — ATP, ADP, LCT, MRH, CI, T3 — plus the claim that "every online action had a cost, every identity is tied to physical hardware, and your reputation followed you everywhere — permanently."
- **First flinch**: "Reputation followed you everywhere — permanently." Wait, that's the *pitch*? My 18-year-old-self mistakes follow me forever and I'm supposed to be excited?
- **Second flinch**: "Hardware-anchored identity" → I have to buy something to use the internet?
- The page does define the acronyms upon first use, credit where due. But six acronyms in 2 minutes is a wall, not a TL;DR. I almost bounced.

### /why-web4 (05:00–11:00)
- This page worked harder. The "five problems" framing (spam wins, reputation siloed, fresh starts, AI deception, platform lock-in) is concrete and I recognized them as real.
- The "Honest Q&A" with 40+ questions is genuinely impressive. Specifically the "isn't this just a social credit system?" question being asked openly — that earned trust from me.
- **But**: "5 min" claimed reading time vs. 40+ Q&A items is a lie. This page is a 30-minute commitment if I actually read the FAQ. The progressive disclosure pattern (collapsed Q&As) saves me, but the time-claim feels like it.
- Verdict: I left with cautious interest, not enthusiasm.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **Do humans face permanent death in Web4, or only AI agents?** The aliveness page hedges. This is THE question for any human contemplating opting in.
2. **How is T3 actually calculated?** Three dimensions × role-context — but what's the formula? What are the inputs?
3. **Who judges "meaningful" vs "low-quality" contribution?** Energy economics depends on this judgment but the judge is unnamed.
4. **What stops coordinated score attacks?** Five enemies who all score me low could push me below 0.5. Mentioned in FAQ but not on /trust-tensor itself.
5. **Why is "permanent reputation" framed as a benefit when most ethical traditions value forgiveness, growth, and second chances?** The decay mechanism reduces but does not erase.
6. **If software-only devices cap at 0.50 trust and 0.50 is the death threshold, isn't every software-only user one bad day away from permanent death?**
7. **Who runs a "society"? Can I start one? Can I be denied membership?** The "starter grant from society's pool" implies gatekeeping but I don't see who the gatekeepers are.
8. **What's the migration story?** If Web4 demands hardware anchoring and 0.50-trust software floors, how does it onboard existing internet users without locking out billions?
9. **Why not focus on solving the ATP allocation problem in existing systems first?** If energy economics are the core innovation, they could be retrofitted onto existing platforms before requiring a whole new stack.
10. **How does the simulator's 12-agent equilibrium scale to 8 billion?** /what-could-go-wrong admits this is untested. So what is the simulator actually showing me?

## Honest Assessment

The site is more sophisticated than I expected and more honest than I'd seen on most "new internet" projects. The /what-could-go-wrong page is a remarkable artifact — it argues against itself with a clarity I rarely see in research projects, and that earned my trust more than any of the explainer pages did.

But the explainer pages have a common failure mode: **they hide the costs that the /what-could-go-wrong page admits exist.** The /trust-tensor page does not warn me about coordinated score attacks. The /aliveness page does not tell me whether *I*, a human, can permanently die. The /lct-explainer page does not flag that software-only LCTs sit at the death threshold. Each page individually presents a clean story; the full picture only emerges if I read all the pages and connect the dots myself.

This is a structural problem. The site is honest in aggregate but optimistic per-page. A naive visitor who clicks through 2-3 pages will get a flattering picture; a skeptical visitor who clicks through all 11 will get a realistic one. The skeptical reading should be the default.

Concrete fix that would change my assessment: **inline the /what-could-go-wrong findings into the deep-dive pages.** When /trust-tensor explains how trust scores work, it should say RIGHT THERE: "What can go wrong: coordinated attacks, witness corruption, untested appeals." When /aliveness explains permanent trust death, it should say RIGHT THERE: "Whether humans face this is unresolved." When /lct-explainer says software-only is fine, it should say RIGHT THERE: "Caveat: 0.50 cap sits at the death threshold."

The vision is intriguing — energy economics + hardware identity + portable reputation is a coherent design that names real problems. But the design choices have life-and-death consequences (in the framework's own terms) and the friction surface around those consequences is too low. A trust-native internet should be aggressively transparent about its trust-determination mechanisms. This site is *eventually* transparent. It needs to be transparent first.

I leave neither convinced nor dismissive. I leave wanting to see the appeals mechanism deployed and tested before I would consider participating in a Web4 society — and I leave thinking the site itself probably agrees, given /what-could-go-wrong. The site's best argument is its own admission of what it has not yet solved. Lead with that.
