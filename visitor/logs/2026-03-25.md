## Unanswered Questions
1. **What does the actual UI look like in practice?** — The Day in Web4 wireframes are conceptual mockups. Is there a working prototype with a real interface? Even a video demo would help.
2. **How does the system handle cultural differences in "quality"?** — What counts as a quality post varies wildly between a coding forum and a poetry community. How does V3 scoring adapt?
3. **What happens during internet outages or unreliable connectivity?** — If devices can't witness each other due to network issues, does your CI drop unfairly? How does the system handle developing-world connectivity?
4. **How do communities migrate from existing platforms to Web4?** — The 5-tier adoption pathway mentions "Wrapper" as tier 1, but what does that look like practically? Who builds the wrapper? What's the incentive for existing platforms?
5. **What about children and minors?** — Hardware-bound identity for a 13-year-old raises different questions than for adults. Is there a family/guardian model?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoughtfully constructed educational websites I've encountered for a technical research project. The layered approach — TL;DR for the impatient, Why Web4 for the curious, interactive simulations for the hands-on learner — respects different learning styles and time budgets.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- The TL;DR page is a model of concise communication
- Day in Web4 wireframes make abstract concepts tangible
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page is devastatingly honest and builds more credibility than any feature list
- Interactive elements (Trust Tensor simulator, Karma Journey, Society Simulator) let me learn by doing
- The concept sequence (LCT→ATP→T3→MRH→CI→Aliveness) builds knowledge incrementally
- Plain English first, acronyms second throughout

**What could be better:**
- The Learn page should be a primary entry point, not something I discovered late in my journey
- Some concept pages have advanced technical sections (identity hierarchy, RDF/SPARQL, energy states) that could be better hidden behind progressive disclosure
- The effective vs. raw trust distinction on the Karma Journey needs inline explanation
- "Your Internet" page has the right idea but needs more depth — it's the weakest of the core pages

**Overall impression:** This project takes itself seriously without taking itself too seriously. The willingness to say "we don't know" and "this is unsolved" is rare and refreshing. The interactive simulations are the killer feature — they let me form intuitions that reading alone couldn't provide. I came in skeptical ("another Web3 rebrand?") and left genuinely curious about whether this could work. The 77-page depth is impressive but never felt like it was trying to overwhelm me — every page I visited felt intentional and well-crafted.

The biggest compliment I can give: I didn't feel like I was being sold something. I felt like I was being invited to think about something.
