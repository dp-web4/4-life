## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clear and unusually honest. "Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain" told me what this *isn't* before I had to guess, which I appreciated. The five-idea summary (Identity / Energy / Trust / Neighborhood / Consistency) gave me five hooks to hang things on, and each one had a plain-English gloss rather than an acronym.
- **Clicked**: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" — the site explicitly recommends one entry point instead of dumping nine, which made the choice easy.
- **Confusion points**: One phrase snagged me and kept snagging me for the rest of the session: "Neighborhood (limited reach: 3 hops maximum)." I filed it away as a hard rule. It turned out not to be one (see MRH Explorer below).

### 2-Minute TL;DR (04:00)
- Genuinely a two-minute read. The spam framing — "The internet has no memory. A spammer can create a new account in seconds" — is the single most effective sentence on the site. It made me want the rest.
- All five acronyms (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI) get defined here. Defining them *before* the concept pages is the right call; I never hit a concept page cold.
- **Confusion**: none worth reporting. This page works.

### Why Web4? (09:00)
- This is where I decided the project was serious rather than a whitepaper stunt. The FAQ admits things I expected it to hide: "there is no live network with real users yet, and nothing on this site is a live product." It concedes that sophisticated collusion "remains an open research problem," that billion-user scale is "unproven," that requiring security hardware creates barriers for the unbanked, and that permanent reputation has "no clean" reconciliation with GDPR deletion rights.
- I trust a project more when it tells me where the body is buried. This page bought a lot of goodwill that later pages spent.
- **Confusion**: The equity concession ("requiring security hardware creates barriers") landed as a *social* problem. Later, on the LCT page, I discovered it may also be a *mechanical* one. More below — this is my top friction point.

### First Contact (14:00)
- Alice's five-act story is the best teaching artifact here. Watching trust rise to 0.56 on good work, fall to 0.48 after spam, and climb back to 0.62 made the mechanics concrete in a way the concept pages didn't.
- The reassurance that "'death' here means Alice's standing is suspended... not that her account is deleted" arrived exactly when I needed it.
- **Confusion**: This page told me that above 0.50 trust I get full access and below it my features are restricted "until rebuilt." Alice sits at **0.48** in Act 3 and simply... recovers. So I learned: 0.5 is an access threshold. Then Aliveness told me 0.5 is a *death* threshold. I never fully reconciled these.

### Aliveness (22:00)
- Three fatal conditions, cleanly listed: ATP hits 0 ("no grace period"), trust falls below 0.5 and stays, CI drops below ~0.5. The distinction between recoverable energy death and permanent trust death is a genuinely interesting idea — you can run out of gas and come back, but if the society decides you're untrustworthy, it "rejects rebirth."
- The 5-turn survival game made the coupling click: low coherence raises *all* your ATP costs, so incoherence quietly starves you.
- **Confusion (major)**: Alice was at 0.48 and lived. Here, below 0.5 is death, and permanent death at that — "identity revoked," per Karma Journey. The escape hatch is the phrase "and remains there," but nothing anywhere tells me how long "remains" is. One turn? Ten? Until some review? Alice was below 0.5 for one act. Was she one tick from permanent revocation, and the page just didn't say?

### Karma Journey (30:00)
- The instruction "Make a few cooperative choices, then switch to selfish ones. Watch how trust builds slowly but erodes quickly" is a perfect one-sentence experiment. It delivered.
- Karma tiers by *effective* trust (Honored >0.7, Neutral 0.3–0.7, Constrained <0.3), with your death-time effective trust seeding your next life. Rebirth-as-grade is a memorable idea.
- **Confusion**: This page introduced a **raw vs. effective** trust distinction that no earlier page had prepared me for. Survival keys off *raw* trust (≥0.5). Karma tier keys off *effective* trust (raw × CI²). So which one does Aliveness mean when it says "Trust (T3) falls below 0.5"? Aliveness just says "Trust (T3)." If it means effective trust, then a brand-new user at 0.50 raw and 0.85 CI has effective trust of 0.36 — and is, by my arithmetic, already below the death line on day one. If it means raw, Aliveness should say "raw."
- **Confusion**: The raw trust formula here is "0.3 × Talent + 0.3 × Training + 0.4 × Temperament." That is not what the Trust Tensor page told me (see below).

### Trust Tensor (38:00)
- Talent / Training / Temperament, each scoped to a *role*, was clear. The point that you can be a trusted plumber and an untrusted poet is the most intuitive thing on the site.
- **Confusion (real contradiction)**: This page says weights are **role-specific**, and shows a Data Analyst at "Talent 40% / Training 35% / Temperament 25%" — talent-weighted. Karma Journey says raw trust is "0.3 × Talent + 0.3 × Training + 0.4 × Temperament" — temperament-weighted, and stated as *the* formula, not an example. As a visitor I can't tell whether (a) Karma Journey shows the default weights for a generic role, (b) one of the two is wrong, or (c) role-specific weights exist in theory but the simulator ignores them. Nobody says.
- V3 shows up here for the first time as a real thing (Valuation / Veracity / Validity), coupled to T3: good work raises who you are.

### ATP Economics (44:00)
- The biology metaphor earns its keep. Spend ATP → get an ADP receipt → recipient confirms value → receipt recharges into ATP. And the site preempts the obvious question: ADP is "a record of an action, not a second currency you can spend." Thank you. That was exactly my next question.
- Numbers I could hold: 100 ATP starter grant, 5% burn on transfers, sub-30%-quality work earns ~nothing, 70%+ earns full, roughly 7× spread. Recharge takes "hours to a day, not seconds."
- **Confusion**: V3 is used here to rate my work's value, but the page sends me elsewhere for the definition, and "elsewhere" turns out to be a section on the *T3* page. V3 has three named components, a role in the ATP loop, and a role in the trust update rule. It feels like a first-class concept living as a subsection of another concept. I went looking for a V3 page and there isn't one.

### Coherence Index (49:00)
- The geometric mean sold me: "(0.9 × 0.9 × 0.9 × 0.1)^(1/4) = 0.52." One bad dimension drags everything down, and you can't average your way out of it. That's a real design choice, explained in one line.
- Effective trust = T3 × CI². ATP cost = 1/CI². Symmetric penalty, easy to remember.
- **Confusion**: New accounts start at CI ≈ 0.85, which the page says is "not a penalty" — it just reflects unverified behavior. But 1/0.85² ≈ 1.38, so every action I take on day one costs 38% more than a veteran pays. That is a penalty. It may be a *justified* penalty, but calling it "not a penalty" while charging me a surcharge reads as spin, and it's the one moment on the site where I felt handled rather than informed.

### LCT Explainer (53:00)
- "A digital identity card stored in your device's security chip — not a password or file you can copy." Best plain-English definition on the site.
- Recovery tiers are concrete and believable: multiple devices → minutes to hours; one device → days of community vouching; software-only → **no recovery, start fresh**. Lose everything → 3–7 day social recovery.
- **Confusion (my top friction point)**: The hardware trust ceilings are TPM 0.90 / Secure Enclave 0.85 / FIDO2 0.75 / **Software-only 0.50**. And the page itself adds, parenthetically, that "0.5 is the alive/dead line."
  So: if I have no security hardware, my trust ceiling is *exactly the death threshold*. Aliveness requires trust **> 0.5** to live. A software-only identity can, at absolute best, sit precisely on the line — and every rule I read says at-or-below the line means restriction, death, or permanent revocation depending on which page I'm on.
  Is a software-only identity simply never alive? Is it alive but permanently one bad interaction from revocation? The page states both facts within a few lines of each other and doesn't notice the collision. Given that Why Web4? already flags hardware access as an equity problem, this reads as much worse than an equity problem: it reads like people without a TPM are structurally excluded from being agents at all. If that's the intended design, say so plainly. If it isn't, the ceiling or the threshold is wrong.

### MRH Explorer (58:00)
- The Markov framing is elegant: "the future depends only on the present — your history lives in your reputation, not in a log of every past step." That's the first thing that made me understand *why* the horizon is bounded rather than just *that* it is.
- Decay: 1 hop ≈0.85, 2 hops ≈0.65, 3 hops ≈ half, 4+ "below the noise floor."
- **Confusion**: The landing page told me "3 hops maximum." This page shows trust values *at 4 hops* and describes them as fading into noise rather than being forbidden. So is 4 hops a hard protocol limit or a soft economic one? Those are very different systems. (This page also mentions ATP costs rising exponentially with distance, which suggests "soft" — but then the landing page shouldn't say "maximum.")

### Society Simulator (63:00)
- Five presets with one-line descriptions, five strategies with one-line rules, and a four-phase narrative telling me what to watch for (Exploration → Coalition Building → Defector Isolation → Equilibrium). That last part is what most simulators forget: telling you what you're looking at.
- Every acronym I'd learned showed up here doing work. That was satisfying — the concept pages paid off.
- **Confusion**: I couldn't tell what a "good" run looks like. The page mentions cooperation settling near 70% as typical, but is 70% good? Is 90% better, or does it mean the defectors were never really tested? Nothing tells me whether I'm supposed to be optimizing, observing, or breaking it.

## Unanswered Questions
1. If my hardware caps my trust at 0.50 and life requires trust above 0.50, am I dead on arrival? Who is this system for?
2. Does the death rule use raw trust or effective trust? The two differ by CI², which for a normal user is a 25–40% haircut.
3. How long is "remains below 0.5" before recoverable death becomes permanent revocation?
4. Which T3 weights are real — role-specific, or 0.3/0.3/0.4?
5. Is 3 hops a protocol limit or just the point where trust stops mattering?
6. What actually happens after permanent trust death? Karma Journey says "identity revoked." Can that person ever participate again, under any identity? If they can (new hardware, new LCT), what stopped them from doing that at 0.51?
7. Who assigns the V3 score to my work, and what stops *that* person from lying?

## Honest Assessment

This site taught me a genuinely unfamiliar system in under an hour, which is rare. The sequencing is deliberate — TL;DR defines every acronym before the concept pages need them, First Contact makes the numbers concrete through a story before the concept pages make them precise, and every acronym I learned showed up doing real work in the Society Simulator. That payoff is the best thing here. The Why Web4? FAQ, which volunteers that there's no live network, that collusion is unsolved, and that GDPR has no clean answer, is the reason I believe the rest of it.

But the numbers don't agree with each other, and once I noticed that, I started checking. The 0.5 trust line is the site's most load-bearing constant — it decides life, death, permanent revocation, feature access, and it's the software-only hardware ceiling — and I read four different accounts of what crossing it does. The one that stopped me cold is the LCT page stating, within a few lines of each other, that software-only identity caps at 0.50 and that 0.5 is the alive/dead line. I don't think that's a typo I'm supposed to shrug past. Either the system genuinely excludes anyone without a TPM from ever being a live agent — which is a real and defensible design choice that the Why Web4? equity FAQ should be discussing in mechanical terms, not just as an access barrier — or one of those two numbers is wrong. As written, the site's most honest page and its most important number are in conflict, and the honest page doesn't know it.

The raw-vs-effective trust split is the same problem in a subtler key. Coherence Index teaches me that effective trust = T3 × CI², Karma Journey teaches me that survival keys off raw trust, and Aliveness — the page whose entire subject is survival — just says "Trust (T3)." Every page is individually careful. The set is not. Working the arithmetic myself, a brand-new user has effective trust of 0.36, which under one reading means every new account starts below the death line. I'm fairly confident that's not what's meant. I'm not confident because the site told me; I'm confident because it would be absurd.

That's the pattern I'd flag to whoever maintains this: the pages are written well individually and reviewed individually. The contradictions all live in the *seams*, where one page's constant becomes another page's threshold. A visitor who reads linearly and takes notes — which is what a system this quantitative invites — will find them, because the numbers are the whole point.

I'd come back. I want to run the Society Simulator properly and I want to know what happens after permanent death. But I'd come back with a notebook, not with trust that the numbers are load-tested — and that's a strange note to end on for a site about trust.
