## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
The eyebrow plus subtitle does a lot of work for me up front: *"Web4: a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor."* I immediately know (a) this is a proposal, not a product, (b) it's not blockchain-flavored, and (c) it doesn't claim to replace Web3. That's three preempted misreads in one line, which I appreciate.

The "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →" CTA is unmissable and labeled "Recommended for first-time visitors." I clicked it.

### TL;DR (02:00)
"The internet has no memory" landed for me. The four mechanics arrive as plain-English headings — energy posting, hardware-anchored identity, portable reputation, equal rules for humans and AI — with acronyms parenthetical, not leading. I could repeat the gist out loud after one pass, which is the bar for a 2-min explainer. Linked me onward to First Contact.

### First Contact (04:30)
This is positioned as a 5-act / 7-minute playable walkthrough following "Alice." I saw the setup and the "Start the 5-act Walkthrough →" button but the actual interactive simulation lives behind that button and I didn't drive it. The four-acronym reference card (ATP/T3/LCT/CI) sits above the play surface; the page promises each will be defined in-story the moment Alice uses it. I'll take their word for it. The framing line *"real rules, shown by simulation — not a demo of a shipped product"* arrived as a calming clarifier rather than a wobble — the same dissonance that bit earlier visitors seems to have been actively addressed.

### Why Web4 (10:00)
Substantial — feels like 15+ minutes if you read it all, which I didn't. The page tells me where to bail and labels itself as reference depth. The Problem section (spam, trapped reputation, cheap identity, AI deception, platform monopoly) is visceral. The "What Web4 Proposes" section gives me the four-mechanism map. The FAQ index sits underneath. I scanned it, read three FAQs, and moved on — exactly how the page told me to use it.

### Karma Journey (14:00)
Playable. Eight action buttons (cooperative / selfish / bold / neutral) with energy deltas visible per click. State shown live: Trust (Raw 0.500), Energy (100), Consistency (0.85). The instruction is one sentence: "Make a few cooperative choices, then switch to selfish ones."

That's the friction: the explanatory text *above* the play surface — Raw vs Effective trust, CI², the squaring formula — is the kind of thing I'd want *after* a few clicks of cause-and-effect, not before. As a naive player I want to push buttons first and see numbers move; the math is meaningless until I've seen the dynamics.

### LCT Explainer (18:00)
The driver's-license analogy at the top is the best single line on the whole site for me: *"your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated from a remote computer."* That's nine words and I now know what an LCT does in the world.

Hardware acronyms (TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2) are in a collapsible "what are these chips?" section — they're glossed as "tamper-resistant security chips already built into devices you probably own," which is exactly what I needed. Trust ceiling table reads cleanly (TPM 0.90, SE 0.85, FIDO2 0.75, software 0.50).

What I still don't have: a screenshot or wireframe of the actual first-five-minutes setup flow. It's referenced as a mockup but not visible.

### Trust Tensor (22:00)
T3 = Talent + Training + Temperament, role-contextualized. The sliders + role selector (Surgeon / Data Analyst / Team Leader) demo helps. V3 (Valuation / Veracity / Validity) gets a sibling callout with the elegant framing *"T3 measures who you are; V3 measures what you produce"* and the explicit permission *"don't need it to follow this page."* That's the right move — it parks V3 without erasing it.

Where I'd get lost if I read line-by-line: the decay half-lives (365d / 180d / 30d) and the "recipient attestation" mechanism, which arrive without being motivated.

### ATP Economics (28:00)
"60-second version" box is good. The cellular biology metaphor (ATP / ADP / recharge from food) clicked for me as someone with a bio background — I'd guess it works for the average reader who's seen ATP in a high-school textbook. The "energy budget, not money, not speculation" framing kills the cryptocurrency confusion fast.

ADP, however — and I see this is a recurring note in the project — is still the weak link. It's defined as "the receipt you get when you spend ATP." OK. But: does ADP have a balance I can watch? Does it expire? Is the rolling confirmation window hours, days, or weeks? The page says "think hours, not seconds" but doesn't pick a number. "Who confirms my value?" gets answered ("the recipients, not a central rater"), but the *timing* of confirmation stays fuzzy.

### Aliveness (32:00)
The two-deaths distinction is the cleanest section on the site:

- **Energy death** = ATP exhausted, recoverable, "like bankruptcy"
- **Trust death** = T3 fell below 0.5, permanent, "like losing a professional license"

The "professional license" spine is consistent throughout the page and rebirth carrying karma forward (start your next life with what your last life earned) feels real, not magical. The 0.5 threshold is hedged as "calibrated, not derived" — I appreciate the honesty rather than a fake derivation.

### Society Simulator (36:00)
Playable, but I face a wall of framing before the "Run Society" button. The "👋 Never seen these Web4 terms before?" link is present and well-placed.

The thing that gave me pause: an upfront concession that *"trust here is a scalar `reputation`, not the full T3 tensor."* I get *why* it's there — honesty about fidelity. But as a first-time visitor reading top-down, it lands as a small "this isn't really the thing" tax right before I press the big button. The framing might land better *after* the run, as a footnote — "by the way, what you just watched is a simplified scalar version of the real T3 tensor; the full version adds depth here." Same fact, different sequencing.

### MRH Explorer / Trust Neighborhood (40:00)
H1 is "Trust Neighborhood Explorer." MRH ("Markov Relevancy Horizon") is the formal name underneath. Good — the plain-English name is leading. Interactive network diagram with adjustable horizon depth is intuitive.

One concrete gap: the per-hop decay number itself isn't stated as text I can read. I'm shown a friend at hop 1 has signal ~0.85, hop 2 colleague ~0.65, hop 4 stranger "below noise floor" — and I'm expected to infer the rate. From those numbers I can back-solve to ~0.77 per hop (0.85 × 0.77 ≈ 0.65), which doesn't match the 0.7 figure documented elsewhere in the project. As a naive reader I'd just want a single sentence: "Trust decays by ~30% per hop." Then the examples confirm rather than substitute for the rule.

### Coherence Index (43:00)
The "account compromise at 3am posting crypto spam from a different country" concrete example was the single best teaching moment for a metric I'd otherwise find abstract. Three CI dimensions failing at once → throttling + extra witnesses. That's the kind of vignette every concept page should have.

The 0.85 starting value gets a clean motivation: "the system hasn't had a chance to witness you yet" — *unproven, not penalized*. I'd internalized that distinction by the time I left the page.

What I still don't follow: how the *relational* CI dimension is measured without something that looks like surveillance. The page hand-waves to "local processing" and "interaction history your device stores," but the privacy implementation feels asserted rather than shown.

### Day in Web4 (46:00)
Ten scenarios across one day. The page opens with the honest disclaimer that "none of it ships today" and frames itself as a thought experiment.

I think this page is still in tension with itself, though less than I gather it once was. The narrative is in present tense ("your phone wakes you up"), the mockups underneath are *clickable*, and the disclaimer is one line at the top. If I bounce in mid-scroll, I see a working-looking interface and lose the "thought experiment" anchor. The fix isn't a tense rewrite — that would kill the texture — but maybe a quiet "thought experiment" sticker next to each clickable mockup, the way Wikipedia tags speculative content inline rather than once at top.

### Playground (49:00)
Five named experiments with explicit hypotheses ("Can spam survive?", "Where is the tipping point?"). This is the best designed of the interactive pages for someone with no prior context — every experiment is a question, the sliders make the parameters legible, and the runs are short. I'd send a first-time friend here before Society Simulator.

### How It Works (52:00)
Both a story and a process diagram. "How The Pieces Fit Together" shows LCT at the foundation, ATP/T3/CI in a feedback loop, aliveness as the outcome. The numbers in the text ("Spend 15 ATP → Earn 40 ATP," "7× quality ramp," "+0.15 trust") are concrete enough that I could imagine how a single transaction would propagate. Research-prototype banner is at the top.

### Glossary (55:00)
40+ terms, five sections, "Essential only" filter to cut noise, jump-to navigation. The plain-English-paired-with-canonical-definition pattern (e.g. *"Your presence lives in your hardware, not in a company's database"*) is exactly right for a reference page. This is where I'd send anyone who got stuck on a single term in any other page.

### Threat Model (57:00)
Refreshingly honest. Sybil, collusion, Goodharting, quality inflation each defined; defenses explained; "Need Real-World Data" section explicitly lists unknowns. The EWMA / Byzantine detection mechanisms get technical, but the threat statements stay plain. Trust this page more than most.

### Learn / Learning Path (60:00)
Four pathways (Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced / Practitioner) with a clear "if it's your first time, take the Beginner path" instruction. The "Start where you are" framing prevents the four pathways from feeling like a choice tax. Good map.

## Unanswered Questions

1. **ADP rolling-window timing.** Hours, days, weeks? The site says "hours not seconds" but won't commit to a number. For a mechanic the entire economy depends on, the vagueness is conspicuous.
2. **Per-hop trust decay rate** isn't stated on the MRH page — only example signal values at hops 1/2/4. I can back-solve to ~0.77, which doesn't match the documented 0.7 elsewhere. Either the examples are off or the rate is.
3. **Relational CI without surveillance.** How does the system know my "established interaction network" without something that looks like tracking? The page says "local processing" but doesn't show how the device-local approach produces a global score.
4. **What the actual UI looks like.** Across multiple pages I see described mockups but no rendered screenshots/wireframes — even an obviously sketched one would close the gap. (I gather this is recurring feedback.)
5. **What a hardware-loss day looks like.** Device recovery via quorum is mentioned, but the practical flow ("you broke your only phone — now what?") isn't walked through end-to-end.

## Honest Assessment

This is a strong site. The recovery arc the project clearly went through (acronym overload → plain-English first, "what is Web4" hidden → eyebrow definition, "is this real?" wobble → consistent caveats) has held. I came in cold, found a recommended entry point, took it, understood the bumper-stickers within 2 minutes, and could productively wander the concept pages from there. The TL;DR / First Contact / Karma Journey / Aliveness chain is a real curriculum — I left feeling like Web4 is *a thing I could explain to a friend*, not just *a thing I read about*.

What remains is fine-grained friction, not structural confusion. The biggest cluster is around **numbers that are present in the system but missing at the read point**: the per-hop decay rate isn't on the MRH page, the ADP confirmation window isn't on the ATP page, the half-life motivations aren't on the Trust Tensor page. Each one is a small "show me the number" gap, and each is small enough that a sentence fixes it.

The second cluster is **sequencing**: explanatory text appearing above interactive surfaces when the dynamics should come first (Karma Journey CI² wall, Society Simulator's "actually it's just a scalar" concession). Move the math under the play, not over it.

The third cluster — and I don't think this one is fully fixable on-site — is **the missing UI**. Every page describes flows; no page renders one. A site explaining a proposed user experience that never shows a screenshot of that experience leaves the reader doing more imagination than the page does, which is asymmetric with the careful design of every other element here.

One thing I want to note as a *positive*: the project's willingness to write down what isn't settled ("calibrated, not derived" for the 0.5 threshold; "Need Real-World Data" on the threat model; "scalar reputation, not full T3" on the society sim) reads as integrity, not weakness. Most sites in this domain oversell. This one undersells, which makes me trust everything it *does* claim more.
