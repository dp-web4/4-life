## Unanswered Questions
1. **What would the actual user interface look like in practice?** The Day in Web4 wireframes are conceptual. I'd love to see even rough mockups of what a "Web4-enabled email client" or "Web4 social feed" would look like. The conceptual wireframes (inbox with trust scores, hiring with verified reputation) hint at this but aren't detailed enough to imagine daily use.
2. **How does the system handle context collapse across roles?** If I'm highly trusted as a data analyst but untrusted as a project manager, and someone only knows me in one context, can they discover my other role-trust scores? The MRH page suggests contexts are separate, but the practical boundary between roles isn't fully clear.
3. **What's the minimum viable community size?** The 100-action cold start walkthrough on What Could Go Wrong is great, but how many people does it take? 5? 50? 500? The economics seem to depend on having enough "recipients" to confirm value.
4. **How are "quality" and "value" actually measured at the protocol level?** The site says "recipients decide" via VCM, but what does the confirmation interface look like? Is it a thumbs up? A multi-dimensional rating? The mechanism is clear in principle but vague in practice.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most comprehensive and honest educational websites I've encountered for a technical research project. The site earns trust by being transparent about its own limitations — the "What Could Go Wrong" page alone would make me take this project seriously.

**What works exceptionally well:**
- **Progressive disclosure**: Landing → TL;DR → Why Web4 → First Contact → Concept pages is a natural learning curve.
- **Problem-first framing**: Every page starts with what's broken before explaining the solution. This keeps me motivated to understand.
- **Honesty**: "This is research, not production" appears consistently. The project doesn't oversell itself.
- **Biological metaphor**: ATP/energy/death is intuitive. It makes abstract economics feel visceral.
- **Interactive elements**: Simulators, sliders, and the survival game make concepts tangible.
- **Narrative stories**: The Meadow/Ironworks/Garden/Tidepool quartet on the Narratives page produces genuine insight through comparison.

**What could improve:**
- **Page length variation**: Some pages (LCT, ATP) are very long. A visitor doing a single session can't absorb it all. The concept sequence assumes you'll read 6 detailed pages in order, which is 40+ minutes of dense reading.
- **Audience split**: The site oscillates between general-public explanations (landing, TL;DR, Day in Web4) and PhD-level content (CI domains, federation game theory, RDF syntax). The collapsible sections help, but some pages front-load the accessible content and then keep going past the point where a general visitor has checked out.
- **The "UI question" persists**: After 50 minutes, I understand the mechanism deeply but still can't picture what I'd see on my phone screen. A single page of realistic mockups would bridge the gap between "I understand the theory" and "I want to use this."

Overall: impressive, thorough, honest. I left understanding Web4's vision, trusting the project's intellectual honesty, and wanting to see it actually built. That's a strong outcome for a research prototype's educational site.
