### Trust Tensor (/trust-tensor) (20:00)

- Role-specificity is a great insight — Alice's data analyst trust says nothing about her mechanic trust. That's intuitive.

## Honest Assessment

This is an impressively comprehensive site for a research project. The quality of writing is consistently high, and the commitment to honesty (especially the "What Could Go Wrong" page) builds genuine trust — which is ironic and fitting for a project about trust.

**What works really well:**
- Problem-first framing. I always understood WHY before WHAT.
- Progressive disclosure. The TL;DR → Why → Deep Dives path respects my time.
- Honesty. The site never oversells. It admits what's unsolved, what's untested, and what might fail.
- Concrete examples. Alice's journey, the "Day in Web4" scenarios, the inbox mockup — these make abstract concepts tangible.
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page is a standout. Publishing your own failure analysis is rare and refreshing.

**What needs work:**
- Jargon density. MRH, LCT, ATP, ADP, T3, V3, CI, SAL, R6/R7 — that's a lot of acronyms. The plain-English explanations are good but the acronyms pile up fast. By page 5 I was losing track of which was which.
- Page length. Several "explainer" pages are actually mini-textbooks. The ATP Economics page alone could be three separate pages. Read time estimates are optimistic.
- Tool/simulator overlap. There are at least 5 different interactive tools (Playground, Society Simulator, Karma Journey, Simulation Sandbox, Trust Tensor Explorer) and it's not immediately clear how they differ or which I should try.
- The research/academic concepts (Epistemic Proprioception, Modal Awareness, Coherence Thresholds from consciousness research) feel like they belong in a different section of the site. They're fascinating but they broke my mental model of "this is about internet trust."
- Forward references. Some pages reference concepts (CI on the Trust Tensor page, T3 on the ATP page) before I'd learned about them. The learning journey helps but if I'm browsing freely, I hit knowledge gaps.

**Bottom line:** I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left understanding the core thesis and most of the key concepts. That's a success. The site taught me rather than just telling me. But it tried to teach me *everything* on every page, which sometimes overwhelmed the core message. The best pages (Landing, TL;DR, What Could Go Wrong, Day in Web4) are the ones that practice restraint.
