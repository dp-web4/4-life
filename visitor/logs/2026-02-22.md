## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" — immediately relatable. I know spam and fake accounts are annoying. This hooks me right away.
- **What worked**: The problem statement is sharp. "Spam wins because accounts are free" — yes, exactly. The three-option scenario (ban, CAPTCHAs, or "make actions cost energy") is a clever way to make me think before reading.
- **What I clicked**: The recommended "Start Here" path: Why Web4 → First Contact → Playground
- **Confusion points**: "Web4" as a term isn't defined right away — I had to infer it means "a trust-native internet." The Big Idea section with four pillars is clear though.
- **Small note**: The "Trust Dilemma" micro-quiz is a nice touch — forces me to engage before I've even scrolled far.

### Why Web4 (02:30)
- **What worked**: Excellent problem framing. It tells me *why* previous solutions (moderation, CAPTCHAs, blockchain, social login) all failed. The honest tone builds trust — they admit what's hard.
- **The four mechanisms**: Energy Budget (ATP), Hardware-Bound Identity (LCT), Trust Tensor (T3), Context Boundaries (MRH) — all introduced with plain-English names first, acronyms second. Good.
- **FAQ section**: 10 questions, all genuine concerns. "Don't hardware manufacturers become the new gatekeepers?" — glad they address this. "What if I lose my hardware?" — they admit it's hard.
- **Impression**: This is a project that respects its skeptics. The honest FAQ builds more credibility than any sales pitch would.

### First Contact (06:00)
- **Format**: Interactive walkthrough with a character named Alice. JavaScript required for the simulation.
- **What I could see**: Learning outcomes (Attention Economics, Trust, Consequences, Self-Regulation) and Alice's starting state (100 ATP, 0.50 trust).
- **What I missed**: The actual interactive simulation (requires JS). The fallback links to static learning pages are appreciated though.
- **Impression**: The concept is strong — learning by watching someone navigate the system, not by reading definitions.

### LCT Explainer (09:00)
- **What is it**: "Hardware-bound, witnessed proof of presence." Identity tied to physical security chips (TPM, Secure Enclave, FIDO2).
- **Best analogy**: "Like co-signers on a document: your phone, laptop, and security key each independently vouch." This clicked instantly.
- **Interactive elements**: Witness slider (1-5 devices), attack scenario comparisons (Web2 vs Web3 vs Web4).
- **Evolution framing**: Web2 (password → server breach = everyone compromised) → Web3 (keys → theft = identity gone forever) → Web4 (hardware + witnesses → distributed resilience). Clear progression.
- **Minor confusion**: "Hardware attestation" used without explicit definition. The recovery section for total device loss is honest ("one of the hardest open problems") but leaves me a bit nervous about the system's robustness.

### ATP Economics (12:00)
- **What is it**: "Every action costs attention. Every contribution earns attention." Energy budget for online activity.
- **Key insight**: Spam dies naturally because it burns energy faster than it earns. Quality contributions pay for themselves. When energy hits zero, you "die." Brilliant in its simplicity.
- **Interactive elements**: ATP simulator with action buttons showing cost/reward breakdowns.
- **Best analogy**: "ADP as a receipt" — when you spend energy, you get a receipt proving you did something.
- **Case study**: An agent's journey across four lives showing karma accumulation. Makes the abstract concrete.
- **Minor confusion**: The relationship between ATP and ADP required re-reading. Also, "semi-fungible packet" in the technical section is dense jargon.

### Trust Tensor (14:30)
- **What is it**: Three-dimensional trust score — Talent, Training, Temperament — specific to each role.
- **Key insight**: "You wouldn't trust either of them to fix your car" — even a brilliant surgeon and a great engineer have role-specific trust. Single trust scores miss this entirely.
- **Interactive**: Simulator where I pick a role (surgeon, analyst, team leader) and see how scenarios affect each dimension differently.
- **Worked well**: The Alice example across three roles (data analyst, project manager, mechanic) made it concrete.
- **Minor confusion**: V3 (Value Tensors) mentioned but deferred — fine for now but left me curious.

### Coherence Index (17:00)
- **What is it**: Behavioral consistency score. Not "who are you?" but "does your behavior make sense?"
- **Best teaching moment**: Maria's stolen phone scenario. Even if the thief is in the same city, the coherence check catches anomalies across four dimensions (spatial, capability, temporal, relational).
- **Key design**: Multiplicative scoring (not averaged) — one bad dimension tanks everything. This prevents gaming.
- **Interactive**: Slider-based simulator adjusting four coherence dimensions with real-time CI score updates.
- **Impression**: This is the concept that made me say "oh, that's clever." The credit-check analogy works perfectly.

### Aliveness (19:30)
- **What is it**: The framework tying ATP + Trust + Coherence together. You're "alive" when all three are above threshold. Any one failing = death.
- **Best analogy**: "Think of it like a professional license" — resources + track record + consistent behavior.
- **Interactive**: Survival Challenge Game (start with 100 ATP, 0.55 trust, 0.80 coherence, make 5 choices). The stakes feel real.
- **Karma/rebirth**: If you die with trust ≥ 0.5, you're reborn with your legacy. Each life starts stronger. This is the most elegant mechanic — it rewards long-term good behavior permanently.
- **Honest gap**: "Web4 does not yet have a formal appeals mechanism" for false positive trust penalties. Important to acknowledge.

### How It Works (22:00)

- **Three-life case study**: Concretizes the entire system with a single agent's progression.
- **Why this design works**: Self-regulation through economics rather than moderation. Four principles clearly stated.
- **Navigation**: Points to Society Simulator and Playground as "see it in action" CTAs.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **How does federation work in practice?** — Multiple communities are mentioned but the mechanics of cross-community trust transfer aren't clear from the beginner path.
2. **What does "witnessed presence" actually look like for a regular user?** — I understand the concept of multiple devices co-signing, but what's the user experience? Is it seamless or do I have to actively "witness" things?
3. **How would Web4 handle edge cases like shared devices?** — Family computer, library terminal, borrowed phone — how does hardware-bound identity work when hardware isn't exclusively yours?
4. **What's the migration path?** — If I'm on today's internet, how do I "enter" Web4? Is it gradual or all-or-nothing?
5. **How does this interact with existing regulations?** — GDPR's "right to be forgotten" vs. permanent reputation. Right to create new identity vs. hardware-bound persistence.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most thoughtfully constructed educational sites I've encountered. Here's what stood out:

**Strengths:**
- **Problem-first framing** works brilliantly. Every page starts with what's broken, not what Web4 does. This means I always understand *why* before *what*.
- **Radical honesty** is the site's superpower. The "What Could Go Wrong" page, the unsolved problems, the "this is a design choice" admissions — these build more trust than any marketing polish would.
- **Progressive disclosure** is well-executed. Landing page → TL;DR → Why Web4 → concept pages → simulators → deep research. I always felt like I could go deeper when ready.
- **Multiple entry points** serve different visitors well. The explore guide, persona paths, and quick links mean nobody has to follow one rigid track.
- **Analogies carry the learning**. "Co-signers on a document" (LCT), "receipt" (ADP), "professional license" (aliveness) — these stick in memory.
- **Interactive experiences** (even the ones I couldn't fully use via static browsing) are designed to teach by doing, not by lecturing.

**Areas for improvement:**
- **JavaScript dependency**: Three major interactive experiences (First Contact, Day in Web4, Your Internet) are essentially blank without JavaScript. A narrative fallback — not just links to other pages — would help visitors who can't or won't enable JS.
- **Federation gap**: The beginner path teaches individual agent mechanics well but doesn't clearly explain how multiple communities interact. This is a key question for anyone thinking "okay, but how does this scale beyond one simulator?"
- **Shared device scenario**: Hardware-bound identity is compelling but raises practical questions about shared or borrowed devices that aren't addressed in the beginner path.
- **Regulatory intersection**: The permanence of reputation creates an obvious tension with right-to-be-forgotten regulations. This deserves at least a FAQ entry.

**Bottom line**: I came in knowing nothing about Web4. I leave understanding the core mechanics (identity costs something, actions cost something, reputation is permanent, everything ties together through aliveness). More importantly, I trust the project *because it's honest about what it doesn't know yet*. That's rare and valuable.

The site has matured significantly — the learning path is coherent, the concept pages build on each other logically, and the interactive experiences (for those who can use them) look genuinely educational rather than decorative.

Would I recommend this? Yes — with the caveat that the person should have JavaScript enabled and 30+ minutes to explore. The 2-minute TL;DR is a good shareable starting point for people who aren't ready for the full journey.
