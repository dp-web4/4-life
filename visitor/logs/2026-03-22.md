## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who validates quality?** The system says helpful answers earn ATP back — but who decides what's "helpful"? Other users? An algorithm? This is the core economic question and it's never clearly answered.
2. **What about single-device users?** LCT is described as a "constellation" of devices — does having only one device (common in developing countries) make you less trustworthy?
3. **How does initial ATP allocation work?** Everyone starts with 100 ATP — but who provides it? Is there an infinite supply? Does this create inflationary pressure?
4. **What prevents trust cartels?** Groups of colluding users could mutually boost each other's trust scores. The 3-hop limit helps, but what about dense clusters of bad actors?
5. **How does the system handle cultural context?** "Temperament" is culturally loaded — directness is valued in some cultures and penalized in others. How does T3 account for this?
6. **Where did ADP go?** The project is called "ATP/ADP" in some places but ADP (the counterpart to ATP — energy consumption?) is barely explained on the site.
7. **What's the actual deployment path?** The "What Could Go Wrong" page identifies adoption as the #1 risk but doesn't discuss any adoption strategy.

## Honest Assessment

This is a remarkably well-built explainer site for what is fundamentally a research proposal. The progression from "here's the problem" → "here's what it would look like" → "here's how it works" → "here's what could go wrong" is exactly right.

**What works best**: The Day in Web4 page and Alice's First Contact journey. These ground abstract concepts in concrete scenarios. The What Could Go Wrong page is the most trust-building content — a project about trust that hides its weaknesses would undermine its own thesis, and they know that.

**What still struggles**: The gap between "this is elegant in theory" and "this could actually work" isn't bridged. The site proves the *mechanics* work in simulation but doesn't address the *social* and *political* challenges of adoption. Who builds this? Who pays for it? How do you get platforms to adopt it when their business models depend on the problems Web4 solves?

**The naming confusion is real**: MRH vs Trust Neighborhood vs Markov Relevancy Horizon — three names for one concept, with broken URL routing. LCT, ATP, T3, CI, V3, SAL — the acronym density is manageable if you go through the learning path, but dropping into any random page mid-site creates an alphabet soup experience.

**The biological metaphor is the site's secret weapon**: Birth, life, death, rebirth, energy, metabolism — these are intuitive in a way that "decentralized trust protocol" isn't. The site should lean into this even harder.

**Bottom line**: I came in skeptical and left genuinely interested. The ideas are sound, the self-criticism is honest, and the interactive tools are well-designed. Whether this *could work* in the real world is a different question — but as a research proposal and thought experiment, it's compelling. I'd come back to run the simulators.
