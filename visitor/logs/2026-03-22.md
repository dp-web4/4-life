## Unanswered Questions
1. **What does the governance appeals process actually look like in practice?** The SAL framework is described abstractly but I haven't seen a concrete walkthrough of a dispute being filed, reviewed, and resolved. The "designed but untested" caveat appears multiple times — I want to see even a simulated example of the full process.
2. **How does Web4 handle gradual behavioral change vs sudden context switches?** If I'm a trusted data analyst who decides to learn woodworking, my "mechanic" trust starts at baseline. But what if my role evolves gradually — e.g., a data analyst who starts doing more project management? Where's the boundary between "same role, evolving" and "new role, fresh start"?
3. **What happens to trust during system outages or network partitions?** If my devices can't reach witness nodes for a period, does my coherence index decay? The site discusses steady-state behavior well but doesn't address infrastructure failure modes from the user's perspective.
4. **How do communities actually decide their ATP costs and trust thresholds?** The site says communities set their own policies but doesn't show the governance UX for *making* those decisions. Is it a vote? Does a founder decide? Can it change over time?

## Honest Assessment

This is an exceptionally well-constructed educational site for a complex technical topic. The progressive disclosure works — I started with a 2-minute TL;DR and naturally went deeper, and at each level the content met me where I was.

**What works best:**
- The "Day in Web4" page is the standout. It transforms abstract concepts into tangible experiences. If I could only show one page to someone, it would be that one.
- The intellectual honesty throughout is remarkable. "What Could Go Wrong" is the most trust-building page on a site about trust. Acknowledging unsolved problems (total device loss, appeals untested, adoption risk) actually makes me *more* interested, not less.
- Plain English first, acronyms second. Every concept page leads with what it means before what it's called. This is the right pedagogical order.
- The narrative approach (Alice in First Contact, the four featured society stories) makes the mechanics memorable. I remember Alice's spam attempt and recovery more than any formula.

**What could improve:**
- The SAL governance section needs a concrete walkthrough. It's the one area where I left feeling like I understood the *structure* but not the *experience* of how governance actually works.
- The landing page tries to serve too many audiences at once. It's effective but long. A first-time visitor might benefit from a clearer single path rather than 5+ entry points.
- Role boundaries in trust are explained for clear-cut cases (surgeon vs mechanic) but not for gradual role evolution, which is how most professional growth actually works.

**Overall:** I went from "what is Web4?" to "I understand why this matters and how it works" in under 50 minutes across 16 pages. The site respects my time, rewards my curiosity, and is honest about its limitations. That last quality is the most impressive — a project about trust practicing transparency about its own uncertainties.
