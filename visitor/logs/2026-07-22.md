## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: The headline "Trust, built into the internet." is strong and clear. The subhead about AI agents that "write code, move money, and make decisions" but where "we cannot prove what an agent did, on whose authority, or by what rules" immediately made me *feel* a real problem. Good hook.
- Then I scrolled and saw "The onramp — A standard alone is inert. These four pieces are how you actually stand it up." Four pieces: the core standard (substrate), the hub (community), hestia (personal), hardbound (enterprise). At a glance that's a clean mental model.
- **Confusion point**: The navigation is HUGE — I counted ~29 links (Whitepaper, Web4 in 2 Minutes, Why Web4, First Contact, How It Works, Running Now, four pieces, How the Pieces Compose, then LCT/T3/V3/MRH/ATP/Coherence/Karma/Identity Constellation/Glossary/Day in Web4/Your Internet/Web4 Explainer/What Could Go Wrong/Coherence Framework/Manifest/Learn/GitHub…). As a newcomer I have no idea which of these 29 doors is the "right" first one. I wanted a single obvious "Start here." I picked "Web4 in 2 Minutes" because 2 minutes felt safe.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes → /tldr (03:00)
- The Web2 / Web3 / Web4 contrast landed well:
  - Web2: platforms declare reputation; it vanishes if you leave.
  - Web3: holding a key = ownership; stolen keys go undetected.
  - Web4: "trust accrues from witnessed behavior — other participants observe what you do and sign a small record of it."
- That one sentence is the clearest thing on the whole site. "Witnessed behavior" is defined right where it's used. 
- **Confusion point**: At the bottom it lists LCT / T3 / V3 / MRH / ATP as the concept set, but none are defined inline here — they're just links. That's fine for a 2-minute page (progressive disclosure), but I left "2 Minutes" holding five acronyms I couldn't unpack yet.

### Why Web4? (06:00)
- The problem framing is the best part of the site. Spam is free, reputation is siloed, banned users just make new accounts, you can't tell human from AI, platforms hold all the power. Every one of those felt familiar and annoying. I nodded.
- The four mechanisms map cleanly onto the problems (ATP → spam costs; LCT → fake accounts cost real hardware; T3 → dimensional trust; MRH → strangers need an introduction).
- **Confusion point (this became my biggest one)**: The page says bad behavior "creates lasting records visible to future interactions" — permanent consequences. But the FAQ *also* says trust decays with 30–90 day half-lives. On first read those sound like a direct contradiction: if my reputation fades in a month, how is the consequence "lasting"? Nothing on this page reconciles the two. I made a note to look for the answer.
- **Minor**: Lots of trust-flavored terms pile up fast (T3, V3, Coherence Index, MRH decay). By the end I was holding ~8 concepts without knowing how they connect.

### First Contact (11:00)
- This is the good stuff. Two live meters up top — Energy Budget (ATP), starts at 100; Trust Score, starts at 0.50 — and a 5-act interactive story (Join → Build → Stumble → Recover → Die & Reborn) where I "drive Alice's choices." Watching the numbers move in response to choices is far better than reading definitions. If I'd landed here first I'd have understood more, faster.
- **Confusion point**: The collapsible "four core concepts" section uses LCT, T3, ATP, CI in the headers before they're explained. And there's a self-aware green banner admitting that hestia/hub references might confuse me — which they did. I appreciate the honesty but it's a patch over a real seam.

### ATP Economics (17:00)
- Solid page. The biological-ATP metaphor works, and the little spend/earn simulator (spam −5, low-quality −10/+5, meaningful −15/+25, high-value −20/+50) made "quality pays, spam drains you" click immediately. The "Is ATP like Bitcoin? (short answer: no)" section is exactly the question I had, answered directly: usage budget, no market, no price, no speculation, can't buy or sell.
- **Confusion point**: Two lines sit uneasily together. "You can't buy or sell ATP" — but also "transfers incur a 5% fee routed to a community redistribution pool." As a newcomer: if I can *transfer* it to someone, how is that not selling it? The distinction (gift/allocation vs. market trade) is probably real but it isn't spelled out at the point where both claims appear.
- **Minor**: "death" if ATP hits zero, then "reborn with a head start" — the resurrection softens the finality the word "death" implies. And recharge timing is stated as "hours to a day" in one place and compared against "weeks" for un-confirmed decay; I wasn't sure which applied to me.

### LCT Explainer (23:00)
- Clearest of the deep concept pages. "A digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip — not a password you type and not a key file you store, but a presence your hardware proves." I got it. Linked / Context / Token broken into three words helped. The trust ceilings by device count (0.50 one device → 0.75 two → 0.90 three+) made "witnessed presence" concrete.
- The "Only have one device? You're still in" note is welcoming, and it's honest that software-only tops out at 0.50.
- **Minor**: "witness" is used a lot in the intro before the device-co-witness vs. network-witness distinction shows up in a collapsed section. I briefly thought "witness" meant a person watching me.

### Karma & Consequences (28:00)
- I came here specifically to resolve the permanent-vs-decay contradiction from Why Web4. **The page does not directly reconcile it.** The comparison table calls consequences "permanent," while the math shows `next_life_trust = prev_trust * 0.95` — i.e. trust *does* fade across lives.
- I eventually inferred the real answer myself: **your identity is permanent (hardware-bound, you can't mint a fresh one), but your trust score decays.** So the consequence is "you can never escape to a clean slate," not "your penalty stays at full strength forever." That's a genuinely good idea — but I had to assemble it from two pages. The page even lists "Should very old mistakes eventually fade?" as an *open question*, which made me unsure whether the contradiction I felt is intended nuance or an unsettled design gap.

### Running Now (32:00)
- Best-in-class honesty. Three badges — Spec (designed, not built) / Reference (built & runnable, not production) / Running (deployed). It flatly says "Web4 is R&D, not a shipped product. Nothing on this page is a fielded production system." Only Hestia claims daily use (4 machines, small pilot). I came away knowing exactly what I could and couldn't touch today. This is the page that earned my trust in the *site*.

### Onramp / How the Pieces Compose (35:00)
- "One shape at three scales, all speaking the same vocabulary" is a nice summary, and the dependency diagram (core standard at the base, hub/hestia/hardbound consuming it) clarified the four-piece model.
- **Confusion point**: The word "onramp" is the site's central metaphor and the page title, but it's never actually defined — I had to infer "entry pathway." And despite a prerequisite disclaimer, the page still throws "T3/V3*MRH," "R6/R7," and "Law Oracle" at me with little explanation.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust from witnessed behavior, not platform decree or key possession
- [x] What the onramp is (the core standard, the hub, hestia, hardbound) and how they fit together — one shape at three scales on one substrate
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound identity, "a presence your hardware proves"
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — an energy budget you spend to act and earn back with quality work; ADP is the spent-energy receipt
- [ ] What Trust Tensors are — I saw T3 (talent/training/temperament) named but never opened the T3 page; V3 stayed fuzzy (I know it's "quality of work" vs T3 "trust in you" only because Why Web4 said so in passing)

## Unanswered Questions
1. If my trust score decays to nothing in a couple of months, in what sense is a past bad act a "lasting consequence"? (I inferred an answer but the site never told me.)
2. If I can *transfer* ATP to another agent, why is that not "selling" it? What actually stops an ATP-for-cash side deal off-platform?
3. Who witnesses a brand-new person with one device and no existing relationships? How do I get my first witness and my first bit of trust from a standing start?
4. What's the difference between T3 and V3 in one sentence, stated on a page I'd actually land on? (I only caught it as an aside in Why Web4.)
5. "R6/R7" and "Law Oracle" showed up on the onramp page with almost no explanation — what are they, and do I need them to understand the four pieces?

## Honest Assessment
The site is honest, well-structured, and the problem framing (Why Web4) plus the interactive First Contact story are genuinely good — better than most technical explainer sites I've seen. The "Running Now" badges earned my trust because they refuse to over-claim.

My single real stumble was the **permanent-vs-decaying** idea. Both words are used confidently, in different places, about the same thing, and the reconciliation is left entirely to me. I happened to assemble it correctly (identity is permanent; the score decays), but a less patient reader would leave thinking the site contradicts itself. That's the one thing I'd fix first.

Secondary friction is cognitive load at the entry: 29 links and no obvious front door, and a handful of acronyms that appear a beat before they're explained. The content to resolve all of it exists — it's an ordering and signposting problem, not a substance problem.

Net: I'd come back, and I'd send this to an engineer friend. I would *not* send it to my non-technical sister — she'd bounce off the link wall before reaching the good part.
