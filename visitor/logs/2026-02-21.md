## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: "Trust-Native Internet Lab" — no idea what that means, but the problem statement ("The Internet Has a Trust Problem") immediately resonates. Spam, fake accounts, AI deception — yes, I experience these daily.
- The "New Here? Start Here" section with three numbered steps is genuinely helpful. It told me exactly where to go.
- "The Big Idea" section makes four claims: hardware-bound identity, energy costs for actions, permanent consequences, humans and AI coexist. These are intriguing but abstract.
- "See It Work" section lists three interactive tools: Society Simulator, Karma Journey, Playground. Having three options feels like a lot before I know what any of them are.
- Clicked: "Why Web4" (following the recommended path)
- Confusion points: What's "trust-native"? The term assumes I know what a non-trust-native internet looks like at a protocol level.

### Why Web4 (02:30)
- This page is excellent. It starts with problems I recognize (spam costs nothing, reputation is siloed, banned users just make new accounts) and then explains why existing solutions fail (CAPTCHAs are solved by AI, blockchain wallets are free to create). The "What Would Actually Work" section sets up clear requirements before proposing Web4.
- The "Honest Questions" FAQ section at the bottom is refreshingly candid — they admit this is "theoretical with working simulations, not deployed" and that bootstrapping the witness network is "unsolved." This builds trust (ironic for a trust project).
- Clicked: First Contact (following recommended path)
- Confusion points: "Federated protocol" — I know what this means but many visitors won't. The mechanism descriptions (ATP, LCT, T3, MRH) are just teasers here — enough to be curious but not enough to understand.

### First Contact (06:00)
- This is clearly meant to be an interactive, JavaScript-powered experience following "Alice" through 10 actions in a Web4 community. The concept is great — showing, not telling, through a character's journey.
- I can see Alice starts with 100 ATP and a trust score of 0.50. The promise is to watch her "spend ATP, build trust, recover from mistakes, and earn recognition."
- **Note**: As a text-only visitor (no JavaScript), I'm hitting a wall here. The narrative content and interactive steps require the browser. This is expected and fine for real users — but I can't experience it.
- Clicked: How It Works (to continue learning)

### How It Works (08:00)
- Very well-structured. Five foundational mechanisms laid out clearly: Identity (LCT), Attention Economics (ATP), Trust (T3), Coherence (CI), Context (MRH). Each gets a one-liner plus a "Learn more" link.
- The lifecycle section (Birth → Life → Death → Rebirth) is the clearest explanation I've found so far. Concrete numbers: start with 100 ATP, reading costs 1, posting costs 10-20, high-quality post nets +25.
- The 0.5 trust threshold for rebirth is stated but the "why 0.5?" question lingers.
- Primary CTA goes to Society Simulator, secondary to Playground. Good choices — these are the "experience it yourself" tools.
- Confusion points: "Cross-life pattern learning" is mentioned but unexplained. What does "coherence" mean precisely? The page gives me enough to follow along but I'm trusting the definitions without fully grasping them.

### Karma Journey (11:00)
- An interactive life simulation with 8 action choices per turn across multiple lives. Choices are categorized: cooperative, selfish, risky, neutral.
- The teaching promise is clear: "cooperative choices build slow trust; selfish choices yield quick gains but erode your legacy."
- Stats tracked: Trust, Energy, Consistency, Talent, Training, Temperament — all starting at 0.500.
- Karma tier determines next life's conditions: Honored, Neutral, Constrained.
- This feels like the most engaging hands-on learning tool. The game mechanics teach the concepts through play.
- **Note**: JavaScript-dependent, so I'm reading descriptions rather than playing. Real users would have a much better experience here.

### LCT Explainer (14:00)
- "A hardware-bound, witnessed, contextual proof of presence." The Web2 → Web3 → Web4 progression is an excellent teaching scaffold: passwords (forgettable) → crypto keys (stealable) → hardware + witnesses (verifiable).
- The "co-signers on a document" analogy for witnesses works well.
- Technical details are properly collapsed — a newcomer can skip them without losing the thread.
- Device loss FAQ is thorough and honest: "Perfect recovery from total device loss without any central authority is one of the hardest open problems."
- Confusion points: What's an "LCT format" like `lct:web4:agent:alice.assistant1@Thor#perception`? This string appears without enough context for a newcomer. The concept is clear but the encoding feels like implementation detail shown too early.

### ATP Economics (16:00)
- "An energy resource you spend on actions" — clear and grounded. The metabolic analogy (eat food → do work → maintain homeostasis) is effective.
- Concrete numbers: spam costs 5 ATP with 0 reward (net -5), meaningful contributions cost 15 but earn 25 (net +10). This instantly shows why spam dies.
- The four-life narrative arc (100 ATP → earned 145 → rebirth → crisis at 15 → recovery to 140) makes the lifecycle tangible.
- ADP explained as "a receipt" — good analogy.
- One gap: "ATP is non-transferable" is mentioned but the WHY isn't immediately obvious. If it's an energy budget, why can't I share my energy?

### Trust Tensor (18:00)
- The Alice example across three roles is the star of this page. Data analyst (90% trust), project manager (76%), mechanic (27%) — immediately shows why one-dimensional trust scores are useless.
- Three dimensions per role: Talent (aptitude), Training (skills), Temperament (consistency). This is intuitive.
- The problem-solution structure works: "single scores lose nuance" → "T3 captures trade-offs."
- Confusion points: V3 (Value Tensors) is mentioned as a "parallel system" measuring Valuation, Veracity, Validity — but this feels like it's opening another rabbit hole before I've digested T3. One new concept at a time, please.

### Coherence Index (20:00)
- "A behavioral consistency score — like a credit check that asks not just 'who are you?' but 'does your behavior make physical sense?'" Great opening.
- Four dimensions: spatial, capability, temporal, relational. The Maria's stolen phone scenario is excellent — valid credentials but broken temporal continuity and relational patterns → CI drops → triggers verification.
- The fraud detection examples (impossible travel, identical datacenter IPs) ground this in familiar security concepts.
- Confusion points: "Geometric mean" is mentioned without intuition for why geometric vs arithmetic. The "synchronism" and "phase transition" references feel like academic asides that could distract newcomers.

### Aliveness (22:30)
- Three conditions to be "alive": energy > 0, trust > 0.5, coherence checks pass. Death = any criterion fails. Simple and memorable.
- The interactive slider (adjust Energy, Trust, Consistency to see live status) is a great teaching tool — though JavaScript-dependent.
- Rebirth if T3 ≥ 0.5, permanent death if below. Stakes feel real even in a simulation.
- The 0.5 threshold is described as inspired by "phase transitions" — I accept this as a design choice but the physics framing might alienate non-technical visitors. "We chose 0.5 as the minimum bar for continued participation" would be more accessible.

### Society Simulator (25:00)
- "12 agents with different strategies form alliances, betray each other, and self-organize — no central authority, just trust dynamics at society scale." This sounds genuinely compelling.
- Observer mode (watch agents) and Player mode (make decisions yourself, earn 33 achievements).
- Five preset scenarios: Cooperative Majority, Hostile World, Reciprocity Rules, Trust Scarce, All Adaptive.
- The page wisely suggests beginners start with First Contact. Self-aware about its own complexity.

## Unanswered Questions
1. **How does the witness network bootstrap?** The Why Web4 page admits this is unsolved. But if it's unsolved, how does anything work? Is there a seed network? Who are the first witnesses?
2. **What prevents hardware manufacturers from being a chokepoint?** If LCT requires TPM/Secure Enclave, don't Apple/Google/Intel become gatekeepers? Isn't this just replacing one centralized trust with another?
3. **How does ATP non-transferability actually work?** If I collaborate with someone and we jointly create value, who gets the ATP? Can organizations have pooled ATP?
4. **What happens to trust history if the underlying hardware standard changes?** TPM v2 → v3, or a post-quantum transition — does everyone's LCT need re-anchoring?
5. **How does MRH work in practice for real-time communication?** The "co-working space" analogy is nice but the computational cost of trust-filtered message delivery at scale seems enormous.
6. **Where are the failure mode analyses?** I see the "Honest Questions" on Why Web4, but a dedicated "What Could Go Wrong" page covering adversarial attacks, economic failures, and edge cases would build more confidence.
7. **Is there a working prototype beyond simulations?** The site is transparent about being theoretical, but even a tiny real-world pilot (e.g., a trust-scored forum or chat) would demonstrate viability.

## Honest Assessment

**This is significantly better than I expected.** The site clearly communicates a complex vision through multiple entry points and progressive disclosure. The recommended learning path (Why Web4 → First Contact → How It Works → concept pages → interactive tools) works well. The "problem before solution" approach on the Why Web4 page is the best thing on the site — it earns the right to propose solutions by first demonstrating a deep understanding of the problem.

**What works well:**
- **Why Web4** is the strongest page. It starts with problems I feel daily, demolishes existing solutions, and only THEN proposes Web4. The "Honest Questions" FAQ builds genuine trust.
- **The learning sequence is well-designed.** Each concept page has Previous/Next navigation, and concepts build on each other logically (identity → economics → trust → consistency → lifecycle).
- **Progressive disclosure is excellent.** Technical details are collapsible. Analogies lead, formulas follow. Plain English first, acronyms second.
- **The interactive tools (Karma Journey, Society Simulator, Playground, Achievements)** provide three levels of engagement: guided narrative, multiplayer dynamics, parameter exploration, and goal-oriented play. This is impressive depth.
- **Glossary and term tooltips** address the acronym problem head-on. Having a floating "Aa" button on every page is a good UX decision.
- **The Explore Guide quiz** respects different visitor types instead of forcing one path. Smart.

**What needs work:**
- **The First Contact page (JavaScript wall)**: If this is the recommended first interactive experience, it absolutely MUST work without JavaScript or provide a meaningful fallback. Visitors with JS disabled, slow connections, or accessibility tools would hit a dead end at the most critical moment in the onboarding flow. (Note: I'm browsing without JS; real users likely won't have this issue. But consider screen readers and very slow mobile connections.)
- **Physics/math language in explanations**: "Phase transitions," "geometric mean," "geodesics" — these appear on pages meant for beginners. The concepts behind them are simple (any failure tanks the whole score; 0.5 is the minimum bar) but the language is unnecessarily intimidating.
- **V3 (Value Tensors) on the Trust Tensor page**: Introducing a parallel concept system while I'm still learning the first one is overwhelming. Save it for the Advanced path.
- **"Non-transferable ATP" without explanation**: This is a design choice with deep implications. A one-sentence explanation would help: "Your energy budget reflects YOUR contributions — you can't buy reputation."
- **The Learn page overview uses bare acronyms**: A newcomer landing here directly would see "LCT, ATP, T3, CI" without expansion. The individual pages do this well; the overview should too.
- **Missing: a "What Could Go Wrong" page**: The Honest Questions FAQ on Why Web4 is good but brief. A dedicated failure analysis page would serve the "skeptic/adversarial thinker" persona from the Explore Guide.

**Overall**: I went from "what is trust-native?" to a solid understanding of the Web4 vision in about 40 minutes. That's genuinely impressive for a system this complex. The site teaches through experience and story rather than lectures, and it's honest about what's theoretical vs. working. I would return to play the Society Simulator and Karma Journey interactively. I'm "maybe" on recommending — not because the content is bad, but because most people I know wouldn't invest 40 minutes in understanding a theoretical internet protocol. The people who WOULD invest that time would find this excellent.

**One last thing**: The site has done an admirable job of killing its own jargon. I notice plain English leads almost everywhere (Energy Budget not ATP, Identity not LCT, Consistency not CI in headers). This discipline is clearly intentional and it works. The only places where jargon still leaks are the edges: the Learn page overview, V3 mentions on the T3 page, and physics terminology in explanations.
