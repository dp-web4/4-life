## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who is this for, practically?** I understand the framework, but who would adopt Web4 first? A social media platform? A company? A community? The "honest questions" on Why Web4 partially answers this but I want concrete adoption scenarios.
2. **What does daily life look like?** I understand the mechanics but not the UX. If I were using a Web4-enabled social network, what would posting a message look like? Would I see my ATP balance? Would I feel the energy cost?
3. **How does this handle edge cases like mental health breaks?** If I go offline for 3 months (depression, illness, sabbatical), does my temporal coherence tank? Do I come back to a degraded identity?
4. **What's the relationship between this simulation and real deployment?** The site says "research, not production" but doesn't paint a picture of what the bridge looks like. Is anyone building a real Web4 app?
5. **How does moderation of truly harmful content work?** The energy system prevents spam, but what about a high-trust member who starts posting harmful content? Does the system handle that, or is that still a human moderation problem?
6. **What happens to privacy?** If my trust tensor and behavioral history are permanent and portable, who can see them? Can I control what's visible?

## Honest Assessment

This is genuinely one of the more thoughtful conceptual frameworks I've encountered on the web. The site does a remarkable job of explaining complex ideas through concrete examples, interactive tools, and honest caveats. The "Why Web4?" page alone is worth the visit — it diagnoses the internet's trust problem better than most published articles I've read.

**What works really well:**
- Problem-before-solution framing (Why Web4 page is A+)
- Biological metaphors (ATP = energy, death = real consequences)
- Honest caveats throughout ("this is research, not production")
- Interactive elements (ATP simulator, Trust Tensor simulator, Karma Journey)
- The lifecycle narrative (Birth → Life → Death → Rebirth)
- Role-specific trust examples (Alice as analyst vs. mechanic)

**What still needs work:**
- The **First Contact** page is the weakest link in the recommended path. It promises an interactive tutorial ("Step 1 of 5") but doesn't deliver one. A visitor who follows the recommended path (Why Web4 → First Contact) hits a dead end where the momentum stalls.
- **Navigation between concepts** is inconsistent. Some pages have prev/next links, others don't. Some pages link to the learning journey, others don't. It's hard to know "what to read next."
- The **personal relevance** gap. Every page explains *how* Web4 works, but few explain *why I should care* beyond abstract idealism. A page that says "Here's what Web4 means for you as a Reddit user / Twitter user / Discord moderator" would be transformative.
- **Content length** varies wildly. Some pages could benefit from progressive disclosure (show the overview, let curious visitors expand details).
- A few **broken or unclear links** (Karma Journey "Next Steps" pointing to "/").

Overall: I came in knowing nothing about Web4, and I left understanding the core framework. The site achieved its educational goal. But the journey from "I understand this" to "I care about this" still has a gap. The intellectual case is strong; the emotional/personal case needs strengthening.

**Rating: 7/10** — Would read more, would share the Why Web4 page with friends, but would need to see real-world adoption paths before becoming a true advocate.
