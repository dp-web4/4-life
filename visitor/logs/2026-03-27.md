## Honest Assessment

This is an exceptionally well-constructed educational site. The progression from "here's the problem" through "here's the idea" to "try it yourself" is pedagogically sound, and the multiple entry points (2-min TL;DR, day-in-life, personal impact, structured learning) respect different visitor attention levels.

What impressed me most:

1. **Intellectual honesty**: The site repeatedly acknowledges what doesn't work yet, what hasn't been tested, and what the genuine risks are. "What Could Go Wrong" and the Threat Model pages are remarkably candid for a project promoting itself. This built more trust in the project than any amount of bold claims would.

2. **Problem-first framing**: Every concept page starts with why the current internet fails before introducing Web4's approach. This prevents the "solution looking for a problem" feeling common in protocol proposals.

3. **Interactive depth**: The Karma Journey, Society Simulator, and Playground aren't gimmicks — they genuinely teach through experience. I understood ATP economics more from spending 100 units in the simulator than from reading the explanation.

4. **Progressive disclosure**: Jargon is introduced gradually with plain English first. The "4 ideas, one at a time" approach on Why Web4 is well-executed.

The one friction point worth highlighting: **ATP naming inconsistency**. I noticed "Attention Transfer Packets" on the landing page but "Allocation Transfer Packets" on the TL;DR page. For a site this meticulous about clarity, having two different expansions of the same acronym is confusing and should be reconciled.

The site successfully convinced me that the *problem* is real and the *approach* is thoughtful. Whether it would work at scale remains genuinely open (and the site honestly says so), but as an educational experience for a complex proposal, this is one of the most effective I've encountered. I'd return to explore the simulator more deeply and would recommend it to anyone interested in internet trust infrastructure.
