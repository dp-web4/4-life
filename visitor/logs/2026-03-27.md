## Unanswered Questions
1. How does the hardware binding actually work? I know it uses a "security chip" but what does that mean practically — is it TPM? Secure Enclave? FIDO2? And what happens on shared/public devices?
2. What does the actual data flow look like? When I post something and it "costs 8 ATP," where does that energy go? Is it burned? Redistributed? The economics were clear at a high level but the plumbing is still opaque.
3. How do communities federate? If I build trust in "Local Photographers" and join "Home Cooking," how much trust transfers? The site mentions federation but I didn't find a clear explanation of the mechanics.
4. What's the relationship between 4-Life (the simulation lab) and Web4 (the protocol)? The site sometimes feels like it's explaining a real system and sometimes like it's explaining a simulation of a system. The boundary is a bit blurry.
5. Who would build the first real Web4 app? The site says "no company, no token sale" — so what's the path from "research" to "something I can actually use"?
6. How does ADP relate to ATP? I see ATP mentioned everywhere but ADP only appears in the site title area and a few links. What's the distinction?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the best-explained technical research projects I've encountered on the web. The progressive disclosure works — I started with 2-minute TL;DR and 25 minutes later I understand a genuinely complex system. The writing respects the reader's intelligence without assuming prior knowledge.

**What worked best:**
- The "Day in Web4" app mockups. These were the moment everything clicked. Abstract concepts became concrete UI.
- The "What Could Go Wrong" page. This page builds trust in the project more effectively than any feature description could.
- The consistent "problem → root cause → Web4 mechanism" pattern across pages. Once I saw it, every new concept slotted in naturally.
- Time estimates on links. Knowing "this is 2 minutes" vs. "this is 15 minutes" let me manage my attention.

**What could improve:**
- There's a redundancy problem across entry points. TL;DR, First Contact, Day in Web4, and the landing page all cover the same four core concepts. If I follow the natural reading order, I'm seeing the same ideas for the fourth time by First Contact.
- The jargon density spikes without warning. The landing page and TL;DR are jargon-free, then suddenly I'm seeing MRH, LCT, T3, V3, CI, ATP, ADP, VCM, SAL, and "synthons." A visitor who came from the friendly landing page may bounce when they hit this wall.
- Some pages are very long (Aliveness, Why Web4) and could benefit from collapsible sections or a "key takeaways first, details below" structure. The Aliveness page in particular buries its best insights (the three criteria, death/rebirth) under a lot of technical detail.
- The relationship between 4-Life (simulation) and Web4 (protocol) is still unclear to me. I think 4-Life *is* this website, and it contains simulations of Web4 mechanics? But the landing page says "A Trust-Native Internet Lab" which could mean many things.

**Overall:** I would return. The intellectual honesty and quality of explanation earned that. But I'd recommend it selectively — to someone interested in internet trust problems specifically, not as casual reading. The site rewards patient, curious visitors but doesn't yet have a strong enough "why should I care about this right now" hook for someone who isn't already frustrated with the internet's trust problem.
