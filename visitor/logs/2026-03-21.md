## Unanswered Questions
1. How would Web4 handle power dynamics? Rich organizations could buy many devices and create legitimate-seeming identities at scale — the 5% fee and device cost might not be enough at corporate budgets.
2. What happens to trust history if the underlying hardware standard changes? The site mentions migration is "designed in theory" but that's vague.
3. How does MRH (context boundaries) actually work in practice? It's mentioned on several pages but never gets a clear standalone explanation with examples. I know it's about visibility limits based on trust, but the mechanics are fuzzy.
4. The "Gratitude Loop" (ATP recharging through recipient confirmation) — what prevents collusion? Two entities could confirm each other's work endlessly. The 5% fee limits this somewhat but doesn't seem to prevent it entirely.
5. What does "coherence" actually measure technically? The CI concept is mentioned everywhere but the four dimensions (spatial, capability, temporal, relational) aren't explained with concrete examples.
6. Is there any real-world analogous system that has succeeded with similar economics? The biological metabolism metaphor is nice, but biological systems evolved over billions of years — can you design one from scratch?

## Honest Assessment

This site is surprisingly good. The core ideas are explained clearly, the narrative approach (Alice's story, Day in Web4 scenarios) makes abstract concepts tangible, and the intellectual honesty about limitations is genuinely refreshing.

**What worked best**: The First Contact page (Alice's story) and the Day in Web4 scenarios. These grounded everything. Without them, the concept pages would feel like academic papers.

**What needs work**: Navigation and discoverability. With 75 pages, I had no idea which ones were important. The landing page links to pages that 404. The explore guide helps but isn't prominent enough. A new visitor could easily get lost or click a broken link and leave.

**The biggest tension**: The site wants to be both an accessible explainer and a deep technical resource. It mostly succeeds, but some pages (Why Web4, Aliveness) try to do both simultaneously and end up feeling long. The progressive disclosure on the Learn page is the right approach — more pages should use it.

**My overall take**: I came in knowing nothing about Web4. After 35 minutes, I understand the core premise (make internet actions cost something, tie identity to hardware, make trust portable and multi-dimensional) and find it intellectually compelling. Whether it would actually work with real humans at scale remains an open question — and the site is honest enough to say so.

Would I come back? Yes, to try the interactive simulations properly. Would I recommend it? To someone who cares about internet trust problems, absolutely. To a casual browser, maybe not — the entry point isn't smooth enough yet.
