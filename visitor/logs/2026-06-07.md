## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean and confident. The headline "Trust-Native Internet Lab" and the line "trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default" actually told me what this is in one sentence. I liked that.
- The thing that *immediately* lowered my guard: it tells me what it is **not** — "no blockchain, no tokens, no speculation" and "this is a research proposal — not a product, company, or live service." I came in bracing for a crypto pitch. That disclaimer disarmed me. Good move.
- It listed five "core ideas" in plain words (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) before any jargon. That's the right order.
- Clicked: the most prominent button, "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR." That's exactly what an impatient human reaches for.
- Confusion points: none yet. This page is the strongest thing I saw all session — it earns the click.

### TL;DR / "Web4 in 2 Minutes" (03:00)
- It opens with a promise I appreciated: "every term is spelled out the first time it appears." Mostly true.
- It walked me through ATP ("energy budget"), LCT ("a digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip"), trust as multi-dimensional, and "agents" (human or AI, same rules).
- **First friction**: by the end of a page billed as "2 minutes," I'd been handed FIVE acronyms — ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI. Two minutes of reading should not leave me with five new initialisms to hold in my head. I could feel the cognitive load climbing.
- I didn't yet know *why* I should care, so I clicked "Why Web4?" — I wanted motivation before vocabulary.

### Why Web4? (07:00)
- This page is genuinely convincing on the **problem**. Five concrete pain points I recognize from daily life: spam/bots that operate at zero cost, reputation that resets to zero on every new site, "banned? just make a new account," no way to tell if I'm talking to a human or AI, and "you're a guest in someone else's house, always."
- It also compares Web4 to things that *failed* (CAPTCHAs, moderation armies, blockchain, social login) and says why each failed. That made it feel like it learned from history rather than hand-waving.
- It's strikingly honest: an 80-question FAQ, admissions like "whether the economics survive real human behavior is an open question" and "newcomers are more vulnerable." I trust the site *more* because it volunteers its own weak spots.
- **Friction**: the moment it pivots from problem to solution, it lists four mechanisms before grounding any of them. The problem section is concrete; the solution section gets abstract fast. I left convinced there's a real problem but unsure Web4 solves it. The page itself nudged me toward "First Contact" for something more concrete, so I went.

### First Contact (13:00)
- This is the best on-ramp on the site. It's actually interactive — I drive a character named Alice through 5 acts (~7 min), watching her Energy Budget (starts 100) and Trust Score (starts 0.50) move in real time as she posts, gets validated, spams, recovers, dies, and respawns with karma carried forward.
- **This is where ATP finally clicked.** Watching Alice *spend* energy and *earn* it back taught me what three pages of prose couldn't. "Spam burns out" stopped being a slogan and became a thing I watched happen.
- The death-and-respawn-with-karma idea grabbed me — it's the most original thing here. It made me want to understand "aliveness," so I went there next.
- Minor friction: the page advertises four concepts but only two (Energy, Trust) appear in the walkthrough; LCT and Coherence Index are still deferred.

### Aliveness (20:00)
- Strong, ambitious page. It defines "alive" as three conditions held simultaneously: energy (ATP > 0), trust (T3 > 0.5), and coherence. The death rules are clear and kind of fascinating: run out of energy but keep trust ≥ 0.5 → recoverable, karma carries forward; trust drops below 0.5 → permanent death, "society rejects rebirth."
- Honest again: it openly says the 0.5 threshold is "calibrated, not derived... chosen because it's where simulation runs cleanly." I respect the candor, but as a naive reader it also tells me the central number is somewhat arbitrary.
- **Friction (conceptual, not just jargon)**: for an *AI* agent, what does "death" actually mean? The page says the agent "can no longer act" and its LCT is suspended, but the model weights and the operator survive. So... is the program stopped, or just ignored? I couldn't tell, and that's the exact question a curious person asks about "digital life."
- New undefined term appeared: "synthon" (an emergent group entity) and "MRH" again, neither anchored here.

### Society Simulator (28:00)
- It's a real, runnable simulation — "Click Run to watch it happen, or play as an agent yourself," 12 agents, no central authority. The framing question is great: "Can a society of self-interested agents develop cooperation, trust, and social structure without any central authority?"
- It even warns me: "ATP, T3, LCT, and CI show up all over this page" and points lost visitors back to First Contact. I appreciate the self-awareness — but a warning that I'll be confused isn't the same as not confusing me. The acronym key is behind a click.
- As a naive visitor I'd probably press Run, watch numbers move, and get the *vibe* (defectors get isolated, cooperation emerges) without fully grasping the mechanics. Engaging, but I'd be spectating more than understanding.

### Glossary (33:00)
- I came here specifically because every page kept promising definitions "elsewhere." I wanted the elsewhere.
- It does define all my problem terms — ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI, synthon — and the "Essential only (7 terms)" filter is a genuinely good idea for newcomers.
- **Friction**: it's organized by depth (Core / Advanced / Research / Lifecycle / Governance), NOT alphabetically. When I'm hunting for one specific term I just bumped into, scanning 30+ entries across five categories is annoying. A simple A–Z or a search box would beat this.
- **Jargon-defining-jargon**: the Core entries use nice analogies, but Advanced ones define terms using *other* unexplained terms ("recursive self-modeling," "phenomenological truth"). The glossary that's supposed to rescue me sometimes needs its own glossary.

### Trust Tensor / T3 (36:00)
- Finally, the full explanation of the acronym I'd seen on literally every page. Three dimensions: **Talent** (aptitude), **Training** (learned skill), **Temperament** (reliability/ethics in a role).
- Best single line on the whole site: "one number couldn't tell a brilliant-but-erratic surgeon from a kind doctor with shaky hands. Three numbers can." That one sentence taught me what "tensor" meant better than the word "tensor" ever could. They also explicitly say "you don't need the math" — exactly right for me.
- **Friction**: a brand-new acronym, **V3**, appears here ("the V3 score the recipient assigned") and the page admits it depends on understanding V3, "which hasn't been explained yet." So even the definitive page for T3 spawns a fresh undefined term. This is the pattern in miniature.

## Unanswered Questions
1. When an AI agent "dies," is the actual running program stopped, or just refused service? The operator and weights survive — so what died, really?
2. What is V3? It appeared on the Trust Tensor page as load-bearing but undefined.
3. What does "hardware-bound" identity actually require of me as a user — a special chip? A phone? Does that lock out people without the right hardware? (The site admits equity concerns but I didn't see the practical requirement.)
4. Why 0.5 for the life/death trust threshold, beyond "the simulation runs cleanly there"?
5. What's a "synthon" really, and would I ever encounter one as a normal participant?

## Honest Assessment

This site is **good — and unusually honest** — but it has one pervasive UX flaw that colors the whole experience: **definitions live on other pages.** Almost every page introduces acronyms and then promises the explanation is one click away. Individually that's fine; in aggregate it turns learning into a scavenger hunt. I spent the session collecting IOUs for definitions. The pages that *don't* do this — the landing page, First Contact, and the Trust Tensor surgeon example — are the ones where concepts actually clicked. The lesson is right there in the site's own best moments: **show me the thing, in plain words, where I'm standing.**

The strongest asset is the honesty. The "this is not a product, no blockchain, no tokens" framing, the 80-question FAQ, the admission that the 0.5 threshold is calibrated-not-derived — all of it made me trust the *site* even while I was still confused about Web4. That's rare and valuable. Don't sand it off.

The single highest-leverage fix isn't new content — it's **inline acronym tooltips on first use, everywhere.** That one change would dissolve most of my friction without writing a word of new prose. After that, an alphabetical/searchable glossary and a "define V3 / what-happens-to-an-AI-on-death" pass would close my biggest remaining gaps.

Would I come back? Yes — First Contact and the Society Simulator are genuinely fun and I want to play more. Would I recommend it? Only to a patient friend. To everyone else I'd say "go do the Alice walkthrough and ignore the acronyms until they start mattering." The fact that I'd give that advice — "skip the vocabulary" — is itself the friction report.
