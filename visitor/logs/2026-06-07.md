## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Calm, confident, and refreshingly honest. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the subtitle did a lot of work up front: *"'Web4': a proposed internet trust model — trust verified by behavior, not assumed by default; no blockchain, not a Web3 successor."* As someone who has only ever heard "Web3 = crypto," having "no blockchain, no tokens, no speculation" said in the first breath defused my skepticism immediately.
- The problem hook landed: *"The Internet Has a Trust Problem"* — spam wins, reputations are trapped, bad actors get fresh starts. I felt that.
- I liked that the five core ideas were given **plain-English names first** (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) with the acronyms (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH, CI) demoted to "shorthand." That ordering matters — I wasn't hit with an acronym wall.
- Clicked: "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR."
- Confusion points: None serious. Five acronyms are still *named* on the page, so a skimmer who ignores the "plain English first" framing could still feel the alphabet soup, but the design clearly anticipates that.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes — /tldr (03:00)
- This is the right first page. Concrete problem statements I recognized: *"a spammer can create a new account in seconds"* and *"your 10-year reputation on Twitter means nothing on Reddit."*
- Three fixes (energy costs, hardware-anchored identity, portable trust) were intuitive and each acronym got a short inline gloss (ATP = "personal energy budget," LCT = "digital ID card that lives in your device's security chip").
- Confusion: "Agents (human or AI)" flickers by without explanation, and *how* energy refills is asserted but not shown. Minor for a TL;DR.
- Followed its recommendation: "First Contact."

### First Contact — /first-contact (06:00)
- Strong. Before the sim it teaches four ideas and explicitly tells me *"you don't need to memorize these"* and that each will be *"explained in the story the moment Alice uses it."* That promise lowered my anxiety.
- The real/simulated question — which I'd normally fixate on — is answered cleanly and repeatedly: *"a model of a proposed system... with nothing to sign up for yet"* and *"you're running a simulation of the proposed model, not using a product."* No dissonance. This used to be a thing I'd trip on; it's handled.
- Minor: "Aliveness" is named in the nav/setup but not explained before the story; MRH is in the menu but absent from the intro.

### How It Works — /how-it-works (10:00)
- Honest banner up top: *"Everything below describes how Web4 would work, validated through simulations."* Good expectation-setting.
- Clear five-system sequence (LCT → ATP → T3 → CI → MRH) **with an acronym table in plain English**, plus a concrete "Life 1 → Life 2" lifecycle walkthrough (start 100 ATP, earn to 105, trust 0.5→0.65, die at 145, reborn). The worked example is exactly what abstract pages need.
- Friction: jargon stacks late in the page — "synthons" and "VCM" appear with little setup and felt like a difficulty cliff after the gentle start.

### Trust Tensor (T3) — /trust-tensor (15:00)
- The 0–1 scale is anchored well: *"0.5 = newcomer baseline, 0.7 = ~70% cooperative behavior in this role, 0.9 = consistently exceptional."* That sentence is the single most useful thing on the page for a newcomer — it tells me what a number *means*.
- Three dimensions (Talent/Training/Temperament) with the surgeon-vs-mechanic role example were intuitive.
- Friction: the update formula `base = 0.02 × (quality − 0.5)` leans on **V3** (a value tensor) which isn't defined on this page. To actually understand how trust updates I'd have to already know V3 — a circular dependency. "Recipient attestation" also appears without a gloss.

### ATP Economics — /atp-economics (20:00)
- ATP is defined consistently as "your energy budget" — no drift between "energy"/"attention"/"usage" that I could detect. Good.
- The keeper line is there: *"ATP as cash and ADP as the receipt you get when you spend it"* plus *"not a second kind of money; it's just proof an action happened."* There's a "60-second version" bullet box, which is the thing I most wanted.
- 5% transfer fee is explained with a worked number (send 105, they get 100, 5 to the redistribution pool) and a rationale (kills circular farming). Clear.
- Friction: **ADP decay timing is vague** — "ages out over a window of weeks" but no actual number, flagged as "still evolving." I couldn't tell how long an unconfirmed contribution survives.

### Trust Neighborhood (MRH) — /trust-neighborhood (25:00)
- The room/strangers-need-introductions metaphor is great, and "you see your connections, their connections, and their connections — nothing more" made the 3-hop rule click.
- Decay is given with numbers: **"0.7× per hop"** → direct 0.70, two hops 0.49, three hops 0.34. The page is honest that "0.7 is a design parameter, not a physical constant."
- Confusion: are strangers beyond 3 hops *invisible* or just *untrusted*? The page reads both ways. And "bridge agents" prevent bubbles — but how, in practice?

### LCT Explainer — /lct-explainer (30:00)
- Best analogy on the site: *"Imagine your phone becoming your driver's license — but one that can't be photocopied, faked from a stolen photo, or impersonated."* The "private key generated inside the silicon and physically can't leave it" line made hardware-binding concrete.
- The page **anchors the 0–1 scale before showing decimals**: *"every trust score on this page runs on a 0–1 scale, where 0.5 is the alive/dead line, 0.75 is solid, and 0.90 is the hardware-bound ceiling."* That's exactly right, and it's done *before* the hardware table (TPM 0.90 / Secure Enclave 0.85 / FIDO2 0.75 / Software 0.50).
- Friction: "witness" is used for two different things (your devices attesting to each other vs. network infrastructure nodes). I had to re-read to keep them straight.

### Why Web4? — /why-web4 (34:00)
- Problem-first and excellent at it. The "Social Credit vs Web4" comparison table is the clearest "what this is NOT" I've seen.
- FAQ is huge but well-organized (Most Asked + topic groups + collapsible). The page itself says *"Stop reading at any point — each section stands on its own,"* which helps.
- Friction: it's genuinely long (~15k words). A sequential reader hits fatigue; a navigator is fine. This is a known trade-off, not a bug. Acronyms (ATP/LCT/T3/MRH/CI/SAL) do hit early here, though the page points skimmers to the TL;DR.

### MRH Explorer — /mrh-explorer (38:00)
- Interactive depth visualizer. Useful, but this is where I got genuinely confused — see the headline friction below.
- It shows **"trust signal ∼0.85" at depth 1 and "∼0.65" at depth 2**, with prose "drops by roughly a quarter with each hop... about half by depth 3."

### Karma Journey — /karma-journey (40:00)
- Fun and well-taught. Inline glosses for Trust ("Talent + Training + Temperament, averaged"), ATP ("run out and you die"), and CI ("erratic behavior lowers it, which makes all future actions cost more ATP").
- The CI² mechanic is both *shown and interpreted*: *"Squaring makes inconsistency compound against you... 0.9² = 0.81; a serious dip nearly halves you, 0.6² = 0.36."* That's the right way to present a formula — with its meaning.
- Minor: "raw trust" (survival) vs "effective trust" (karma tier) — two trust numbers with different jobs took a second read.

## Honest Assessment
This is, again, an unusually well-built educational site. The on-ramp is genuinely good: every concept page now anchors the 0–1 scale before throwing decimals at me (LCT and Trust Tensor both nail this), the real-vs-simulated question is answered calmly and repeatedly instead of with a scary banner, and the interactive pages (Karma Journey especially) teach formulas *with their meaning* rather than just displaying math. I reached "solid" understanding and would happily recommend it.

The one thing that actually tripped me — and it's a real trip, not a nitpick — is that the **two pages dedicated to MRH disagree on the headline number.** The concept page drills "0.7× per hop, 0.70 at one hop" into me; then I click into the interactive Explorer that's supposed to *demonstrate* that same concept and it shows 0.85 at one hop. Both pages are individually honest ("design parameter, not a constant"), but neither acknowledges the *other's* number, so the mismatch reads as a mistake rather than two models. For a topic the whole site treats as a core pillar, having its flagship pages quote different first-hop values undercuts the otherwise-strong trust the site builds. Reconcile the two, or name the relationship out loud on both pages, and this jumps from "solid" to "airtight."
