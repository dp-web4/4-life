## Unanswered Questions
1. **What would a real Web4 deployment timeline look like?** The "What's Coming" section lists milestones but no timeframes (intentionally, per "no timeline commitments"). Still leaves me wondering: is this 2 years away or 20?
2. **How does the 5% ATP transfer fee work in practice for everyday transactions?** I understand the circular farming prevention, but does buying a coffee cost 5% overhead? The Day in Web4 market scenario shows "Escrow cost: 2 ATP" for a 350 ATP camera — is that the 5% or a separate fee?
3. **What happens to a synthon when a key member leaves?** The early warning system detects decay, but what does actual dissolution look like? Does everyone's trust take a hit?
4. **How are "witnesses" incentivized to participate?** Universities, nonprofits, companies can run witness nodes — but what's their economic motivation? Is there an ATP reward for witnessing?
5. **If I have high trust in Community A and join Community B, what's the actual transfer mechanism?** The MRH page says ~65% weight discount, but who decides that number? Is it Community B's choice?

## Honest Assessment

This is an exceptionally well-constructed educational site. The progression from "Internet has a trust problem" through concrete scenarios to formal mechanisms is pedagogically sound. The Day in Web4 page alone justifies the visit — it answers "but what would this actually look like?" more effectively than any other page.

What impressed me most:

1. **Honesty**: The "What Could Go Wrong" page doesn't pull punches. "#1 existential risk: nobody adopts it" is the kind of candor that builds trust (ironically appropriate for a trust project).

2. **Concrete before abstract**: The site mostly follows this principle well. When it works (Day in Web4, First Contact, Narratives), it's excellent. When it doesn't (Compare page without simulation context, Coherence Index as abstract math), the friction shows.

3. **The narratives**: The four featured society stories (Meadow, Ironworks, Garden, Tidepool) are the most effective teaching tool after Day in Web4. The insight that 29% cooperators + reciprocators outperforms 100% cooperators is genuinely surprising.

4. **Interactive depth**: Five interactive experiences (Karma Journey, Society Simulator, Playground, Sandbox, Trust Explorer) offer different entry points for different learning styles.

What could improve:

1. **The Why Web4 FAQ wall**: 30+ questions is comprehensive but overwhelming. The "Most Asked" category helps, but the page still feels like drinking from a fire hose on first visit.

2. **Learning Journey discoverability**: This should be a primary navigation item, not buried under "Start Here." It's the roadmap visitors need.

3. **Synthon explanation gap**: This concept appears frequently but is only fully explained deep in the Aliveness page. Either introduce it earlier with a simple analogy or reduce its appearances until visitors reach that page.

4. **The "research prototype" vs "real product" tension**: The site oscillates between "here's how your day would look" (aspirational) and "this is a simulation" (current state). Both framings are valid and honest, but the gap between them is the main source of "would I recommend?" hesitation. It's the difference between "look at this cool idea" and "try this tool."

Overall: One of the most thorough and honestly-presented technical education sites I've encountered. The trust mechanics are intellectually compelling, the interactive elements make them tangible, and the honest acknowledgment of unsolved problems builds the very trust the project is about. I'd return to run the simulations and explore the interactive tools more deeply.
