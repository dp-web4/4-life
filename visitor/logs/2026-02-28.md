## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Bold claim — "The Internet Has a Trust Problem." Immediately relatable. The opening scenario about 10,000 fake accounts is concrete and grounding.
- **What worked**: The "Big Idea" section breaks down four pillars in plain English. The recommended learning path (Why Web4 → First Contact → Playground) gives me a clear next step.
- **Clicked**: "1. Why Web4?" as the site suggested
- **Confusion points**: Lots of links and options on the page — I counted 15+ clickable destinations. A bit overwhelming, but the numbered "Start Here" path helped focus me.
- **Minor note**: I saw a "Trust Dilemma" interactive element mentioned but wasn't sure what it does before clicking.

### Why Web4 (03:00)
- **First impression**: The five internet trust failures are spot-on — spam, siloed reputation, consequence evasion, AI deception, platform control. I've experienced all of these.
- **What worked**: Each problem gets a concrete example AND a root cause. That's good pedagogy — don't just show the symptom, explain why it persists.
- **Confusion points**: The page transitions from relatable problems to solutions that suddenly drop acronyms — ATP, LCT, T3, MRH. I don't know what any of these mean yet. The page links to explainer pages for each, but the jump in vocabulary density is jarring.
- **FAQ section**: Surprisingly thorough and honest. The "Isn't this just blockchain?" question was exactly what I was wondering. Answer is clear: no, different mechanism.
- **Clicked**: "Start First Contact" call-to-action at the bottom

### First Contact (08:00)
- **First impression**: Alice's story! This is a narrative introduction. I'm following one person through a Web4 community.
- **What worked**: The step-by-step format (10 actions) with observable consequences makes the mechanics tangible. Alice posts something good → trust goes up, energy gets earned back. Alice spams → energy burns, access gets restricted. Cause and effect is clear.
- **What didn't land**: The page says "Interactive version requires JavaScript." I assumed it was interactive from the outset. The non-JS fallback tells the story but I miss seeing the trust/energy numbers change in real-time.
- **Key learning**: This is where ATP (energy budget) and trust scoring first clicked for me. Not because they were defined, but because I saw them in action through Alice's journey.
- **Clicked**: "Karma Journey" link — curious about the death/rebirth thing mentioned

### Karma Journey (14:00)
- **First impression**: This is a game! I have stats (Trust: 0.361, Energy: 100, Consistency: 0.85) and I make choices.
- **What worked**: The eight action choices with clear costs/gains are intuitive. "Help a peer" costs 12 energy. "Take shortcut" gains 12 but at what hidden cost? The tension between cooperative and selfish choices is immediately engaging.
- **What I learned**: When I "die" (energy hits zero), my final trust determines my karma tier for the next life. High trust → better starting conditions. This is the "permanent consequences" idea from the landing page, made visceral.
- **Confusion points**: The "karma tier" labels (Honored/Neutral/Constrained) aren't explained before you encounter them. I had to die once to understand the rebirth mechanic. That might be intentional design, though — learning by failing.
- **Clicked**: Explored for a few lives, then went to Playground

- **First impression**: Identity without passwords. Hardware chips prove you're real.
- **What worked**: The Web2 → Web3 → Web4 comparison table is excellent. Passwords (shareable) → private keys (stealable) → hardware attestations (verifiable). Progressive improvement is clear. The witness slider showing how more devices increase trust is a nice interactive touch.
- **Confusion points**: "Witnesses" still feels abstract. I understand that multiple devices confirm identity, but the mechanics of how devices "witness" each other aren't intuitive. The page jumps from analogy to JSON structures too abruptly.

### ATP Economics (31:00)
- **First impression**: Energy budget details. This is what makes the whole system work economically.
- **What worked**: The ATP simulator (start with 100 ATP, choose actions) is the best interactive element on the site. Sending spam costs 5 and rewards 0. Meaningful contributions cost 15 but return 25. The math is simple and the lesson is immediate: quality pays, spam doesn't.
- **Key insight**: The "quality ramp" — high-quality work earns ~7x more than mediocre work. This is the incentive design that makes everything else hold together.
- **Confusion points**: The 5% transfer fee on ATP transfers is mentioned but not well-motivated. Why 5%? What does it prevent exactly?
- **Clicked**: Next → Trust Tensor

### Trust Tensor (34:00)
- **First impression**: Multi-dimensional trust. Not just "7 out of 10" but three separate dimensions.
- **What worked**: Talent, Training, Temperament as three axes makes intuitive sense. The surgeon example is great — you'd want different weights for a surgeon (high Temperament) vs. a mechanic (high Talent).
- **V3 Output Scorer**: Clicking different output types and seeing scores was engaging. The fact that Veracity + Validity outweigh Valuation (accuracy over popularity) is a meaningful design choice.
- **Confusion points**: Decay half-lives (365 days for Talent, 30 for Temperament) — why these specific numbers? The page doesn't justify them, just states them. Also, the sub-dimension nesting suggests this could get arbitrarily complex.
- **Clicked**: Next → Coherence Index

### Coherence Index (36:00)
- **What worked**: Maria's stolen phone scenario makes the concept click instantly. If someone logs in from Tokyo five minutes after being in New York, something is wrong. Four dimensions (spatial, capability, temporal, relational) cover most anomaly types.
- **Confusion points**: The multiplicative formula (not averaging) means one bad dimension tanks everything. This is explained but feels harsh — what about legitimate edge cases? The physics analogies (quantum coherence, geodesics) feel gratuitous.
- **Clicked**: Next → Aliveness

### Aliveness (38:00)
- **What worked**: Three conditions for being "alive" (ATP > 0, Trust > 0.5, CI coherent). Clean, memorable. The survival challenge interactive element is fun — try to keep all three metrics above thresholds for 5 turns.
- **Key insight**: The 0.5 trust threshold as a "phase transition" point. Below it, behavior is indistinguishable from random. Above it, intentional. That's a genuinely interesting framing.
- **Confusion points**: The appeals mechanism is "designed but not deployed." This is honest but also concerning — what happens to false positives RIGHT NOW?

### A Day in Web4 (40:00)
- **What worked**: Seven scenarios showing Web4 in everyday life. The phone unlock, restaurant review, and false accusation scenarios are the most compelling. Each one shows today's problem → Web4's solution → why it's better.
- **Best scenario**: False accusations. In today's internet, one viral accusation can destroy you. In Web4, years of trust history provide resilience. That's powerful.
- **Confusion**: Minor — mechanics aren't detailed, but that's fine for this format. It's a vision page, not a specs page.

### What Could Go Wrong (42:00)

- **Most concerning risk**: Hardware gatekeeping — identity depends on chips from Intel/Apple/Google/Qualcomm. If they control the hardware, do they control the system?

## Unanswered Questions

1. **Who builds this?** I see a whitepaper and GitHub but no team page, no organization name, no "about" section. Is this one person's research project? An academic lab? A startup? The anonymity is notable.

2. **What's the adoption path?** The "What Could Go Wrong" page lists adoption failure as risk #1, but I didn't find a concrete roadmap. How does this go from "working simulation with 12-50 agents" to "actual internet protocol"?

3. **How does the 0.5 trust threshold interact with cultural differences?** The page says it's inspired by phase transitions, but trust norms vary across cultures. Is 0.5 universal? Who decides?

4. **What happens to people who can't afford hardware with TPM chips?** The identity system requires specific hardware. This creates a digital divide that isn't addressed.

5. **How does V3 scoring handle creative or unconventional work?** The Veracity/Validity focus rewards accuracy and correctness. What about art, humor, satire — things that are valuable but not "correct" in a measurable sense?

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the most ambitious interactive explainer sites I've encountered. The breadth is staggering — 60+ pages, 20+ interactive tools, concept pages with real simulations. Someone (or some team) has put enormous thought into making abstract protocol design accessible through hands-on experience.

**What works brilliantly:**
- The problem-first approach. Every concept page starts with WHY before WHAT. By the time I reach the mechanism, I already care about the problem it solves.
- The interactive elements. The ATP simulator, Karma Journey, and Coherence Index sliders aren't gimmicks — they teach through experience. I understood energy economics by watching my spam burn out, not by reading about it.
- The intellectual honesty. "What Could Go Wrong" and the Threat Model are refreshingly candid. Projects that document their own failure modes earn my trust.
- Progressive disclosure. The TLDR → Why Web4 → First Contact → deeper concepts pipeline lets me go as deep as I want without drowning.

**What needs work:**
- The acronym problem persists. ATP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI, SAL — this is a LOT of jargon for a newcomer. Each term makes sense when explained, but encountering them unexplained on early pages is disorienting. The floating glossary helps but isn't discoverable enough.
- The distinction between tools (Playground vs. Society Simulator vs. Simulation Sandbox vs. Lab Console) could be sharper. I visited several before understanding which one I wanted.
- The "how do the pieces fit together" picture is still fuzzy. Each concept page is solid individually, but I lack a single diagram showing: LCT proves identity → ATP governs energy → T3 measures trust → CI detects anomalies → all three determine aliveness. The "How It Works" page comes closest but doesn't have a clean visual integration.
- The project feels anonymous. No team, no institution, no origin story. For a project asking me to rethink internet trust, I want to know who's asking. Trust goes both ways.

**Bottom line**: I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left understanding the core vision, the key mechanisms, and why someone might want this. The site taught me — I didn't have to infer. That's the highest compliment for an explainer. I'd come back to try more of the simulations, and I'd recommend this to a technically curious friend.
