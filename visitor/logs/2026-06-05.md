## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Calm and clear. The headline — *"4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab — explore Web4 through simulations"* — immediately tells me this is a sandbox, not a product. I appreciated the early disclaimers: *"a proposed internet trust model,"* *"no blockchain, not a Web3 successor."* That preemptively answered the first two questions in my head ("is this crypto?" and "is this Web3 hype?").
- The problem framing landed fast: *"Spam wins because accounts are free. Reputations are trapped in silos. Bad actors get unlimited fresh starts."* I felt the problem before being asked to learn a solution. Good.
- **Clicked**: The site clearly steers me — *"Start here: 2-minute TL;DR →"* is the primary, recommended button. I followed the recommendation rather than wandering. The funnel is well-designed; I never felt lost about where to begin.
- **Confusion points**: The "Five Core Ideas" (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) are nice plain words, but I didn't yet know they each map to an acronym I'd meet later. Mild.

### TL;DR (03:00)
- This page is honest about its own approach: *"Plain English first, so you can read this cold — every term is spelled out the first time it appears."* I believed it.
- The framing *"The internet has no memory. A spammer can create a new account in seconds"* is vivid and relatable.
- **But here's where it started**: four acronyms hit me at once — **ATP** (Allocation Transfer Packets), **LCT** (Linked Context Token), **T3** (Talent/Training/Temperament), **MRH** (Markov Relevancy Horizon). Even spelled out, "Markov Relevancy Horizon" is opaque to a normal human. I understood the *gist* (energy, identity, trust, neighborhood) but the acronyms themselves felt like homework.
- Liked the clean split: Web4 = *"the protocol — the rules and math,"* 4-Life = *"the lab where you explore those rules."* That distinction stuck.

### Why Web4? (08:00)
- Strongest page for *motivation*. The five problems (spam is free, reputation trapped in silos, unlimited fresh starts, AI verification, platform control) are concrete and convincing. I nodded along.
- The **"Honest Questions"** FAQ genuinely raised my trust in the site. It admits hard things: appeals process *"designed but not yet deployed,"* numbers *"calibrated, not derived,"* internet-scale performance *"unproven."* A naive visitor reads this and thinks "okay, these people aren't selling me snake oil." This is the site's best feature.
- **Hand-wavy bit**: the honesty about "calibrated, not derived" is refreshing but also leaves me unable to judge *why* the system works. I'm told it behaves well in simulation, but I can't tell a good calibration from an arbitrary one.

### First Contact (13:00)
- Billed as the page that makes the acronyms "land easier." It's an intro/scaffold to a 5-act interactive walkthrough ("drive Alice's choices"). I couldn't run the interactive part in this session, but the framing is good.
- **Problem**: this page introduced a *fifth* acronym I hadn't met — **CI (Coherence Index)** — while *not* explaining **MRH**, which had been one of the four "core" acronyms on the TL;DR. So the acronym set is inconsistent page-to-page. That's disorienting: I'm collecting acronyms but the set keeps shifting.

### Karma Journey (17:00)
- Promising interactive concept: *"Live multiple lives. Make choices. Watch how trust and karma compound across reincarnations."* The reincarnation metaphor is memorable.
- I face 8 choices like "Help a peer (–12 energy)" vs "Exploit a loophole (+18 energy)." The cost/reward asymmetry teaches the lesson viscerally — *if* it works (I read the ruleset but couldn't see choice resolution this session).
- **Confusion**: starts me at CI = 0.85 which makes my actions cost 40% more, described as "non-punitive" but it sure *feels* like a penalty for a newcomer. And "raw vs. effective trust" needs careful reading.

### ATP Economics (22:00)
- The biology analogy is excellent: *"adenosine triphosphate is the molecule your cells spend to do work — they burn ATP, get ADP back, and recharge ATP from food."* That's the clearest single explanation on the whole site.
- **But this was my most frustrating page.** Friction:
  - **ADP stays vague.** *"ADP is the spent-energy counterpart to ATP: proof an action happened, not a second currency."* If it auto-converts invisibly, why am I being told to track a receipt? I left not understanding why ADP exists as a named thing.
  - The **"7x gap"** is named before it's derived, so I had to backtrack.
  - A section flagged **"Still evolving"** mid-page quietly undermined my confidence that this is settled enough to bother learning.
  - **No flow diagram** of ATP → ADP → confirmation → recharge. This page screams for one picture.

### Glossary (27:00)
- Best-executed page for reducing overwhelm. The **"Essential only" filter** (7 foundational terms) and "Jump to" sections (Core / Advanced / Research / Lifecycle / Governance) are exactly the progressive disclosure a naive visitor needs.
- Honest framing: *"Academic terms like Epistemic Proprioception or Synthon live in Advanced/Research — safely ignorable until you're curious."* Permission to ignore complexity is a gift.

### Society Simulator (30:00)
- The payoff page: watch 12 agents *"form alliances, betray each other, and self-organize"* with no central authority. The premise question — *"Can a society of self-interested agents develop cooperation... without any central authority?"* — is the most compelling hook on the site.
- **But it front-loads SIX acronyms (ATP, T3, LCT, CI, MRH, V3) immediately.** There's an escape hatch ("never seen these terms? First Contact is a 7-minute intro"), but if I haven't done First Contact, this page is a wall.

### Aliveness (33:00)
- **The single best explanation on the site**: *"Think of it like a professional license: you need resources to operate (ATP), a track record people trust (T3), and consistent behavior (CI). Lose any one... like a doctor who loses their license."* This made three acronyms click at once via one analogy.
- The two-deaths distinction is intuitive: energy death (*"like recovering from bankruptcy,"* recoverable) vs trust death (*"like losing a professional license,"* permanent).
- **Confusing**: the 0.5 threshold is again "calibrated, not derived"; CI is *squared* (why?); and the "pattern corpus" that karma distills into is abstract — what does it actually look like?

### Trust Tensor (35:00)
- The surgeon analogy nails it: *"You might trust a brilliant surgeon who's unreliable differently from a steady surgeon with less raw talent."* I instantly understood why one trust number is too crude.
- The Alice role-switching example reinforces it well.
- **Lingering fog**: the T3-vs-V3 boundary stays subtle, and "0.85 = 70% cooperative behavior" feels like an asserted mapping I can't verify. Decay half-lives (365-day vs 30-day) are narrated but feel arbitrary.

## Honest Assessment

This site is *good* — noticeably more honest and better-scaffolded than I expected from something explaining a novel protocol. Three things stand out: (1) the **"Honest Questions" FAQ** that admits what isn't built yet, (2) the **professional-license analogy** on Aliveness that makes three concepts click at once, and (3) the **glossary's "Essential only" filter** that gives me permission to ignore complexity. The entry funnel (landing → TL;DR) never left me wondering where to start.

My single biggest friction is **acronym creep**. By the time I'd browsed six pages I was juggling ATP, LCT, T3, MRH, CI, and V3 — and worse, the "core" set shifts from page to page (TL;DR pushes MRH, First Contact pushes CI instead). The *concepts* are intuitive when explained with analogies; the *acronyms* are the tax I keep paying. I'd happily learn "energy, identity, trust, neighborhood, consistency" — the plain words from the landing page — and meet the acronyms only when a page actually needs them. Right now the site teaches the codes before the concepts have earned them.

Second-biggest: the **interactive pages** (First Contact walkthrough, Karma Journey, Society Simulator, the inline simulators) are clearly the heart of the pedagogy — "learn by doing" is the right bet — but as a reader I only saw their *setup* text, not their payoff. If a real visitor bounces before clicking "Start," they never get the experiential lesson and are left with just the acronym tax. The static framing needs to carry enough value on its own that a non-clicker still leaves having learned *something* concrete.

Would I return? Yes — I'm curious to actually *run* the Society Simulator. Would I recommend it? To a patient, curious friend, yes. To a casual person who hates acronyms, not yet — they'd bounce at the TL;DR's four-acronym wall before reaching the analogies that would have saved them.

**So what?** The site's analogies are excellent but arrive *after* the acronyms that need them. Flip the order — concept and analogy first, acronym last — and most of this session's friction disappears.
