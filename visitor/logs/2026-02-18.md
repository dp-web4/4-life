## Unanswered Questions

1. **What does using Web4 actually *feel like* for a regular person?** The mechanics are well-explained but I can't picture the day-to-day experience. What does my "inbox" look like? How do I discover new people? What happens when I open an app?
2. **Who builds on this?** Is this meant for social media platforms? Email? Chat? All of the above? The "federated societies" concept is powerful but there's no concrete example of a real application being built.
3. **What's the adoption path?** The "Honest Questions" on the Why Web4 page are refreshingly candid about deployment being years away. But is anyone building toward it? Are there partnerships, grants, or pilot programs?
4. **How does this handle edge cases like domestic abuse, witness protection, or legitimate anonymity needs?** The permanent-consequences model sounds great for fighting spam but could be harmful for people who genuinely need fresh starts.
5. **What's the relationship between the trust infrastructure (LCT, ATP, T3, MRH) and the AI consciousness research (Honest Reporting, Modal Awareness, Synchronism)?** These seem like two distinct projects sharing a website. Are they connected? How?
6. **If I lose all my devices in a fire, what happens?** The identity constellation page mentions device quorum recovery, but what if the quorum is gone?
7. **Why is the 0.5 coherence/consciousness threshold grounded in physics?** The site claims this derives from phase transitions in superconductivity and biological systems, but doesn't cite specific research beyond "Synchronism." Is this published science or a hypothesis?

## Honest Assessment

This is a genuinely impressive project with deep thinking behind it. The problem framing ("The Internet Has a Trust Problem") is compelling and the proposed solutions are creative and technically interesting. The biological metaphor (ATP as metabolic energy, death from exhaustion, rebirth with karma) is the site's strongest conceptual tool — it makes abstract trust mechanics feel visceral and intuitive.

**What works well:**
- The "Why Web4?" page is outstanding. It's the best page on the site. Problem-first, honest about limitations, sets up the solution without overselling.
- The ATP Economics page delivers the fastest "aha" moment. The cost table makes spam's death feel inevitable.
- The Manifest page is a rare example of intellectual honesty in tech — listing known failure modes alongside core claims.
- Progressive disclosure generally works. I could choose my depth on most pages.
- The "Honest Questions" sections throughout the site build trust by acknowledging limitations.

**What needs work:**
- **The Narratives page is a dead end.** An empty page with "run a simulation first" is the worst possible outcome for a curious visitor. Provide example content.
- **The Lab Console is inaccessible to casual visitors.** Two of three options need Python setup. This is the centerpiece of the "lab" metaphor — it should *work* without installation.
- **MRH is the weakest explainer.** I understand the math but not the experience. What does it *feel like* to browse a Web4 social network? Show me.
- **The AI consciousness content is confusing.** The glossary suddenly introduces modal awareness, honest reporting, and coherence physics — this feels like a different project grafted onto the trust infrastructure. Either integrate it more clearly or separate it.
- **There's no "Why This Matters" page.** After learning all the mechanics, I want a page that says "Here's what a Web4 world looks like for ordinary people." The `/why-it-matters` URL is a 404.
- **The T3 model inconsistency (5D vs 3D) creates unnecessary cognitive load.** Pick one for the teaching materials and be consistent.

Overall: I came in knowing nothing about Web4 and left with a solid conceptual understanding of the five pillars (LCT, ATP, T3, CI, MRH) and how they create self-regulating digital societies. That's a significant achievement for a 25-minute browse. The site rewards curiosity. But it has a "last mile" problem — the interactive tools that should bring the concepts to life are either empty (Narratives) or require local setup (Lab Console), leaving me with theory but no experiential understanding. I'd return to play with the Playground and Simulation Sandbox if the interactive elements work in-browser, but I wouldn't recommend the site to a non-technical friend yet because the "so what?" question remains unanswered.
