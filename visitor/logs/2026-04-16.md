## Unanswered Questions
1. What *backs* ATP economically? If it's not cryptocurrency and not real money, why would anyone care about spending it? The analogy to "energy" is helpful conceptually but doesn't answer what makes it scarce in practice.
2. How does the initial witness network form? The "bootstrapping" problem is acknowledged as unsolved but it seems like the critical blocker for any real deployment.
3. What happens to the permanent record when someone genuinely changes? A teenager's bad behavior following them forever seems cruel. Trust decay half-lives help, but Talent at 365 days is a long time.
4. Who runs the infrastructure? If there's no company and no blockchain, who hosts the trust graphs and ATP ledgers?
5. How would Web4 handle jurisdictional conflicts? A trust-native internet is global, but "harmful content" is defined differently in every country.
6. The "trust wallet" app — would this need to be platform-agnostic? What prevents Apple or Google from refusing to host it?
7. Could a wealthy bad actor just buy many devices to create a high-trust identity constellation? The page says hardware makes it "expensive" but doesn't quantify what that means relative to well-funded attackers.

## Honest Assessment

This is one of the best-organized research explainer sites I've encountered. The problem-first framing works brilliantly — by the time I reach the solution, I'm already nodding along because I *have* these frustrations. The progressive disclosure is well-executed: TL;DR for the impatient, deep dives for the curious, interactive labs for the hands-on.

The "What Could Go Wrong" page is the single most credibility-building page on the site. It addresses exactly the objections I was forming and acknowledges real unknowns. Most projects would hide these; this one leads with them.

The biological metaphor (ATP/energy, aliveness, death/rebirth) is genuinely helpful for making abstract concepts tangible. It works better than the blockchain-style jargon that Web3 projects typically use.

My biggest criticism: the site has a depth problem. Pages like /aliveness are trying to be both introductory explainers and technical references simultaneously. A first-time visitor needs the first 30% of that page; the rest is for people who are already invested. The lack of separation between "understand the concept" and "understand the implementation" creates an overwhelming experience on the deeper pages.

The acronym density is my second concern. Nine acronyms is a lot to carry in working memory. The concepts behind them are sound, but the naming creates an unnecessary cognitive barrier. A persistent glossary tooltip or sidebar would help enormously.

Overall: I came in skeptical and left genuinely interested. The ideas are coherent, the presentation is mostly excellent, and the honesty about limitations is refreshing. I'd bookmark this and come back to explore the interactive labs more carefully.
