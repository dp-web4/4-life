## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- First impression: Clean and surprisingly honest. "Not a product, not a company, not a blockchain" earned instant goodwill. The subtitle preempting "is this crypto?" was smart — that was literally my first suspicion.
- The five foundational ideas (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency) in plain words before any acronyms appeared was the right call.
- Clicked: the recommended "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR" door. Having ONE recommended starting point was genuinely helpful — the page has a LOT of doors otherwise.
- Confusion points: minor — "hestia" and "the hub" are name-dropped as running components before I knew what a trust layer even was, but they linked out so I moved on.

### Web4 in 2 Minutes (04:00)
- Did what it promised. The four numbered principles were digestible, each acronym expanded at first use.
- "Witness constellation" made me pause — evocative, but at this point I didn't know what a witness was, so it read as poetry rather than explanation.
- "Markov Relevancy Horizon" is a genuinely intimidating name for something the page itself summarizes as "trust neighborhood with a 3-hop maximum." The friendly gloss saved it, but I wondered why the scary name leads.
- The "Where is this now?" section (protocol vs. lab, what's deployed vs. future) is exactly the honesty I wanted. Good.

### Why Web4? (10:00)
- The problem section is the strongest writing on the site. "Actions have no cost. Bad actors can operate indefinitely at zero expense" — I felt that.
- The FAQ format worked for me; I could skip what I didn't care about. The deployment-status answer ("Research prototype... no live network with real users yet") kept trust high.
- One phrase stopped me: the Coherence Index "penalizes behavioral inconsistency squared." Squared? I filed it away hoping a later page would explain (it eventually did, but I carried the question for 30+ minutes).

### First Contact (18:00)
- The setup is clear: two metrics (ATP starts at 100, trust starts at 0.50), five acts of Alice's life. The "real rules, shown by simulation — not a demo" framing made me pay attention.
- The metric explainer says trust below 0.50 = "restricted features." Alice's arc preview shows her dipping to 0.48 in Act 3 (Stumble) and recovering to 0.62 in Act 4. So I concluded: dipping below 0.5 is survivable — you get restricted, you climb back. **Remember this — it gets contradicted later.**
- Interactive requires JavaScript; the static preview of the five acts was enough for me to follow the shape of it.

### Karma Journey (26:00)
- I came here because First Contact ended with "+12 karma bonus carries forward" and I wanted to know what karma meant. The page answers immediately: "your actions in this life become starting conditions for the next." Good.
- **Then I hit the wall.** The page says: "Raw Trust: Decides survival. Drop below 0.5 and this life ends," and lists trust collapse below 0.5 as PERMANENT. But First Contact just showed Alice at 0.48 recovering to 0.62. Which is it? Is 0.48 death or a bad week? These are the two pages the site funnels beginners through, back to back, and they state opposite rules for the exact same number.
- Second confusion: my starting stats show Consistency: 0.85, yet actions carry a "low consistency tax" and my action cost multiplier is 1.4x. To a newcomer, 0.85 out of 1.00 sounds like a B+ — why am I being taxed for it? Nothing on the page explains why 0.85 counts as "low," or what value would count as normal.
- The effective trust math (0.500 raw → 0.361 effective via "raw × CI²") I could actually verify myself: 0.5 × 0.85² ≈ 0.361. Satisfying — the numbers on screen obey the formula they state. More of this.

### Aliveness (54:00)
- Exhaustive — almost too much, but the expandable sections kept it navigable.

### Coherence Index (58:00)
- "Why is it CI², not just CI?" as a named section — thank you. This should have been linked from Why Web4 where "squared" first appears.
- "Why doesn't a new account start at CI 1.0?" (starts ~0.85) FINALLY explained the Karma Journey "low consistency tax" mystery from half an hour earlier. The explanation lives here, but the confusion happens there.

So one catastrophically failed dimension (0.1!) leaves you... alive and merely expensive? The math is fine; the framing oversells it, and a reader who checks the numbers against the threshold they just learned comes away confused about whether one bad dimension matters or not.
- The Maria's-stolen-phone and bot-farm scenarios were the most convincing security content on the site.

## Honest Assessment
This is the most honest explainer site I've encountered for a speculative technology. The repeated "not a product, not a blockchain, no live network yet" disclosures, the Spec/Reference/Running maturity badges, and the "What's Real, What's Simulated" table on the Society Simulator all bought my trust — and the site spends that trust wisely by then asking me to sit through genuinely dense material.

The concept sequencing works: I went from zero to correctly computing effective-trust values myself (0.5 × 0.85² = 0.361) in under an hour. ATP Economics and the Society Simulator's acronym-status table are the gold standard here.

But the site has a numbers-integrity problem at its most important boundary. The 0.5 trust threshold is presented everywhere as THE existential line — "the alive/dead line" — and then First Contact, Karma Journey, and Aliveness each describe crossing it differently. For a project whose entire pitch is "trust rules that are precise, mechanical, and verifiable," being imprecise about its own central rule is the worst possible place to be imprecise. Same story, smaller stakes, with the CI cost multipliers that disagree between the Aliveness table and the CI page's own formula. The site taught me to check its math — it should expect me to.

I'd come back. I want to run the Society Simulator with JavaScript and see whether the defectors really do get isolated.
