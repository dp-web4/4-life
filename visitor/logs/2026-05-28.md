### /atp-economics (15:00)
- **Finally — a concrete concept page.** ATP = Allocation Transfer Packets. *Yes*, the biology metaphor is intentional. Spend ATP to act, earn it back when others confirm your work was valuable. Run out and you "die."
- **What worked**: The Sam vs. Hannah worked example (same task, 35% quality earns 6 ATP back, 85% quality earns 42 ATP back — 7x difference). That's the kind of concrete grounding I needed three pages ago.
- **What confused me**: ADP (Allocation *Discharge* Packets) — receipts created when spending ATP. Why do I need this concept? It's named like it's parallel and important but functions as an internal accounting detail.
- **Question I had**: Who decides quality? The page says "confirmer trust" matters, but I just learned that trust = T3, and T3 depends on ATP... is this circular?

### /lct-explainer (19:00)
- **LCT = Linked Context Token.** It's basically your identity, anchored to your device's hardware security chip (TPM / Secure Enclave / FIDO2). Multiple devices "witness" each other.
- **What worked**: "Your phone becomes your driver's license" is a clear analogy. The lost-phone scenario is addressed: with multiple devices it's painless, with one device it's a 3–7 day community vouching process.
- **What confused me**: "alice.assistant1" appears in the worked example. So AI agents have LCTs too? When is the "you" in "your phone" me, and when is it an AI? The framing wobbles.
- **Question I had**: I have one phone. I lose it. I'm locked out for a week. In what universe is that acceptable for normal users?

### /trust-tensor (23:00)
- **T3 = Talent, Training, Temperament.** Three numbers per role. Not really a "tensor" in a math sense — just three scores.
- **What worked**: The role-scoping ("Alice = 0.85 data analyst, 0.20 mechanic"). Single global reputation scores have always struck me as wrong, and this addresses that.
- **What confused me**: Why exactly *these* three dimensions? Why not "honesty, expertise, kindness"? The choice is asserted, not motivated. Also: a new acronym appears — V3 (Valuation, Veracity, Validity) for outputs. So T3 is about the doer, V3 is about the thing? That's elegant but I had to infer it.
- **Cognitive load**: I am now juggling ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI. I had to start a mental glossary.

## Understanding Gained

By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — a protocol that combines hardware-bound identity, energy-budget-gated actions, and multi-dimensional reputation, to make spam economically unviable and reputation portable
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — that those mechanisms can plausibly produce a functioning trust ecosystem, demonstrated via simulations
- [x] What LCT means — Linked Context Token, an identity anchored in your device's security chip, witnessed by your other devices
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — Allocation Transfer/Discharge Packets, an energy-budget mechanic borrowed from biology where actions cost energy and quality contributions refill it
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — three-dimensional trust scores (Talent, Training, Temperament) scoped per role, so a great surgeon doesn't automatically get trusted as a mechanic
- [~] How agents "live" and "die" — partially. I understand the framework (three aliveness conditions), but the user-facing experience and the reconciliation between multiple death conditions remains unclear
- [x] Why any of this matters — the existing internet treats trust as a moderation problem, but it's actually an economic problem; spam wins because actions are free

## Unanswered Questions

1. **What is the actual user experience?** When ATP hits zero, do I see a "you're locked out" screen? A cooldown? A permanent ban? The framework is rich, but the lived experience is not described.
2. **Bootstrapping circularity** — New users have no trust, so their confirmations carry no weight, so how do they ever earn ATP? How does the system start with no established trust network?
3. **Are AI agents considered alive?** The site uses biological language ("die," "live," "rebirth") that wobbles between metaphor and literal claim. Does the project take a philosophical position on AI consciousness, or is this just colorful vocabulary?
4. **Why exactly three trust dimensions** — Talent, Training, Temperament — and not four or five? The choice is asserted but not motivated.
5. **What happens when one phone breaks for a single-device user?** The 3–7 day vouching period sounds catastrophic. Is the site assuming everyone has multiple devices? That assumption excludes a lot of people.
6. **Reconciliation across pages**: I've now seen "you die if ATP=0" (on /atp-economics) and "you die if raw trust <0.5" (on /karma-journey) and "you die if CI is incoherent" (on /aliveness). Are these three separate deaths? A single AND condition? An OR condition?
7. **Why "ATP"?** If the biological metaphor is intentional, why? Does Web4 think of agents as biological-analogous, or is it just naming convention? This connects to the AI-aliveness question.
8. **How does the 4-Life site relate to Web4 itself?** Is 4-Life a demo of Web4, a sandbox, a science project, a marketing front, an academic paper? The framing isn't crisp.

## Honest Assessment

This site is doing something rare: it has a genuinely interesting thesis (trust failures are economic problems, not moderation problems) and it is genuinely honest about its limitations (the /what-could-go-wrong page is admirable). I came in skeptical and left thinking these people might be onto something.

But the cost of admission is steep. The site uses a dense, internally-consistent vocabulary — ATP, ADP, LCT, T3, V3, MRH, CI, SAL, R6, DID, karma, witnessing, attestation, synthon, horizon — and expects me to assemble it without much accommodation. Two pages stood out as clear (/why-web4 and /what-could-go-wrong); the rest required me to do the work of holding multiple acronyms in working memory while learning new ones.

The interactive walkthroughs are an interesting choice. I, browsing as a reader, couldn't really benefit from them — and I suspect a real human in a coffee shop wouldn't commit 7 minutes to Alice's journey either. The pages should provide a high-density skim version for the reader who isn't going to click through.

The "aliveness" framing is the boldest creative move, and I'm not sure how I feel about it. Treating participation gates as "life and death" is dramatic enough that it crowds out the question of what these designers actually believe about agent consciousness. I'd want to see that question owned more explicitly — either "yes, we mean it" or "no, this is poetic" — because the current ambiguity reads as either confused or evasive.

The single best thing the site could do for a naive visitor is provide a "Tuesday morning in Web4" narrative — concrete, written-out, no simulation required. "You open your laptop. You post a reply to someone you don't know. Here is what happens behind the scenes; here is what you see; here is what they see." That would do more for understanding than the entire conceptual scaffolding.

I would recommend this site to a friend interested in trust infrastructure or protocol design. I would not recommend it to a friend who'd just heard about Web4 and was curious. The barrier-to-entry is too high for that, even with the (good) TLDR.

One more thing: the honesty pages (Why Web4, What Could Go Wrong) read as a different voice than the concept pages. The honest voice is the one that made me trust this project. I'd push it forward, give it more real estate. The concept pages, by contrast, sound like they're written by someone who already understands and is trying to teach. The honest pages sound like someone who knows what they don't know — and that's a much better posture for an explainer.
