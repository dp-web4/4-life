## Journey

### Landing Page (00:00)
- **First impression**: Clean and oriented. The headline "4-Life: A Trust-Native Internet Lab" plus the immediate "The Internet Has a Trust Problem" gave me a reason to keep reading before any jargon arrived. I appreciated that it tells me upfront this is *research, not a product* and "not a blockchain, not a Web3 successor" — it pre-empted my skepticism.
- **Clicked**: The single recommended button, "Start here: 2-minute TL;DR." There were many competing CTAs (Explore Topics, What it looks like · 5 min, Structured learning path, Read the spec, See what's live) but the TL;DR was clearly the primary one, so I didn't feel torn about where to go. Good.
- **Confusion points**: The five foundational concepts are shown as acronyms (LCT/ATP/T3/MRH/CI) right on the landing page. As a blank slate these meant nothing yet, but they were paired with plain-English labels (Identity, Energy, Trust, Neighborhood, Consistency), so it read as a preview rather than a wall. Fine.

### TL;DR (03:00)
- **What worked**: This is the best page for a newcomer. "The internet has no memory. A spammer can create a new account in seconds." — that one line did more to motivate the whole thing than anything else. Then "What if every online action had a cost, every identity was tied to physical hardware, and your reputation followed you everywhere — permanently?" Now I understood the *shape* of the idea.
- It introduced ATP, LCT, T3 with one-line definitions each, and gently linked MRH/CI deeper without forcing them on me.
- **Clicked**: It pointed me to "First Contact" as "the fastest way to feel how Web4 actually works." I followed that.

### First Contact (06:00)
- It's an interactive 5-act walkthrough of "Alice" — join, build reputation, spam mistake, recovery, death, karma rebirth. Two numbers track on screen: Energy/ATP (starts 100) and Trust (starts 0.50).
- **What worked**: The promise that "each concept is explained in the story the moment Alice runs into it" is the right teaching model.
- **Confusion**: Before the walkthrough begins, there's a block of four jargon-heavy concept summaries (Trust/T3, ATP, LCT, CI). There's a "Lost in the acronyms?" escape link, but the upfront summary still front-loads "Linked Context Token," "coherence index," "hardware-bound identity" before the story has earned them. I'd rather just be dropped into Alice's story and learn as I go.

### Why Web4 (11:00)
- **What worked**: Genuinely strong problem framing *before* mechanics. "The Problem" section lists four things I already recognize (spam is free, reputation is trapped in silos, bad actors get unlimited fresh starts, AI makes verification harder). "Creating accounts is free. Sending messages is free. There's no cost to flooding platforms with garbage." — that grounded the whole pitch in my own experience.
- The FAQ is huge (40+ questions). Reassuring that the hard questions are answered, but also a signal this page is *long* — easily 10,000+ words. A true first-timer could lose momentum partway down.

### Karma Journey (16:00)
- This is the big interactive one: live multiple lives, make cooperative/selfish/bold choices, watch trust and energy evolve, die, get reborn seeded by past performance.
- **What worked (notably improved-feeling)**: The two-deaths distinction is now crisp and easy to find. Energy death (ATP→0 while raw trust ≥0.5) = recoverable. Trust collapse (raw trust <0.5) = "the one door that doesn't reopen: society rejects rebirth." This was one of the clearest things on the site — I never had to guess which death was which.
- The raw-vs-effective trust split is also clearly labeled: raw = (Talent+Training+Temperament)/3 governs survival; effective = raw × CI² governs your karma tier at death.
- **One genuine snag (see Friction)**: In the worked example, raw trust = 0.500 but effective trust = 0.361 (because CI=0.85, and 0.50×0.85²=0.361). The survival threshold is 0.5. So I'm staring at a number (0.361) that is *below* the survival line (0.5), while the page insists "You're not dead!" I had to stop and reason out that only *raw* trust is compared to 0.5, and effective is a different axis used only at death. I got there, but it was a "wait — am I dead or not?" beat.

### Running Now (21:00)
- This page tours software that's apparently already running.
- **What worked (and I want to flag this as a win)**: There's an orientation banner right at the very top: "New to Web4? This page tours software that's already running, so it assumes the vocabulary (LCT, trust tensors, witness chain)... If those terms are new, start with the 2-minute TL;DR and come back." As a naive arrival, this caught me immediately and told me exactly where to go. I did *not* end up lost — I knew this page wasn't for me yet.
- **Still confusing if I stay**: If I ignore the banner, the body dumps "Cryptographic identity (LCT) + an encrypted vault... Witness chain — a hash-linked, tamper-evident record... T3/V3 tensors, sealed channels, MCP server." It reads like internal documentation. But — crucially — the banner gave me an honest exit before I hit that.
- **A small meta-thought**: the banner is good, but it left me mildly wondering "why does this page exist if I'm told to leave it first?" A one-line "come back here once the terms click — this is the proof it's real, not just theory" might turn the redirect into a *promise* rather than a *dead end*.

### A Day in Web4 (25:00)
- 10 concrete everyday moments (phone unlock, neighborhood post, hiring a freelancer, reading reviews). "Hi everyone! Just moved to Oak Street. Anyone know a good plumber?" (costs 3 ATP, visible to 89 members).
- **What worked**: This is the gentlest, most accessible page on the whole site, and a sharp contrast to Running Now. It opens with "One thing to know before the numbers start" and defines the 0–1 trust scale before using it. Every scenario links to a deeper explainer without requiring it. If anything, *this* feels like a stronger "what it looks like" landing than the deployed-reality pages.

### ATP Economics (27:00)
- **What worked**: Plain language first — "ATP is your energy budget, a finite per-agent allowance" before any formulas. Burn fee (105 spent → recipient gets 100, sender pays) and the ~7x quality gap were both clear.
- **Minor snag**: ADP is first introduced as "think of ATP as cash and ADP as the receipt you get when you spend it" — metaphor first, but the *reason receipts matter* ("others confirm your work → unlocks fresh ATP back") arrives a beat later. I momentarily wondered why I should care about a receipt at all. Small.

## Understanding Gained
By the end of this session, I understood:
- [x] What Web4 is (at a high level) — trust verified by behavior, identity bound to hardware, actions cost energy
- [x] What 4-Life is trying to demonstrate — an interactive lab to *feel* those mechanics
- [x] What LCT means — hardware-bound identity / "witness constellation," though the full "Linked Context Token" stays jargony
- [x] What ATP/ADP means — energy budget + the receipt that unlocks it back
- [x] What Trust Tensors are — T3: Talent/Training/Temperament, scored per role
- [x] How agents "live" and "die" — clearest part: two deaths (energy = recoverable, trust = permanent)
- [x] Why any of this matters — the Why Web4 problem framing landed solidly

## Unanswered Questions
1. On Karma Journey, if effective trust (0.361) is below 0.5, what *would* it take to actually fail by the effective measure — or is effective trust never a fail condition, only a tier seed? The page implies the latter but I inferred it rather than being told.
2. The "Running Now" pages claim software is already running — but as a newcomer steered away, I never learned *what I'd actually see or do* there in plain terms. A one-line newcomer-safe summary ("it's a chat/vault app two AIs use to talk with verified trust") would let me leave with a mental picture instead of a blank.

## Honest Assessment
This was a good visit. The front door (Landing → TL;DR → First Contact) is well-sequenced and the single recommended CTA meant I never felt lost about where to start. The Why Web4 problem framing is the emotional hook and it works. The standout is the Karma Journey's death model — recoverable vs permanent death is now genuinely one of the clearest concepts on the site.

Two things to celebrate from a UX standpoint: (1) the orientation banner on "Running Now" did exactly what a naive visitor needs — it caught me before I drowned in LCT/T3/V3/MCP jargon and pointed me back to the TL;DR. That's a real save. (2) "A Day in Web4" is a beautifully gentle on-ramp.

The one spot I genuinely stumbled was the Karma Journey worked example: seeing effective trust (0.361) printed *below* the survival number (0.5) while being told I'm alive made me briefly doubt I understood the rules. The two numbers are labeled correctly — the problem is the example's specific values make the secondary number look like a failing primary score. A single clarifying clause right at that number would close it.

Net: I'd come back, and I'd send this to a curious friend — with the instruction "click the 2-minute TL;DR first, ignore the 'what's live' tiles until you've done that."
